Abstract 24 Torpor is an adaptive hypometabolic state that enables homeotherm to survive periods of 25 energetic challenge. This strategy ranges from short bouts of daily torpor to prolonged 26 hibernation. During torpor, animals markedly suppress metabolic rate, body temperature, heart 27 rate, and respiration, while retaining the ability to rewarm. Torpor therefore comprises two 28 critical transitions -entry into a hypometabolic state and active rewarming- both essential for 29 organismal viability. Although neural mechanisms controlling torpor entry have begun to 30 emerge, the circuits that initiate active rewarming and restore euthermia remain poorly defined. 31 Here we identify corticotropin-releasing hormone (Crh)-expressing neurons in the anterodorsal 32 preoptic area (ADP) as a key population for rewarming from fasting-induced torpor in mice. 33 These neurons become active around natural rewarming and are selectively required to limit the 34 depth and duration of torpor. Unlike pathways mediating acute cold defence, stress 35 hyperthermia, or LPS-induced fever, this circuit is dedicated to promoting timely recovery to 36 euthermia. Closed-loop optogenetic activation of ADP Crh neurons during torpor entry rapidly 37 initiates rewarming, and thermographic recordings show that brown adipose tissue (BAT) 38 thermogenesis precedes locomotor arousal. ADP Crh neurons are predominantly GABAergic and 39 project monosynaptically to the lateral preoptic area, whose terminal activation is sufficient to 40 increase body temperature and locomotor activity. Finally, we find robust activation of ADP Crh 41 neurons during rewarming in a hibernator, suggesting conserved logic for exiting deep torpor. 42 Together, our results define a discrete preoptic circuit that drives recovery from torpor and 43 provide a framework for understanding and potentially controlling timely rewarming from 44 profound hypothermia. 45 46 47 48

Main 50 Torpor is a regulated and reversible hypometabolic state that enables homeotherms to survive 51 energetic challenges such as cold and food scarcity. This adaptive strategy is widespread across 52 mammalian and avian lineages and ranges from short daily torpor bouts to prolonged seasonal 53 hibernation 1. During torpor, animals markedly suppress metabolic rate, body temperature, heart 54 rate, and respiration, while maintaining the ability to rewarm and return to euthermia. 55 Throughout torpor, the central nervous system operates in a hypometabolic state with reduced 56 electrical and metabolic activity 2,3 . Despite this global suppression, the brain retains the capacity 57 to initiate brown adipose tissue (BAT)-dependent thermogenesis to promote rewarming 4. While 58 recent studies have begun to define the neural circuits that induce torpor entry 5–9 , the specific 59 neural mechanisms that trigger the thermogenic drive required for active exit from torpor remain 60 largely unknown. 61 Here, we combine brain-wide activity mapping, a spatial transcriptomic atlas, and causal 62 manipulations to define a hypothalamic circuit that governs torpor rewarming. We identify 63 corticotropin-releasing hormone ( Crh )-expressing neurons in the anterodorsal preoptic area 64 (ADP) and their projections to the lateral preoptic region as a discrete pathway that limits the 65 depth of torpor and promotes a timely return to euthermia. 66 67 

Brain-wide mapping of rewarming-associated regions 68 To identify the neural circuits involved in rewarming from torpor in mice, we performed a brain-69 wide mapping of active neurons. Mice were housed at 16 °C and assigned to fed or fasted 70 conditions at the onset of the dark phase. We monitored body surface temperature in real time 71 using an infrared camera. In this screening, torpor bouts were defined as periods in which body 72 surface temperature remained below 28 °C for at least 5 min. All fasted mice entered torpor, and 73 brains were collected approximately 30 to 60 minutes after the onset of rewarming (Fig. 1a, 74 Extended Data Fig. 1a). To minimize circadian effects, we processed fed and fasted mice in pairs 75 and collected the fed control brain at the corresponding time point. The collected brains were 76 subsequently subjected to immunostaining with the anti-phospho S6 (pS6) antibody 10 , an 77

ADP Crh neurons promote timely rewarming 108 To dissect the functional contributions of the two candidate populations, we employed 109 chemogenetic inhibition. We first targeted ADP neurons expressing Nos1 (ADP Nos1 ) by injecting 110 a Cre-dependent AAV encoding the Gi-coupled DREADD hM4Di into the ADP of Nos1-cre 111 mice. Inhibiting ADP Nos1 neurons produced no detectable changes in torpor depth and bout 112 duration (Extended Data Fig. 2). We therefore focused on ADP neurons expressing Crh 113 (ADP Crh ) in subsequent loss-of-function experiments. To inhibit ADP Crh neurons, we injected a 114 Cre-dependent AAV encoding hM4Di-mCherry into the ADP of Crh-iCre mice (Fig. 2a, b). 115 After intraperitoneal implantation of a temperature logger, mice were fasted at dark onset and 116 injected with clozapine N-oxide (CNO) 3.5 h after food removal (Fig. 2c). In Cre-dependent 117 mCherry-only controls, CNO did not alter torpor, indicating that CNO itself does not account for 118 the phenotype (Fig. 2c). In contrast, inhibiting ADP Crh neurons prolonged and deepened the 119 torpor bout (Fig. 2c-e, Extended Data Fig. 3a). Consistently, taCasp3-mediated ablation 15 of 120 ADP Crh neurons produced longer and deeper torpor bouts compared with controls (Fig. 2f-j, 121 Extended Data Fig. 3b). Together, these results indicate that ADP Crh neurons are required to limit 122 the depth and duration of torpor and to promote timely rewarming. 123 To determine whether ADP Crh neurons function as general thermoregulatory effectors or are 124 specifically involved in torpor rewarming, ADP Crh neuron-ablated mice were exposed to various 125 thermogenic stimuli. The ablation of ADP Crh neurons did not impair the baseline body 126 temperature (Extended Data Fig. 4a) nor the maintenance of body temperature during acute cold 127 exposure at 4 °C for 12 hours (Extended Data Fig. 4b). Additionally, both cage change stress-128 induced hyperthermia and the fever response to lipopolysaccharide were indistinguishable 129 between ablated and control mice (Extended Data Fig. 4c, d). These results indicate that ADP Crh 130 neurons are not required for cold defence, stress responses, or fever, but instead play a specific 131 role in mediating torpor rewarming. 132 133

Having established the necessity of ADP Crh neurons for natural rewarming, we next examined 135 whether their activation is sufficient to drive rewarming from torpor. We bilaterally injected a 136 Cre-dependent AAV encoding ChRmine 16 into the ADP of Crh-iCre mice and implanted optical 137 fibres above the injection site (Fig. 3a, b). Because the timing of torpor entry varies across 138 individual animals, we used a closed-loop system to deliver photostimulation with high temporal 139 precision. Light was delivered continuously whenever maximum body surface temperature 140 remained below 33 °C (Fig. 3b, c). In Cre-dependent hrGFP controls, light delivery did not alter 141 the torpor–arousal cycle, indicating that photostimulation alone does not affect torpor phenotype 142 (Fig. 3d). In contrast, closed-loop activation of ADP Crh neurons rapidly initiated rewarming, 143 increasing the nadir temperature (Fig. 3d–f). Post hoc histology showed robust c-Fos expression 144 in ADP Crh neurons, confirming the specific activation of the targeted population (Fig. 3b, 145 Extended Data Fig. 5). Taken together, these results indicate that activation of ADP Crh neurons is 146 sufficient to trigger efficient rewarming from torpor. To investigate the effector mechanism 147 engaged by ADP Crh neurons, we stimulated this population under euthermic conditions. 148 Optogenetic activation of ADP Crh neurons increased body surface temperature by 1.24 ± 0.30 °C 149 and increased locomotor activity (Fig. 3g-k). To determine whether rewarming is driven by 150 brown adipose tissue (BAT)-dependent thermogenesis or is secondary to locomotor activity, we 151 next performed thermographic recordings at 1 Hz during optogenetic activation of ADP Crh 152 neurons. Thermal imaging revealed a rapid increase in temperature in the interscapular region, 153 the anatomical site of BAT (Fig. 3l, m). Crucially, quantitative analysis demonstrated that the 154 increase in BAT temperature significantly preceded the onset of locomotor activity (Fig. 3n, o). 155 This temporal dissociation indicates that ADP Crh neurons trigger rewarming primarily via BAT-156 dependent thermogenesis, rather than as a consequence of behavioural arousal. 157 We next examined whether ADP Crh neurons are naturally recruited around torpor rewarming. To 158 monitor the temporal dynamics of ADP Crh neurons using fibre photometry calcium imaging, we 159 unilaterally injected a Cre-dependent AAV encoding jGCaMP8s into the ADP of Crh-iCre mice 160 and implanted an optical fibre above the injection site (Extended Data Fig. 6a-d). To avoid 161 confounding effects of temperature-dependent changes in the GCaMP signal, we analysed 162 calcium transient events using a local z-score calculated within a 5-min sliding window and 163 defined events as those exceeding 3 standard deviations (>3 s.d.). The ADP →LPO pathway mediates the increase in body temperature and locomotor 169 

activity 170 We next sought to identify the downstream pathway mediating the thermogenic response evoked 171 by activation of ADP Crh neurons. To delineate the output architecture of ADP Crh neurons, we 172 labelled them with a Cre-dependent EYFP and performed whole-brain clearing. Light-sheet 173 fluorescence imaging revealed widespread projections from the ADP to multiple hypothalamic 174 and brainstem nuclei (Fig. 4a). We then expressed synaptophysin-EGFP in ADP Crh neurons to 175 map putative presynaptic terminals with higher resolution (Fig. 4b). Fluorescence imaging 176 revealed dense terminal innervation in several candidate thermoregulatory regions, including the 177 lateral and dorsomedial hypothalamus (LH/DMH), supramammillary nucleus (SuM), and the 178 lateral preoptic area (LPO) (Fig. 4b). We next asked which projection is sufficient to induce the 179 thermogenic response. We expressed ChRmine in ADP Crh neurons and delivered bilateral 180 photostimulation to axon terminals in candidate target regions with dense terminal fields, such as 181 the LPO, LH/DMH, and SuM (Fig. 4c). Stimulation of ADP Crh terminals in the LPO robustly 182 increased body temperature and locomotor activity (Fig. 4d). In contrast, terminal stimulation in 183 the LH/DMH or SuM produced little or no change in body temperature and locomotor activity 184 (Fig. 4e, f). Fibre placements were verified histologically (Extended Data Fig. 7). These results 185 identify the ADP Crh →LPO pathway as a key downstream route sufficient to drive the 186 thermogenic and behavioural effects of ADP Crh neuron activation. Consistently, closed-loop 187 activation of the ADP Crh →LPO pathway during torpor rapidly triggered rewarming (Extended 188 Data Fig. 8). 189 To characterize the neurotransmitter phenotype of ADP Crh neurons, we next performed multiplex 190 in situ hybridization. This analysis revealed that ADP Crh neurons are predominantly GABAergic, 191 approximately 90% of Crh -positive cells co-expressed Slc32a1 (Vgat), whereas only ~15% co-192 expressed Slc17a6 (Vglut2) (Fig. 4g, h). Discussion 214 In this study, we identify ADP Crh neurons as a key population promoting rewarming from 215 fasting-induced torpor in mice. Loss- and gain-of-function manipulations show that these 216 neurons limit torpor depth and promote timely recovery to euthermia by engaging BAT 217 thermogenesis, which precedes locomotor arousal. This pathway acts through a GABAergic 218 projection to the lateral preoptic region and is recruited during rewarming in a hibernating 219 species, suggesting conserved logic for exiting deep torpor. 220

ADP Crh neurons were dispensable for acute cold defence, stress hyperthermia and LPS-induced 221 fever, yet were required to limit torpor depth and duration. Canonical models place thermogenic 222 control within a POA–DMH–RPa axis 17 , and several studies have implicated the DMH in torpor 223 induction 9,18,19 . Yet projection-specific stimulation in our study identified the LPO, but not the 224 DMH or LH, as a sufficient downstream target of ADP Crh neurons for promoting BAT-mediated 225 thermogenesis. Together, these findings suggest that torpor rewarming is not simply mediated by 226 re-engagement of a general thermoregulatory pathway. Instead, the ADP Crh →LPO projection 227 appears to selectively support timely exit from torpor. Whether this pathway ultimately 228 converges on classical DMH- and/or RPa-dependent thermogenic effectors remains unresolved. 229 The LPO contains intermingled populations implicated in torpor and sleep-related cooling, 230 including avMLPA Adcyap1 neurons 5 and subsets of galanin neurons 20,21 . Although the postsynaptic 231 targets remain unknown, ADP Crh inputs may promote rewarming by inhibiting LPO pathways 232 that stabilize hypothermia and quiescence, thereby permitting rapid thermogenesis and 233 behavioural arousal. 234 Rewarming is initiated despite a global reduction in neural activity during torpor. Therefore, the 235 circuitry that drives rewarming must remain excitable at low body temperature, and an 236 appropriate trigger must be able to engage it. One possibility is that ADP Crh neurons are activated 237 by ascending subcortical pathways that convey thermosensory information to the preoptic area 22 .238 Brainstem catecholaminergic neurons innervate the preoptic region and have been implicated in 239 torpor induction 7, suggesting that these inputs may modulate the activity of ADP Crh neurons 240 during rewarming. Identifying the presynaptic inputs to ADP Crh neurons via retrograde trans-241 synaptic tracing will be important for testing this idea. In addition to neural inputs, circulating 242 energy-balance signals may modulate the excitability of ADP Crh neurons. Leptin suppresses 243 torpor 23,24 , whereas ghrelin can deepen torpor bouts 25 , and the relatively permeable blood–brain 244 barrier of the hypothalamus raises the possibility of direct actions on ADP neurons. However, as 245 these hormones are unlikely to exhibit abrupt concentration changes during a single bout, they 246 may act as permissive gates rather than acute triggers of rewarming. 247 Together, our findings identify a preoptic circuit that promotes recovery from an extreme energy-248 saving state and suggest similar circuit logic in a hibernating species. 249

Torpor induction 570 To record core body temperature, mice were anaesthetized with isoflurane and intraperitoneally 571 implanted with a temperature logger (Star-Oddi, DST nano-T). The mice were then fasted, or 574 ad libitum fed for 24 hours. Water was available ad libitum for both groups throughout the 575 experiment. Data on core body temperature were collected and analysed by Mercury software 576 (Star-Oddi). 577 578

Brain sampling from Syrian Hamsters during torpor rewarming 590 4- or 8-week-old male Syrian hamsters were purchased from an outbred colony (Japan SLC., 591 Inc) and housed under summer-like long-photoperiod and warm conditions (24–25 °C with a 592 14/10 h light-dark cycle) with ad libitum access to water and food (MR standard diet, Nihon 593 Nosan). 3–4 individuals were housed per cage, and their body mass was recorded weekly when 594 the cages were replaced. 595 At 12 weeks old, all animals were transferred to individual polypropylene cages with ad libitum 596 access to water and food. The control group was continuously housed under summer-like 597 conditions after that. The TB logger (iButton, #DS1925L-F5, Maxim Integrated), calibrated by 598 the manufacturer, was coated with rubber (total mass approx. 3.5 g; Plasti Dip, Performix 599 Coatings) and surgically implanted into the abdominal cavity of hamsters to induce hibernation 600 under 2% isoflurane anaesthesia. The logger recorded Tb every 10 min with a resolution of 601 0.0625 °C. After surgery, animals were allowed to recover for 2 weeks. 602 For hibernation induction, the animals were transferred to winter-like short-photoperiod and cold 603 conditions (4–5 °C with an 8/16 h light-dark cycle) with ad libitum access to water and food. 604 Body mass was measured weekly, and cages were replaced every other week, except for 605 hibernating animals. 2–3 months after the transfer to winter-like conditions, animals began to 606 hibernate and displayed typical torpor-arousal cycles. 607 For collecting brain samples during the rewarming phase from deep torpor, hibernating hamsters 608 were set to cages in which the oxygen consumption rate of the animals was monitored in real-609 time using the respiratory gas analyser ARCO-2000 (ARCO System). 2 hours after observing a 610 significant increase in oxygen consumption, we monitored the oral temperature of rewarming 611 animals every 10 min using a wired temperature meter (BWT-100A, Bioresearch center). When 612 the detected oral temperature reached 30 °C (while, the iButton recorded Tb of 25–30 °C), the 613 animals were immediately perfused with PBS, followed by 4% PFA/PBS, under 2% isoflurane 614 anaesthesia. Data processing was performed using custom-written Python code. Large-amplitude electrical 863 noise artifacts were removed using a despiking procedure that detected rapid local transients and 864 residual high-amplitude outliers and corrected them based on a median-filtered trace. To improve 865 the signal-to-noise ratio, the despiked signals were low-pass filtered with a Butterworth filter (5 866 Hz cutoff). To correct for photobleaching, the filtered 405-nm and 470-nm signals were 867 independently baseline-estimated using adaptive iteratively reweighted penalized least squares 868 (airPLS), and the estimated baselines were subtracted to obtain baseline-corrected traces. To 869 remove movement-related artifacts, the baseline-corrected 470-nm signal was regressed against 870 the baseline-corrected 405-nm signal using least-squares linear regression, and the fitted 405-nm 871 component was subtracted from the baseline-corrected 470-nm signal to obtain a residual trace. 872 The residual trace was then divided by the fitted 405-nm signal to calculate ΔF/F, and the 873 resulting ΔF/F trace was z-scored across the recording. 874 Because the z-scored ΔF/F trace retained temperature-dependent signal changes, calcium events 875 were detected using a local z-score calculated within a 5-min sliding window. Peaks were 876 identified as local z-score events exceeding 3 standard deviations (s.d.).
