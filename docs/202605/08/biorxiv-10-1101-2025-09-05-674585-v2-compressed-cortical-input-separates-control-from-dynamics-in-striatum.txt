Abstract 1

The dorsolateral striatum (DLS) supports diverse time-sensitive behaviors—action chunking, duration estimation, 2

and motor timing—yet no single framework is able to explain all of these phenomena. Here, we propose that 3

the massive convergence of cortical projections onto the striatum provides such a framework. We develop 4

a corticostriatal neural network model in which a recurrent cortical module communicates with a recurrent 5

striatal module through a low-dimensional, noisy bottleneck, with the whole system trained via reinforcement 6

learning. Across three DLS-associated tasks, compression produces a consistent computational motif whereby 7

cortex provides low-dimensional control signals while the striatum generates stable, time-encoding dynamics. 8

This separation gives rise to chunking behavior with action slipping, intensity-biased duration judgements with 9

stimulus-modulated time coding, and stereotyped motor timing programs. Perturbation of the compressed cortical 10 

signal causally shifts behavior while preserving sequential structure in striatum activity. In sum, our results 11 

unify information-theoretic and dynamical systems perspectives of basal ganglia function to link anatomical 12 

compression to a variety of temporally-sensitive sensorimotor behaviors implicated in the DLS. 13 

Introduction 14 

The dorsolateral striatum (DLS), the sensorimotor input territory of the basal ganglia, has been implicated in a 15 

remarkably broad range of time-sensitive behaviors: the acquisition and execution of action sequences (Graybiel, 16 

1998; X. Jin & Costa, 2015; Martiros et al., 2018), the estimation and comparison of elapsed durations (Gouv ˆea 17 

et al., 2015; Monteiro et al., 2023; Rodrigues et al., 2024; Toso et al., 2021), the temporal coordination of learned 18 

motor habits (Dhawale et al., 2021; Hidalgo-Balbuena et al., 2019; Rueda-Orozco & Robbe, 2015), and more broadly, 19 

the selection of actions appropriate to the current moment within a temporally extended behavioral context 20 

(D. Z. Jin et al., 2009; Klaus et al., 2017; Markowitz et al., 2023). A unifying theme across these functions is the 21 

need to produce the right action at the right time (Haimerl et al., 2025). Yet despite decades of work exploring 22 

each of these phenomena individually, a computational framework explaining why a single brain region supports 23 

such diverse temporally-sensitive functions has remained elusive. Here, we propose that the answer lies in a 24 

defining anatomical feature of the corticostriatal pathway: the massive convergence of cortical projections onto 25 

the striatum. 26

Neural projections from the cortex to the striatum undergo a dramatic dimensionality reduction: the number of 27 

cortical neurons projecting to the striatum exceeds the number of receiving striatal neurons by more than an order 28 

of magnitude (Figure 1A). The principal neurons of the striatum, medium spiny neurons (MSNs), which comprise 29 

approximately 95% of the striatal population (Kita & Kitai, 1988), possess large, extensively branching dendritic 30 

arbors studded with spines that receive convergent excitatory input from thousands of cortical neurons (Kincaid 31 

et al., 1998; Wilson & Groves, 1980). Bar-Gad, Morris, & Bergman (2003) termed the resulting population-level 32 

compression “anatomical funneling” and showed that information transmitted through the basal ganglia is 33 

decorrelated relative to cortical input, consistent with dimensionality reduction at the corticostriatal interface. 34 

Their theoretical framework of “reinforcement-driven dimensionality reduction” (RDDR) proposed that this 35 

compression is optimized for reward-relevant dimensions — akin to PCA, but with a loss function defined 36 

by reinforcement rather than variance explained (Bar-Gad, Havazelet-Heimer, et al., 2003). However, RDDR 37 

characterizes corticostriatal compression in static, information-theoretic terms; it does not address how this 38 

compression shapes the dynamics of the downstream circuit. 39 

More recent work has shifted focus from the static properties of corticostriatal compression to the dynamics 40 

of the striatal circuit itself. Striatal population activity evolves along reproducible trajectories in neural state 41 

space, and the timecourse of this evolution provides a temporal basis for the computations the striatum performs 42 

(Monteiro et al., 2023; Tsao et al., 2022). Cortical and thalamic input appears to parametrically control these 43 

dynamics — modulating the speed and gain of striatal trajectories — while the striatum retains autonomy over 44 

the structure of the activity patterns themselves (Hidalgo-Balbuena et al., 2019; Lee et al., 2019; Murray & Escola, 45 

2017). Yet this dynamical systems perspective has developed largely independently of the information-theoretic 46 

account of corticostriatal compression, and the two have rarely been connected within a single computational 47 

framework. A framework that bridges these ideas could explain how a single anatomical motif simultaneously 48 

gives rise to both the behavioral and dynamical signatures observed across DLS-dependent tasks. 49 Cortex Striatum MSN A CObservation Cortex 2-4 Neurons Noise Striatum V

We propose that corticostriatal compression is precisely this bridging principle: dimensionality reduction at the 50 

corticostriatal interface forces a separation of function in which the cortex provides low-dimensional control 51 

signals while the striatum generates intrinsic stable time-encoding dynamics, and it is this separation that gives 52 

rise to the structured temporal behaviors associated with the DLS (Figure 1C). 53 

To instantiate this hypothesis, we developed a multiregion recurrent neural network model inspired by the 54 

architecture of Mizes et al. (2024), who modeled the corticostriatal pathway as a cortical module projecting to a 55 

recurrent striatal module, with sensory cues provided to cortex but not directly to the striatum (reflecting their 56 

previous finding that DLS activity can be insensitive to specific sensory cues, Mizes et al. 2023). Our model (Fig-57 

ure 1B) consists of a cortical module (a recurrent neural network with 64 units) that communicates with a striatal 58 

module (an LSTM with 64 units) through a low-dimensional noisy bottleneck ( 2–4 units). The striatal module 59 

produces both a policy and a value estimate, and the system is trained end-to-end via reinforcement learning using 60

of RDDR — cortical information is compressed through a capacity-limited channel optimized by reinforcement 70 

— while simultaneously implementing a dynamical system in which compressed cortical input parametrically 71 

controls the temporal evolution of striatal trajectories. 72 

We test this model on three time-sensitive sensorimotor behaviors associated with the DLS (Figure 1D): a 73 

sensorimotor chunking task inspired by human behavioral experiments (Lai et al., 2025), a duration comparison 74 

task based on recordings from rat DLS (Rodrigues et al., 2024; Toso et al., 2021), and a motor timing task (Hidalgo-75 

Balbuena et al., 2019). Across all three tasks, compression produces a consistent computational motif: the 76 

cortex provides low-dimensional, task-relevant control signals while the striatum intrinsically generates stable, 77 

time-encoding dynamics. This separation gives rise to human-like chunking with action slipping, intensity-78 

biased duration judgements with stimulus-modulated time coding, and stereotyped motor timing programs 79 

with perturbation-sensitive timing. In each domain, perturbation of the compressed cortical signal causally 80 

shifts behavior while preserving the sequential structure of striatal activity. Our results thus provide a unifying 81 

mechanistic link between corticostriatal compression and the diverse temporally-sensitive functions of the DLS, 82 

bridging the information-theoretic and dynamical systems frameworks that have each been used separately to 83 

study this circuit. 84 

Results 85 

Sensorimotor Chunking 86 

We first asked whether corticostriatal compression can account for sensorimotor chunking, a core DLS-associated 87 

behavior in which frequently co-occurring actions are grouped into single behavioral routines. To test this, 88 

we trained our model on a task inspired by Lai et al. (2025) in which participants observe sequences of stimuli 89 

drawn from a set of 16 possible items and must select the corresponding action for each stimulus to receive a 90 

reward (Figure 2A,B). The key manipulation is that eight of these stimuli (S0–S7, “chunk stimuli”) frequently 91 

appear in a fixed temporal order, forming a “chunk” during structured episodes, while the remaining “non-chunk” 92 

stimuli always appear in random order. Although chunk stimuli can appear individually in random contexts, 93 

they more frequently co-occur together in their fixed sequence via structured episodes (see Methods for full task 94 

parameterization). This design creates an opportunity for behavioral policy compression: rather than treating 95 

each stimulus independently, an efficient learner can recognize and exploit the predictable chunk sequence. 96 

Human performance on this task reveals two signatures of capacity-limited processing that serve as qualitative 97 

targets for our model. First, performance decreases as the total number of stimuli increases, reflecting the 98 

cognitive cost of maintaining more stimulus-response mappings (Figure 2C, light vs. dark bars). Second, humans 99 

perform significantly better on structured trials containing the chunk than on random trials, demonstrating that 100 

they successfully exploit the temporal regularity (Figure 2C, red vs. blue bars). These two effects — degraded 101 

performance with increased memory load and improved performance with predictable structure — are hallmarks 102 

of policy compression in capacity-limited systems (Lai & Gershman, 2021). 103 

To test whether corticostriatal compression could reproduce these behavioral signatures, we trained models 104 

with varying bottleneck dimensions and evaluated performance on both structured and random trials across 105 

set sizes, matching the conditions used in the human experiments. The uncompressed model (64-dimensional 106 

bottleneck) performed equally well across all conditions, showing neither the set size effect nor the structured trial 107 

advantage (Figure 2D, right), indicating that without a communication bottleneck, the model simply memorizes 108 

all stimulus-response mappings independently. By contrast, the compressed model (4-dimensional bottleneck) 109

Structured Behavior and Striatal Time-Encoding Dynamics 113 

If the compressed model genuinely chunks actions rather than simply showing a statistical advantage on structured 114 

trials, it should exhibit “action slipping” — the classic chunking phenomenon in which a familiar sequence is 115 

executed inflexibly even when inappropriate (Norman, 1981). We tested this by presenting partial chunk initiations 116 

(e.g., stimuli S0–S2) followed by random stimuli requiring different actions. The compressed model inflexibly 117 

completed the entire chunk sequence, ignoring subsequent non-chunk stimuli (Figure 3A, top), whereas the 118 

uncompressed model remained responsive to the actual stimuli presented (Figure 3A, bottom). This action slipping 119 

confirms that the compressed model has consolidated the chunk into a single behavioral unit that, once triggered, 120 

runs to completion. 121 

Corticostriatal compression reduces the sensory information available to the striatum, which should force the 122 

network to rely more on internally generated temporal dynamics. Consistent with this reasoning, stimulus 123 

identity was significantly harder to decode from the hidden state in models with smaller bottlenecks (Figure S1), 124 

suggesting that the striatum must rely more on internally generated activity patterns. We therefore examined the 125 

structure of the striatum’s dynamics directly using PCA of the hidden state trajectories (Figure 3B). Importantly, 126 

both the compressed and uncompressed models have the same striatal dimensionality (64 units); they differ 127 

only in the dimensionality of the input the striatum receives from cortex. In the compressed model, chunk 128 

stimuli evolved along a consistent, smooth trajectory while non-chunk stimuli collapsed into a single cluster, with 129 

position along the trajectory corresponding to elapsed time. By contrast, the uncompressed model’s striatum 130

To quantify these observations, we developed two metrics computed on the full 64-dimensional hidden state 132 

(Figure 3C): trajectory variance , the mean trial-to-trial variability of the hidden state at each timestep, and time 133 

encoding , the cross-validated R2 of a linear decoder trained to predict elapsed time from the hidden state. Across 134 

both the chunking task and a control task where stimuli were drawn fully IID, models with corticostriatal 135 

compression showed significantly lower trajectory variance and significantly higher time encoding than models 136 

without compression (Figure 3D; all comparisons p < 0.001 , two-sided t-tests, 11 seeds per condition). The IID 137 

result is particularly noteworthy: this environment contained no external temporal structure whatsoever, yet the 138 

compressed model still produced stable, time-encoding trajectories. These findings held when trial lengths were 139 

varied randomly, dissociating absolute time from task progress (Figure S2), and were robust to using a vanilla 140 

RNN instead of an LSTM for the striatal module (Figure S3). Together, these results confirm that corticostriatal 141 

compression produces stable time-encoding striatal dynamics across a range of task and architectural conditions. 142 

A particularly striking result emerged from models that had never been trained. Random-weight models with a 143 

corticostriatal bottleneck already exhibited strong time-encoding trajectories, whereas random-weight models 144 

without a bottleneck did not (Figure S4). Both models use the exact same recurrent architecture and differ only 145 

in the dimensionality of the striatum’s input: the bottleneck model’s cortical input is randomly projected into 146 

a lower-dimensional space, whereas the non-bottleneck model retains the full cortical dimensionality. When 147 

input dimensionality is sufficiently constrained, the variance of the hidden state is dominated by the network’s 148 

internal dynamics rather than by input-driven variability, producing stable trajectories that implicitly encode 149 

time. This finding demonstrates that the capacity for temporal encoding arises from corticostriatal compression 150 

itself, existing independently of and prior to task-specific learning. 151

We first confirmed that chunk and non-chunk stimuli occupy separable regions of bottleneck space using PCA 158 

(Figure 4A). We defined the “chunk direction” as the vector from the non-chunk centroid to the chunk centroid, 159 

and applied additive perturbations of varying magnitude along this direction during random episodes — trials in 160 

which stimuli are randomly drawn and thus never contain the chunk sequence (Figure 4B). In the compressed 161 

model (4-dimensional bottleneck), perturbation along the chunk direction induced a graded increase in correctly 162 

timed chunk actions per episode (Figure 4C). Critically, perturbations along random orthogonal directions in 163 

the same bottleneck space had no such effect, confirming direction specificity. The uncompressed model (64-164 

dimensional bottleneck) showed a weaker effect along the chunk direction, consistent with the control signal 165 

being less consolidated in the absence of compression. These results demonstrate that corticostriatal compression 166 

consolidates chunk identity into a single direction in the bottleneck — a low-dimensional cortical control signal 167 

sufficient to drive the striatum’s intrinsic dynamics to produce a structured, temporally extended action sequence. 168 

Duration Judgments 169 

If the separation of cortical control from striatal dynamics is a general principle of corticostriatal function, it 170 

should extend beyond chunking to other time-sensitive behaviors associated with the DLS. We next tested our 171 

framework on a duration discrimination task in which Rodrigues et al. (2024), re-analyzing data from Toso et al. 172 

(2021), showed that DLS time-encoding dynamics are modulated by stimulus intensity and that this modulation 173 

systematically biases temporal judgements. 174 

Structured Behavior and Striatal Time-Encoding Dynamics 175 

In this task, Toso et al. (2021) trained rats to compare two sequential vibrotactile stimuli ( S1, S2), each varying 176 

independently in both duration ( T1, T2) and intensity ( I1, I2), and to report which stimulus was longer (Figure 5A). 177

judgements were systematically biased by the intensity of the second stimulus: higher I2 led rats to report T2 as 179 

longer, as if intense stimuli were perceived as lasting longer. Critically, this behavioral bias had a neural correlate 180 

in the DLS — decoded time estimates during S2 were modulated by stimulus intensity, with higher intensity 181 

producing faster progression of the neural time estimate and thus inflating perceived duration. We simulated this 182

Control of Behavior from Compressed Cortical Input 218 

Having established that corticostriatal compression reproduces both the intensity-biased behavior and the 219 

intensity-modulated time dynamics, we next asked whether the compressed cortical representation causally 220 

drives these biases. PCA of the bottleneck activations in the compressed model revealed a clear direction separating 221 

high- and low-intensity stimuli (Figure 6A). We perturbed the bottleneck along this intensity direction during 222 

S2 presentation (Figure 6B): on trials where I2 was high, we shifted the bottleneck toward the low-intensity 223 

centroid, and vice versa. We quantified the effect using a perturbation swing — the summed shift in choice 224 

probability P (T2 > T 1) across both perturbation conditions, measured in the expected direction (see Methods). 225 

The compressed model showed a significant swing while the uncompressed model did not (Figure 6C). The 226 

effect was asymmetric, with perturbation in the high-to-low direction producing a larger behavioral shift than 227 

low-to-high (Figure S8), potentially paralleling evidence that high-intensity stimuli engage somatosensory circuits 228 

more strongly (Mountcastle et al., 1969). 229 

The strongest evidence that the bottleneck serves as the causal pathway for intensity biases comes from the 230 

correspondence between perturbation susceptibility and natural behavioral bias. The magnitude of each seed’s 231 

perturbation swing was near-perfectly correlated with its natural I2 bias — the degree to which that seed’s 232 

unperturbed behavior was influenced by intensity ( r = 0 .921 , p = 0 .0001 ; Figure 6D). This is precisely what one 233 

would expect if the bottleneck is the channel through which intensity biases duration judgements: seeds whose 234 

cortical drive carries a stronger intensity signal naturally show larger behavioral biases and are most susceptible 235 

to artificial perturbation of that signal. The uncompressed model showed no such correspondence ( r = 0 .269 ,236 

p = 0 .42 ), indicating that without compression, intensity information is distributed across cortex and striatum 237 

and cannot be causally manipulated via a single direction. Together, these results demonstrate that corticostriatal 238 

compression consolidates task-relevant stimulus features into a compact cortical control signal that causally 239 

shapes temporal judgements through the striatum’s time-encoding dynamics. 240

Our framework makes a specific prediction about cortical perturbation: if the cortex provides a low-dimensional 295 

temporal control signal while the striatum intrinsically generates the stable sequentially-ordered activity, then 296

Hidalgo-Balbuena et al. (2019) tested exactly this using optogenetic perturbation of somatosensory cortical and 298 

thalamic input to DLS during the hold phase: the timing of the FBF habit shifted, but the sequential structure was 299 

preserved. 300

This action-dependent information content suggests that in the compressed model, actions serve a dual role: they 305 

are not only motor commands but also gating mechanisms that control what information flows from cortex to 306 

striatum at each moment. This finding provides a principled basis for restricting our perturbation to the hold 307 

phase, when the agent relies on its internal temporal estimate to decide when to sprint. 308

Discussion 324 

Across three time-sensitive behavioral paradigms associated with the dorsolateral striatum — sensorimotor chunk-325 

ing, duration comparison, and motor timing — we find that a single architectural constraint produces a consistent 326 

computational motif: the separation of control from dynamics between cortex and striatum. Corticostriatal com-327 

pression forces the cortex to provide low-dimensional control signals while the striatum generates intrinsic, stable, 328

This separation accounts for domain-specific behavioral and neural signatures — human-329 

like chunking with action slipping, intensity-biased duration judgements with stimulus-modulated time coding, 330

that the compressed cortical signal causally controls behavior while the striatum’s sequential structure remains 332 

intact. A key mechanistic insight comes from the untrained model result (Figure S4): when input dimensionality 333 

to a recurrent network is sufficiently constrained, internal dynamics dominate over input-driven variability, 334 

creating a temporal scaffold that learning can subsequently exploit (Rodrigues et al., 2024). This suggests that 335 

compression itself, not just reward-optimized compression, creates the conditions for temporal encoding. Our 336 

framework thus bridges two theoretical traditions that have developed largely in parallel: reinforcement-driven 337 

dimensionality reduction (Bar-Gad, Morris, & Bergman, 2003), which characterizes corticostriatal compression in 338 

information-theoretic terms, and the dynamical systems perspective (Haimerl et al., 2025; Lee et al., 2019; Murray 339 

& Escola, 2017), which emphasizes cortical parametric control of striatal temporal evolution. We show that these 340 

are two facets of the same mechanism: input-based compression is what creates the conditions for parametric 341 

control of stable dynamic trajectories. 342 

The behaviors produced by our compressed models (Figure 3A, Figure 7E, Figure S10) share the defining features 343 

of habits: they are stereotyped across repetitions, inflexible to changes in sensory context, temporally rigid, 344 

and stimulus-invariant (Graybiel, 1998; Yin et al., 2004). This is consistent with the longstanding association 345 

between the DLS and habitual behavior (Yin et al., 2004), and with the proposal that chunking is a fundamental 346 

mechanism by which habits are assembled (Graybiel, 1998). Our model provides a specific account of how this 347 

arises: compression forces the cortical drive to the striatum to discard stimulus-specific detail, retaining only 348 

low-dimensional categorical signals. The striatum, receiving this impoverished but structured input, learns to 349 

generate behavior from its own internal dynamics rather than from moment-to-moment sensory guidance — a 350 

functional signature of automaticity that can also explain subcortical consolidation of automatic motor sequences 351 

(Mizes et al., 2024). 352 

Several prior models have addressed related phenomena. Botvinick & Plaut (2004) showed that simple recurrent 353 

networks under degraded conditions produce action slips resembling human behavior, establishing that recurrence 354 

supports sequential structure; our work extends this by demonstrating that compression at the input to the 355 

recurrent network is what produces structured sequential behaviors. Nassar et al. (2018) proposed chunking as 356 

rational lossy compression in visual working memory, and Soni & Frank (2025) showed that prefrontal-basal 357 

ganglia circuits can learn adaptive chunking through dopaminergic reinforcement, with the striatum acting as 358 

a gate controller. These works complement ours by establishing the normative rationale for compression and 359 

showing how gating policies can be learned, but the basal ganglia in those models serve a switching function 360 

rather than exhibiting dynamic sequential activity. Our contribution is mechanistically distinct: we show that 361 

anatomical compression at the corticostriatal interface shapes the geometry of striatal dynamics , forcing the 362 

striatum into a regime where stable temporal structure dominates over input-driven variability, producing specific 363 

testable neural signatures — stable time-encoding trajectories and direction-specific cortical control that leaves 364 

striatal sequential structure intact. 365 

Our framework relies on several simplifying assumptions that future work can relax. First, cortical recurrent 366 

weights are frozen throughout training, reflecting the assumption that cortical and striatal plasticity are governed 367 

by different learning signals operating on different timescales (Doya, 2000; Pasupathy & Miller, 2005). While 368 

this is a common modeling choice for cortical reservoirs (Buonomano & Maass, 2009; Dominey, 1995), cortical 369 

plasticity clearly exists and likely interacts with striatal learning during skill acquisition. Future work could 370 

model cortical learning explicitly and study how the co-evolution of cortical representations and striatal dynamics 371 

shapes the emergence of temporal behavior. Second, the bottleneck dimensionality in our model is hand-set rather 372 

than learned. We do not attempt to match the exact biological compression ratio; rather, the bottleneck layer 373 

is a convenient modeling device to enforce dimensionality reduction and study its functional consequences. In 374 

biology, the effective compression may be shaped by development, neuromodulation, or task demands in ways our 375 

model does not capture. Third, our comparison to neural data is indirect: we reproduce high-level behavioral and 376 

neural signatures on the same tasks used in experimental studies but do not fit neural data directly. However, our 377 

framework suggests concrete approaches for future multi-region recording analyses — for instance, measuring 378

The computational motif we describe at the corticostriatal interface also operates at the output stage of the basal 383 

ganglia. After processing within the striatum and the pallidal nuclei, basal ganglia output projects through the 384 

thalamus and back to cortex, forming the cortico-basal ganglia-thalamocortical loops that are a fundamental 385 

organizing principle of this circuit (Alexander et al., 1986). At this output stage, a second bottleneck exists: the basal 386 

ganglia output nuclei (GPi/SNr) contain far fewer neurons than their thalamic and cortical targets (Humphries, 387 

2025). Humphries (2025) argues that the primary function of this output bottleneck is to control cortical dynamics, 388 

proposing that each output neuron sets the weight of a basis function defined by its synaptic contacts. Logiaco 389 

et al. (2021) provide a mechanistic account of how this control is implemented: basal ganglia output selects 390 

which thalamic neurons are disinhibited, and these few neurons deliver low-rank perturbations to a recurrent 391 

cortical network, controlling which motor motif is executed and when transitions occur. This is fundamentally the 392 

same principle we describe — a low-dimensional signal at the interface between regions parametrically controls 393 

dynamics within the downstream circuit. Importantly, the corticostriatal and thalamocortical bottlenecks are not 394 

independent. Haber et al. (2000) characterized their relationship as an ascending spiral: information processed 395 

through one cortico-basal ganglia loop feeds forward into the next, progressively transforming limbic signals into 396 

sensorimotor output. Our framework may thus describe a general principle that operates at multiple nodes of 397 

this spiral: compression at each interface creates the conditions for low-dimensional control of stable dynamics 398 

in the downstream circuit. 399 

The ascending spiral architecture raises a further question: does the same compression mechanism give rise to 400 

both habitual and goal-directed behavior? An intriguing clue comes from the parallel between our architecture 401 

and the meta-reinforcement learning framework of Wang et al. (2018), which uses a similar design — a recurrent 402 

network (in their case, also an LSTM) trained by reward prediction errors that develops its own internal learning 403 

algorithm — to model goal-directed behavior typically associated with prefrontal cortex. The basal ganglia 404 

contain parallel corticostriatal loops linking different cortical regions to different striatal territories: prefrontal 405 

cortex projects to the dorsomedial striatum (DMS), associated with goal-directed behavior, while sensorimotor 406 

cortex projects to the DLS, associated with habitual behavior (Alexander et al., 1986; Haber, 2003; Yin et al., 407 

2004). The architectural similarity between our model and Wang et al. (2018) raises the possibility that goal-408 

directed and habitual behaviors emerge from a shared corticostriatal compression mechanism, with the distinction 409 

arising not from fundamentally different circuit computations but from differences in the cortical inputs and 410 

task demands each loop receives. Testing this hypothesis would require extending our framework to model both 411 

loops simultaneously and examining how their interaction gives rise to the well-documented transition from 412 

goal-directed to habitual control (Thorn et al., 2010). 413 

More broadly, the principle that anatomical bottlenecks separate control from dynamics may extend to any 414 

circuit in which convergent input drives a recurrent downstream population. For example, hippocampal place 415 

cells integrate highly multimodal sensory information from diverse sources (Jeffery, 2007) and also exhibit rich 416 

temporal coding relevant for episodic memory (Eichenbaum, 2014). Recent neural network models of episodic 417 

memory that parallel hippocampus have been shown to produce stable neural trajectories which implement 418 

free recall via serial position encodings (i.e. time codes ) that are independent of item identity (Li et al., 2025), 419 

directly paralleling the trajectories that organize action chunks in the present work (Figure 3B). Similarly, 420 

the cerebellum transforms converging, compressed sensory inputs into temporally precise neural dynamics 421 

that support conditioned responses and motor coordination (Medina et al., 2000; Wagner et al., 2019). In each 422 

case, compression at the interface between regions may jointly facilitate inter-regional control and temporal 423 

computations in the downstream circuit. If this principle proves general, it would suggest that a unified theory of 424 

inter-regional communication may be within reach — one in which the dimensionality of the projection between 425 

any two regions predicts the degree to which the downstream circuit exhibits time-encoding dynamics. Testing 426 

this prediction across brain circuits represents a natural next step for the field. 427

Methods 428 

Model Architecture 429 

Our model implements corticostriatal compression through three modules arranged in series (Figure 1B). The 430 

cortical module is an recurrent neural network layer (RNN) with 64 hidden units, whose recurrent weights are 431 

randomly initialized and frozen throughout training. Specifically, the recurrent-to-recurrent weights ( Whh ) and 432 

recurrent biases ( bhh ) of the encoder RNN are fixed at their initial values (PyTorch default uniform initialization, 433 

U(−1/8, 1/8) ), while all other weights remain trainable. This design reflects the assumption that cortical recurrent 434 

dynamics and striatal dynamics are learned via different learning signals and timescales. 435 

The corticostriatal bottleneck is a feedforward layer that maps the cortical RNN’s output ( 64 dimensions) to a 436 

low-dimensional space ( dbn units), followed by additive Gaussian noise with fixed standard deviation σ. The noise 437 

is applied at every forward pass (both training and evaluation) and models the stochasticity of corticostriatal 438 

transmission. We used dbn = 4 for the chunking and Toso tasks (compressing from 64 to 4 dimensions), and 439 

dbn = 2 for the treadmill task (see task-specific sections below). The uncompressed baseline used dbn = 64 in all 440 

tasks, effectively removing the compression constraint. 441 

The striatal module is a single-layer LSTM (Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, 1997) with 64 hidden units. The LSTM 442 

receives the bottleneck output as input at each timestep and maintains its own hidden and cell states across 443 

timesteps within an episode (states are reset at episode boundaries). A linear policy head maps the LSTM hidden 444 

state to action logits (softmax policy π), and a separate linear value head maps to a scalar state value estimate 445 

V . The policy and value heads share the LSTM hidden state but use independent linear projections. All striatal 446 

weights (LSTM parameters, policy head, value head) and corticostriatal weights (bottleneck projection, encoder 447 

input weights) are trained via reinforcement learning. Cortical recurrent weights remain frozen. 448

Action Chunking Task 459 

We used a serial decision-making task inspired by Lai et al. (2025). At each timestep t ≤ T , a stimulus st is drawn 460 

from a set S = {0, 1, . . . , 15 } of 16 possible stimuli. Stimuli are presented as one-hot vectors. Each stimulus si461 

has a single correct action ai = i. The agent receives a reward of 0 for correct actions and a penalty of −1/T for 462 

incorrect actions. 463

remaining probability mass. Episode length was fixed at T = 8 timesteps. 469 

The observation at each timestep consisted of the stimulus one-hot vector ( 16 dimensions), a one-hot encoding of 470 

the previous action ( 16 dimensions), and the previous reward ( 1 dimension), yielding a 33 -dimensional observation 471 

consistent with the meta-RL framework (Wang et al., 2018) that helps models learn fast adaptation within trials. 472 

Models were trained for 3,000 epochs with a learning rate schedule decaying from 1 × 10 −3 to 2 × 10 −4 at epoch 473 

2,800 . Bottleneck noise was σbn = 0 .4.474 

For the IID control environment, the same trained models were evaluated on episodes where all 16 stimuli were 475 

drawn independently with equal probability (no chunk structure). This tests whether time-encoding properties 476 

persist in the absence of temporal regularities. 477 

Poisson Duration Comparison Task 478 

We simulated the duration comparison task from Toso et al. (2021), with observations designed to capture the 479 

statistical properties of somatosensory spike trains as highlighted by the reanalysis of Rodrigues et al. (2024). 480 

Trial structure. 481 

Each trial consisted of five phases presented sequentially: a pre-stimulus period ( 2 timesteps), stimulus S1 (T1482 

timesteps), an inter-stimulus interval (ISI; 16 timesteps), stimulus S2 (T2 timesteps), and a post-stimulus period ( 2483 

timesteps), followed by a single response timestep. Stimulus durations T1 and T2 were independently sampled 484 

as integers from {2, 3, . . . , 8} with the constraint T1̸ = T2. Stimulus intensities I1 and I2 were independently 485 

sampled from three levels: low ( λ = 0 .6), medium ( λ = 1 .2), and high ( λ = 2 .4), expressed as Poisson rate 486 

parameters. 487 

Observations. 488 

At each timestep, the agent observed a 16 -dimensional vector of spike counts drawn from independent Poisson 489 

distributions. During stimulus periods, each of the 16 “cochlear” neurons fired with rate equal to the current 490 

stimulus intensity ( λI1 during S1, λI2 during S2). During non-stimulus periods (pre-stimulus, ISI, post-stimulus), 491 

neurons fired at a baseline rate of λbase = 0 .42 . This observation model entangles intensity and duration: the total 492 

spike count during a stimulus is approximately 16 × λ × T , making it impossible to perfectly separate intensity 493 

from duration based on total activity alone. The observation was augmented with a one-hot encoding of the 494 

previous action ( 2 dimensions) and the previous reward ( 1 dimension), yielding a 19 -dimensional input. 495 

Task and reward. 496 

On the response timestep, the agent selected one of two actions: 0 (“ T1 > T 2”) or 1 (“ T2 > T 1”). Correct responses 497 

received a reward of 1.0; incorrect responses received 0.0. No reward was given during non-response timesteps. 498 

Training. 499 

Models were trained for 1,000 epochs with a learning rate decaying from 5 × 10 −4 to 5 × 10 −5, a batch size of 500 

1,024 , 5 parallel environments per worker, and bottleneck noise σbn = 0 .1.501 

Gaussian variant (supplementary). 502 

For comparison with the original analysis of Toso et al. (2021), we also trained models on a Gaussian-observation 503 

variant of the task. In this version, each stimulus was represented as a 16 -dimensional vector with 5 signal 504 

dimensions (mean = I, σ = 0 .05 ) and 11 noise dimensions (mean = 0 , σ = 0 .1), shuffled randomly at each 505 

timestep to prevent position-specific encoding. Intensities were drawn from {0.3, 0.6, 0.9}. The ISI was 4506 

timesteps and the bottleneck noise was σbn = 0 .4. Models were trained for 250 epochs. 507

Treadmill Motor Timing Task 508 

We developed a reinforcement learning environment inspired by the treadmill navigation task of Hidalgo-Balbuena 509 

et al. (2019). 510 

Environment. 511 

The agent navigates a one-dimensional track of length 100 units. A virtual treadmill displaces the agent backward 512 

at a constant speed of 2.0 units per timestep. The agent starts at a position drawn uniformly from [75 , 89 .5] (i.e., 513 

85 .0 ± 10 .0, clipped to remain below the goal zone). The goal zone begins at position x = 90 .0. The agent must 514 

reach the goal zone at or after timestep 20 (the target arrival time); arriving before timestep 20 incurs a harsh 515 

penalty. The maximum episode duration is 30 timesteps. 516 

Actions. 517 

The agent selects from three discrete actions at each timestep: 518 

• Drift (action 0): no self-generated motion; the treadmill pushes the agent backward at −2.0 units/step. 519 

• Hold (action 1): the agent runs at the treadmill speed, producing net velocity ≈ 0.520 

• Sprint (action 2): the agent runs at maximum speed ( 6.0 units/step), producing net forward velocity of 521 

+4 .0 units/step. 522 

These actions correspond to the three phases of the rat FBF strategy observed by Hidalgo-Balbuena et al. (2019): 523 

passive backward drift, steady-state running in place, and forward sprinting to the goal. 524

Behavioral Metrics (Treadmill) 622 

FBF fraction. 623 

For each trial, the action sequence was simplified using run-length encoding: consecutive timesteps with the same 624 

action were collapsed into a single block, and transient blocks ( ≤ 2 timesteps) were merged into the adjacent block. 625 

A trial was classified as FBF-consistent if the simplified sequence consisted of a sequential ordering containing 626 

drift, hold, and sprint phases (e.g., [0 , 1, 2] , [0 , 2] , or [1 , 2] ). The FBF fraction is the proportion of trials classified as 627 

FBF-consistent. 628 

Action consistency. 629 

At each timestep t, we identified the modal (most frequent) action across all trials for a given seed. Action 630 

consistency is the mean fraction of trials matching the mode, averaged across timesteps. This metric captures 631 

how stereotyped the behavioral policy is across repeated executions, including across trials with different starting 632 

positions ( 75 –89 .5). 633

Fig. S7. Gaussian observations produce stimulus-invariant time coding, reconciling Toso and Rodrigues findings. (A) 

Observation statistics for Gaussian (left) and Poisson (right) models. Violin plots show the distribution of total spike counts across neurons at three intensity levels. In the Gaussian model, only the mean scales with intensity while variance remains constant. In the Poisson model, both mean and variance scale with intensity, creating intensity-dependent temporal dynamics. (B) Mean separation of time decoding curves across all conditions (S1/S2 × intensity/duration splits) for the Gaussian duration task (left) and intensity task (right). Purple: bn=4; red: bn=64. The compressed model consistently shows lower separation across all conditions (all p < 0.001 ), indicating more stimulus-invariant time coding. (C) Example time decoding curves from the Gaussian duration task: S2 split by I 2 intensity for bn=4 (left) and bn=64 (right). The three intensity curves nearly overlap for the compressed model, matching the stimulus-invariant pattern reported by Toso et al. (2021). (D) Example curves from the Gaussian intensity task: S2 split by duration for bn=4 (left) and bn=64 (right), showing the same pattern. These results demonstrate that the same compression architecture produces Toso-like invariance with Gaussian observations and Rodrigues-like modulation with Poisson observations.

Fig. S10. Corticostriatal compression reliably induces the Front-Back-Front motor program. (A) Modal action at each normalized timestep for each training seed, computed across successful trials only (reward > 0) after time-warping to a common trial duration. All compressed model seeds (left) converge on the stereotyped drift →hold →sprint (FBF) sequence, with drift occupying the first ∼25% of the trial, hold the middle ∼50%, and sprint the final ∼25%. Uncompressed model seeds (right) show heterogeneous strategies: some approximate FBF while others show disorganized action switching, indicating that without compression the emergence of structured sequential behavior is much less reliable. (B) Mean action profile over time, averaged across all 11 seeds and all trials (including unsuccessful). The compressed model (left) shows sharp phase boundaries, while the uncompressed model (right) shows gradual, overlapping transitions with drift and hold intermixed throughout the trial. Dashed line indicates the goal time ( t = 20 ). (C) Conditional transition probabilities given that the agent switches actions, computed across all seeds. The two FBF-consistent forward transitions (Drift →Hold, Hold →Sprint) are significantly higher in the compressed model, while their backward complements (Hold →Drift, Sprint →Drift) are significantly higher in the uncompressed model (Welch’s t-test; n = 11 seeds per condition).
