Abstract 37 

The inability to selectively trigger therapeutic hypothermia independent of 38 

environmental cooling has hindered causal analysis of its broader physiological 39 

benefits. Here, we employed P57, a natural product that pharmacologically 40 

induces therapeutic hypothermia circumventing external cold stress, to identify 41 

the neuronal substrates underlying hypothermia-induced antitumor effects. 42 

Integrating functional ultrasound imaging, activity-dependent neuronal labeling, 43 

and single-nucleus RNA sequencing, we identify a previously unrecognized 44

Temperature is a fundamental biophysical variable that shapes biological processes 57 

across molecular, cellular, and systemic scales, ultimately influencing physiology, 58 

behavior, survival, and disease susceptibility, whether through environmental exposure 59 

or changes in core body temperature 1 . For instance, long-lived bowhead whales 60 

inhabiting Arctic waters exhibit enhanced DNA repair 2 , hibernating brown bears 61 

maintain low body temperature and are protected from venous thromboembolism 3 , and 62 

hypothermic torpor in laboratory mice markedly slows epigenetic aging 4 . Reduced 63 

ambient or core temperature has been associated with beneficial effects in cancer, 64 

obesity, chronic inflammatory disorders, and age-related physiological decline 5-9 .65 

Clinically, hypothermia has also long been applied from ancient cold compresses to 66

modern cardiac surgery 10,11 . However, adaptive thermogenic and metabolic responses 67 

triggered by environmental cold exposure are fundamentally distinct from the systemic 68 

metabolic suppression associated with true hypothermia. Environmental temperature 69

physiological effects specifically to hypothermia. Besides, conventional physical 71 

cooling approaches suffer from uneven temperature control, local cold injury, and 72 

limited precision, restricting their broader clinical applicability. These observations 73 

raise two central questions motivating our work: does hypothermia itself directly 74 

contribute to health promotion and disease intervention, and whether hypothermia can 75 

be induced in a safer and more precisely controlled manner than conventional physical 76 

cooling thereby unlocking its substantial therapeutic potential. 77 

Understanding how the brain controls body temperature offers a path to overcome the 78 

limitations of current cooling methods. Recent studies have identified distinct 79 

populations of thermoregulatory neurons, providing an unprecedented view into central 80 

control of temperature. Different stimuli activate different subsets of these neurons, 81 

such as those regulating torpor 12 , sensing light 13,14 , expressing neuropeptides 15 ,82 

responding to hormonal signals 16 , or reacting to cold and heat 17-19 , resulting in stimulus-83 

specific patterns of temperature change 20 . These findings highlight the heterogeneity of 84 

thermoregulatory neurons and raise the possibility that certain neuronal populations 85 

may be optimally suited for inducing a therapeutically beneficial hypothermic state due 86 

to their unique downstream effectors. Following this rationale, we previously found that 87 

the natural product P57 induces hypothermia by inhibiting Pdxk, a key kinase in 88

P57 activates neurons in MPA and PVH 101 

We previously showed that P57 induces hypothermia via a pharmacological mechanism 102 

involving altered vitamin B6 metabolism 21 . However, its effect on the central nervous 103 

system, especially on neuronal activity in the thermoregulatory center of the 104 

hypothalamus, remains unclear. To address this, we used functional ultrasound (FUS) 105 

imaging, which captures brain hemodynamic activity based on neurovascular coupling, 106 

to reflect real-time activity changes across brain regions during P57-induced 107 

hypothermia by calculating the change of average relative cerebral blood volume 108 

compared to baseline and control ( ΔrCBV) 22 . Given that anesthetics can substantially 109 

alter cerebral blood volume (CBV), we performed FUS imaging in awake, head-fixed 110

P57-activated MPA neurons project to PVH 247

MPA neurons, we next sought to identify their downstream projection circuits. 249

Discussion 326 

Our study provides a real-time, whole-brain view of the hypothermic response induced 327 

by the small molecule P57, identifying the MPA as P57-specific activate region, and 328 

revealed the triggered neuronal population is characterized by the expression of Foxp2 329 

and Prlr . We further demonstrate that the newly identified Foxp2 MPA neurons are both 330 

> (which was not certified by peer review) is the author/funder. All rights reserved. No reuse allowed without permission. The copyright holder for this preprint this version posted May 7, 2026. ;https://doi.org/10.64898/2026.05.04.722579 doi: bioRxiv preprint

sufficient and necessary for P57-induced hypothermia, and that the MPA-PVH circuit 331 

serves as the critical downstream conduit. Crucially, chemogenetic activation of 332 

Foxp2 MPA neurons establishes a proof-of-concept for neuromodulation-induced 333 

therapeutic hypothermia, providing direct evidence that reduced core temperature itself, 334 

separate from the systemic stress of ambient cold exposure, exerts therapeutic efficacy 335 

in oncology. 336 

Building on previous work that defined the molecular targets of the Hoodia gordonii –337 

derived compound P57 21 , we here extend those findings into a systems-level 338 

neurobiological framework. By mapping the neural circuits through which P57 induces 339 

hypothermia, we provide a template for neuromodulation strategies. A central 340 

implication is that Foxp2 MPA neurons represent a promising target for inducing 341 

therapeutic hypothermia. Because P57-induced hypothermia is a non-toxic, protective 342 

state, the Foxp2 MPA neurons are inherently suited for neuromodulation-based 343 

hypothermia treatment. Unlike traditional physical cooling, which triggers deleterious 344 

thermogenic defenses, targeting this population allows for a stable and controlled 345 

hypothermic state. 346 

Notably, the hypothermic state induced by Foxp2 MPA neurons is not functionally or 347 

molecularly equivalent to natural hibernation or torpor. While we did not perform a 348 

systematic comparison, existing data distinguish Foxp2 MPA neurons from previously 349
