Abstract 37 Nearly all the blood supplying the cortex exits via the bridging veins (BVs) that drain into the 38 superior sagittal sinus (SSS), making these vessels key chokepoints for cerebral blood flow. 39 Using optical imaging in head-fixed mice, we found that the SSS and BVs exhibit ultrafast 40 contractions (<0.1 s) at the onset of locomotion, following whisker stimulation, and upon 41 awakening from sleep. Contractions of the BV and SSS were strongly correlated with 42 abdominal muscle EMG activity and were tightly correlated with respiration at rest. The rapid 43 decrease in blood volume caused by venous constrictions resulted in spurious increases in 44 fluorescence in mice expressing fluorescent reporter proteins, creating artifacts that mimic 45 functional signals. Venous contractions with the same amplitude and dynamics could be 46 generated in anesthetized mice by abdominal pressure application, showing that these 47 contractions were generated by mechanical coupling with the abdomen. Externally imposed 48 abdominal pressures also drove a rapid but transient increase in blood flow. Unlike the pial and 49 parenchymal microvasculature whose diameters are controlled by local signals, the diameters of 50 SSS/BV are dynamically controlled during behavior by abdominal muscle regulation of 51 intracranial pressure, establishing a pathway for regulation of cerebral hemodynamics via 52 mechanical coupling between the central nervous system and the viscera. 53

Introduction 57 Under physiological conditions, the flow of blood in the brain is regulated by signals from 58 neurons and glia on a time scale of seconds to tens of seconds (1, 2). Relaxation of smooth 59 muscle around arterioles, penetrating arteries and pial arteries is the most rapid of these 60 processes, occurring within a few hundred milliseconds after neural activity increases (3-6). In 61 behaving mice, pharmacological infusions silencing local neural activity block arterial dilation in 62 the awake animal (7), showing arterial dilation is driven by local neural activity. Arterial dilation 63 is followed by a much slower dilation of ascending and pial veins (4, 8) and capillaries (3, 9). 64 Because of their sluggish (minutes) response to vasoconstrictors (10), the control of the 65 cerebral venous system has received less attention and veins have been assumed to have slow 66 and passive dynamics. 67 Blood flow from the dorsal cortex drains into the superior sagittal sinus (SSS), a large 68 dural sinus formed by bifurcation of the dural membrane ( Figure 1 A). The SSS drains the 69 dorsal and frontal portions of cortex (11) as well as dural vessels (12). The SSS is fed by the 70 large bridging veins (BV) that run nearly perpendicular to the SSS. The bridging veins have 71 sphincter-like rings of collagen surrounding the entrance of the bridging vein to the SSS (13, 14) 72 which have been hypothesized to play a role in venous pressure regulation (15). Any changes 73 in caliber of the SSS will change the vascular resistance and could thus change the blood flow 74 for a large portion of cortex. The blood pressure decreases through the cerebral vascular 75 network, so that in anesthetized animals (15-18) and in awake humans (19), SSS pressure is 76 lower than intracranial pressure (ICP). This negative transmural pressure is maintained due to 77 the relative stiffness of the venous wall which can resist collapse in the face of small negative 78 pressure gradients, much like the hull of submarine allows the crew compartment to have a 79 lower pressure than the water around it. Because nearly all the blood flowing through the cortex 80 drains through the bridging veins and superior sagittal sinus, changes in the diameters of these 81 vessels could have an influence on perfusion of all the cortex. In anesthetized animals, artificial 82 elevation of ICP causes bridging veins to constrict (20). In behaving mice, ICP is dynamic and 83 can rise from a baseline of 5-10 mmHg up to 25 mmHg or more during locomotion (21, 22). 84 These ICP increases are driven by abdominal muscle contraction that drive increased intra-85 abdominal pressure which is transmitted to the central nervous system via a vascular network 86 that spans the vertebral bones (23). Determining whether these behaviorally driven ICP 87 increases have any impact on bridging veins and the SSS is important because diameter 88 changes in these vessels could drive hemodynamic changes and affects fluid drainage. 89

Here, we visualized the dynamics of bridging veins and the SSS using optical imaging in 90 head fixed mice and found a constriction of the SSS and bridging veins (but not more lateral pial 91 veins) with ultra-rapid onset (~100ms) during bouts of voluntary locomotion. Sensory stimulation 92 (to the whiskers) also drove abdominal muscle contraction and corresponding SSS/BV 93 constriction, and in mice expressing fluorophores, this constriction decreased light absorbance 94 by hemoglobin which could drive artifactual functional signals. The SSS/BV constriction was 95 tightly correlated with abdominal muscle contraction (as measured with electromyography) and 96 could be recapitulated in anesthetized mice by squeezing the abdomen. Our results show that, 97 unlike the pial and parenchymal microvasculature whose diameters are controlled by local 98 neural and glial signals, the diameters of SSS/BV are dynamically controlled during behavior by 99 abdominal muscle regulation of intracranial pressure. Cerebral hemodynamics do not just 100 reflect local neural activity, but also are impacted by abdominal muscle contractions via 101 mechanical coupling between the central nervous system and the viscera. 102 

Results 103 We measured brain hemodynamics using widefield intrinsic optical imaging, two-photon laser 104 scanning microscopy (2PLSM) and laser Doppler flowmetry in 7 head fixed C57BL/6 mice, as 105 well as using mini-scope in one free-behaving C57BL/6 mouse. We also measured GFP 106 responses in 6 head fixed CAG-EGFP mice. Intracranial pressure measurements in 10 C57BL/6 107 mice were from our previous publications (21, 22) and re-analyzed here. 108

Whisker stimulation drives contraction of BV and SSS . Sensory evoked responses can 164 drive both covert and overt muscle contractions (32, 33). Since many imaging experiments 165 make use of sensory-evoked responses, such as whisker stimulation (34), we then asked if 166 constriction of the SSS and BV could be driven by sensory stimulation of the whiskers, 167 potentially mediated by abdominal muscle contractions. Brief (0.1s at 10 psi) unilateral 168 stimulation of the whiskers drive vasodilation of arteries in the contralateral sensory cortex with 169 a ~1 second delay (4). However, we also observed a rapid (< 0.2 seconds of stimulation onset) 170 constriction in the SSS and BV, but not of the smaller veins in the whisker representation of the 171 somatosensory cortex ( Figure 2 B and C). Quantification of ∆HbT also showed a decrease in 172 response around stimulation onset ( Supplementary Figure 3 B, C and E), and in contrast to 173 locomotion, there was a brief dip in net oxygenation of the blood ( Supplementary Figure 3 D174 and F), likely because the stimulus was unexpected and was not accompanied by an increase 175 in respiration that raises systemic blood oxygenation (25). The dip in oxygenation is reminiscent 176 of the “initial dip” seen in some imaging experiments (35). Electromyography recordings showed 177 a large increase in abdominal EMG (EMG Abd ) activity time locked to the whisker stimulation 178 (Figure 2 B and Supplementary Figure 4 C). As a control we looked at the responses to 179 auditory stimulation, which drives whisking and similar (though somewhat smaller) functional 180 hyperemia in the whisker representation of the cortex due to whisking (4, 36). Auditory 181 stimulation did not drive abdominal EMG activation ( Figure 2 B and Supplementary Figure 182 

4C), and there was no constriction of the SSS and BV ( Figure 2B), consistent with the 183 hypothesis that abdominal muscle constriction drove the constriction of the largest veins. 184

Intracranial pressure dynamics during behavior. The rapid contraction of veins could be 211 mediated by increases in intracranial pressure (ICP), as artificial elevations of ICP are known to 212 compress bridging veins (20) and ICP is elevated during locomotion (21, 22, 24). To address 213 this, we reanalyzed a previously published dataset (21, 22) of intracranial pressure recordings 214 made from mice head fixed on a spherical treadmill. In the reanalysis, we used broadband 215 signals with minimal temporal filtering in order to capture the fast temporal dynamics. An 216 example ICP trace, along with locomotion dynamics was shown in Figure 4 A. It is apparent that 217 there are large (>10 mmHg) increases in ICP during locomotion that dominate all other 218 fluctuations, consistent with ICP increases being the drives of the contractions of the largest 219 veins. The locomotion triggered averages show an increase in ICP that precedes the onset of 220 locomotion, rises ~8 mmHg above the resting pressure, and then returns to baseline shortly 221 after the cessation of locomotion ( Figure 4 C), consistent with the dynamics of the venous 222 contractions during locomotion and EMG activation. SSS and bridging veins dilate during sleep and constrict upon awakening. Next, we 241 looked the dynamics of the large veins during sleep. Sleep is accompanied by large dilations of 242 arteries (47, 48) and global increases in blood volume (49) that are much larger than what are 243 seen in the awake state. Sleep, particularly rapid eye movement (REM) sleep, is accompanied 244 by large decreases in muscle tone (50), and drops in blood pressure. We hypothesized that the 245 net effect of these changes during sleep would drive dilation of the SSS and BV. We performed 246 widefield imaging on head-fixed mice (n = 4, all male) in a tube (36, 47) and tracked whisker 247 position, body movement, and abdominal muscle EMG ( Figure 5A), which were used to 248 determine the arousal state of the animal. We performed sleep scoring as previously described 249 (47, 48) and categorized the arousal state into three stages: awake, rapid eye movement (REM) 250 and non-rapid eye movement (NREM) sleep. In contrast to voluntary locomotion and whisker 251 stimulation evoked venous constrictions, we found venous dilation associated with sleep, and 252 saw large constrictions upon transitions from REM or NREM to the awake state ( Figure 5 B-D). 253 We also saw large decreases in total hemoglobin and, in contrast to the constrictions during 254 locomotion, decreases in blood oxygenation around these vessels in transitions to the awake 255 state ( Figure 5 B-D). Passively applied abdominal pressure drives SSS and BV constriction and increases in 259 

blood flow. Externally applied abdominal pressure drives brain movement like that seen during 260 behavior, mediated through a network of valveless veins (vertebral venous plexus) that link the 261 spinal cavity with the abdominal cavity (23). We asked if this pressure application could drive 262 contraction of the SSS and BV. We anesthetized mice (1% isoflurane) and placed them in a 263 computer controlled pneumatic pressure cuff (23) and applied one-second long squeezes of the 264 abdomen while monitoring both cortical vessel diameter and cerebral blood flow (CBF) using 265 laser Doppler flowmetry (51, 52). During cuff inflation (1 s), the pressure applied to the 266 abdominal muscle increases and reaches half-max level within ~0.17s. We found that externally 267 applied abdominal pressure drove reliable constrictions of the SSS and BV, but not in the 268 smaller pial veins ( Figure 6 B-D), recapitulating the patterns seen during locomotion ( Figure 1 269 and Supplementary Figure 2 ) and during sensory stimulation ( Figure 2 and Supplementary 270 

Figure 3 ). Interestingly, the constriction also drove a flow increase in the parenchyma (3.02 ± 271 1.47%, Figure 6 F) and oxygenation increases across the whole cortex ( Figure 6 E). It seems 272 that the abdominal pressure can drive a very rapid blood flow increase, and this could be a 273 mechanism by which flow to the brain is increased peremptorily by abdominal muscle 274 contractions, such as those that accompany the startle response (53). 275 

Discussion 276 We observed rapid constrictions of bridging veins and the superior sagittal sinus during 277 voluntary locomotion, whisker stimulation, and upon awakening from sleep, all conditions were 278 accompanied by abdominal muscle contraction. Externally imposed abdominal pressure 279 generated patterns of venous constriction that were very similar in amplitude compared to those 280 seen during locomotion and sensory stimulation. These results are consistent with a model 281 where abdominal pressures are transmitted to the central nervous system. These forces can 282 cause brain movement (23), and here we show they can cause compression of the largest veins 283 on a sub-second time scale. 284 Previous studies looking at more lateral and smaller veins have observed dilation (4, 51), 285 something we have seen here as well. The cause of the size dependent difference in venous 286 responses is likely due to differences in blood pressure throughout the network. Blood pressure 287 decreases monotonically as blood transits through the vascular network (54), so the larger veins 288 will have a lower pressure than the smaller veins that feed them. ICP changes will depend on the coupling from the abdominal cavity to the spinal 291 cord ( Figure 7 A). These results, coupled with the observed ICP increases during locomotion, 292 point to a model where the central nervous system is mechanistically linked to the abdominal 293 cavity, and pressures generated in the abdomen are transmitted to the brain, leading to 294 increase in ICP and brain motion (23). If the ICP rises, it will be above the intraluminal pressures 295 of the bridging veins and SSS (but not above the intraluminal pressures of the smaller 296 veins)( Figure 7 B, C and D), causing a spatial pattern of venous responses like the one we see 297 during locomotion ( Figure 1 F). Notably, there are several caveats to our work. The superior 298 sagittal sinus does not have a circular cross section, it is roughly triangular in histological 299 sections, so our estimates of diameter changes may over or underestimate the actual changes. 300 There are other large veins in the brain that we did not image (e.g., transverse sinuses) that 301 may undergo similar ICP-driven compression. Finally, ICP will not be completely spatially 302 homogenous, and the venous pressure could vary dynamically, which will affect the sensitivity 303 of veins to elevations in ICP. 304 Previous work has observed rapid contractions of bridging veins (25, 55) and dural 305 venous sinus (38, 56), though their origin was not understood. There have also been reports of 306 stimulation-induced small, rapid blood volume decreases whose origin is mysterious (57), and of 307 artifactually increased fluorescence just prior to locomotion onset in mouse two-photon imaging 308 (37) that likely have contributions from the venous constriction we describe. Our work extends 309 on previous work showing the brain is mechanically coupled to the abdominal cavity (23, 58, 310 59), and shows that the mechanical coupling to the abdomen can control blood flow in the brain 311 with rapid dynamics. 312 Besides the increase in blood flow elicited by the compression of these veins, there may 313 be other possible roles for this constriction. Firstly, the contractions of these veins could help 314 compensate for ICP increases by providing compliance in the cranial cavity (60). Secondly, the 315 SSS is a dural sinus, contraction of the SSS would apply tension to the mechanosensitive dura 316 (61), which may account for some of the movement or deformation in the dura seen during 317 locomotion in mice (62). Thirdly, the SSS is innervated by trigeminal and cervical spinal nerves 318 (63) and is pain sensitive (64), so changes in the mechanical dynamics of the SSS could also 319 play a role in migraine and headache (61). Fourthly, the contractions of these veins could also 320 help move cerebrospinal fluid, which is known to be moved by dilations or contractions in 321 arteries (65-67). Mechanical coupling between the abdomen and brain is especially 324 interesting considering the functional mechanosensitive channels in central nervous system 325 neurons (72) and glia (73), and the rapid contractions of these veins could also activate 326 mechanosensitive channels in the brain, acting as a direct interoceptive signaling pathway to 327 the brain. However, our results could have implications 332 for brain pathologies as well. Pathologies like obesity elevate intra-abdominal pressure (79) and 333 ICP (80), which could disrupt the normal flow of blood back and forth between the abdominal 334 cavity and spinal canal. Elevated blood pressure may attenuate or block these venous 335 contractions, potentially playing a role in the adverse effects of hypertension on brain health. 336 From the clinical perspective, changes in the normal dynamics of SSS/BV constrictions could be 337 used as non-invasive indicators of intracranial pressure (81-83). In summary, this work supports 338 a model in which the brain and cerebral vasculature is mechanistically coupled with the viscera, 339 providing a dynamic signaling pathway that can rapidly communicate information about the body 340 state to the brain. 341 342

Widefield intrinsic optical signal and fluorescence imaging processing. We mapped the 546 spatiotemporal dynamics of oxyhemoglobin and deoxyhemoglobin concentrations using their 547 oxygen-dependent optical absorption spectra (41). Reflectance data (either green and blue, or 548 green and red when performing fluorescence imaging) were converted to changes in oxy- and 549 deoxyhemoglobin concentrations using the modified Beer-Lambert law with Monte Carlo-550 derived wavelength-dependent path length factors (41). We used the cerebral oxygenation 551 index (94) (i.e., HbO-HbR) to quantify the change in oxygenation, as calculating the percentage 552 change requires knowledge of the concentration of hemoglobin on a pixel-by-pixel basis, which 553 is not feasible given the wide heterogeneity in the density of the cortical vasculature (95, 96). 554 Interspersed fluorescence images of fluorescence signals were hemodynamic corrected and 555 converted into ∆F/F 0 , where ∆F is the mean subtracted raw fluorescence signal and F 0 is the 556 mean of the entire time course. To correct hemodynamics contamination, we tested three widely 557 used methods: (1) hemodynamic correction using only green reflectance (i.e., single-wavelength 558 method)(43), (2) hemodynamic correction using estimated excitation and emission attenuation 559 (i.e., Ex-Em method)(40, 41), and (3) hemodynamic correction using a spatially detailed 560 regression-based method to estimate hemodynamics contamination (i.e., spatial model)(38). 561 The spatial model is based on a linear form of the Beer-Lambert relationship but is fit at every 562 pixel in the image and does not rely on the estimation of physical parameters. 563 To calculate venous diameter using widefield intrinsic optical imaging, vessel diameters were 564 calculated from the normalized full width at half minimum of the image intensity along a line 565 perpendicular to the vessel, average of results from 5 parallel lines were used for vessel 566 diameter (25). 567
