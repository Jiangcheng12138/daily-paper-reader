Abstract: 14 

Circadian clocks  rely on evolutionarily conserved transcriptional –translational 15 

feedback loops to align physiology and behavior with the 24 -hour day –night cycle .16 

Post -translational modifications, especially ubiquitination, critically control core clock 17 

protein stability and circadian pace . In both  fruit flies and mammals , the abundance of 18 

key pacemaker protein PERIOD (PER/PER2) must be tightly regulated to sustain 19 

circadian rhythms. However,  the canonical phosphorylation -dependent pathway cannot 20 

fully account for turnover of PER  in  Drosophila . Here we identify Yippee as a critical 21 

regulator of PER  level  through a genetic screen  of downregulating ubiquitination 22 

pathways . Knockdown of  Yippee  in  circadian  pacemaker neurons  significantly 23 

lengthens the circadian period by ~2 hours and elevates PER levels.  Indeed,  YIPPEE 24 

physically interacts with PER  in fly head . Mechanistically, i ts mammalian homolog 25 

YPEL5 binds PER2, promotes K63 -linked polyubiquitination and proteasomal 26 

degradation of PER2 independently of FASP -region phosphorylation.  Lastly, 27 

downregulating  YPEL5/Ypel5  prolongs circadian periods in human U2OS cells and 28 

mice. Our findings reveal an evolutionarily conserved mechanism in which 29 

Yippee/YPEL5 regulates circadian rhythms by mediating ubiquitin -dependent 30 

degradation of PER/PER2. 31

Introduction 37 

To adapt to  the  predictable environmental changes caused by Earth ’s rotation, most 38 

organisms have evolved an endogenous  circadian  timing system, which operates on a 39 

~24 -hour cycle. The circadian clock s help organisms adjust to daily environmental 40 

fluctuations  by  synchronizing  daily rhythms of physiology and behavior [1] . In mammals, 41 

circadian rhythms are coordinated by the central pacemaker located in the 42 

suprachiasmatic nucleus (SCN) of the hypothalamus [2] . In  Drosophila melanogaster ,43 

circadian rhythms are primarily regulated by about 150 clock neurons located in the fly 44 

brain [3] . Among these neurons,  the  small ventrolateral neurons (sLNvs)  are  termed 45 

pacemaker neurons , as they synchronize other clock neurons by secreting  the pigment -46 

dispersing factor (PDF) [4, 5] . At the molecular level, circadian clock is maintained by a 47 

highly conserved transcription -translation feedback loop (TTFL) across species [6] . In 48 

Drosophila , the  transcriptional activator s, CLOCK (CLK)  and  CYCLE (CYC), 49 

promote the expression of  PERIOD (PER) and TIMELESS (TIM) , which inhibit 50 

CLK/CYC activity  after dimerization [7, 8] . A similar TTFL operates in mammals, in 51 

which CLOCK and BMAL1 activate the expression of  Per  and  Cry  genes, and the 52 

resulting PER/CRY complexes subsequently repress their own transcription [9, 10] .53 

As the principal transcriptional repressors in the TTFL, PER in flies and PER2 in 54 

mammals are central determinants of c ircadian  speed and robustness [11, 12] . In particular, 55 

PER p rotein levels are tightly correlated  with  the speed of molecular oscillators.  Post -56 

translational modifications (PTMs)  of  PER  have  been shown to play fundamental roles 57 

in regulating the pace of circadian clocks,  such as  glycosylation and  phosphorylation [13,

sequence  iden tity ) and  participate s in processes  like  cell proliferation, immune response 74 

and tumorigenesis , but  its  molecular  function  is poorly understood [20 -23] . A drug target 75

cereblon’s  CULT domain , which  is highly homologous to  Drosophila  YIPPEE [24] . This 77

Yippee -mediated circadian  regulation  is conserved across species.  Together, these 89 

findings  establish  YIPPEE/YPEL5 as an evolutionarily conserved regulator of PER 90

Discussion 276 

In this study, we conducted a genetic screen and identified  YIPPEE  as a previously 277 

uncharacterized post -translational regulator of circadian rhythms. Behaviorally, 278 

depleting  Yippee  expression in  Drosophila  circadian neurons  and mouse SCN  marked ly 279 

lengthen ed the  circadian  period. Mechanistically, YPEL5, the human  ortholog  of 280 

YIPPEE , binds to  the core clock protein PER2  and  promot es  its K63 -linked 281 

poly ubiquitination , thereby modulating PER2 stability . Together, these findings  reveal 282 

an  evolutionarily  conserved pathway  whereby  YIPPEE /YPEL5 fine -tunes circadian 283 

rhythms by  governing  PER/PER2 protein turnover.  Specifically, YIPPEE/YPEL5 284 

interacts  with PER/PER2  to facilitate  its ubiquitination  and subsequent  degradation ,285 

ultimately shaping  circadian period length (Fig. 6D). 286 

We identified  YIPPEE as  a novel  regulator  that  reduces  PER  protein  stability . To 287 

date, SLIMB  is  the  most well -characterized  E3 ligase  responsible for  the degradation 288 

of PER  in  Drosophila [19, 32] . Notably , loss of SLIMB  fails to fully  block  PER  turnover [18] ,289 

implying the existence of additional regulatory factors.  Early  sequence  analyses 290 

predicted that  YIPPEE  may function within  a ubiquitin ligase complex . Here, we 291 

experimentally validated  YIPPEE  as  a critical modulator of  protein stability.  Alt hough 292 

YIPPEE  lack s canonical  E3 ligase  domains , it likely acts as  an  adaptor protein . Our IP -293 

MS  profiling identified multiple  E3 ligase s that interact with  YIPPEE , such as 294 

CG31357 . Whether  these E3 ligases mediate  YIPPEE -dependent  PER  regulation 295 

remains to be  further investigat ed .296 

One of the most unexpected findings of this study is that YPEL5 promotes K63 -297 

linked  poly ubiquitination of PER2. Traditionally, K63 -linked  poly ubiquitin  chains are 298 

associated with non -degradative  processes,  such as signal transduction and intracellular 299 

trafficking,  whereas  K48 -linked  polyubiquitin  chains serve as  the canonical signal for 300 

proteasomal degradation [33] . However, emerging evidence suggests that K63 -linked 301 

ubiquitination can also participate in protein degradation under certain contexts. For 302 

example, K63 ubiquitination has been shown to facilitate proteasome -dependent 303 

degradation by serving as a seed for the assembly of K48/K63 -branched ubiquitin 304

PER2 remains to be determined, our findings broaden the current understanding of how 313 

distinct ubiquitin linkages regulate clock protein stability and highlight a previously 314 

unappreciated layer of post -translational control in circadian timing. 315 

Our cross -species  data demonstrates  that  Ypel5  knockdown in the mouse SCN 316 

disrupts  circadian locomotor activity,  while  human YPEL5 interacts with PER2 to 317 

regulate its stability and oscillation in cultured cells. This evolutionary conservation 318 

underscores the importance of the Y IPPEE –PER regulatory axis in circadian biology 319 

and raises intriguing questions about its potential relevance to human health. Given that 320 

both  circadian  dysregulation  and  aberrant  YIPPEE /YPEL5  expression  are linked to 321 

oncogenesis  and immun e regulation , it will be valuable to explore whether YPEL5 322 

mediates the connection between  circadian rhythm  disruption and  pathological 323 

conditions .324 

In summary,  our  results  strongly support a  novel  role for Y IPPEE /YPEL5 in 325 

promoting PER/PER2 degradation to  regulate circadian  period lengt h. However, 326 

several  key  questions remain  unanswered . First, how does YPEL5 enhance PER2 327 

degradation  by  promoting K63 -linked ubiquitination —a modification  traditionally 328 

associated with  signal transduction  rather than proteasomal degradation? Second,  under 329 

which (patho)physiological  contexts  do YIPPEE/YPEL5 and SLIMB/ β-TrCP 330 

differentially  regulate  PER degradation?  Finally, it will be  critical  to determine whether 331 

YIPPEE /YPEL5  targets  additional  protein  substrates beyond PER  and  the  circadian 332 

clock , as well as  the mechanism by which YIPPEE/YPEL5  modulates  cellular biology. 333
