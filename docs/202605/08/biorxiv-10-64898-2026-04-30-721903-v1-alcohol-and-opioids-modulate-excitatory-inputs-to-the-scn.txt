Abstract 30 

Background: Disturbance of circadian rhythms is a hallmark of substance use disorders ,31 

with depressant drugs often causing soporific effects such as reduced sleep latency . The 32 

suprachiasmatic nucleus (SCN) of the hypothalamus is the central circadian pacemaker 33 

in mammals, regulat ing daily rhythms in physiology and behavior. However, the cellular 34 

mechanisms through which depressants alter SCN function remain poorly defined. 35 

Methods : We used whole -cell patch clamp electrophysiology in acute brain slices to 36 

examine how alcohol and opioid s modulate excitatory glutamatergic transmission onto 37 

SCN neurons. Ethanol effects were examined both acutely and following chronic 38 

exposure paradigms. Optogenetic stimulation was used to activate either RHT input or μ-39 

opioid receptor -expressing (MOR ⁺) terminals, and MOR agonists were used to assess 40 

opioid -mediated effects on synaptic transmission. 41 

Results : W e show that a cute application of ethanol paradoxically enhances SCN firing 42 

rates . In contrast, chronic alcohol exposure reduces glutamatergic drive. We also found 43 

that activating MOR + terminals produced bidirectional modulation of SCN firing and that 44 

MOR+ inputs formed functional glutamatergic synapses onto SCN neurons . Notably, this 45 

transmission could be suppressed by the MOR agonist s DAMGO and fentanyl .46 

Conclusions: Together, these findings reveal that both alcohol and opioids modulate 47 

glutamatergic input to the SCN. This work establishes the SCN as a novel target of 48 

depressant substances and highlights glutamatergic transmission as a key point of 49 

vulnerability in circadian dysregulation associated with substance use. 50

disorders, depressants 52

Introduction 53 

Aberrant s ubstance use is a global public health crisis, with 16.8% of the United 54 

States population having a diagnosable substance use disorder (SUD) 1. Among drugs of 55 

abuse, central nervous system depressants such as alcohol and opioids are heavily used 56 

and often lead to effects such as compulsive drug seeking, loss of intake control, a nd57 

cognitive impairment 2,3. Beyond these immediate impairments, the sedative nature of 58 

alcohol and opioids can impair natural sleep -related rhythms, lead ing to circadian 59 

dysregulation that exacerbate s drug -related pathology 4,5. In particular, alcohol use has 60 

been shown to alter circadian phase and phase -shifting 6, sleep latency 7,8, and melatonin 61 

secretion 9. Similarly , o pioid use, including prescription analgesics and illicit drugs such as 62 

fentanyl, is also associated with disrupted circadian timing 10  and increased prevalence of 63 

sleep disorders 11 . However, d espite the fact that drug use has been clearly associated 64 

with circadian dysregulation 12 ,13 , the cellular and synaptic mechanisms by which these 65 

depressants alter circadian timing remain incompletely understood .66 

Circadian rhythms regulate a wide array of physiological processes, including 67 

sleep -wake cycles, hormone release, metabolism, and reward -seeking behavior 14 ,15 .68 

These rhythms are generated and maintained by the suprachiasmatic nucleus (SCN), a 69 

bilateral structure in the ventral hypothalamus that functions as the central circadian 70 

pacemaker in mammals 16 ,17 . The SCN receives dense glutamatergic innervation from the 71 

retinohypothalamic tract (RHT), originating from intrinsically photosensitive retinal 72 

ganglion cells (ipRGCs), which convey s photic input to entrain the circadian clock to the 73 

external light –dark cycle 18 ,19 . Within the SCN, coordinated synaptic and intrinsic 74

One point of convergence for alcohol - and opioid -related dist ur bances may be their 77 

shared influence on glutamatergic signaling , an aspect critical for SCN function . Alcohol 78 

has been reported to modulate excitability and synaptic transmission in multiple brain 79 

regions, including both enhancement and suppression of excitatory drive depending on 80 

context and region 2,21 ,22 . Interestingly, alcohol has previously been shown to inhibit 81 

glutamate -induced phase resetting of the SCN clock 23 , further indicating glutamatergic 82 

transmission is involved in mediating alcohol’s effects on circadian rhythms . Specifically, 83 

multiple ex vivo investigations have show n that application of alcohol consistently inhibits 84 

light -like effects of exogenous glutamate on SCN rhythms 24 ,25 . These reports are 85 

corroborated by findings that acute exposure to alcohol in vivo has the same effect on 86 

photic shifts in free -running conditions 26 . Ex vivo effects of chronic exposure and 87 

withdrawal on the master clock are aligned with reports in other brain areas showing 88 

sensitization of glutamate receptors following withdrawal from chronic drinking 27 .89 

Like alcohol, o pioids exert many of their effects by impacting glutamatergic 90 

transmission . Modulation of glutamate signaling by opioids is mediated primarily through 91 

pre - or postsynaptic binding of mu opioid receptors (MORs) to suppress neuronal 92 

activity 28 ,29 . These MORs are highly expressed throughout the central nervous system 93 

and are well known to contribute to both alcohol and opioidergic rewarding effects 30 ,31 . Of 94 

note, it has previously been shown that MOR activation inhibit s ipRGCs 32 -34 , and that mice 95 

lacking MORs selectively in ipRGCs have altered sleep -wake activity 32 . Additionally, it 96

Here, we tested the hypothesis that alcohol and opioids alter SCN physiology by 99

electrophysiology and optogenetics in VGlut2 -Cre;Ai32 and MOR -Cre;Ai32 mice, we 101

their sensitivity to pharmacological modulation by alcohol and the MOR agonist DAMGO. 104 

Our results reveal distinct, converging mechanisms through which depressants modulate 105 

glutamatergic signaling to the SCN, positioning the SCN as a novel node for substance -106 

induced circadian dysregulation .107

SCN neuronal firing in slices. First, to determine the net effect of acute alcohol on the 184 

overall activity of the central clock, we measure d the effect s of bath -applied alcohol on 185 

baseline spontaneous firing. We initially hypothesized that alcohol would lower SCN firing, 186 

as these neurons receive tonic glutamatergic input 40 ,41 , and acute alcohol has been 187 

shown to inhibit the effects of glutamate 23 . Since SCN firing can be largely divided into 188

MOR+ projections to the SCN bidirectionally modulate neuronal firing 272

long -term disruptions in sleep when abused 52 . Additionally , t he endogenous opioid 274 

system is repeatedly implicated with alcohol use disorder 53 , with one of the few FDA -275 

approved pharmacological treatments for AUD, naltrexone, being an MOR antagonist. In 276 

vivo alcohol exposure has been shown to diminish MOR -mediated suppression of 277 

excitatory synaptic transmission in various corticostriatal synapses 54 , and chronic drinking 278 

impairs MOR coupling with G -proteins in multiple brain areas 55 . This sensitivity of MORs 279 

to alcohol may have important implications for circadian entrainment, as it is known that 280
