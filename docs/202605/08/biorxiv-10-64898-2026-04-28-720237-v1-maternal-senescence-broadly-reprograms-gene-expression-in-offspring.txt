# Abstract 20 

Offspring of older parents have reduced fitness in many species , but the mechanisms 21 

mediating this cross -generational dimension of ageing remain poorly understood. Senescence 22 

is associated with genome -wide epigenetic changes that alter transcription , raising the 23 

possibility that older parents transmit dysregulated gene expression patterns . Here we show 24 

that maternal senescence induces deleterious, transcriptome -wide reprogramming of gene 25 

expression in offspring . Gene ontology and pathway enrichment analyses reveal that broad 26 

changes in gene expression that characterise maternal senescence in the clonally reproducing 27 

arthropod Folsomia candida are also observed at a young age in the offspring of old er mothers .28 

These c oncordant fold -changes are apparent at the whole -transcriptome level , and encompass 29 

conserved sequalae of senescence , including reduced carbon and energy metabolism .30 

However, senescent mothers and their offspring also exhibit some contrasting gene expression 31 

patterns , representing distinct transcriptomic signatures of senescence that are not reflected in 32 

its cross -generational effects . The broad reprogramming of gene expression in offspring of old er 33 

mothers is associated with substantially reduced fitness. Our findings show that older females 34 

can transmit a senescen ce -like gene expression syndrome to their offspring , inducing 35 

deleterious phenotypes from a young age .36

# Introduction 37 

As organisms age, cellular processes deteriorate throughout the body (Lu et al. 2026) , leading to 38 

a global decline in physiological function that typically drives increased mortality risk and 39 

reduced reproductive performance (Jones et al. 2014) . Beyond effects of senescence on fertility 40 

(Serre and Robaire 1998; Andersen 2000) and mating success (Bonduriansky and Brassil 2002; 41 

Ruhmann et al. 2018) , parental ageing can also shape offspring phenotypes (Lansing 1947, 42 

1954; Monaghan et al. 2020) . Such “Lansing” or “parental age ” effects have been documented 43 

across diverse taxa, often resulting in developmental abnormalities (Pellestor et al. 2003; Keefe 44 

et al. 2005; Wu et al. 2017) as well as reduced pre -adult survival , adult lifespan , and 45 

reproductive success (Amarya et al. 2018; Ivimey -Cook and Moorad 2020) in offspring of older 46 

parents (al though neutral or even beneficial outcomes have also been reported (Ivimey -Cook 47 

and Moorad 2020; Monaghan et al. 2020) ). This cross -generational dimension of senescence 48 

could represent an important source of variation in fitness, and potentially influence the 49 

evolution of reproductive strategie s and life historie s (Priest et al. 2002; Wylde et al. 2019) . From 50 

a public health perspective, the increasing trend toward delayed motherhood in many human 51 

populations (Burkimsher 2015; Bongaarts et al. 2017; Oecd 2024) also highlights the 52 

importance of understanding the biological mechanisms mediating parental age effects. 53 

Several conserved cytological hallmarks of aging have been identified across diverse animal 54 

taxa (López -Otín et al. 2013; Frenk and Houseley 2018; López -Otín et al. 2023) . These 55 

cytological hallmarks include widespread downregulation of genes encoding mitochondrial 56 

proteins (e.g. components of the electron transport chain (De Magalhães et al. 2009; Glass et 57 

al. 2013; Kumar et al. 2013; Van Den Akker et al. 2014; Peters et al. 2015; Ma et al. 2016; 58 

Cannon et al. 2017; Webb and Sideris 2020) ), protein synthesis and recycling (e.g. ribosomal 59 

proteins and ribosome biogenesis factors (Berchtold et al. 2008; Philipp et al. 2013; Kamei et al. 60 

2014; Janssens et al. 2015; Bryois et al. 2017; Choi et al. 2024) ), and growth factors (Zahn et al. 61 

2005; Schumacher et al. 2008; Ma et al. 2016) . Aging is also associated with the dysregulation 62 

of immune system functions, typically involving an overexpression of pro -inflammatory 63 

mediators and upregulation of immune response genes (e .g. IL -1β, IL -6, COX -2, TNF - α, 64 

Casp1 )(Kim et al. 2002; Terao et al. 2002; Chung et al. 2006; De Magalhães et al. 2009; Mirza et 65 

al. 2011; Lee et al. 2012; Carlson et al. 2015; Bartling et al. 2019) , and a decline in n eur al 66 

function (Burke and Barnes 2006) . The typically deleterious effects of advanced parental age at 67 

reproduction on offspring suggest that some age -related physiological changes are transmitted 68 

across generations . Yet , the mechanisms mediating the effects of parental age on offspring 69 

remain poorly understood .70 

Senescence is associated with extensive changes in gene expression across the genome ,71 

potentially re flecting age -related epigenetic dysregulation (Bollati et al. 2009; Horvath et al. 72 

2012; Horvath 2013; Horvath and Raj 2018; Teschendorff and Horvath 2025; Lu et al. 2026) .73 

Such age -related changes (represented by “epigenetic clocks”) encompass the parental germ -74 

line epigenome (Kawai et al. 2018; Perez and Lehner 2019) , rais ing the possibility that older 75 

parents transmit senescent patterns of gene expression to their offspring and thereby inducing 76 

deleterious phenotypes in offspring from a young age (Bonduriansky and Day 2018; Ashapkin et 77 

al. 2023) . Indeed, human cohort studies show that maternal age is associated with patterns of 78 

DNA methylation at some loci in children (Jiang et al. 2024; Yeung et al. 2024) , and affects gene 79 

expression in human oocytes (Steuerwald et al. 2007; Ntostis et al. 2022) . Moreover, some 80 

genes show similar patterns of DNA methylation and expression in aged parents and their 81 

childre n (Hua et al. 2022) . However, the potential for broad transmission of senescent gene 82

# Methods 97 

# Lab colonies 98 

Folsomia candida is a collembolan arthropod (“springtail”) that reproduces clonally, allowing 99 

for the establishment of an isogenic line (isoline) from a single female (Fountain and Hopkin 100 

2005) . Clonal reproduction makes it possible to investigate effects of maternal age on offspring 101 

while avoiding the potentially confounding effects of selection on genetic variation and the 102 

complications introduced by recombination and sexual reproduction. Ten isolines were 103 

established in 2023 from single females derived from our laboratory -reared stock population 104 

(fed on yeast provided on a substrate of cow dung and cocopeat). This stock originated from 105 

individuals from a wild population collected by Dr. Penelope Greenslade in the Jenolan Caves, 106 

NSW, Australia, and was maintained in our laboratory from 2020. 107 

Before creating the experimental populations for this study, a standardization step was 108 

implemented as follows: Eggs were collected from each isoline. Once these eggs hatched and 109 

nymphs reached 7 –10 days old (large enough to not be damaged during aspirator transfer), they 110 

were standardized to a density of ~50 individuals per container. When these populations 111 

reached 20 –25 days old, offspring from this generation were used to establish the parental 112 

generations for the subsequent experiments described below. 113 

114 

# Animal husbandry 115

1 cm depth) of plaster of paris ( 3201, Uni -Pro Pty Ltd, QLD, Australia ) and activated charcoal 117 

(CL026, ChemSupply Australia Pty Ltd, SA, Australia ) according to OECD guidelines (Oecd 2016) 118 

for rearing of F. candida . Springtail housings were kept at 25 (± .5) ºC and 100% RH (substrate 119 

saturated with RO water) in complete darkness. All adults were fed ad libitum from one batch of 120

ºC until required. Housings were renewed weekly to avoid the proliferation of other fungi and 122 

accumulation of waste products, and to keep population densities constant. Adult springtails 123 

were transferred using an aspirator, and eggs were carefully placed onto black filter paper 124

# Experimental design 129 

We aimed to test whether maternal age can imprint offspring transcription profiles , such that 130 

old mothers produce offspring with a senescence -like transcription profile from a young age 131 

(Fig . 1) . We also examined age -related differences in fitness -related phenotypes between 132 

offspring produced by young versus older mothers. This project was pre -registered with the 133 

Open Science Framework ( 10.17605/OSF.IO/H87BU ). Data and code are available from the 134 

Zenodo repository (Miller et al. 2026) .135 

For gene expression analysis, we established six replicate F0 populations from each of 10 136 

isolines. Each replicate population consisted of a clone produced by a single founder female 137 

nymph, and all replicate populations consisted of 30 individuals per population. From three 138 

replicate F0 populations per isoline, eggs were collected at age 20 -25 days (young maternal age 139 

at breeding: PAY) to generate F1 offspring (OFY). From another three replicate F0 populations 140 

per isoline, eggs were collected at age 70 -75 days (older maternal age at breeding: PAO) to 141 

generate F1 offspring (OFO). Immediately after egg collection, all F0 replicate populations were 142 

frozen in liquid nitrogen and then stored at -80 ºC. Offspring (F1) from each of the replicate 143 

populations were kept at the same density and environmental conditions as their mothers and 144 

then harvested at age 20 -25 days (n = 40 offspring per replicate population) for RNAseq. 145 

Additional individuals from each replicate population were harvested for morphometric 146 

analysis. All F1 samples were immediately frozen in liquid nitrogen and then stored at -80 ºC147 

until RNA extraction . One isoline (isoline 3) was excluded from analysis because of poor RNA 148 

yield. 149

# Reproduction and survival 165 

The same 10 isolines were used to establish another twenty replicate populations, each 166 

replicate population consisting of five same -age individuals. From 10 replicate F0 populations 167 

per isoline, eggs were collected at age 20 -25 days (PAY treatment) to obtain F1 offspring (OFY). 168 

From the other 10 replicate populations per isoline, eggs were collected at age 40 -45 days (PAO 169 

treatment) to obtain F1 offspring (OFO). OFO eggs were thus harvested at younger maternal age 170 

for reproduction and mortality analysis than for gene expression analysis, providing a 171 

conservative assessment of the phenotypic effects of maternal senescence on offspring 172 

fitness. 173 

For each replicate population, development time was quantified as the number of days from 174 

eggs laid to first F1hatching, and age at first reproduction was quantified as the number of days 175 

from first F1 hatchling to first F2 eggs laid. The number of live F1 adults was recorded weekly to 176 

estimate mortality rate. To minimize build -up of waste products, populations were provided with 177 

a substrate composed of plaster mixed with activated charcoal and each replicate population 178 

was transferred to a fresh container weekly. The 20 F1 populations (OFY and OFO) were 179 

maintained, and their fecundity quantified weekly (see below), until at least 3 of the 5 180 

individuals in the population had died or the population reached 167 days of age. 181 

To estimate age -specific fecundity , e ach F1 replicate population container was photographed at 182 

least once per week from above using a Sony RX10 camera and new eggs or hatchlings were 183 

counted, resulting in a total of 2,210 weekly fecundity estimates comprising 274,493 F2 184 

offspring. For practical reasons, fecundity was estimated from a combination of egg and 185 

hatchling counts, depending on the stage of F2 development at each observation . Combining 186 

egg and hatchling counts is unlikely to introduce bias because hatching success was typically > 187 

80% and egg/hatchling counts were randomly distributed among replicates and ages. All egg 188 

counts were done manually, but some hatchling counts were done using automated image 189 

analysis. For automated counts, i mages were automatically cropped to the plaster surface area 190 

using a circular Hough transform and subdivided into overlapping tiles (640 × 640 pixels, 25% 191 

overlap) prior to analysis. A YOLOv8 object detection model implemented using the Ultralytics 192 

framework (Jocker et al. 2023) was trained to detect individual hatchlings using 470 manually 193 

annotated image tiles containing bounding boxes for each hatchling , together with 30 194 

background tiles containing no hatchlings . Of these, 400 tiles were used for model training and 195 

100 were withheld for internal validation during training. Models were trained for 40 epochs .196 

During inference, detections were generated at an image resolution of 640 pixels and filtered 197 

using a confidence threshold of 0.35 and non -maximum suppression with an intersection -over -198 

union threshold of 0.5. Predictions from overlapping tiles were recombined to produce 199 

hatchling counts for each container image.  Model performance was evaluated using the 200 

independent validation set of 100 container images not used during model training. Automated 201 

counts showed strong agreement (r = 0.99) with manual counts ( see Figure S4 ). For images 202 

containing hatchlings, oviposition dates were estimated based on a temperature -specific mean 203 

egg development time of 7 days at 25 °C. Code, trained model weights, and image -processing 204 

scripts are available on the Github repository ( https://github.com/wyldescience/FolSum ).205

# RNA isolation, sequencing, and quality control 207 

From each replicate population, f orty springtails were included i n each cryotube as a pooled 208 

sample. Total RNA was extracted using QIAzol® Lysis Reagent (QIAGEN® , Hilden, Germany )209 

following the manufacturer’s protocol. DNase treatment (DNase I Solution, Thermo Scientific ™)210 

was then performed in a thermocycler per the manufacturer’s instructions . The samples were 211 

washed with ethanol to remove the remaining DNAse . Samples were tested for quantity and 212 

quality with a NanoDrop® ND -1000 (Thermo Scientific ™) and a Qubit ™ 4 Fluorometer (Invitrogen, 213 

Thermo Fisher Scientific, Singapore) with the Broad -Range RNA Assay Kit . Samples were stored 214 

at -80 ºC until sequencing. 215 

A total of 64 purified total RNA samples , comprising all combinations of isoline × treatment × 216 

generation, with some combinations represented by two replicate populations (see Table S 2), 217 

were submitted to the Ramaciotti Centre for Genomics at UNSW Sydney, Australia, for library 218 

preparation and sequencing. Stranded paired -end RNA -seq libraries were generated and 219 

sequenced using a NovaSeq X Plus system with an S4 flowcell, producing 2 × 100 bp paired -end 220 

reads . After library preparation and sequencing , qual ity control of the raw sequencing data was 221 

performed using FastQC v ersion 0. 11 .9 (Andrew 2010). To assess the impact of trimming, we 222 

tested the effect of removing 10 bp from the 5' end and 2 bp from the 3' end due to slight bias in 223 

per base sequence content. However, untrimmed reads exhibited higher mapping rates to the 224 

reference genome, and no (or modest) trimming results in the most biologically accurate gene 225 

expression estimates (Williams et al. 2016; Jocker et al. 2023) , so we proceeded without 226 

trimming. Untrimmed paired -end reads were aligned to the Folsomia candida reference 227 

genome and transcriptome (GenBank accession: GCF_002217175.1 ) using STAR aligner v ersion 228 

2.7.11b (Dobin et al. 2013) with default parameters. A total of 2,829,509,816 reads were 229 

obtained from 64 samples with an average of 44 million reads per sample . Unique mapping 230 

rates to the Folsomia candida reference genome ranged from 70.45% to 85.92% (mean = 231 

79.60%; see Table S 2). The resulting BAM files were processed with featureCounts from subread 232 

v.2.0.2 (Liao et al., 2014) to generate unnormalized gene -level read count matri ces. Replicate 233 

populations were collapsed to generate 36 groups (9 isolines × 2 generations × 2 treatments) for 234 

differential expression analysis .235

# Variability of gene expression 237 

We first investigated whether age influenced the variability of gene expression profiles. 238 

According to the mutation accumulation theory of senescence (Medawar 1952; Hughes et al. 239 

2002) , selection strength becomes weaker as a cohort ages, allowing mutations with late -life 240 

expression to accumulate and result ing in greater transcriptional variability. To test this, we 241 

assessed gene expression variability across treatment groups in both the parental (PAO vs. PAY) 242 

and offspring (OFO vs. OFY) generations. First, we visualized variation in gene expression using 243 

principal component analysis (PCA) implemented via the prcomp() function from the stats 244 

package (version 3.6.2) in R . We then tested for differences in expression variability using a 245 

multivariate homogeneity of group dispersions test using betadisper() from the vegan pack age 246 

(version 2.6 -1 (Dixon 2003) ), applied to the normalized (rlog -transformed) count matrix. 247

# Differential gene expression and functional enrichment analyses 249 

To identify differentially expressed genes (DEGs) between older and young F0 adult s (PA O vs. 250 

PA Y, denoted ΔF0; Figure 1 ) and between the ir F1 offspring at age 20 -25 days (OF O vs. OF Y, 251 

denoted ΔF1 ; Figure 1), we performed two separate genome -wide differential expression 252 

analyses using DESeq2 (version 1.48.1; Love et al., 2014 ). DESeq2 accounts for multiple testing 253 

by applying the Benjamini –Hochberg correction to control the false discovery rate (FDR), 254 

generating adjusted p-values. Genes with an adjusted p-value < 0.05 and an absolute log ₂ fold 255 

change > 1 were considered DEGs. 256 

DEGs were functionally annotated with Gene Ontology (GO) terms using the orthology -based 257 

annotation tool eggNOG -mapper (emapper version 2.1.12; http://eggnog -mapper.embl.de) with 258 

default settings. Although a greater proportion of DEGs could be annotated using the broader 259 

Eukaryotic database ( see Supplementary Information ), we prioritised Arthropoda -based one -to -260 

one ortholog annotations as they provide a more phylogenetically relevant functional context for 261 

springtail genes. This taxon -restricted approach reduces potential annotation noise from 262 

distant homologs and improves the reliability of downstream GO -based interpretation. KEGG 263 

pathway annotations were retrieved for Folsomia candida from the AnnotationHub resource 264 

(accession=AH115244). Functional enrichment analyses were conducted for both comparisons 265 

(PA O vs PA Y and OF O vs OF Y) in R (version 4.5.0) using the enricher function for GO term 266 

enrichment , enrichKEGG for KEGG pathway enrichment , and gseKEGG for gene set enrichment 267 

analyses (GSEA) from the package clusterProfiler (v ersion 4.16.0). 268

# Gene expression differences across generations 270 

To test whether age -related gene expression changes in mothers (F0) were reflected in their 271 

offspring (F1) , we first identified genes that were upregulated or downregulated in both 272 

generations (i.e., showing the same direction of fold -change in ΔF0 and ΔF1) , and genes 273 

showing opposing regulatio n in F0 vs. F1 generations (i.e., showing opposite directions of fold -274 

change in ΔF0 versus ΔF1). We used ggVennDiagram to visualize the overlap of differentially 275 

expressed genes (DEGs) between generations. 276 

We then used the rstatix package (v0.7.2) to calculate Pearson’s correlation coefficient between 277 

the log₂ fold changes in older versus young mothers (ΔF0) and the log₂ fold changes in offspring 278 

of older versus young mothers (ΔF1) . We quantified the fold -change correlation ( ΔF1 ~ ΔF0) 279 

separately across all genes (n=22,555), as well as genes upregulated in both generations ( n =280 

19), genes downregulated in both generations ( n = 112), genes upregulated in older mothers but 281 

downregulated in their offspring ( n = 7), and genes downregulated in older mothers but 282 

upregulated in their offspring ( n = 30). 283 

To determine the probability that the se ΔF1 ~ ΔF0 correlations occurred by chance or as an 284 

artefact of our design , we performed a randomization test to generate null distributions for 285 

comparison. Specifically, we simulated gene expression count matrices for each of the four 286 

experimental groups (PAY, PAO, OFY, OFO) using a negative binomial distribution, which is well -287 

suited for bulk RNA -seq data due to its ability to model overdispersion , which is typically 288 

present in RNA -seq count data (Robinson et al. 2010) . For each gene, simulated counts were 289 

constrained by the observed overall mean and variance of that gene in the real dataset. Using 290 

these simulated matrices, we performed differential gene expression analyses for ΔF1 and ΔF2,291 

following the same pipeline as used for the observed data. From each simulation, we recorded 292

# Reproduction analysis 300 

To investigate effects of maternal (F0) age at reproduction on offspring (F1) fitness -related 301 

phenotypes, linear mixed models were fitted in R version 4.4.2 using the package glmmTMB 302 

version 1.1.14 (McGillycuddy et al. 2025) . Where required, heterogeneity of variances was 303 

accounted for using the function dispformula and zero -inflation was accounted for using the 304 

function ziformula . Model assumptions were tested using the packages DHARMa version 0.5.0 305 

(Hartig 2025) and performance version 0.16.0 (Lüdecke et al. 2021) . F1 development time (days 306 

from oviposition to first hatchling for each replicate population) was analysed using a mixed 307 

model with maternal (F0) age at reproduction (OFO vs. OFY) as a fixed, categorical factor, isoline 308 

identity as a random factor, and Gaussian error structure. F1 age at first reproduction was 309 

analysed using a mixed model with maternal (F0) age at reproduction (OFO vs. OFY) as a fixed, 310 

categorical factor, isoline identity as a random factor, and negative binomial exponent error 311 

structure (with one very high value excluded to improve the distribution of residuals). Total 312 

fecundity of F1 replicate populations (up to age 167 days) was analysed using a mixed model 313 

with maternal (F0) age at reproduction (OFO vs. OFY) as a fixed, categorical factor, isoline 314 

identity as a random factor, and negative binomial exponent error structure. The total fecundity 315 

model was then re -fitted with reproductive lifespan (days between first reproduction and last 316 

reproduction for each F1 replicate) and mean number of individuals alive throughout the 317 

reproductive lifespan of each F1 replicate as additional fixed continuous predictors in order to 318 

estimate effects on individual daily reproductive rate. F1 age -specific fecundity was summed 319 

across 24 -day age intervals and the number of eggs laid per age interval was then modelled with 320 

maternal (F0) age at reproduction (OFO vs. OFY) as a fixed categorical predictor and the mean 321 

number of individuals alive during the time interval and replicate population age at the end of 322 

the time interval as fixed continuous predictors, isoline identity and replicate population 323 

identity as random factors, and negative binomial error structure. To test overall effects of 324 

replicate population age and its interaction with maternal age at reproduction, we carried out a 325 

likelihood ratio test comparing the full model and a simplified model lacking the offspring age x 326 

maternal age interaction using the anova function from the car package version 3.1 -5 (Fox and 327 

Weisberg 2019) . For detailed statistical results, see the Supplementary Information (Tables S5 –328 

S10). 329

# Concordance across generations 473 

To investigate whether offspring of older mothers exhibit gene expression patterns that are 474 

concordant with the senescen t gene expression of their mothers ( ΔF1 ~ ΔF0), we calculated 475 

Pearson’s correlation coefficients (r) between ΔF1and ΔF0 based on log₂ fold changes (log₂FC) 476 

for differentially expressed genes and all genes (Fig. 5a, b). Across all 22, 555 genes, we found a 477 

significant positive correlation between offspring and parental log₂FC values (r = 0.1 4, p<0.001; 478 

Figure 5b), indicating that age -related changes in maternal gene expression tend to be mirrored 479 

from a young age across the offspring transcriptome . Much stronger c oncordance was observed 480 

for the subset of genes showing significant fold -changes in both maternal and offspring 481 

generations, including genes upregulated in both generations (r = 0.90, p<0.001) and genes 482 

downregulated in both generations (r = 0.37, p<0.001) . These results show that older females 483 

transmit a broad syndrome of senescen ce -like gene expression patterns to their offspring. 484 

Notably , the subset of genes that were significantly downregulated in older mothers but 485 

upregulated in their offspring showed negative correlat ion of fold -changes , suggesting 486 

compensatory mechanisms that may counteract some age -related effects across generations (r487 

= -0.55, p<0.001; See Table S3). No correlation of fold -changes was observed for g enes that 488 

were upregulated in older mothers but downregulated in their offspring ( r = 0.03 , p > 0.95 ; Fig. 489 

5b). Simulation analyses confirmed that these cross -generational expression patterns are highly 490 

unlikely to have arisen by chance (Supplementary Information , Figure S2).491

# Discussion 550 

Our results reveal that maternal senescence causes extensive reprograming of offspring 551 

transcriptional profiles in F. candida . Senescent changes in mothers’ gene expression reflected 552 

a multifaceted ageing signature, including reduced neural activity, widespread metabolic 553 

reprogramming, and increased transcriptional investment in DNA repair, chromatin 554 

organization, and proteostasis . In the offspring of older mothers, gene expression was broadly 555 

downregulated for processes related to protein synthesis and proteostasis , mitochondrial 556 

function and core metabolic pathways, indicating reduced cellular biosynthetic and energetic 557 

activity . Intriguingly, four times as many genes showed significant differential expression in 558 

offspring of older mothers than in older mothers themselves, highlighting the importance of 559 

maternal age in shaping transcription al profiles in offspring .560 

Older mothers and their offspring exhibited concordant patterns of differential gene expression 561 

across the entire transcriptome , and especially for loci showing significant differential 562 

expression of similar sign in both generations. For loci of known function, observed fold -563 

changes pointed to reduced capacity for energy production and metabolism in both older 564 

mothers and in their offspring. Older mothers thus appear to induce a senescence -like 565 

transcriptomic syndrome in their offspring via locus -specific transmission of gene expression 566 

patterns . Reprogramming of modifiers that drive consistent gene expression changes at 567 

multiple loci could also contribute to concordance . Nonetheless, t he overlap in genes and 568 

pathways exhibiting significant differential expression in maternal and offspring generations 569 

was not perfect . In particular, we found significantly downregulated expression in pathways 570 

associated with neuronal and muscle function in older mothers but not in their offsprin g.571 

Moreover, some genes exhibited differential expression of opposite sign in older mothers versus 572 

in their offspring , and upregulation of some pathways in older mothers was not mirrored in their 573 

offspring. Thus, despite broad concordance across generations, some gene expression patterns 574 

appear to be unique to older individuals , while other gene expression patterns may be unique to 575 

older individuals ’ offspring .576 

Concordant fold -changes between maternal and offspring generations are consistent with 577 

germ -line inheritance of a senescent epigenome despite epigenetic reprogramming between 578 

generations . This inheritance could involve transmission of senescent patterns of DNA 579 

methylation (Hua et al. 2022) , histone modification (Wu et al. 2022) , noncoding RN A (Li and 580 

Casanueva 2016; Zhou et al. 2025) , or factors engendering self -sustaining metabolic “loops” 581 

(Jablonka and Raz 2009) . It remains unknown whether offspring of older mothers not only begin 582 

life with a senescence -like gene expression profile but also undergo more rapid changes in gene 583 

expression as they age , perhaps as a result of an accelerated “epigenetic clock” (Horvath and 584 

Raj 2018) .585 

Maternal senescence was associated with strongly reduced offspring fitness. O ffspring of older 586 

mothers were smaller , exhibited delayed onset of reproduction , and had lower reproductive rate 587 

and lifetime fecundity . These offspring also exhibited longer lifespans, likely associated with 588 

their reduced investment in reproduction (Reznick 1985, 1992; Stearns 1998) . Our results thus 589 

show that maternal senescence can reduce offspring fitness despite extending offspring 590 

longevity . Observed effects of maternal senescence on offspring gene expression, such as 591 

reduced energy metabolism and protein synthesis, are consistent with the deleterious effects 592 

on offspring fitness . The smaller body size of older mothers’ offspring also suggests r educed 593 

nutrient provisioning of eggs , which could affect offspring fitness . However , reduced nutri ent 594

observed in older mothers’ offspring. There is evidence that deleterious effects of maternal 596

and mitigated by maternal dietary restriction (Gribble et al. 2014; Li et al. 2023) . Environment -598 

induced transcriptional changes could mediat e such effects .599 

Transmission of parental senescence to offspring represents a potential ly important source of 600 

within -population variation in fitness and could have implications for the evolution of ageing 601 

(Priest et al. 2002; Bonduriansky and Day 2018; Wylde et al. 2019; Barks and Laird 2020) . If older 602 

parents produce low -fitness offspring , selection may favour alleles that induce earlier 603 

reproduction. However , if offspring of older parents also have extended lifespan s, fitness 604 

advantages of longer life in environments where background mortality is low could help 605 

maint ain genetic variation in ageing rates within populations. 606 

In human s and other mammals , older parental age is associated with increased risks of 607 

pregnancy failure (Zhang et al. 2022) as well as life -long health problems in offspring (Auroux 608 

1983; Malaspina et al. 2005; Aviv 2012; Wu et al. 2017) . Yet, correlational data from human 609 

populations also suggest that the effects of parental age are highly multifaceted and complex 610 

(Carslake et al. 2017) . Correlational studies in human populations and experimental studies on 611 

animal models suggest that epigenetic reprogramming /dysregulation contribute s to such 612 

effects (Teschendorff and Horvath 2025) . Our findings provide evidence that such 613 

reprogramming involves locus -specific transmission of gene expression across the 614 

transcriptome, induc ing a senescence -like syndrome from a young age . Research on diverse 615 

species is needed to establish how phylogenetically conserved such reprogramming is across 616 

animals .617
