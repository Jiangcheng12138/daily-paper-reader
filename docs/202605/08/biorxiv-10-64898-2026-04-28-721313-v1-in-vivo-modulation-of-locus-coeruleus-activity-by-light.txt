Abstract 1

Dysfunction of the Locus coeruleus (LC), a small brainstem nucleus which consists of 2

the brain's primary source of norepinephrine, is linked to various neurological and 3

psychiatric disorders. Light exposure influences many non-image-forming (NIF) 4

biological functions including those modulated by LC activity. Yet direct evidence of light-5

driven modulation of the LC has remained elusive due to the structure’s small size and 6

deep location. Using ultra-high-field (7 Tesla) functional MRI during an emotional task 7

under varying illuminances, we demonstrate that light modulates LC activity in an 8

emotion-dependent manner. We show that increased illuminance dampens LC 9

responses to negative emotional stimuli while enhancing responses to neutral stimuli. 10 

Furthermore, we provide tentative evidence that within an emotional context, light may 11 

affect LC activity through the basomedial nucleus of the amygdala as well as through a 12 

region of the hypothalamus encompassing the lateral hypothalamus. These findings 13 

open avenues for the development of targeted, non-pharmacological treatments 14 

leveraging light to modulate norepinephrine tone and/or LC activity in several 15 

neuropsychiatric disorders. 16

Significance Statement 18 

Dysfunction of the Locus Coeruleus (LC), a small nucleus of brain and the main source 19 

of norepinephrine, is linked to various brain disorders. Here we provide in vivo evidence 20 

that exposure to light can alter the activity of the LC with higher illuminance leading to 21

a key target for the therapeutic effects of light on emotion, offering a biological 23 

framework for using light to treat neuropsychiatric disorders. 24

Introduction 25 

The locus coeruleus (LC) is a small nucleus of the brainstem - it measures 26 

approximately 12 to 17 mm in length, roughly comparable to the diameter of a penny -27 

that holds great promise as an intervention target for many neurological conditions. The 28 

LC is the main source of norepinephrine in the brain and has widespread monosynaptic 29 

projections to all cerebral territories, including regions essential to sleep, cognition and 30 

mood (1). Interest in the LC is notably rising because it is among the first brain sites 31 

showing neuropathological signs of neurodegenerative diseases, such as Alzheimer’s 32 

disease (AD) and Parkinson’s disease (PD), decades before the typical onset of the 33 

symptoms. Furthermore, changes in LC MRI integrity have been related to early 34 

Alzheimer’s disease-related cognitive symptoms (2). Dysfunction of the LC may also 35 

contribute to the emergence of insomnia disorder, the second most common psychiatric 36 

disorder worldwide (3, 4). In addition, altering LC function, through vagus nerve 37 

stimulation (VNS) or pharmacological interventions, may improve memory function in 38 

healthy older individuals, in Alzheimer’s disease and epilepsy (2, 3, 5–7), while reducing 39 

the severity of seizures in patients with refractory epilepsy (8). Furthermore, 40 

norepinephrine neuromodulation is the target of many anti-depressant drugs (9). Aside 41 

from these interventions, research on animal models and in humans has provided 42 

indirect evidence that the LC activity is acutely affected by light exposure (10–13). Light 43 

could therefore constitute a simple intervention to affect LC-dependent functions, 44 

resulting in positive effects on cognitive, mood and sleep disorders (14–16). 45 

Exposure to light is required for the fine-tuning of numerous non-image-forming 46 

(NIF) biological processes, including aspects that depend in part on LC activity, such as 47 

the regulation of cognition, anxiety, mood, alertness and sleep (17–19). At the retinal 48 

level, the NIF effects of light heavily depend on intrinsically photosensitive retinal 49

melanopsin (10). Depending on light illuminance, ipRGCs combine the inputs of rods 51 

and/or cones with their intrinsic melanopsin-driven responses to funnel light signal to the 52 

brain with a larger efficiency around the blue portion of visible light (20, 21). IpRGCs 53 

target multiple, mostly subcortical, brain structures, especially within the hypothalamus 54 

(10, 19). Critically, magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) was previously used to report 55 

light-induced modulation of a dorsal part of the pons, but the neuroimaging procedure 56 

did not allow ascertaining whether it was within the LC (13). Likewise, the transient pupil 57 

dilation associated with a salient emotional stimulus, a response that is in part mediated 58 

by the LC (22), was affected by light illuminance (23). Beyond this indirect evidence, 59 

whether ambient light affects LC activity has not yet been established in animals or in 60 

humans, in part because of the difficulty of imaging such a small and deep structure. 61 

Here, we tested whether light illuminance affects the responses of the LC to 62 

emotional and neutral auditory stimuli in healthy young adults of both sexes. We 63 

leveraged ultra-high-field 7 Tesla MRI to precisely delineate the LC – using an LC-64 

dedicated MRI sequence (24) – and extract its activity while exposed to varying 65 

experimental light conditions. We further explored linear relationships between the 66 

modulation of activity by light of the LC and of subparts of the amygdala and 67 

hypothalamus. These regions were selected as they are key nodes in the NIF pathway 68 

that exhibit high sensitivity to light (12, 25) in humans and project directly or indirectly to 69 

the LC in animal models (26–28). We anticipated that, compared to neutral items, the 70 

responses of the LC to emotional stimulation would be particularly modulated by light 71 

illuminance – but did not have a priori hypothesis regarding the direction of this 72 

modulation. 73

Discussion 151 

The LC is attracting interest as a potential intervention target for neurological and 152 

psychiatric disorders (34). While pharmacology and VNS stand as promising means to 153 

stimulate the LC (35), here, we report that modulating activity of the LC while executing a 154 

cognitive task can be achieved by modifying the quality of light exposure. We show that 155 

the activity of the LC in response to auditory emotional stimuli progressively decreases 156 

with increasing illuminance, while the pattern is reversed for neutral stimuli. We further 157 

provide evidence that the impact of light on the LC may increase the coupling between 158 

the LC and the posterior lateral part of the hypothalamus, notably the LH and TMN, as 159 

well as the basomedial nuclei of the amygdala. These findings provide more insight into 160 

the neural mechanisms underlying light-induced changes in brain function and may have 161 

implications for the design of interventions for brain disorders using light or mimicking 162 

light effects. 163 

The relationship between behavior and the functioning of the LC is considered to 164 

follow an inverted-U shape dynamic (36, 37). Accordingly, optimal arousal and cognitive 165 

performance is achieved when tonic activity of the LC is at intermediate level. This level 166 

would allow relatively pronounced transient responses of the LC to incoming salient 167 

stimulus, whereas low or excessively high tonic activity is associated with drowsiness or 168 

distractibility, respectively (36, 37). Our results suggest that light affects the activity of the 169 

LC in a stimulus-type-dependent manner. Specifically, as light illuminance increases, the 170 

LC's response to the more salient negative stimuli is progressively dampened. This 171 

could be the result of a reduction in the phasic response of the LC or a progressive 172

increasing illuminance, as reported for other non-emotional items in different cognitive 175 

contexts [e.g. (13)]. 176 

We did not include positive emotional stimulation in the protocol – positive 177 

emotions are more difficult to trigger consistently across participants (38) - so we do not 178 

know whether positive stimuli, which are also salient and presumable affected by 179 

alertness and the level of tonic activity of the LC, trigger an enhanced response of the 180 

LC with increasing illuminance. Given the accepted positive impact of light therapy (39), 181 

we interpret our findings as a reflection of a positive impact of light on emotional brain 182 

function, which could reduce responses to negative stimulations. Importantly, a control 183 

analysis of the adjacent 4th ventricle elicited no significant changes in BOLD signal, 184 

confirming that our results do not stem from non-specific physiological noise. We further 185 

emphasize that the impact of light we report is of limited effect size (η² = .291 186 

corresponding to medium effect size). While our protocol focused on the immediate 187 

effects of transient light exposure, we hypothesize that sustained and repeated exposure 188 

of such modulatory effects over weeks contributes to the sustained clinical 189 

improvements in affective state associated with long-term light therapy. 190 

Animal data did not report direct projections from ipRGCs to the LC. However, 191 

there are indirect projections from the ipRGC targets to the LC. The SCN, site of the 192 

main circadian clock, receives dense ipRGC inputs and indirectly projects to the LC 193 

through the dorsomedial hypothalamus (DMH) (11, 40). The orexinergic LH also 194 

received ipRGC inputs and projects to the LC to regulate arousal and the maintenance 195 

of wakefulness (41). Here, we find a statistical trend that the activity modulation of the 196 

LC by light illuminance is correlated to the activity of the posterior subpart of the 197

We further note that while a seasonal variation in the activity of the amygdala and 207 

in the impact of light illuminance on its activity was observed (25), we did not find 208 

seasonal changes in LC activity in the present study. We assume therefore that in 209 

contrast to other structures of the brain (42) LC activity is not showing prominent 210 

seasonal variations, at least within our experiment set up. In addition, even though 211 

reaction times to the auditory stimuli were affected by light illuminance, we did not find 212 

significant correlation between the activity of the LC (or of the amygdala like previously 213 

reported (25)) and reaction time. This is potentially a reflection of the fact that behavior is 214 

primarily mediated by the prefrontal cortex, a region that receives dense noradrenergic 215 

projections from the LC, and that a network of cortical and subcortical areas may be 216 

jointly affected by light to set behavior. 217

corresponds to the commonly recommended time-of-day for light therapy, we did not 219 

vary the time-of-day of light administration when it is known to influence the impact of 220 

light (43, 44). This could also be addressed in future investigation, together with other 221 

factors including prior sleep duration and age (45, 46). Future research could include 222

Study Overview 260 

The data analyzed in this study were collected as part of a larger research initiative, with 261 

methodological details previously reported (23, 55, 56). The study protocol was 262 

approved by the Ethics Committee of the University of Liège Faculty of Medicine (ref. 263 

2020/11). All participants provided written informed consent and received financial 264 

compensation. Data acquisition occurred at the University of Liège between December 265 

2020 and September 2023. 266 

Participants 267

psychiatric and neurological disorders, sleep disorders, use of psychoactive drugs or 270

night shift work during the last year or recent trans-meridian travel during the last 2 272

Overall Protocol 283 

Structural brain images were acquired 1 to 2 weeks before the experiment, during a visit 284 

which served as habituation to the experimental conditions. Participants then followed a 285 

loose sleep-wake schedule (±1h from habitual sleep/wake-up time) for 7 days, to 286 

maintain realistic entrained life conditions and avoid excessive sleep restriction (verified 287 

using wrist actigraphy -AX3, Axivity, UK- and sleep diaries). Participants arrived at the 288 

laboratory 1.5 to 2h after their habitual wake time. To standardize participants' recent 289 

light history, they were exposed to 5min of bright white light (1000 lux) and were then 290 

maintained in dim light (<10 lux) for 45min. During the dim light exposure, participants 291 

were given instructions about the fMRI cognitive tasks and completed practice tasks on a 292 

luminance-controlled laptop (<10 lux) (Figure 1A) . The fMRI sessions consisted of an 293 

executive task (25min), an attentional task (15min), and an emotional task (20min), 294 

which is the only task included in the present paper (Figure 1B) . Participants always 295 

completed the executive task first as it was the most demanding task. The order of the 296 

following two tasks was counterbalanced across participants. While in the MR-scanner, 297

Light Exposure 301 

We delivered light from a light box (SugarCUBE, Ushio America, CA, USA) to the MRI 302 

coil using an 8m optic fiber (1 inch diameter, Setra Systems, MA, USA). This 303

time, a stand positioned the dual ends of the optic fiber with reproducible fixation and 306 

orientation. In addition, we used a filter wheel (Spectral Products, AB300, NM, USA) and 307 

optical fiber filters (monochromatic narrowband orange filter - 589mn; full width at half 308

The task consisted of a sex-related voice discrimination task with emotional and neutral 332

Quantification and Statistical Analysis 414 

First-level statistical analyses were conducted within the general linear model (GLM) 415 

framework implemented in SPM12. The onsets of emotional and neutral prosody stimuli 416 

were modeled as stick functions and convolved with the canonical hemodynamic 417 

response function (HRF). Low-frequency drifts were removed using a 256 s high-pass 418 

filter. Physiological (cardiac and respiratory), estimated using the PhysIO Toolbox 419 

(Translational Neuromodeling Unit, ETH Zurich, Switzerland), were included as 420 

covariates of no interest to account for physiological noise (68). 421

Figure 1. Experimental protocol 1

(A) Light Adaptation. Following 7 days of actigraphy-verified regular sleep-wake 2

schedule (± 1h), participants arrived at the laboratory 1.5h after their habitual 3

wake-up time. They underwent a light adaptation protocol consisting of 5 minutes 4

of bright light adaptation (1000 lux) followed by 45 minutes of dim light (<10 lux) 5

adaptation before entering the fMRI scanner. 6

(B) fMRI Session. Within the session, 35 participants (24.49 ± 3.32 y; 22 women) 7

performed an executive task, an emotional task, and an attentional task. Only the 8

emotional task is analyzed in this manuscript. 9

(C) fMRI Set Up. Participants were positioned with their heads inside a 7T MRI coil. 10 

Light was delivered via an optic fiber to illuminate the inside of the coil, exposing 11 

participants to four light conditions defined by their Spectral Power Distribution 12 

(SPD): Monochromatic orange (0.16 mel EDI lux) and three polychromatic blue-13 

enriched levels (6500K; Low: 37 mel EDI; Mid: 92 mel EDI; High: 190 mel EDI; 14 

see Suppl. Table S2 ). Blue-enriched illuminances were selected to maintain 15 

photon flux consistency with prior studies (29, 30). While under these light 16 

conditions, participants performed an emotional task involving gender 17 

classification of meaningless vocalizations (32). Unknown to participants, 50% of 18 

vocalizations were neutral (white) and 50% were pronounced with 19 

angry/emotional prosody (red) by professional actors. For current ROI analyses, 20 

conditions were indexed solely by melanopic EDI lux. Panels A-C copied from 21 

under a CC BY 4.0 license (12) . 22 

(D) LC Measures. Continuous pupillometric data were recorded using an MR-23 

compatible infrared eye-tracker. Pupil diameter is a well-established non-invasive 24 

proxy for central norepinephrine (NE) activity. Whole-brain BOLD signal was also 25 

recorded during the task, allowing activity extraction of the LC. 26
