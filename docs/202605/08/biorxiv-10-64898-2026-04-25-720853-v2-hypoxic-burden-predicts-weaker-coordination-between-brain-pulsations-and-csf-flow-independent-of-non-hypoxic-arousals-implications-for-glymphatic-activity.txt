> Abstract Objective.  To examine whether hypoxic burden is distinctly associated with key drivers of glymphatic activity, independent of sleep fragmentation.
> Background.  OSA is a robust risk factor for multiple neurodegenerative conditions, including Alzheimer’s disease, with glymphatic impairment representing a potential mechanistic pathway. However, it remains unknown whether distinct pathological features of OSA, including hypoxic burden and sleep fragmentation, are differentially associated with the two key physiological drivers of glymphatic activity: (1) coordination between brain pulsations and CSF flow and (2) brain pulsation strength.
> Methods.  Twenty-eight individuals with newly identified OSA and eight healthy individuals without OSA completed either in-lab polysomnography or WatchPAT, providing estimates of hypoxic burden, quantified as time spent below 90% oxygen saturation (T90), and sleep fragmentation, quantified as non-hypoxic respiratory effort-related arousals (RERAs). Participants also completed 7T resting-state fMRI to quantify the coordination between brain pulsations and CSF flow using gBOLD-CSF coupling (i.e., cross-correlation between gray matter blood oxygen level dependent signal pulsations and CSF inflow) and brain pulsation strength using gBOLD amplitude. Hierarchical regression and Pearson correlations were used to examine associations between sleep measures and fMRI-derived metrics.
> Results.  Greater T90 was associated with weaker gBOLD-CSF coupling, independent of age, sex, race, and RERAs (Table 2; β=0.07,  p=0.008). T90 also significantly improved model-explained variance in gBOLD-CSF coupling (Table 2; ∆R 2=0.20,  p=0.008). Contrary to expectations, greater T90 was associated with higher gBOLD amplitude across the temporal lobe and multiple frontal and parietal regions. Within regions showing T90-linked elevations in gBOLD amplitude, higher gBOLD amplitude was not associated with stronger region-specific gBOLD-CSF coupling. > 1. Introduction
> Obstructive sleep apnea (OSA) is a disorder characterized by recurrent upper airway obstruction, and affects nearly one billion adults worldwide. 1 OSA is a robust risk factor for multiple neurodegenerative conditions including Alzheimer’s disease and related dementias (ADRD), 2 with glymphatic impairment representing a potential mechanistic pathway. 3-5  The glymphatic system is a recently proposed brain waste clearance pathway, which transports metabolic waste products out of the brain via bulk cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) flow through perivascular spaces. 6-11

A key driver of glymphatic transport is anti-phase coordination between brain pulsations, defined as rhythmic expansions and contractions of the vasculature and surrounding tissue, and CSF flow. 12-14  Because the brain must maintain constant intracranial volume, blood inflow and displacement during each pulsation cycle are accompanied by reciprocal CSF volume change, generating the anti-phase fluid motion necessary for solute transport. 12-15 

> Prior research has shown that individuals with OSA exhibit weaker coordination between brain pulsations and CSF flow, with greater discoordination observed at higher levels of apnea-hypopnea index (AHI). 16-18  However, AHI reflects a composite of two distinct consequences of intermittent upper airway closure in OSA, namely intermittent hypoxemia and respiratory effort-related sleep fragmentation. 1 Thus, the respective contributions of hypoxic burden and respiratory effort-related sleep fragmentation to the coordination between brain pulsations and CSF flow, independent of one another, remain unknown. In addition to the coordination between brain pulsations and CSF flow, the strength of brain pulsations themselves is also central to glymphatic transport. 13,15  Increases in the amplitude of rhythmic expansions and contractions of the vasculature and surrounding tissue can produce larger perivascular space volume changes, which can translate into stronger pulsatile CSF flow. 14  Nonetheless, whether the two distinct pathological mechanisms of OSA, i.e., hypoxic burden and respiratory effort-related sleep fragmentation, are differentially associated with brain pulsation strength remains unclear.

> Distinguishing between potential impact of hypoxic burden and respiratory effort-related sleep fragmentation is important because the two OSA features may differentially modulate brain pulsation-CSF flow. Hypoxic burden, often quantified as time spent below 90% oxygen saturation, 19  may induce hypoxic injury to the vasculature, leading to vascular stiffening, 20  which may nterfere with brain pulsations and their coordination with CSF flow. Regions with high metabolic demand, such as the frontotemporal cortex, may be especially vulnerable to hypoxic injury of cerebral vessels, 21  which could translate into greater alterations in brain pulsations. On the other hand, respiratory effort-related sleep fragmentation without hypoxemia can be captured by respiratory effort-related arousals (RERAs), defined as respiratory events that lead to arousal without a significant drop in peripheral oxygen saturation. 22,23  Such incidences of sleep fragmentation may disrupt oscillations in norepinephrine tone, which contribute to rhythmic vasoconstriction and vasodilation of the cerebrovasculature that drive glymphatic activity. 11,14,24
> To address these gaps, this study examined the associations of hypoxic burden and non-hypoxic sleep fragmentation with the coordination between brain pulsations and CSF flow, with particular emphasis on distinguishing their independent associations. In addition, the study examined the associations of hypoxic burden and non-hypoxic sleep fragmentation with region-specific brain pulsation strength, quantified as pulsation amplitude. From a clinical perspective, these findings may help clarify whether interventions that preferentially reduce hypoxemia, improve sleep continuity, or target both processes are more likely to improve fluid dynamics that support glymphatic activity.

> This study examined 36 individuals aged 55 to 75 years. The sample included 22 adults with a new clinical diagnosis of OSA recruited from the Mount Sinai Integrative Sleep Center and 14 community-dwelling, cognitively normal, healthy, older adults who completed in-lab polysomnography at the Mount Sinai School of Medicine. While the community-recruited subjects did not report any sleep complaints, OSA (AHI4% > 5/hour) was incidentally identified in 6 out of the 14 subjects, resulting in a final sample of 28 individuals with OSA and 8 OSA-free healthy controls. Exclusion criteria included having any self-identified cognitive complaints, any sleep disorders other than OSA, sleep duration of less than 5 hours or greater than 10 hours per night based on actigraphy, any significant neurological or psychiatric disorders, chronic usage of any psychoactive drugs for a duration over 8 weeks aside from single selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors, any unstable comorbid conditions, and metal in the body. All 36 participants met the study’s eligibility criteria.
> 2.2. Diagnostic sleep studies
> Participants completed either in-laboratory polysomnography or WatchPAT-based home sleep testing, a clinically validated method for assessing OSA that has shown excellent agreement with in-laboratory polysomnography. 25-27  The procedures for each modality are described below.
> 2.2.a. Polysomnography:  Fourteen participants recruited from the community completed an overnight, attended in-laboratory polysomnography at the Mount Sinai Integrative Sleep Center using Compumedics E-series (Melbourne, Australia). 28  Participants were connected to polysomnography and the lights off time was scheduled to approximate their self-reported habitual bedtime. Signal acquisition included electroencephalography, electro-oculography, electromyography, a nasal cannula, pressure transducer, oral thermistor, rib or abdomen impedance plethysmography, single-channel electrocardiography, and pulse oximetry. All polysomnography recordings were scored by 1 or 2

For all participants, hypoxic burden was defined as time spent under 90% oxygen saturation (henceforth referred to as T90), which was calculated as the cumulative duration in which peripheral oxygen saturation as measured by pulse oximetry remained below 90% (in minutes). In addition, non-hypoxic sleep fragmentation was defined as respiratory effort-related arousals (RERAs), calculated by following steps. 29,30  First, AHI3%a was calculated and included all apneas, defined as an absence of airflow for ≥ 10 seconds, 28  and hypopneas, defined as a reduction in breathing amplitude by 30% or more for ≥ 10 seconds terminating in ≥ 3% decline in blood oxygen saturation or an arousal. 29,30  AHI3% was calculated in a similar manner and included all apneas, 28  but only the hypopneas where breathing amplitude was reduced by 30% or more for ≥ 10 seconds terminating in ≥ 3% decline in blood oxygen saturation, without arousals. RERAs were calculated by taking the difference between AHI3%a and AHI3%.

> 2.3. 7T MRI Protocol
> Participants completed 9 to 15 minutes scans of resting-state functional MRI on a 7T scanner (Magnetom, Siemens, Erlangen, Germany) with a 32-channel head coil (repetition time [TR]=1.5 seconds, echo time [TE]=0.025, flip angle [FA]=55°, iPAT acceleration factor=3, bandwidth=33.921/pixel, 1.5 mm isotropic resolution). For the blood oxygen level dependent signal (BOLD) sequence, the bottom slice was aligned to the 4 th  ventricle to capture CSF inflow, consistent with prior research. 31  In addition, whole-brain T1-weighted gradient echo (MP2RAGE) anatomical images were acquired (TR=4500 ms, TE=3.37 ms, TI1=1000s and 3200 ms, FA=4 and 5°, iPAT acceleration factor=3, bandwidth =130 Hz/pixel, 0.7 mm isotropic resolution). Processing steps for resting-state scans included deobliquing, motion-correcting, skull-stripping, spatial smoothing, grand mean-scaling, detrending of linear and quadratic trends, and registration to the structural scan.

> Coordination between brain pulsation and CSF flow was measured using gBOLD-CSF, which quantifies temporal coupling between gray matter blood oxygen level dependent signal and CSF inflow. 12  Using processed resting-state scans in participants’ native space, gBOLD-CSF coupling was calculated as the cross-correlation between the gray matter-wide average BOLD signal time series and the CSF signal time

> This frequency range is thought to be particularly important for glymphatic activity because it often exhibits the greatest pulsation amplitude and contributes to bulk CSF flow. 13,15,31,35  The gray matter-wide average BOLD timeseries were extracted by registering a gray matter mask to the processed resting-state fMRI scans in participants’ native space and averaging signals across the gray matter voxels. In addition to the global measure of coordination, we quantified regional gBOLD-CSF coupling by calculating the cross-correlation between region-specific gray matter time series and CSF timeseries captured from the 4 th  ventricle, also at a 3-second lag time.
> 2.3.b. Brain pulsation strength at resting-state
> Brain pulsation strength was defined for each cortical region as the amplitude of gray matter BOLD signals, also in the vasomotion frequency range (<0.1 Hz). 13,15,31,35 .13,15,31,35  Following procedures used in previous studies, 16  the 3dRSFC function in AFNI was applied to processed resting-state scans with a gray matter mask to produce voxelwise maps of resting-state fluctuation amplitude. Average amplitudes were calculated for each region as defined by the Desikan-Killiany atlas. 39

> Sample characteristics were examined using means and standard deviations (SDs) for continuous variables and as counts and proportions for categorical variables. Differences in sample characteristics by OSA status were assessed using t-tests for continuous variables and chi-square tests for categorical variables. The primary analysis examined the variance in coordination between brain pulsation and CSF flow attributable to the two distinct types of OSA burden (i.e., T90 and RERAs) using hierarchical linear regression. Specifically, gBOLD-CSF coupling was modeled as a function of age, sex, and race, non-hypoxic RERAs, and T90. Statistical significance of non-hypoxic RERAs and T90, as well as the incremental variance explained by respective each term’s inclusion were evaluated using the change in R 2. As RERAs include non-hypoxic arousals only, we examined the sensitivity of results by including AHI3%a, which also includes hypoxic arousals, in place of RERAs in the hierarchical regression approach. In addition, we examined associations between brain pulsation strength in each gray matter region, quantified as gBOLD amplitude, and the two distinct OSA burden measures, T90 and RERAs. For each brain region, Pearson correlations were calculated between gBOLD amplitude and T90 or RERAs, with p-values derived from 10,000 permutation resamples. We then tested whether the association between regional gBOLD amplitude and regional gBOLD-CSF coupling differed according to whether that region’s amplitude was significantly associated with the corresponding OSA measure. Specifically, region-specific gBOLD-CSF coupling was modeled as a function of region-specific gBOLD amplitude, an indicator denoting whether the amplitude-OSA association reached statistical significance for that region, and their interaction. T90 and RERAs were examined separately using mixed models in which brain regions were nested within individuals, with regions at level 1 and individuals at level 2. Statistical significance was defined a p<0.05. In all analyses, T90, RERAs, AHI3%A, region-specific gBOLD amplitude, and region-specific gBOLD-CSF coupling were log-transformed to satisfy the condition of normality. All analyses were conducted in MATLAB R2024a and Stata/MP 17.0.

> 3. Results
> Sample characteristics can be found in Table 1. Mean age was 66.3 years [SD = 5.5], 58.3% of participants were female, and 66.7% were White. The average Montreal Cognitive Assessment score was 26.9 [2.8], and mean body mass index was 30.3 [5.5]. On average, the AHI4% of 26.3 [21.6] events per hour, participants experienced 2.1 [2.1] RERAs per hour, and T90 was 23.4 [46.3] minutes. Average sleep duration was 385.1 minutes (SD=63.3). While the majority of characteristics were comparable between the two groups, the OSA group had fewer females and higher values for BMI, AHI3%, AHI4%, and T90. Pearson correlation analysis showed that greater T90 was associated with weaker global gBOLD-CSF coupling (Figure 1A; Pearson r=0.32, permutation p=0.020; positive coupling values indicate weaker coordination between gray matter pulsations and CSF inflow). RERAs were not associated with gBOLD-CSF coupling (Figure 1B; Pearson r=0.02, p=0.46). Sensitivity test replacing RERAs with AHI3A also yielded a non-significant association (Pearson r=0.24, p=0.09). After controlling for age, sex, race, and RERAs, greater T90 significantly predicted weaker gBOLD-CSF coupling (Table 2; β=0.003,  p=0.01). > 4. Discussion
> The primary motivation of the current study was to investigate whether two distinct pathological mechanisms of OSA, hypoxic burden and sleep fragmentation, are differentially associated with fluid dynamics that support glymphatic activity. Given that hypoxic injury to cerebral arteries may promote vessel stiffening, we hypothesized that greater hypoxic burden would be associated with disrupted coordination between brain pulsations and CSF flow. 14,15  Supporting this hypothesis, greater hypoxic burden was robustly associated with weaker gBOLD-CSF coupling, independent of non-hypoxic sleep fragmentation and demographic characteristics. Interestingly, neither RERAs, which represent non-hypoxic sleep fragmentation, nor AHI3%A, which captures sleep fragmentation regardless of hypoxemia, was associated with coordination between brain pulsations and CSF flow, despite the potential impact of sleep fragmentation on norepinephrine tone, which modulates vasomotion. 14,24,40  Together, these findings suggest that hypoxic burden may be the primary driver of deficits in fluid dynamics that support glymphatic activity in OSA. Prior research has shown that coordination between vasomotion and CSF flow is a critical driver of CSF-ISF exchange and solute transport within the glymphatic system. 14,15
> Accordingly, brain pulsations and vasomotion show tight anti-phase coordination with CSF movement in normal brains and together support solute transport. 13-15  In comparison, reduced anti-phase coordination between brain pulsations and CSF flow has been linked to ADRD pathology. 33,41,42  Our study extends this literature by suggesting that hypoxic burden in OSA may play a unique role in impairing fluid dynamics that support glymphatic activity, which may in turn help explain the elevated risk of ADRD in

To the best of our knowledge, this is the first study to disentangle the contributions of hypoxic burden and non-hypoxic sleep fragmentation to coordination between brain pulsations and CSF flow. In addition to the coordination between brain pulsations and CSF flow, brain pulsation strength also plays a key role in glymphatic activity by inducing convective flow and modulating perivascular space size. 13,15  Thus, regional analyses were conducted to examine associations between hypoxic burden and region-specific brain pulsation strength, as measured by gBOLD amplitude. Contrary to our expectations, greater hypoxic burden was associated with higher gBOLD amplitude across the temporal lobe and in multiple frontal and parietal regions, but not in the occipital cortex. Effect sizes were particularly large in temporal regions, and no regions showed a negative association with hypoxic burden. Sleep fragmentation, measured using RERAs, was not associated with gBOLD amplitude. The mechanisms underlying the positive association between hypoxic burden and gBOLD amplitude remain unclear. Nonetheless, the large effect sizes observed in the temporal lobe, a region with high metabolic demand that is vulnerable in OSA, may suggest a pathological rather than adaptive process. 43,44  Future research is needed to determine whether these higher-amplitude pulsations in OSA reflect compensatory or pathological responses to hypoxic injury, potentially reflecting impaired maintenance of stable vascular tone or increased vasodilation to preserve oxygen delivery. 21,44-46
> Interestingly, elevated brain pulsation strength did not appear to translate into improved coordination with CSF inflow. Across the temporal cortex and multiple frontal and parietal regions showing T90-linked elevations in gBOLD amplitude, greater gBOLD amplitude was not associated with stronger regional gBOLD-CSF coupling. This pattern contrasts with the strong association found between gBOLD amplitude and gBOLD-CSF coupling within regions unaffected by T90, which is also consistent with previous studies

Hypoxic injury to the vasculature is known to promote vascular stiffening, 20  which may impair the ability of vascular pulsations to generate appropriately timed CSF displacement, thereby contributing to the observed dissociation between elevated gBOLD amplitude and regional gBOLD-CSF coupling. This study has multiple strengths. First, it distinguishes the association of hypoxic burden with fluid dynamics that support glymphatic activity from that with non-hypoxic sleep fragmentation using objective, validated sleep measures. Second, the sample included racially diverse individuals, improving generalizability to the population experiencing OSA. Third, MRI was conducted at 7T, providing improved resolution that can help enhance signal quantification, registration, and gray matter segmentation. Fourth, the OSA group consists of individuals newly diagnosed with OSA who has yet to begin treatment, precluding potential confounding by OSA treatment history. However, this study is not without limitations. First, brain pulsations and their temporal coupling to CSF flow were not assessed during sleep, when glymphatic activity is thought to be most active. 3,14,47
> Nonetheless, growing evidence suggests that the glymphatic system is also active during wakefulness, 48
> and wake-based measurements may provide meaningful information about abnormalities in brain pulsation and CSF flow dynamics relevant to the glymphatic system among individuals with OSA. Second, sleep assessments were conducted using different modalities. However, prior studies, together with our supplementary data, show that the WatchPAT demonstrates excellent agreement with in-lab polysomnography for all characteristics examined in the current study. 25-27,49  Third, our study included a

> 4.1. Conclusions
> The current study shows that OSA is associated with deficits in fluid dynamics that support glymphatic activity, with hypoxic burden emerging as the primary driver of impaired coordination between brain pulsations and CSF inflow. Although hypoxic burden was associated with greater brain pulsation strength across the temporal lobe and multiple frontal and parietal regions, this did not translate into improved coordination with CSF inflow. Together, these findings suggest that reduced compensatory CSF inflow in response to brain pulsations in the aforementioned regions may, at least in part, contribute to the observed disruption in coordination between global brain pulsations and CSF inflow. Neither non-hypoxic respiratory effort-related sleep fragmentation nor AHI3%A, which captures respiratory effort-related sleep fragmentation regardless of hypoxemia, was associated with brain pulsation-CSF dynamics. In sum, hypoxic burden may represent a distinct pathway through which OSA contributes to impaired glymphatic transport of metabolic waste products and, in turn, ADRD.
