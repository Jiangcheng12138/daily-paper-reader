Amyloid plaques are a pathological hallmark of Alzheimer ’s disease, but how they drive 10 

widespread neuronal dysfunction remains unclear. While studies in anesthetized animals show that 11 

plaques drive local hyperactivity 1,2 , it is unknown how this pathology shapes functional 12 

hippocampal maps in freely behaving animals. We combined chronic 1 -photon calcium imaging, 13 

local field potential recordings, and post hoc 2-photon plaque imaging in freely behaving APP/PS1 14 

mice across behavior and sleep to correlate real -time hippocampal activity and place coding with 15 

precise plaque topography. Here we show that p laques exert nonlocal, long -range effects on 16 

hippocampal activity that depend on plaque size, laminar position, and the animal ’s behavior al 17 

state . Place cells , which encode spatial position and are normally uniformly distributed, are 18 

preferentially enriched near plaques, revealing an aberrant reorganization of plaque -adjacent 19 

neurons into the hippocampal map of space . In longitudinal  experiments , pre -existing  place cell 20 

locations  do  not predict future plaque sites , whereas hyperactivity during slow -wave s leep w eakly 21 

predicts future amyloid deposition . These  findings  identify  a mechanism  by which  amyloid 22 

pathology reorganizes brain circuits , degrading the functional architecture of the hippocampus 23 

and contributing to widespread dysfunction and cognitive impairment in Alzheimer ’s disease .24

During exploratory behavior, many hippocampal neurons form stable spatial representations 26 

known as place fields . Notably , spatial representations deteriorate in parallel with spatial memory as 27 

plaques accumulate in murine amyloid -42  (A models of Alzheimer ’s disease 3–5, mimic king early 28 

navigational deficits in patients (e.g., 6). Aberrantly hyperactive and silent neuronal subpopulations in 29 

hippocampus and cortex of murine Alzheimer ’s disease models suggest that disrupted excitatory -30 

inhibitory balance unde rlie s A-associated circuit  dysregulation 1,2,7 . Fibrillar and oligomeric 31 

Aspecies are both neurotoxic  and synaptotoxic –  and are well -positi oned to disrupt circuit function .32 

In anesthetized Alzheimer ’s disease model mice, aberrantly hyperactive cells have been found 33 

to cluster exclusively near amyloid plaques, while silent cells have been found to be uniformly 34 

distributed 1,2 . However, behavioral state critically influences hippocampal activity 15,16 , and anesthetics 35 

have complex effects on hemodynamics, neurophy siology , and calcium homeostasis 17 –19 . Therefore ,36 

whether plaque -associated hyperactivity occurs during normal behavior s and across the sleep -wake 37 

cycle , and how it impacts place coding, remains unknown . Furthermore, the impact of aberrant 38 

clustering of hyperactive cells proximal to plaque s on place coding has not been established; this 39 

clustering and hyperactivity is likely to undermine place coding and degrade pattern separation needed 40 

for accurate storage of unique experiences .41 

To address these gaps , we quantified CA1 calcium dynamic s and concomitant 42 

electrophysiological activity in freely -behaving APP/PS1 mice , and then co -registered these data to 43 

high -resolution plaque topography. We show that plaques induce nonlocal, long -range , state -dependent 44 

effects on neuronal activity and place coding that depend on plaque size and laminar location within 45 

CA1 . These findings provid e a new framework in which amyloid plaque s act as foci of network -level 46 

circuit reorganization , rather than as purely focal lesions .47

Behavioral state transforms the spatial influence of amyloid plaques on hippocampal circuits. 49 

To investigate how amyloid plaques impact hippocampal function across behavioral states , we 50 

acquired simultaneous chronic GCaMP6f calcium imaging and local field potential (LFP) recordings in 51 

the CA1 region of freely behaving APP/PS1 mice across the sleep -wake cycle , followed by high -52 

resolution plaque imaging (Fig. 1a,b and Supplementary Fig . 1; see Methods ). We stratified neurons 53 

into three activity classes, high activity, moderately active, and quiescent cells, based on state -specific 54 

calcium event rates (Fig. 1 b,c ). 55 

In contrast to previous observations made in anesthetized Alzheimer ’s mice 1,2 , high -activity 56 

cells  were not  restricted to the immediate vicinity of amyloid plaques,  but rather  were detected at all 57 

distances from the nearest plaque  across all  behavioral state s (Fig.  1d  and  Supplementary Fig.  2). 58 

Although the proportion of high -activity cells visually decreased with distance from the nearest plaque 59 

during running and slow -wave sleep, Spearman correlations between distance and the fraction of cells 60 

in each activity class were not significant in any  state after Bonferroni correction ( Fig. 1d ).  By contrast, 61 

CA1 activity  profiles  varied significantly  with  behavioral state ( Fig.  2a ), indicating that  behavioral 62 

state, rather than local plaque pathology , primarily determines the distribution of activity strata. 63 

We next modeled calcium rate as a continuous function of distance to the nearest plaque .64 

Proximity to plaques was associated with higher activity  in high -activity cells across  states,  with  effect s65 

extending  over hundreds of microns , and  with lower activity  in  quiescent  cells  during running  (Fig. 2b ).66 

Moderately  active cells  showed  a nuanced  response,  with slightly lower activity  near plaques during 67 

running  and slightly higher activity  during  QW, SWS, and REM sleep.  Importantly,  activity  class  was 68 

state -dependent rather than  an intrinsic property of the cell , supporting  plaque -induced dysfunction  as  a69 

dynamic circuit -level phenomenon.  The  specific relationship  between plaque proximity and  neuronal 70 

activity varied markedly across the sleep -wake cycle  (Fig.  2b ).  These results suggest that plaques  induce 71 

differential effects on neuronal activity  that  depend on  both  activity type and behavioral state. 72

Plaque size and laminar position determine the magnitude of spatial circuit disruption 74 

Amyloid plaques varied in size and laminar position, with some centered in the pyramidal cell 75 

layer  and  others in dendritic layers of s tratum  radiatum and s tratum  oriens ( Fig.  3a ).  Because  amyloid 76 

plaques disrupt both the neuronal processes that traverse them 20  and local inhibitory synapses 21 , we 77 

next  determine d whether  plaque size or  laminar  position  within CA1  correlated  with  specific  activity 78 

patterns .79 

Neuronal activity levels were strongly associated with both plaque size and laminar position 80 

(Fig.  3b ). Large  pyramidal -layer plaques  were associated with  the  highest  plaque -adjacent  activity  and 81 

the  steepest  decay  with distance. Small  pyramidal -layer  plaques  showed  intermediate  effect s, and 82 

dendritic -layer  plaques  had the smallest  impact . Th us,  the spatial extent and magnit ude of amyloid -83 

driven  neuronal dysfunction are  shaped  by  specific plaque characteristics and anatomical location. 84

Place cell topography does not prefigure future amyloid deposition sites 105

fluid 25 . To  determine whether  place cells aggregate near amyloid plaques  or whether pre -existing  place 107 

cell clusters drive future  plaque  deposition , we acquired dynamic calcium imaging in CA1 of young 108

Discussion 117 

Alzheimer ’s disease is characterized by progressive hippocampal failure, yet local plaque density 118 

correlates poorly with cognitive impairment. Our findings resolve this paradox by showing that amyloid 119 

plaques act as drivers of long -range , state -dependent circuit dysfunction in both neuronal activity and the 120 

hippocampal map of space. This framework implies that the cognitive burden of the disease reflects 121 

network -level collapse due to nonlocal plaque -associated disturbances , rather than sim ply the volume or 122 

density of local plaque deposition .123

Place coding is a key function of the hippocampus, and its deterioration in Alzheimer ’s patients 124 

and in Alzheimer ’s models is well established 22 . The biased participation of plaque -adjacent cells in the 125 

representation of space reveals a novel mechanism whereby A  deposits reorganize existing functional 126 

circuits to degrade the functional architecture of the hippocampus. With accumulation of amyl oid plaques 127 

in Alzheimer ’s disease , excessive reliance on a reduced pool of available neurons to represent space 128 

within and across environments would be expected to progressively impair both hippocampal remapping 129 

and pattern separation, fundamental feature s of hippocampal mnemonic function 26 .130 

Our results also suggest that amyloid plaques drive the clustering of place cells rather than vice 131 

versa, as our longitudinal studies did not detect a predictive effect of place cell locations on future 132 

plaque positions. Although the mechanisms underlying the reorganization of place cells to cluster near 133 

amyloid plaques are unknown, loss of inhibitory synapses in the region around amyloid plaques, as 134 

recently reported 21 , is well -positioned to contribute, as may the disruption of local processes 20 .135 

Alternatively , as a smaller proportion of neurons was found to represent space in this Alzheimer ’s136 

model compared to WT mice, another possibility is that place coding was lost in cells far from plaques. 137 

Unexpectedly, we detected long -ra nge gradients of neuronal activity centered on amyloid plaques 138 

rather than highly local plaque effects. Although the biological basis of these effects is not yet clear, and 139 

our estimates are necessarily limited by the spatial extent of the imaging window, we anticipate that 140 

spatial gradients of plaque -derived soluble A  oligomers may contribute, as  these species may diffuse 141 

through the interstitial space to disrupt synapses far beyond the physical boundaries of the plaque 10,12 .142 

Prior reports of aberrantly high and low activity of hippocampal and cortical neurons in amyloid 143 

plaque -bearing Alzheimer ’s models have supported the concept of perturbed excitatory -inhibitory 144 

balance in Alzheimer ’s disease 1,2,7 . Behavioral state has a marked effect on such imbalance 16 . The pre sent 145 

results extend these findings, showing that the spatial effects of plaques on neuronal activity are also 146 

markedly state -dependent. In Run behavior, aberrant hyper - and hypoactivity was greatest close to 147

Consistent with the toxicity of A  and the local impact of amyloid plaques on the brain 151 

parenchyma, large plaques were more disruptive to neuronal activity than small plaques. Interestingly, 152 

large plaques deposited in the pyramidal cell layer were most disruptive, while plaqu es in the dendritic 153 

layers were least disruptive. In contrast, we observed that place cells were clustered more around 154 

dendritic layer plaques than cell layer plaques. These observations – which suggest a laminar -specific 155 

vulnerability of the hippocampal m ap – may reflect differential effects on inhibitory versus excitatory 156 

synapses, given their laminar distribution within CA1 27 as well as the possible contribution of loss of 157 

local inhibitory synapses 21,28 .158 

Intrigui ngly, we found that the locations of hyperactive cells in SWS predicted future plaque 159 

locations, albeit weakly. As SWS is the one behavioral state associated with amyloid clearance 29,30 , it is 160 

possible that release of A  in SWS by such hyperactive ce lls overwhelms local clearance mechanisms, 161 

enabling local A  concentrations to rise above the local critical threshold for oligomerization and 162 

deposition. Future work is needed to evaluate this hypothesis , and larger longitudinal cohorts will be 163 

required t o determine the robustness of this association .164 

Key highlights of this study include linking spatial representations and neuronal activity across 165 

behavioral states to the local influence of amyloid plaques in the hippocampus, a region critically 166 

involved in early Alzheimer ’s pathology and memory loss. A further strength was the use of 2P 167 

microscopy to image amyloid plaques, providing an accurate assessment of plaque burden across the 168 

laminar extent of CA1. While dynamic calcium imaging provides a robust m easure of large -scale 169 

neuronal ensembles, future studies incorporating high -density electrophysiology may provide 170

Behavior and sleep staging 386 

Data were acq uired as animals explored a linear track without reward and were then moved to 387 

their home cage within the same recording room to sample subsequent quiet wakefulness (QW), slow -388 

wave sleep (SWS), and REM sleep. Animals were exposed to the track for 1 to 3 da ys (2. 5 ± 0. 3 days) 389 

before experiments. Cortical delta (1 -4 Hz), hippocampal theta (4 -10 Hz) and ripples (100 -300 Hz), 390 

and mouse position and posture were used to identify behavioral states: Run was defined as animals 391 

explored the linear track with speeds exceeding 1 cm/sec and was characterized by a strong theta 392 

oscillation; QW was identified when animals were stationary (< 0.5 cm /sec) for at least 5 seconds with 393 

their eyes open; SWS was identified by the presence of sharp wave ripples (SWRs) and high delt a/theta 394 

LFP power ratio (>2) in animals in a sleep posture; REM sleep was identified as epochs of high 395 

theta/delta LFP power ratio (>2) in animals in a sleep posture 32 .396

Calcium imaging: Inscopix Data Processing Software (IDPS, Inscopix, Palo Alto, California) was used 415 

for movie preprocessing, spatial filtering, motion correction, normalization ΔF/F, and constrained non -416

per calcium event, and was compared to 1000 shuffled spatial information distributions to test 426 

significance (Monte Carlo p -value < 0.05) 36 , and taken as the larger of the LR and the RL trajectories. 427
