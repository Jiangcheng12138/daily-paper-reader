# Abstract 14 

Interventions that extend lifespan do not necessarily preserve healthspan, the portion of life 15 

spent in good health. This disconnect has intensified interest in biological aging clocks as 16 

quantitative proxies of organismal health. However, most existing clocks rely on invasive or 17 

endpoint measurements, providing static estimates that capture biological age at a single time 18 

point and offer limited insight into aging trajectories – the dynamic patterns through which 19 

physiological resilience and functional capacity change within individuals over time. 20 

Here we combine standardized, high -frequency imaging of individual Caenorhabditis 21 

elegans across the lifespan with machine learning to develop MOSAIC (Modular Organismal 22 

Signature of Aging In C. elegans ), a non -invasive phenotypic clock that estimates biological age 23 

longitudinally at single -organism resolution. Leveraging ~3 ’750 animals, ~230 ’000 observations 24 

and 29 phenotypic features, MOSAIC predicts biological age with high accuracy and resolves 25 

organism -wide aging trajectories at high temporal resolution. Beyond age prediction, MOSAIC 26 

decomposes biological age into contributions from distinct physiological modules, enabling 27 

mechanistic interpretation of organismal decline. 28 

Applying MOSAIC to natural lifespan variation, dietary restriction, longevity mutants and 29 

pharmacological interventions reveals that lifespan extension can emerge through distinct, time -30 

dependent phenotypic trajectories rather than a uniform slowing of aging. Interventions with 31 

similar effects on longevity produce divergent biological -age trajectories and distinct 32 

combinations of younger and older traits, highlighting context -dependent physiological trade -33 

offs. MOSAIC provides a scalable, non -invasive framework to repeatedly quantify biological age 34 

across the lifespan and to compare interventions based on how they reshape aging trajectories .35

# Introduction 39 

With populations aging worldwide, identifying genetic, dietary and pharmacological 40 

interventions that extend not only lifespan but also healthspan has become a major biomedical 41 

priority 1. Numerous lifespan -extending interventions have been reported across species, 42 

from Caenorhabditis elegans to humans 2–6. However, longer life does not necessarily mean 43 

healthier life. In C. elegans , several long -lived mutants exhibit extended periods of frailty, and the 44 

relationship between lifespan and healthspan varies across interventions and genetic 45 

backgrounds 7,8 . In humans, gains in life expectancy have likewise outpaced improvements in 46 

healthy life expectancy 9,10 . Together, these findings highlight a key limitation of lifespan as a sole 47 

metric of aging and underscore the need to understand how interventions reshape aging 48 

trajectories – the dynamic progression of physiological decline within individuals. 49 

Tracking healthspan requires quantifying biological age, a measure of functional state that 50 

captures organismal condition more accurately than chronological time. Over the past decade, 51 

machine -learning -based aging clocks have emerged as powerful tools to estimate biological age 52 

from molecular, clinical and phenotypic data 11 –15 , often predicting morbidity, functional decline 53 

and mortality risk better than chronological age 16 . Yet most existing clocks rely on cross -54 

sectional or endpoint measurements collected in cohorts, capturing population -level variation 55 

rather than the dynamics of aging within individuals 17,18 . As a result, these approaches provide 56 

limited insight into whether interventions slow the pace of aging, alter its trajectory, or both – key 57 

distinctions for understanding mechanisms that promote healthspan. 58 

The nematode C. elegans , with its short lifespan (~3 weeks) and optical transparency, provides 59 

a tractable system for longitudinal, non -invasive measurement of aging. Several molecular aging 60 

clocks based on transcriptomic data have achieved accurate prediction of biological age in this 61 

organism 19 –21 , but require destructive sampling and therefore cannot track individuals 62 

continuously across life. Phenotypic clocks based on functional or morphological traits offer a 63 

complementary strategy for longitudinal assessment 22 –26 , but require large standardized 64 

datasets that are difficult to generate reproducibly across laboratories and experimental 65 

conditions, as highlighted by initiatives such as the Caenorhabditis Intervention Testing 66 

Program²⁷ ˒²⁸ . Here we combine standardized high -frequency imaging with machine learning to 67 

develop MOSAIC (Modular Organismal Signature of Aging in C. elegans ), a longitudinal 68 

phenotypic clock that predicts biological age at the level of individuals with high accuracy 69 

(median R² = 0.84) and resolves organism -wide aging trajectories from ~230 ’000 observations 70 

across 29 phenotypic features in ~3 ’750 animals. Applying MOSAIC across genetic, dietary and 71 

pharmacological interventions reveals that comparable lifespan extensions can arise from 72 

distinct, nonlinear biological age trajectories, reflecting independent dynamics of physiological 73 

modules. Our work establishes a non -invasive framework to quantify aging trajectories across 74 

the lifespan and reveals unexpected diversity in organismal aging dynamics. 75

Dietary Restriction (DR) modalities converge on a shared phenotypic aging signature 199 

Dietary restriction (DR) is a robust lifespan -extending intervention conserved across taxa 37 . In C. 200 

elegans , DR can be achieved by reducing bacterial food availability or through genetic 201 

perturbations that mimic reduced nutrient intake or activate DR -associated pathways 38,39 . We 202 

leveraged three complementary DR paradigms: reduced bacterial density (12.5% of ad libitum; 203 

N2 DR), the feeding -defective mutant eat -2(ad465) , characterized by reduced pharyngeal 204 

pumping 40 , and slcf -1(tm22 58 ), in which loss of the monocarboxylate transporter SLCF -1205 

activates DR -associated molecular programs 41,42 . These independent modalities allowed us to 206 

test whether our phenotypic clock detects intervention -dependent changes in aging trajectories 207 

and whether distinct DR paradigms converge on a common phenotypic signature. 208 

Consistent with prior work, DR significantly extended lifespan (Fig. 4a). Wild -type animals 209 

exposed to reduced bacterial density from the L4 stage throughout adulthood exhibited a 31% 210 

increase in median lifespan and a 46% increase in maximal survival (95th percentile; p < 0.0001) 211 

relative to ad libitum (AL) controls (Fig. 4a). Genetic DR models also showed increased longevity, 212 

albeit to a lesser extent: median lifespan increased by 21% in eat -2(ad465) and 18% in slcf -213 

1(tm2286) , while maximal survival increased by 46% and 24%, respectively (Fig. 4 a) .214 

Our phenotypic clock detected consistent reductions in phenotypic age across DR conditions 215 

(Fig. 4 b). Wild -type animals subjected to DR displayed younger phenotypic ages than AL controls 216 

across most of adulthood, with divergence emerging shortly after intervention onset (Fig. 4 b, Fig. 217 

S13 ). Similarly, eat -2(ad465) and slcf -1(tm22 58 ) mutants exhibited reduced phenotypic age 218 

compared with wild -type animals under AL condition. The magnitude and kinetics of phenotypic 219 

age reduction varied between modalities, with weaker effects observed in slcf -1(tm22 58 ) and 220

Distinct phenotypic aging trajectories underlie similar lifespan extension after early - and late -242 

onset DR 243 

The timing of geroprotective interventions can critically influence their impact on aging 244 

trajectories, yet whether comparable lifespan extensions arise from similar or distinct 245 

organismal states remains unclear. To address this, we implemented four feeding regimens 246 

differing in the timing of DR: ad libitum feeding throughout adulthood (AL), lifelong DR at 20% 247 

food availability (DR), ad libitum feeding followed by DR initiated on day 6 of adulthood (AL -DR), 248 

and early -life DR followed by a return to ad libitum feeding from day 6 onward (DR -AL) (Fig. 5a). 249 

Lifelong DR produced the strongest lifespan extension, increasing median lifespan by 49% 250 

relative to AL controls (Fig. 5b). In contrast, DR limited to early adulthood (DR -AL) or initiated 251 

post -reproduction (AL -DR) resulted in smaller but comparable increases in median lifespan 252 

(+18% relative to AL), indicating that distinct intervention schedules can yield similar survival 253 

outcomes. 254 

Analysis of phenotypic age trajectories revealed pronounced differences in organismal 255 

responses to DR timing (Fig. 5c,d). Worms exposed to early -life DR (DR -AL) closely followed the 256 

phenotypic trajectory of lifelong DR animals until approximately day 12 of adulthood and 257 

remained phenotypically younger than AL controls until day 17. In contrast, worms switched from 258 

AL to DR at day 6 (AL -DR) exhibited a delayed and transient reduction in phenotypic age, 259 

appearing younger than AL controls between days 8 and 16. However, neither phenotypic age 260 

trajectories nor SHAP feature profiles converged toward those observed under lifelong DR (Fig. 261 

5c -e), indicating that late -onset DR only partially recapitulates the phenotypic effects of 262 

continuous DR .263

Longitudinal phenotyping reveals time -dependent genetic interactions shaping aging 270 

trajectories 271 

We next asked whether longitudinal phenotypic age measurements could resolve epistatic 272 

interactions in aging pathways that are not apparent from survival outcomes alone. To address 273 

this question, we analyzed slcf -1(tm22 58 ) and daf -18(e1375) mutants and their corresponding 274 

double mutant. In C. elegans , daf -18 , the ortholog of the tumor suppressor PTEN, is required for 275 

normal lifespan and contributes to hormetic stress responses induced by slcf -1276 

downregulation 41,42 . However, the extent to which daf -18 contributes to slcf -1-mediated 277 

longevity remains unclear. 278 

Lifespan measurements obtained in our microfluidic system recapitulated previous 279 

observations (Fig. 6a). Loss of daf -18 shortened lifespan relative to wild type, whereas mutation 280 

of slcf -1 increased median lifespan by ~20% in both wild -type and daf -18 mutant backgrounds. 281 

These results suggest that lifespan extension conferred by slcf -1 is largely independent of DAF -282 

18 activity when evaluated at the level of survival. 283 

To determine whether these mutations different ial ly influence biological age , we compared two 284 

complementary measure ments : transcriptomic age and phenotypic age. Transcriptomic age 285 

was estimated using the BiT age clock 19 . In young adults, all genotypes exhibited similar 286 

transcriptomic ages (Fig. 6b). By day 7, however, daf -18 mutants were predicted to be 287 

biologically older than wild -type animals, whereas slcf -1 mutants appeared younger. These 288 

trends were recapitulated by our phenotypic clock, which detected significant genotype -289 

dependent differences in biological age (Fig. 6c). In double mutants, the two clocks showed 290 

partially divergent predictions: transcriptomic age more closely resembled the daf -18 profile, 291 

whereas phenotypic age was intermediate between the two single mutants. These single time -292 

point measurements suggest partial suppression of the slcf -1 phenotype by daf -18 , but do not 293 

reveal when divergence in aging trajectories emerges. 294 

Longitudinal phenotypic trajectories provided additional temporal resolution (Fig. 6d). 295 

Consistent with earlier results, slcf -1 mutants exhibited slower phenotypic aging than wild -type 296 

animals from early adulthood onward. In contrast, daf -18 mutants initially followed a trajectory 297 

similar to wild type until approximately day 5 of adulthood, after which phenotypic aging 298 

accelerated. In daf -18; slcf -1 double mutants, divergence from the slcf -1 trajectory emerged as 299 

early as day 3, earlier than in the wild -type background, and was followed by accelerated 300 

phenotypic aging relative to slcf -1single mutants. These results indicate an early genetic 301 

interaction between daf -18 and slcf -1 that alters the temporal dynamics of phenotypic aging. 302 

Notably, when trajectories were expressed as a function of relative age, the double mutant 303 

aligned with daf -18 single mutants (Fig. 6e), indicating that daf -18 phenotype predominates in 304 

shaping the aging trajectory of slcf -1 mutants across the lifespan. 305

transcriptomic age while providing substantially higher temporal resolution. Although slcf -307 

1 extends lifespan in a daf -18 mutant background, DAF -18 is required for the characteristic 308 

temporal pattern of slcf -1-mediated slowing of aging. More broadly, these results illustrate how 309

Phenotypic clock enables early detection of multidimensional effects of candidate 312

Lifestyle interventions such as dietary restriction (DR) robustly extend lifespan, yet their long -314 

term implementation in humans remains challenging, motivating the identification of 315 

pharmacological alternatives (geroprotectors) 43 . However, candidate geroprotective 316 

compounds often produce modest lifespan extensions that approach the intrinsic variability of 317 

survival measurements, making their evaluation slow, costly, and frequently inconclusive when 318 

relying solely on lifespan. We therefore applied our longitudinal phenotyping framework to 319 

pharmacological interventions expected to produce subtler effects than the aging mutants 320 

described above , t he biguanide metformin and the NAD ⁺ precursors nicotinamide riboside (NR) 321 

and nicotinamide (NAM ). 322 

Metformin has been shown to extend lifespan in C. elegans and mice 44,45 and recent work 323 

indicates that it can slow transcriptomic aging clocks in non -human primate s3. Age -associated 324 

depletion of nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide (NAD ⁺) has been linked to multiple age -related 325 

pathologies, including sarcopenia, prompting interest in interventions that restore NAD ⁺326 

homeostasis 46,47 . We first evaluated metformin hydrochloride at 25 mM and 50 mM, 327 

concentrations previously reported to increase C. elegans lifespan 44 . Under our experimental 328 

conditions, both doses produced modest increases in median lifespan (+13.5% and +8.6%, 329 

respectively; Fig. 7a) . In contrast, longitudinal phenotypic analysis revealed consistent 330 

reductions in phenotypic age under both metformin treatments (Fig. 7b,c). Metformin -treated 331 

animals could be distinguished from untreated controls as early as day 4 of adulthood, well 332 

before differences in survival outcomes became apparent. The highe r concentration produced a 333 

more sustained shift toward younger phenotypic age across adulthood. SHAP decomposition 334 

indicated that th is reduction was primarily driven by improvements in motility, transparency, and 335 

reproductive output, whereas intestine -associated traits shifted toward relatively older states, 336 

yielding a multidimensional profile partially reminiscent of DR -associated phenotypic 337 

remodeling (Fig . 7d and Fig 4d ). 338 

We next examined NAD ⁺-boosting compounds by testing NAM and NR across three 339 

concentrations each. None of the tested conditions significantly reduced overall phenotypic age. 340 

Nevertheless , high er doses induced selective functional improvements, most prominently 341 

increased motility and transparency at 2 mM NR (Fig. S15), demonstrating that longitudinal 342 

phenotyping can detect domain -specific physiological effects even in the absence of 343 

measurable lifespan extension. 344 

To directly compare pharmacological interventions with established longevity paradigms, we 345 

projected SHAP -derived trait differences induced by drug treatments together with those 346 

associated with DR conditions (DR, eat -2, slcf -1) and daf -18 mutants into a shared phenotypic 347 

space using PCA (Fig. 7e). NAD ⁺-boosting conditions clustered together, with the highest 348 

concentrations (NAM 250 μM and NR 2 mM) producing the strongest phenotypic shifts. 349 

Metformin -treated animals localized closer to DR -associated phenotypic states, with the highest 350 

tested concentration producing the largest displacement toward the DR region of phenotypic 351 

space. 352 

Together, these results demonstrate that l ongitudinal phenotypic age measurements provide a 353 

sensitive framework to evaluate candidate geroprotective compounds beyond survival outcomes 354

# Discussion 360 

Understanding how interventions influence aging requires quantitative frameworks that capture 361 

organismal state across time rather than relying solely on survival outcomes. Here we developed 362 

a longitudinal phenotyping platform that integrates high -frequency behavioral, morphological, 363 

and physiological measurements to quantify multidimensional aging trajectories in C. elegans .364 

Across genetic perturbations, dietary interventions, and candidate geroprotective compounds, 365 

our results indicate that aging cannot be fully described by a single rate parameter but instead 366 

reflects dynamic remodeling of organismal state throughout the lifespan. Distinct lifespan -367 

extending interventions converged on shared phenotypic signatures associated with a younger 368 

phenotypic age, yet similar longevity outcomes also arose from divergent aging trajectories 369 

depending on intervention timing or genetic background. By resolving these temporal dynamics, 370 

longitudinal phenotypic age provides a quantitative framework to dissect how environmental and 371 

genetic factors reshape biological aging trajectories and reveals multiple physiological path s to 372 

lifespan extension. 373 

Microfluidic technologies have transformed C. elegans research by enabling precise 374 

environmental control and automated longitudinal measurements under standardized 375 

conditions 48 –51 . Using the SydLab ™ One platform, we monitored multiple aging biomarkers in 376 

parallel at high temporal resolution, generating a high -content atlas of organism -level aging 377 

trajectories across the lifespan. Age -associated changes in motility 33,52,53 , behavior 22,34,54 ,378

Leveraging this dataset, we developed MOSAIC, a non -invasive video -based phenotypic clock 383 

capable of estimating relative age from short (2s) recordings obtained at any stage of adulthood. 384 

Multiple machine learning architectures achieved similar predictive performance, indicating that 385 

accuracy primarily derives from the biological depth of the phenotypic features rather than the 386 

modeling approach itself. The contribution of individual traits to phenotypic age prediction varied 387 

across the lifespan, underscoring the context -dependent nature of aging biomarkers. Compared 388 

with previous phenotypic predictors of biological age 22,25,35 , the improved performance of our 389 

approach likely stems from the combination of high sampling frequency, large cohort size, and 390 

multidimensional feature integration. Phenotypic age estimates correlated with transcriptomic 391 

age predicted by the BiT age clock, supporting the biological relevance of this non -invasive 392 

measure. Because it relies exclusively on bright -field imaging, this strategy enables repeated 393 

measurements in the same individuals and provides a scalable framework to quantify how 394 

interventions dynamically modulate biological aging trajectories. 395
