Abstract 10 The adult brain retains a capacity for adaptation, yet the long-term sequence of plasticity remains 11 poorly understood. To address this, we performed longitudinal mesoscopic calcium imaging of 12 the dorsal cortex in spontaneously behaving adult mice before and after bilateral enucleation 13 (BE). We uncovered behaviour-dependent and spatially distinct changes in cortical activity 14 organised into two sequential, overlapping windows. An early window, spanning from day 1 to 15 week 3–5, was marked by reduced activity in visual and retrosplenial cortices during locomotion, 16 with movements suppressing rather than enhancing activity, as is typically observed. A second, 17 delayed window (week 1 to week 7–10, peaking around 3 weeks) was characterised by increased 18 slow-wave activity in primary visual cortex (V1) and lateromedial visual area (LM), indicative of 19 heightened excitability. Consistently, activity increased—rather than decreased—at the transition 20 from locomotion to quiescence. In parallel, we observed a rapid, long-lasting, and behaviour-21 dependent reorganisation of cortical network structure. Together, these findings demonstrate that 22 cortical states in the adult brain are dynamic and can undergo substantial, months-long plasticity. 23 The temporal and spatial dissociations between windows identified suggest distinct plasticity 24 mechanisms in the circuit regulating cortical states. Therefore, our study provides a 25 spatiotemporal framework to guide future investigations into the mechanisms of plasticity in the 26 mature brain. 27

Introduction 28 The mature brain retains a remarkable capacity for adaptation beyond the closure of critical 29 periods of plasticity (Gilbert & Li, 2012; Hübener & Bonhoeffer, 2014; H.-K. Lee & Whitt, 30 2015). Years after vision loss in adult humans, the visual cortex is recruited by many non-visual 31 tasks, reflecting robust cross-modal reorganisation of deprived visual areas (Kupers & Ptito, 32 2014; Paré et al., 2023). However, the sequence of plasticity mechanisms underlying this long-33 term adaptation remains poorly understood. 34 In adult mice, the early stages of this process in the primary visual cortex (V1) have been 35 partially characterised. Within 1–2 days following visual deprivation, excitatory activity is 36 restored through homeostatic plasticity mechanisms (W. Wen & Turrigiano, 2024), involving a 37 disengagement of inhibitory neurons and a strengthening of excitatory synapses onto pyramidal 38 neurons, thereby re-establishing the excitation–inhibition balance (Barnes et al., 2015; J. L. Chen 39 et al., 2011; Keck et al., 2011, 2013). At a much later stage—approximately seven weeks after 40 deprivation—post-mortem analyses reveal the emergence of cross-modal plasticity driven by 41 whisker inputs (Van Brussel et al., 2011). Together, these findings point to a substantial temporal 42 gap between rapid homeostatic adjustments and the onset of cross-modal reorganisation, 43 spanning several weeks during which the dynamics of cortical adaptation remain largely 44 unknown. 45 To address this gap, we performed longitudinal mesoscopic calcium imaging (Cardin et al., 46 2020; Couto et al., 2021; Silasi et al., 2016; Vanni et al., 2017) in head-fixed behaving mice 47 before and after bilateral enucleation (BE). During initial data exploration, we observed an 48 unexpected shift in V1 activity across behavioural states following BE. In sighted mice at 49 baseline, V1 activity was lower during quiescence than during locomotion, consistent with well-50 established state-dependent modulation of cortical activity (Polack et al., 2013; Poulet & 51 Crochet, 2019; Reimer et al., 2014; Stringer et al., 2019; Vinck et al., 2015; West et al., 2022). 52 Strikingly, in the same animals, three weeks after BE, this relationship reversed, with V1 activity 53 becoming higher during quiescence than during locomotion. This unexpected reversal motivated 54 a systematic investigation of behavioural state as a key dimension of cortical plasticity—an 55 aspect that has been largely overlooked in longitudinal studies of adult plasticity. 56 We identified months-long, behaviour-dependent plasticity of cortical activity following adult 57 vision loss. This process unfolded in two distinct temporal windows. First, during an early phase 58 spanning from day 1 to approximately 3–5 weeks after bilateral enucleation (BE), locomotion 59 exerted an inverted effect on cortical activity, suppressing rather than activating the visual and 60 retrosplenial cortices. Second, during a later phase extending from week 1 to approximately 7–10 61 weeks, the primary visual cortex (V1) and the lateromedial visual area (LM) exhibited increased 62 slow-wave activity during quiescence. In parallel with these state-dependent changes, we 63

Longitudinal mesoscopic calcium imaging during spontaneous behaviour 69 We recorded dorsal cortical calcium activity in adult Thy1-jRGECO1a mice (Dana et al., 2018) 70 during head-fixed spontaneous behaviour up to 10 weeks following BE. Mice were implanted 71 with a chronic imaging chamber, enabling optical access to most dorsal cortical regions (Silasi et 72 al., 2016) (Fig. 1a; see Methods). Mesoscopic calcium imaging was performed by illuminating 73 the cortex with a lime LED to excite jRGECO1a and recording red fluorescence at 30 Hz with a 74 sCMOS camera (Fig. 1a). Images were segmented using the Allen Mouse Brain Atlas (Oh et al., 75 2014) aligned to functional visual maps (and somatosensory maps when available), and pixel 76 values were averaged within an area around the centroid of each region. Calcium signals were 77 corrected for hemodynamic absorption artefacts using red and green reflectance imaging (See 78 Methods) (S. Shahsavarani et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024). Behaviour was monitored using 79 infrared video recordings at 30 Hz, and movement (motion energy) was extracted from a 80 manually defined region of interest using Facemap (Syeda et al., 2024) (Fig. 1a; see Methods). 81 This approach was applied in both short-term (1–25 days post-BE) and long-term (1–10 weeks 82 post-BE) longitudinal protocols (Fig. 1b). 83

Sequential and overlapping windows of behaviour-defined plasticity 129 To characterise the temporal dynamics of behaviour-related cortical activity following bilateral 130 enucleation (BE), we segmented mouse behaviour into quiescence and locomotion epochs based 131 on motion energy (Fig. 1c). Transitions were identified using a change-point detection algorithm 132 (Lohani et al., 2022; Vinck et al., 2015), and included epochs ≥10 s and separated by 3 s from 133 transitions. Overlaying behavioural states onto activity distributions revealed a clear segregation 134 of V1 activity between locomotion and quiescence (Fig. 2a). For each epoch, we computed the 135 power spectral density and quantified activity as the maximum power within the 0.2–4 Hz band. 136 During quiescence, BE significantly altered activity in V1 and the lateromedial area (LM) across 137 both protocols (Fig. 3a). V1 activity exhibited a transient increase at day 1, recovered by day 2, 138 and then progressively increased again from week 1 onward, remaining elevated relative to 139 baseline at week 10. Spectral analysis revealed that this increase was driven by enhanced slow-140 wave activity: power in the 0.2–4 Hz range increased at day 1 (peaking near ~1 Hz), returned to 141 baseline at day 2, and rose again from week 1 to 10, with peak changes initially in the 0.4–0.6 Hz 142 range and shifting toward lower frequencies over time (Fig. 3b, Fig. S2a). These results indicate 143 a sustained, slow-wave–dominated state during quiescence following BE. 144 In contrast, during locomotion, BE led to a marked suppression of activity in visual and 145 retrosplenial cortices (Fig. 3c). In V1, activity was significantly reduced from day 1 to week 3, 146 reaching a minimum around day 5 before recovering to baseline by week 5. Power spectra 147 showed a broad reduction across frequencies, consistent with a global decrease in activity (Fig. 148 S2c,d). 149 Direct comparison between behavioural states revealed a reversal of the canonical relationship 150 between locomotion and quiescence. At baseline, V1 activity was higher during locomotion, 151 whereas after BE, locomotion-associated activity became significantly lower than quiescence 152 until approximately week 7, after which no significant difference remained (Fig. 3d). Movement initiation suppresses posterior cortical activity after BE 178 As stated above, the analysis of activity during quiescence and locomotion revealed two 179 sequential, overlapping windows of plasticity. We next asked whether these changes in activity 180 were present at transitions between behavioural states. Transition times were identified using a 181 change-point detection algorithm applied to motion energy, and for each transition, we computed 182 ΔF/F₀ relative to the pre-transition baseline. 183 At baseline, the transition from quiescence to locomotion (locomotion onset) was marked by a 184 rapid increase in motion energy accompanied by a robust increase in V1 activity, which 185 remained elevated for several seconds (Fig. 4a). A similar response was observed in other 186 cortical regions, including the hindlimb (HL) area of the primary somatosensory cortex (Fig. 187 S4a). In contrast, from day 1 after bilateral enucleation (BE), locomotion onset failed to elicit 188 this activation in V1, while responses in HL remained largely unchanged (Fig. 4a, Fig. S4a). 189 190

At baseline, these events elicited transient increases in activity 205 across multiple cortical regions, including V1 (Fig. 5c, Fig. S5a). Following BE, however, visual 206 cortical responses were selectively reduced, with V1 activity significantly suppressed from day 1 207 to week 5 (Fig. 5c). Notably, the temporal profile of this suppression 212 depends on movement type, with locomotion producing stronger and more prolonged effects than 213 micro-movements. These differences likely reflect the larger baseline activation evoked by 214 locomotion. After BE, however, both movement types converge toward similarly reduced activity 215 levels (Fig. S5b), indicating a generalised, state-dependent reorganisation of movement-related 216 cortical responses over a week-long period. 217

Rapid, long-lasting and behaviour-dependent reorganisation of the cortical network after 262 

BE 263 Our regional analyses revealed two sequential, overlapping windows of behaviour-dependent 264 plasticity with spatially distinct effects across the cortex. Such localised changes are expected to 265 impact large-scale functional network organisation. To test this, we computed the pairwise 266 correlation matrices from activity during spontaneous behaviour. Following BE, we observed a 267 marked reduction in functional connectivity within the visual cortex, and between visual and 268 retrosplenial, somatosensory, and motor regions (Fig. S7a,b). To quantify the temporal evolution 269 of this reorganisation, we measured network similarity by correlating each time-point matrix 270 with the first baseline matrix. This analysis revealed a sharp drop in similarity as early as day 1, 271 which persisted throughout the 10-week recording period (Fig. S7c). 272 Given the behaviour-dependent changes in regional activity after BE, we next asked whether 273 changes in cortical network organisation were similarly influenced by behavioural state. During 274 quiescence, functional connectivity decreased within the visual cortex and between visual, 275 retrosplenial, somatosensory, and motor regions (Fig. 7a). Network similarity analyses revealed 276 modest and protocol-dependent effects: a transient reduction at day 2 in the short-term dataset, 277 but a sustained decrease across all time points in the long-term dataset (Fig. 7b), indicating 278 relatively limited reorganization in this state. Homotopic connectivity changes were restricted to 279 V1, with a transient increase at week 2 followed by a decrease at week 10 (Fig. 7c), suggesting 280 interhemispheric synchronisation of slow-wave activity during quiescence. Regionally, V1 intra-281 hemispheric correlations decreased, particularly with anterior areas, while showing a transient 282 increase with LM between weeks 2 and 5—coinciding with the previously identified window of 283 heightened excitability (Fig. 7d). On average, V1 correlations declined from day 1 to week 10 284 (Fig. 7e). 285

Discussion 326 This longitudinal exploratory study revealed month-long plasticity of cortical states following 327 adult vision loss. We identified two sequential and overlapping windows of plasticity defined by 328 behaviours, with spatially distinct effects on the cortex. The first one, from day 1 to week 3-5, was 329 characterised by a reduction in visual cortex activity during locomotion and a suppressive effect 330 of movements. This effect was observed in the visual and retrosplenial cortex. The second one, 331 from week 1 to week 7-10, was characterised by an increase in slow-wave activity during 332 quiescence and a rapid increase in activity at the transition from locomotion to quiescence. It was 333 localised in V1 and LM and suggests increased excitability. These behaviour-dependent changes 334 in activity induced by BE were accompanied by a rapid and long-lasting cortical network 335 reorganisation, which was more extensive during locomotion than during quiescence (Fig. 9). 336

Plasticity and cortical states 340 Our results suggest long-term plasticity in the circuit regulating cortical states. In normal adult 341 mice, locomotion desynchronizes the visual cortex spontaneous activity (Polack et al., 2013; 342 Reimer et al., 2014; Vinck et al., 2015) and increases the gain of visual responses while 343 maintaining their selectivity (Niell & Stryker, 2010). In the mesoscale calcium signal, this effect 344 is evident as a sustained increase in fluorescence (West et al., 2022), as seen during our baseline 345 recordings. This cortical state change is thought to be mediated by a disinhibitory circuit (Fu et 346 al., 2014). When mice run, cholinergic neurons in the basal forebrain are activated, which in turn 347 activate VIP inhibitory neurons (Alitto & Dan, 2013; A. M. Lee et al., 2014). VIP interneurons 348 are known to suppress mainly SST interneurons (Muñoz et al., 2017; Pfeffer et al., 2013), 349 leading to a disinhibition of excitatory neurons. This circuit has already been suggested to have a 350 role in adult cortical plasticity (Stryker, 2014). In adult mice, after early-onset, long-term 351 monocular deprivation, visual stimuli presented through the deprived eye during locomotion 352 recovered their cortical responses, whereas other stimuli did not (Kaneko & Stryker, 2014). 353 Activating VIP cells or silencing SST cells recapitulated the effect of locomotion on visual 354 cortex plasticity (Fu et al., 2015). Therefore, it suggests that locomotion, through the VIP-SST 355 disinhibitory circuit, enhances adult cortical plasticity. Our study adds to this by showing that 356 cortical states undergo long-term plasticity in adults. 357 

Early window of opposite effect of movements on cortical activity 358 The observed plasticity of cortical states is initiated from the very first day of recording, with 359 movements suppressing rather than increasing activity of the visual and retrosplenial cortex. This 360 rapid reversal in the effect of locomotion can be caused by a reorganisation of the cortical 361 circuitry. It could be mediated by homeostatic plasticity mechanisms, which are rapidly engaged 362 to maintain excitatory activity around set points (W. Wen & Turrigiano, 2024). An important 363 homeostatic mechanism is a reduction in inhibition that helps restore excitatory activity levels. 364 The activity of inhibitory neurons decreases 12h after monocular enucleation but does not 365 recover in the subsequent days, in contrast to the excitatory neurons. A structural disengagement 366 of inhibitory neurons in the network mediates this sustained reduction in inhibitory activity. At 367 day 2, the density of excitatory dendritic spines on inhibitory neurons decreases in V1, 368 suggesting that they are less excited by excitatory neurons (J. L. Chen et al., 2011; Keck et al., 369 2011). Additionally, there are fewer inhibitory synaptic boutons on excitatory neurons, directly 370 reducing the potential inhibition of their activity (Keck et al., 2011, 2013). Therefore, there is a 371 profound reorganisation of the neuronal network structure that favours the stabilisation of 372 excitatory activity after visual deprivation. This is likely to change the cortical circuit regulating 373 cortical states, as locomotion's effect on pyramidal neuron activity depends on a specific 374 disinhibitory circuit. The modulation is maintained from day 1 to week 5, as indicated by 375

Interestingly, at weeks 7 and 10, there was no 378 difference between continuous epochs of quiescence and locomotion activity, but an effect of 379 movements was still present at the transition. This dissociation between the acute and sustained 380 effects of the cortical state change might reflect distinct circuit components. 381 Cortical states are also controlled by the thalamus (Poulet & Crochet, 2019). Pharmacological 382 inactivation of the thalamus has been shown to alter cortical states related to movement in the 383 somatosensory (Poulet et al., 2012) and visual cortices (Nestvogel & McCormick, 2022). At whisking onset, the 386 initial rapid depolarisation remained, but the amplitude was reduced. However, the sustained 387 component that follows was completely abolished (Nestvogel & McCormick, 2022). This suggests that, in addition to plasticity in cortical circuitry, thalamocortical plasticity 390 could contribute to the reversal of movements' effects on cortical activity after BE. Structural and 391 functional plasticity has been observed in thalamocortical neurons after BE in adults (Bhandari et 392 al., 2022), but more evidence is needed to better understand the role of the thalamus in adult 393 cortical plasticity. 394 

Delayed window of increased cortical excitability 395 We identified a window from week 1 to week 7-10 during which the visual cortex shows 396 increased slow-wave activity only when mice were quiescent, suggesting a state of increased 397 excitability. 398 Increased excitability or responsiveness of the visual cortex has been observed following 399 different types of visual deprivation in adults. In protocols of ocular dominance plasticity, the 400 open-eye response increased only when the monocular deprivation lasted at least 7 days (Sato 401 and Stryker, 2008). This is similar to juveniles, but they rely on different plasticity mechanisms. 402 In mice lacking synaptic scaling, an important homeostatic plasticity mechanism, the open-eye 403 potentiation was absent in juveniles but conserved in adults. However, in mice lacking the 404 autophosphorylatable αCaMKII, this effect was absent in adults (Ranson et al., 2012). The 405 autophosphorylation of αCaMKII is an essential process for the proper expression of adult long-406 term potentiation (Kirkwood et al., 1997), suggesting that this synaptic plasticity mechanism 407 might be involved in the delayed increase in cortical responsiveness. A similar effect has been 408 observed following monocular retinal lesions. In cats, spontaneous activity in the lesion 409 projection zone of the visual cortex increases relative to the surround area at 2 and 4 weeks 410 following a lesion, then recovers after 12 weeks (Giannikopoulos & Eysel, 2006). This is 411 accompanied by an increase in αCaMKII autophosphorylation (Van den bergh et al., 2003), 412

Altogether, it suggests that the delayed increase in cortical 415 excitability is a fundamental mechanism of adult cortical plasticity across species, which seems 416 mediated by Hebbian plasticity mechanisms. This hyperexcitable state has been hypothesised to 417 facilitate plasticity (Eysel & Jancke, 2024), and our study shows that it is behaviourally gated 418 and persists for several weeks. 419 Our data showed that the increase in spontaneous activity was specific to the slow band. Cortical 420 slow oscillations are known to mostly occur during sleep and anaesthesia, but can emerge locally 421 during wakefulness after prolonged waking (Vyazovskiy et al., 2011), around cortical lesions 422 (Sarasso et al., 2020), and when the thalamus is silenced (Nestvogel & McCormick, 2022; Poulet 423 et al., 2012). These oscillations are characterised by a sequence of high firing (ON-period) 424 followed by a period of hyperpolarisation (OFF-period) (Massimini et al., 2024; Steriade et al., 425 1993). During sleep, cortical slow waves have been shown to promote long-term plasticity 426 (Chauvette et al., 2012; Timofeev & Chauvette, 2017). Therefore, it would be possible that, 427 following BE in adults, the visual cortex “falls asleep” during quiescence to help reorganise 428 cortical circuitry. However, it remains to be tested whether the slow oscillations detected in the 429 calcium signal share the same electrophysiological signatures as those found during slow-wave 430 sleep. 431 Prior to the window of increased excitability during quiescence, we found a transient increase in 432 activity at day 1 after BE. This peak of activity was not found using two-photon calcium imaging 433 of excitatory neurons following complete bilateral retinal lesion (Keck et al., 2013) or monocular 434 enucleation (Barnes et al., 2015). However, the correlation between excitatory neurons was 435 transiently increased at day 1, whereas activity was not, following BE (Mesik et al., 2026). With 436 mesoscale calcium imaging, we mostly measure the correlated activity of large groups of 437 neurons, as the signal likely arises from the sum of fluorescence from cell bodies and the 438 neuropil (Barson et al., 2020; Cardin et al., 2020). Therefore, the change in correlation between 439 excitatory neurons at day 1 can explain the transient increase we observed. It might result from 440 homeostatic plasticity mechanisms, specifically synaptic scaling in excitatory neurons that 441 occurs after visual deprivation to restore excitatory activity levels (Hengen et al., 2013; Keck et 442 al., 2013; G. Turrigiano, 2011; G. G. Turrigiano et al., 1998). 443

Cortical state plasticity as a transition to cross-modal plasticity? 459 Interestingly, the gradual recovery of activity during locomotion observed from week 1 onward 460 (Fig. 3d) parallels the time course reported by Van Brussel et al., 2011, who found a progressive 461 restoration of V1 activity—assessed via the immediate early gene zif268 —between 1 and 7 462 weeks following monocular enucleation. This method likely reflects an average of the activity in 463 the last hour before brain sampling (Kaczmarek & Chaudhuri, 1997), a timescale necessary for 464 gene transduction and mRNA accumulation in the cytoplasm. Although the mouse behaviour 465 was not specified prior to sacrifice, our data would suggest that the mice were active. When the 466 mice were also deprived of whiskers, activity recovery in V1 did not occur, suggesting that 467 cross-modal plasticity underlies this recovery. We do not have evidence of cross-modal 468 recruitment of the visual cortex, as this would require sensory stimulation (Hashimoto et al., 469 2023) or additional sensory deprivation (Van Brussel et al., 2011). However, we can hypothesise 470 that it occurred several weeks after BE. It has been proposed that increased excitability following 471 sensory deprivation could cause pre-existing subthreshold cross-modal inputs to reach the 472 spiking threshold in the deprived cortex (Kral & Sharma, 2023). Activity during locomotion 473 started to recover at the same time the activity increased during quiescence, at day 7. This state 474 of increased excitability might strengthen cross-modal inputs, restoring activity levels during 475 locomotion and partially restoring movement-evoked activity from week 5 onward, a period 476 where activity during quiescence declined toward baseline levels. 477 478

Processing behaviour 648 To quantify the behaviour, we used Facemap to extract the motion energy (Syeda et al., 2024). 649 We drew an ROI that included the forelimbs, hindlimbs, and the rest of the body below the face 650 (Fig. 1d). The motion energy was calculated as the sum of the absolute differences between 651 consecutive frames within the ROI. Because the camera view was kept consistent across the 652 protocols, the ROI was placed once and used every subsequent session. 653 An initial rough estimate of locomotion periods was established by using the moving standard 654 deviation (2s window) and smoothed motion energy (moving average with a 0.33s window) that 655 exceeded a threshold. To precisely identify the start and stop transition times, we applied a 656 change point detection algorithm, which detected changes in root-mean-square level 657 (findchangepts in MATLAB) around each estimated transition. Sustained locomotion was 658 defined as periods between locomotion onset and offset of at least 10s and separated from 659 transitions by 3s. 28 To identify quiescence periods, a process similar to that used for locomotion has been employed. 663 Sustained quiescence was defined as periods between the onset and offset of at least 10s and 664 separated from transitions by 3s. 665 During quiescence, we observed that the mice made small, brief movements that elicited a 666 cortical response. To identify these micro-movements, we thresholded the smoothed motion 667 energy (moving average with a 0.33s window) during quiescence. A change point detection 668 algorithm was used to find the onset time. Only micro-movements with at least 5s without any 669 movements before were kept. 670 

Data inclusion criteria 671 Considering the mice’s spontaneous behaviour in longitudinal protocols was challenging, as they 672 did not necessarily express all measured behaviour at each time point (Fig. S1, S6). For example, 673 a mouse could have run during the whole 20 min session, resulting in no data at that time point 674 for quiescence and state transitions. Additionally, for a given mouse in the long-term protocol, 675 we might have lost the signal of regions near the edge of the dorsal cortex because of implant 676 degradation (Fig. S9). Therefore, for each region, behaviour and mouse, we included the data 677 only if there was at least one baseline session and at least one session after BE. Moreover, each 678 time point required data from at least three mice to be considered. 679 

Analysis of activity in sustained states 680 To quantify the activity levels for the correlation analysis (Fig. 2), we calculated the maximum 681 power of the signal. For each region, the raw calcium fluctuations were normalised by 682 calculating the ΔF/F 0, which is the raw fluorescence minus the mean fluorescence, divided by the 683 mean fluorescence. The continuous wavelet transform (CWT) of the ΔF/F 0 was calculated using 684 a symmetrical Morse wavelet with a time bandwidth of 1s. A Morse wavelet with these 685 parameters is similar to a Morlet wavelet, but it was somewhat faster to compute. We extracted 686 the power as the squared absolute values of the CWT. The output is a frequency-by-time matrix 687 of power values. As a measure of ongoing activity, we calculated the maximum power values in 688 the 0.1-4Hz band at every time point. 689 For the analysis of activity segmented by behavioural states (Fig. 3, S2), we calculated the power 690 spectral density (pwelch in MATLAB) of ΔF/F 0 for each behavioural-state block, using a 10s 691 window (the minimum duration of a behavioural state) with 50% overlap. The result was a 692 power spectrum per block. For each behavioural state, the power spectra were averaged. Activity 693 levels were quantified as the maximum power values in the 0.2-4Hz band. Figures and statistics 694 were generated using log10 power values. 695

Analysis of functional connectivity 706 The raw calcium signal was converted to ΔF/F 0 and bandpass-filtered to 0.1-4Hz. The Pearson 707 correlation coefficient was calculated between all pairs of regions during the whole session or 708 during quiescence and locomotion periods. From the correlation matrices, we calculated V1 intra-hemispheric 710 connectivity as the average of connectivity within each hemisphere and homotopic connectivity 711 for each region. Post-analysis processing 714 In cohort 1, two 10 min sessions were done per week. All analyses were done on each 10 min 715 session. Then the data were weighted averaged to end up with one data per time point. The 716 weights were the number of behavioural epochs. 717 The first baseline from cohort 1 (w-3) was removed as it was not present in cohort 2. 718 All analyses were done on every available cortical region. To simplify the results presentation, 719 we averaged the data between hemispheres. 720 

Statistical analysis 721 For statistical tests, baseline sessions were averaged to create a single baseline time point. To 722 account for missing data points from each mouse across the longitudinal protocols, we performed 723 a linear mixed-effects model (lme from the nlme package in R) with time as a fixed effect and 724 mice as a random effect. For the frequency power spectrum over time (Fig. 4b, S2c), frequencies 725 were added as a second factor to the model. For the activity following state transition (Fig. 5b,d; 726 6b), the time from transition was added as a second factor to the model. For comparison between 727 behavioural states, the state was added as a second factor to the model. To test whether there was 728 an effect of time of BE, a two-sided ANOVA was performed on the model. For region data or 729 connectivity matrices, the test was conducted independently for each region or pair of regions. 730 The p-values for the time effect were adjusted using the False Discovery Rate (FDR) method to 731 account for multiple comparisons across regions.
