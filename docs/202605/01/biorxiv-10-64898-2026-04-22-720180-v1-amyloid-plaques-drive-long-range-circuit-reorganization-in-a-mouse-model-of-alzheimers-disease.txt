ABSTRACT 

Amyloid plaques are a pathological hallmark of Alzheimer’s disease, but their specific contribution to 

widespread neuronal dysfunction remains unclear. Here, using a mouse model of Alzheimer’s, we 

combine dynamic calcium imaging and electrophysiological recordings with co-registered plaque 

mapping to show how plaques affect hippocampal activity. We found that plaques provide nonlocal, long-

range effects that depend on plaque characteristics and behavioral state. Place cells clustered near 

plaques, suggesting aberrant recruitment of plaque-adjacent neurons into spatial representations. In 

contrast, place cells before plaques formed had no spatial relationship to the location of future plaques. 

These results reveal a mechanism where local amyloid pathology reorganizes brain circuits, driving 

widespread dysfunction commensurate with cognitive impairment in Alzheimer’s.

INTRODUCTION 

Early in the course of Alzheimer’s disease (AD), amyloid plaques composed of fibrillar species of 

amyloid-b42 (A b) deposit in the extracellular space, initiating a cascade that underlies hippocampal 

failure and memory loss. While much is known about the molecular mechanisms of AD pathology, the 

causes and circuit features of hippocampal disruption in AD are less well understood. 

During exploratory behavior, many hippocampal neurons form spatial representations known as place 

fields, and these representations deteriorate in A b models of AD  (1, 2). Aberrantly hyperactive and 

silent subpopulations of neurons have also been observed in hippocampus and cortex of such AD 

models, implicating excitatory-inhibitory imbalance in A b-associated circuit failure  (3–5). Fibrillar and 

oligomeric species of A b appear to contribute, as both have been shown to be neurotoxic and 

synaptotoxic  (6–15 ).

In anesthetized AD model mice, aberrantly hyperactive cells have been found to cluster exclusively 

near amyloid plaques, while silent cells have been found to be widely distributed  (3, 4). However, as 

behavioral state critically influences hippocampal activity  (16 –18 ), and as anesthetics have complex 

effects on hemodynamics, neurophysiology, and calcium handling  (19 –21 ), it remains unknown 

whether this plaque-associated hyperactivity occurs during normal behaviors and across the sleep-wake 

cycle. Furthermore, the impact of such clustering of hyperactive cells near plaques on place coding has 

been unclear. Such hyperactivity could degrade place coding near plaques, but the opposite would be 

true if spatial tuning is preserved. 

To investigate the spatial relationship of Aβ plaques and hippocampal neuronal activity across the 

sleep-wake cycle, we acquired dynamic calcium imaging and concomitant electrophysiological 

recordings in freely-behaving APP/PS1 mice, and then mapped neuronal activity and spatial 

representations to the local plaque topography.  We  found  that  plaques  provide  nonlocal,  long-range 

effects  that  depend  on  plaque characteristics and  behavioral  state , thereby providing  a critical  window 

into  the  plaque-driven  hippocampal  circuit  reorganization  underlying cognitive impairment  in 

Alzheimer's  disease.

Spatial effects of amyloid plaques on neuronal activity depend on behavioral state

We  acquired freely behaving hippocampal CA1 dynamic calcium imaging with  GCaMP6f  using the 1-

photon (1P) Inscopix miniscope TM  along with simultaneous, ipsilateral cortical and hippocampal local 

field potential (LFP) recordings  of aged APP/PS1 mice ( 11 ± 0.3 months, n=7 mice ), as animals 

explored a linear track (Run), subsequently rested in quiet wakefulness (QW), and transitioned into 

slow wave sleep (SWS) and rapid eye movement (REM) sleep ( Fig. 1A, B ). Dynamic calcium activity 

varied widely across cells and behavioral states, and we stratified cells by their activity within each 

state into the most active 5% (‘hyperactive’), the least active (‘hypoactive’, < 1 event per 4 minutes 

(16 , 18 )), and the remaining population of normally active cells. Examples of each cell type are shown 

in  Fig. 1B , linked to the LFP recorded in each behavioral state. To map CA1 neuronal activity to plaque 

locations, mice then underwent  1P  and  2P  vascular  imaging  with  Dextran  Green,  followed  by  2P 

plaque  imaging  with  methoxy-X04  (Fig. 1A ). For spatial analyses, 1P neuronal and 2P plaque images 

were co-registered using the angiogram fiducials ( Fig. 1C; Suppl. Fig. 1 ). In contrast to observations 

made in anesthetized AD mice that hyperactive cells are restricted to the immediate vicinity of amyloid 

plaques  (3, 4), hyperactive cells were detected at all distances from the nearest plaque in all behavioral 

states ( Fig. 1D ). This result persisted when hyperactivity was defined using an even more restrictive 

threshold: the top 1% of event rates for each state ( Suppl. Fig. 2 ). On inspection, the proportion of 

hyperactive cells appeared to gradually diminish with distance from the nearest plaque in Run and 

SWS; while the proportion of hypoactive cells also appeared to gradually diminish with distance from 

the nearest plaque in Run. However, in a mixed effects model, using data from all animals across the 

distance bins and accounting for repeated measures by animal via a random intercept, hyperactive cells 

in Run alone showed a trend towards a negative relationship with distance from the nearest plaque ( b =

-0.008, p = 0.08; uncorrected for multiple comparisons across behavioral states and cell types). 

Spatial profiles of CA1 dynamic calcium activity in amyloid plaque-laden AD mice varied across 

behavioral states ( Fig. 2A ). To evaluate how plaques impact local neuronal activity, we ran a backward 

eliminated mixed effects model for the dependent variable calcium event rate, using behavioral state 

(Run, QW, SWS, and REM) as a fixed predictor along with type of activity (hypoactive, normal, and 

hyperactive), the distance of each cell to the nearest plaque, and the distance of each cell to the nearest 

edge of the 2P field of view (edge distance) to account for the potential effects of plaques outside the 

field of view); as well as all 2-way interactions (see  Methods ). The model was significant, revealing 

several significant 2-way interactions, including of greatest interest, an interaction of distance to the 

nearest plaque and type of activity (p<0.0001) ( Fig. 2B, Suppl. Fig. 3A ). For hyperactive cells, proximity 

to plaques was associated with higher activity in all states, detectable over hundreds of microns. In

Spatial effects of amyloid plaques depend on their size and axial position in CA1 

Amyloid plaques varied by size and axial position within CA1, with some centered in the pyramidal 

cell layer while others were located in the dendritic layers of s. radiatum and s. oriens ( Fig. 3A ). As 

amyloid plaques have been shown to disrupt both the neuronal processes that traverse them  (22 ) and 

local inhibitory synapses  (23 ), we set out to determine if plaque size or axial position within CA1 

affected neuronal activity. To do so, we dichotomized plaques by size as well as by their axial position 

(pyramidal cell layer vs. dendritic layers;  Fig. 3B ), and we extended the above mixed effects model to 

include size and axial position of the nearest plaque (see  Methods ). In addition to the same significant 

2-way interactions described above, the model detected a significant 3-way interaction of plaque axial 

position, plaque size, and distance to the nearest plaque (p=0.0039). Specifically, large amyloid plaques 

(define size here) of the pyramidal cell layer were associated with the highest activity of plaque-

adjacent neurons and the steepest decline in activity with distance ( Fig. 3C ). Small plaques of the 

pyramidal cell layer were intermediate, while dendritic plaques were associated with the smallest effect 

of plaque adjacent neurons on activity ( Fig. 3C ). There was also a small but significant interaction of 

plaque axial position and edge distance (p<0.0001), as well as a modest interaction of activity type and 

edge distance (p<0.0046) similar to that observed in the previous model ( Suppl. Fig. 4 ). The model 

accounted for 51.8% of the variance.

Amyloid plaque-driven changes in local spatial encoding 

As spatial memory impairment is a hallmark of AD  (24 ) and hippocampal place cell recruitment during 

navigation plays an important role in spatial memory formation  (25 ), we next evaluated the local 

impact of amyloid plaques on hippocampal spatial representations. APP/PS1 mice had a decreased 

proportion of  place cells compared to nontransgenic wildtype mice (APP/PS1 vs WT: 11.7 ± 2.3% vs 

24.5 ± 2.7%, Mann-Whitney U test,  p = 0.03;  Fig. 4A, B, C ). To evaluate whether amyloid plaques 

influenced the spatial distribution of place cells, we first compared the distance between place cells in 

APP/PS1 and WT  mice. Compared to WT mice, the distribution of place cell distances in APP/PS1 

mice over-represented short distances, consistent with clusters evident on inspection  (Kolmogorov-

Smirnov test, p = 4.09 x 10-15;  Fig.  4D,  E). 

To determine whether amyloid plaques contributed to this clustering, we ran a backward elimination 

mixed effects model to determine whether proximity to amyloid plaques influenced place cell identity. 

The fixed effect predictors were distance to the nearest plaque, the nearest plaque axial position (cell 

layer vs. dendritic layers), nearest plaque size (big vs. small), distance to the edge of the field of view 

(edge distance), and all two-way interactions. The dependent variable was the binary variable of place 

cell identity (Yes/No). This analysis revealed a significant interaction between distance to the nearest 

plaque and edge distance (p<0.0001), reflecting an increasing probability of place cell identity with 

decreasing distance to the nearest plaque, with the rate of this increase growing more pronounced towards 

the center of the field of view ( Fig. 4F ). There was also a main effect for plaque axial position, with the 

probability of place cell identity higher near plaques of the dendritic layers than the pyramidal cell layer 

(p=0.005). 

In contrast to place cell identity, we found no significant effect of proximity to amyloid plaques on place 

cell spatial information when we ran an analogous model ( Methods ; Suppl. Fig. 5 ). Together, these 

results show that place cells cluster near amyloid plaques .

DISCUSSION 

While AD is associated with progressive hippocampal failure, how amyloid plaques influence spatial 

coding in their local microenvironment has been unclear. Here, we show that amyloid plaques exert long-

range effects both on aberrant neuronal activity across behavioral states as well as on place cell coding. 

With the progressive accumulation of amyloid plaques in AD, these disruptive effects on hippocampal 

function are likely to contribute to increasing hippocampal failure. 

Place coding is a key function of the hippocampus, and its deterioration in AD patients and in AD models 

is well established  (24 ). The biased recruitment of plaque-adjacent cells into the representation of space 

is well-positioned to underlie this degraded place coding. With accumulation of amyloid plaques in AD, 

excessive reliance on a reduced pool of available neurons to represent space within and across 

environments would be expected to progressively impair both hippocampal remapping and pattern 

separation, fundamental features of hippocampal mnemonic function  (28 ).

Our results also suggest that amyloid plaques drive the clustering of place cells rather than vice versa, 

as our longitudinal studies did not detect a predictive effect of place cell locations on future plaque 

positions. Although the mechanisms underlying the clustering of place cells near amyloid plaques are 

unknown, loss of inhibitory synapses in the region around amyloid plaques, as recently reported  (23 ), is 

likely to contribute, as may the disruption of morphology of local processes ( 22 ).

Unexpectedly, we detected long range gradients of neuronal activity centered on amyloid plaques rather 

than highly local plaque effects. Although the biological basis of these effects is not yet clear, we

even before plaques predominate  (6–15 ).

Prior reports of aberrantly high and low activity of hippocampal and cortical neurons in amyloid plaque-

bearing AD models have supported the concept of perturbed excitatory-inhibitory balance in AD  (3–5).

We and others have found that behavioral state has a marked effect on such imbalance  (17 ). The present 

results extend these findings, showing that the spatial effects of plaques on neuronal activity are also 

markedly state-dependent. In Run behavior, aberrant hyper- and hypoactivity was greatest close to 

plaques. However, plaque-associated hyperactivity declined faster with distance from plaques in non-

Run states than in Run, and hypoactivity was unaffected by plaque proximity in non-Run states. 

Consistent with the toxicity of A b and the local impact of amyloid plaques on the brain parenchyma, 

large plaques were more disruptive than small plaques on neuronal activity. Interestingly, large plaques 

deposited in the pyramidal cell layer were most disruptive, while plaques in the dendritic layers were 

least disruptive. In contrast, we observed that place cells clustered more around dendritic layer plaques 

than cell layer plaques. These observations may reflect differential effects on inhibitory versus 

excitatory synapses, given their axial distribution within CA1  (29 ) as well as the possible contribution 

of local inhibitory synapse losses, which are particularly enriched around pyramidal cell somas  (23 ,

30 ).

Intriguingly, we found that the locations of hyperactive cells in SWS predicted future plaque locations, 

albeit weakly. As SWS is the one behavioral state associated with amyloid clearance ( 31 , 32 ), it is 

possible that release of A b in SWS by such hyperactive cells overwhelms local clearance mechanisms, 

enabling local A b concentrations to rise above the local critical threshold for oligomerization and 

deposition. Future work is needed to evaluate this idea. 

Key strengths of this study include linking spatial representations and neuronal activity across 

behavioral states to the local influence of amyloid plaques in the hippocampus, a region involved in 

early AD pathology and memory loss. A further strength was the use of 2p microscopy to images 

amyloid plaques, providing an accurate assessment of plaque burden across the axial extent of CA1. 

Limitations of this study include the use of dynamic calcium imaging to identify place cells, rather than 

single unit electrophysiology. Even so, dynamic calcium imaging has proven to be a robust measure of

Together, the present findings show that amyloid plaques are associated with state-dependent, long-range 

spatial gradients of neuronal activity, and with local clustering of place cells. These effects are anticipated 

to contribute to the progressive impairment of hippocampal-dependent memory in AD.

Surgical preparation 

The APP/PS1 mouse strain used for this research project, B6C3-Tg(APPswe,PSEN1dE9)85Dbo/Mmjax, 

RRID:MMRRC_034829-JAX, was purchased from the Mutant Mouse Resource and Research Center 

(MMRRC) at The Jackson Laboratory, an NIH-funded strain repository, and was donated to the MMRRC 

by David Borchelt, Ph.D., McKnight Brain Institute, University of Florida  (33 ). Surgical preparation has 

been previously prescribed  (16 , 18 ). Briefly, APP/PS1 mice and their age/sex matched non-transgenic 

control littermates underwent injection of 1 μL AAV-hSyn-GCaMP6f (Addgene, titer > 7x10 12  vg/ml) 

into the hippocampal CA1 region (AP -2.1 mm, ML -1.65 mm, DV 1.4 mm from bregma) under 

anesthesia (isoflurane 1.5-2%). A gradient index (GRIN) lens (1 mm diameter, AP -2.1 mm, ML -1.50 

mm, DV -1.2 mm from bregma) and a custom drivable probe holding 7 stereotrodes (AP -2.8 mm, ML 

2.6 mm, -36 o relative to the AP axis, -39 o relative to the inter-aural axis; DV 1.2 mm) were implanted 

ipsilaterally 2 weeks later with cerebellar ground. The microscope baseplate was attached three weeks 

later. Animals were housed in individual cages with a 12 hr light/12hr dark standard light cycle. 

1P Dynamic calcium imaging and electrophysiology recordings

The Inscopix TM  nVista3 mini-microscope and acquisition system (IDAS) were used for dynamic 

calcium imaging, sampling at 20 fps. Local field potential (LFP) (Neuralynx TM ) was sampled at 32 kHz 

followed by 0.5-900 Hz filtering and down sampling to 1000 Hz. Animal position was measured with 

overhead camera tracking of two LED diodes mounted on the headstage. Electrophysiological 

recordings and position sampling were acquired continuously. Simultaneous dynamic calcium imaging 

was acquired in 10-min ON and 5-min OFF blocks to avoid photobleaching and was synchronized with 

electrophysiological data with TTL pulses ( 16 , 18 ).

Behavior and sleep staging 

Data were acquired as animals explored a linear track and were then moved to their home cage within 

the same recording room to sample subsequent quiet wakefulness (QW), slow-wave sleep (SWS), and 

REM sleep. Cortical delta (1-4 Hz), hippocampal theta (4-10 Hz) and ripples (100-300 Hz), and rat 

position and posture were used to identify behavioral states:  Run was defined as animals explored the 

linear track with speeds exceeding 1 cm/sec and was characterized by a strong theta oscillation; QW 

was identified when animals were stationary (< 0.5 cm/sec) for at least 5 seconds with their eyes open; 

SWS was identified by the presence of sharp wave ripples (SWRs) and high delta/theta LFP power 

ratio (>2) in animals in a sleep posture; REM sleep was identified as epochs of high theta/delta LFP 

power ratio (>2) in animals in a sleep posture  (18 ). 

2P imaging of amyloid plaques and acquisition of angiograms 

Following 1P imaging of dynamic calcium activity, Methoxy-X04 (10 mg/kg, i.p.) was injected to label 

amyloid plaques  (34 ). For 1P vascular imaging at least one day later, animals were then lightly 

anesthetized with isoflurane (~1.5%; with concentration sufficient to inhibit dynamic calcium activity) 

and fluorescein dextran (0.3 ml, i.v.) was injected into the tail vein and imaging was acquired. Animals 

were then transferred to a 2P microscope and head-fixed for 2P vascular and amyloid plaque imaging 

on a commercial multiphoton system as previously described  (35 ) (Olympus Fluoview 1000MPE) 

mounted on an Olympus BX61WI upright microscope. A mode-locked titanium/sapphire laser (MaiTai; 

Spectra-Physics, Fremont, CA) was used to generate two-photon fluorescence with 800 nm excitation. 

Detectors consisting of photomutliplier tubes (Hamamatsu, Ichinocho, Japan) collected light in the 

following ranges: 380-480 and 500-540 nm. 2P imaging with 800 nm excitation of hippocampal CA1 

was acquired through the GRIN lens, acquiring a stack of images spanning the CA1 cell layer, across 

150  mm.

Data analysis.  All analyses were performed using MATLAB, Python, or SAS unless specified 

otherwise  (16 –18 ). 

Calcium imaging:  Inscopix Data Processing Software (IDPS, Inscopix, Palo Alto, California) was used 

for movie preprocessing, spatial filtering, motion correction, normalization ΔF/F, and constrained non-

negative matrix factorization (CNMFE) cell identification  (36 ). Each identified cell was manually 

examined and accepted with oval cell shape and high signal-noise-ratio (SNR). Calcium events were 

then detected at 2 Z scores above the mean, with onset defined at 30% of peak amplitude  (16 –18 ). Low 

rate cells were defined as < 0.25 transients/min  (16 –18 ); high rate cells were defined using a calcium 

event rate threshold set at the top 5% of calcium rates for each behavioral state of each animal. 

Place cell:  Spatial tuning curves (3 cm bins) were constructed for each cell for each running direction 

(LR and RL) using all detected calcium events acquired during running (velocity > 1 cm/s). Spatial 

information was measured for each cell as the amount of information (in bits) conveyed per calcium 

event, and was compared to 1000 shuffled spatial information distributions to test significance (Monte 

Carlo p-value < 0.05)  (37 ), and taken as the larger of the LR and the RL trajectories. 

Single timepoint 2P-1P image co-registration:  Custom Python code was used to co-register 1P and 2P 

angiograms, implementing a coherent point drift algorithm and Python package  pycpd 2.0.0  (38 , 39 ). In 

brief, landmark points marked on 1P and 2P angiograms were used to compute the parameters for a 

similarity transformation (that included translation, rotation, and scaling) that maximized their overlap. 

These transformation parameters were then applied to the 1P angiograms and dynamic calcium imaging 

data to register them in the 2P space. Co-registration accuracy was quantified by calculating the 

nearest-neighbor distance between corresponding features of the transformed 1P and 2P angiogram 

datasets. Across the datasets, the root mean square error (RMSE) was 1.9 ± 0.08 μm. 

Young 1P- old 2P image co-registration : The spatial alignment of 1P young calcium imaging data and 2P 

amyloid plaque image stacks was achieved through a two-stage registration procedure.  Stage 1: 

Longitudinal 1P image co-registration . First, a similarity transformation was computed to align the initial 

angiogram (1P young) with the follow-up angiogram (1P old).  Landmark Selection: Corresponding 

anatomical landmarks were identified on both the 1P young and 1P old angiograms. These landmarks 

were used to compute the parameters for a first similarity transformation, which included translation, 

rotation, and uniform scaling. The transformation parameters were optimized to maximize the spatial 

overlap of the landmark points. The calculated transformation was then applied to the 1P young 

angiogram and its associated dynamic calcium imaging data, registering them into the coordinate space 

of the 1P old angiogram.  Stage 2: 2P-1P image co-registration . To integrate the 1P and 2P datasets, a 

second registration was  performed. A new set of landmarks was selected from major vessels that
