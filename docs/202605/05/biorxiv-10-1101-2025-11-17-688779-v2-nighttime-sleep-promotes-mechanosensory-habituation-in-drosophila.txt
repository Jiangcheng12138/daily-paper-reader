ABSTRACT 8

Sleep is a highly conserved behaviour that facilitates episodic, procedural, and/or 9associative modes of memory across species 1-3 , a process hypothesised to involve 10 structural downscaling of synapses potentiated during waking episodes 4-6 . Habituation, an 11 ancient form of non-associative memory in which repetition of a stimulus leads to diminishing 12 behavioural responses, is considered a prerequisite for such complex forms of learning 7,8 .13 Yet whether sleep also influences the capacity of organisms to habituate is poorly 14 understood. Here we examine this question using the fruit fly, Drosophila . We describe an 15 automated system that yields long-term analogue measurements of habituation to 16 mechanosensory stimuli in adult flies. Using this platform, we find that Drosophila lacking 17 neuronal calcium sensor Neurocalcin – which exhibit reduced sleep specifically during the 18 night 9 – display impaired mechanosensory habituation during the day. Mimicking a method 19 used to treat insomnia 10 , we show that compressing nighttime duration both restores 20 consolidated night sleep and renormalises habituation in Neurocalcin mutants. Conversely, 21 in wild type flies without sleep deficits, decreasing night length reduces total night sleep and 22 disrupts daytime habituation. In wild type flies, exposure to longer nights – which increases 23 overall night sleep – enhances synaptic downscaling in sensory circuits. Strikingly, this effect 24 is abolished in Neurocalcin mutants, which do not exhibit increased sleep under these 25 conditions. Collectively, our findings reveal a role for nighttime sleep in facilitating habituation 26 and suggest that this may occur by promoting synaptic downscaling. 27

RESULTS AND DISCUSSION 29 

Induction of mechanosensory habituation in adult Drosophila 30 

Numerous assays have been developed to induce habituation of adult Drosophila to visual, 31 olfactory, and gustatory, stimuli 11-14 . However, while recently examined in Drosophila larvae 32 15 , habituation to mechanical stimuli has been comparatively understudied in adult fruit flies. 33 More broadly, whether sleep quality modulates the ability of adult flies (or other organisms) 34 to habituate to environmental stimuli remains an open question. 35 

We therefore sought to investigate potential links between sleep and habituation to 36 mechanosensory stimuli in adult Drosophila . The synaptic homeostasis hypothesis stipulates 37 that sleep facilitates downscaling of synapses potentiated during waking experience, thus 38 enabling memory by preventing neuronal hyper-metabolism 16 and enhancing signal-to-noise 39 ratios across circuits 5. Ongoing locomotor activity occurring during wakefulness could 40 induce an opposite change, potentiating synaptic connections in circuits mediating 41 movement 17,18 and mechanosensory feedback 19-21 . We therefore hypothesised that 42 enhancing this potentiation by limiting sleep (thus prolonging locomotion) might reduce 43 sleep-induced synaptic downscaling and impair the ability of habituation-promoting inhibitory 44 mechanisms 22,23 to suppress motor circuit activity in response to repeated mechanical 45 stimuli. 46 

To test this hypothesis, we developed a simple, high-throughput habituation assay 47 using the DART ( Drosophila ARousal Tracking) system (Fig. 1A) 24 , which combines 48 continuous video-tracking of individual flies with mechanical stimuli delivered via platform-49 associated motors. DART can be programmed to deliver consistent mechanosensory stimuli 50 that elicit robust, transient, locomotor responses in control flies (Figure S1A), the magnitude 51 and dynamics of which can be quantified. We repurposed the DART system to test for 52 mechanosensory habituation in adult flies by delivering multiple identical stimuli at intervals 53 of 10 min (Fig. 1B). Flies responded to repeated mechanical stimuli with locomotor 54 responses that gradually attenuated in magnitude (Fig. 1C), quantified as the mean peak 55 speed in the 1 min following each stimulus (Fig. 1D). 56

If this decrement represents true habituation rather than generalised sensory 57 adaptation, fatigue, or structural damage, it should exhibit key properties that define 58 habituation: spontaneous recovery, potentiation of habituation over repeated bouts, and 59 stimulus-specificity 7,8 . We sought to validate the assay by testing for these characteristics. 60 

After attenuation of locomotion following 20 mechanical stimuli, locomotor speeds 61 exhibited spontaneous recovery over time in response to single mechanical stimuli, with ~ 62 80% recovery occurring following 24 h (Figure S1B-F). We found that the rate of 63 mechanosensory habituation could be significantly enhanced by applying a habituation-64 inducing paradigm of 20 identical mechanical stimuli on three consecutive days (Fig. 1E, F). 65 Finally, after 20 consecutive mechanical stimuli, we delivered a distinct ‘lights-off’ visual 66 stimulus expected to similarly induce a startle response (Fig. 1G). Despite the robust 67 decrease in locomotor response to repeated mechanical stimuli (Fig. 1G, H), locomotor 68 responses to the visual stimulus did not differ in magnitude between naïve and mechanically 69 habituated flies (Fig. 1G, I). Hence, progressive reductions in locomotor responses in our 70 paradigm are consistent with well-defined characteristics of habituation 7,8 .71 

A number of platforms analogous to DART can track locomotor activity of populations 72 of flies using video- or infrared beam-based analysis, and can deliver mechanosensory 73 stimuli or be modified to do so. These include the Drosophila Activity Monitor 25 , Ethoscope, 74 26 and Zantiks 27 systems. We designed a similar habituation protocol using the Zantiks 75 automated-tracking system (Figure S1F; see Methods). The Zantiks system could detect and 76 quantify robust locomotor responses of adult flies to single motor-driven stimuli (Figure 77 S1G), as well as a progressive decline in locomotor response to repeated, identical 78 mechanosensory stimuli that was qualitatively similar to that observed using the DART 79 system (Figure S1H, I and Fig. 1D). These data provide cross-platform validation of our 80 findings using the DART. 81

Similarly to humans, deep sleep in Drosophila primarily occurs during the night 28 . We 84 therefore sought to test whether night sleep influences mechanosensory habituation induced 85 during the day. Mechanically disrupting sleep provides a potential means to do so. However, 86 homeostatic rebound sleep induced by sleep deprivation 29 could potentially cause 87 confounding changes in stimulus-induced movement during the day. Hence, we utilised a 88 genetic manipulation that selectively disrupts night sleep whilst leaving daytime sleep intact: 89 loss of the highly conserved neuronal calcium sensor Neurocalcin 9.90 

In contrast to mutations such as shaker , sleepless , fumin , and insomniac – which 91 disrupt both day and night sleep in Drosophila 30-34 – loss of Neurocalcin specifically impairs 92 sleep during the night in oscillating light: dark conditions 9, providing a means to examine the 93 relationship between night sleep and daytime habituation. Indeed, using our paradigm to 94 measure the rate and amplitude of mechanosensory habituation in control flies and flies 95 homozygous for a Neurocalcin null allele ( Nca KO1 ) 9, we found that loss of Neurocalcin 96 significantly reduced the degree of mechanosensory habituation, an effect that was 97 particularly prominent after ~ 10 mechanical stimuli (Fig. 2A, B). We observed similarly 98 reduced mechanosensory habituation in flies homozygous for an independent Neurocalcin 99 null allele ( Nca KO2 ) (Figure S2A-B), confirming the above genotype-phenotype correlation. 100 

Neurocalcin could potentially modulate mechanosensory habituation by enhancing 101 sensitivity to mechanical stimuli. We therefore tested locomotor responses in control and 102 

Neurocalcin knockout flies to mechanical stimulus strengths ranging from 1% to 100% of the 103 strength used in the habituation protocol. Neurocalcin knockout flies showed statistically 104 similar amplitudes of locomotor responses to isogenic controls at 6 out of 7 stimulus 105 strengths tested, and a slightly enhanced response to stimuli 10% of the strength used in the 106 habituation protocol (Figure S2C). These data therefore did not support a general increase in 107 mechanosensory sensitivity in Neurocalcin knockout flies, and confirmed that the habituation 108 protocol utilised a stimulus strength that was beyond the plateau at which there was no 109 difference between control and Neurocalcin knockout flies. 110

Since our habituation protocol yielded long-term quantitative recordings of individual 111 flies, we were able to examine whether the reduction in habituation in Neurocalcin knockout 112 flies was due to a change in number of flies responding to mechanical stimuli, or a decrease 113 in the magnitude of the individual flies’ responses. To separate response to a stimulus from 114 background noise, we defined a threshold for a “response” to each stimulus as > 10% of the 115 response to the mean naïve response. We found that for both control and Neurocalcin 116 knockout flies, the attenuation in response came entirely from the proportion of flies 117 responding (Fig. 2C). When only “responding” flies were included in analysis, there was no 118 decrease in the amplitude of locomotor responses across repetitive stimuli, nor any 119 difference between genotype (p = 0.46, main effect of Genotype & of Stimulus #, 2-way 120 ANOVA) (Fig. 2D). The all-or-nothing nature of locomotor responses in our paradigm 121 supports the premise that the decrease in mean amplitude across repeated stimuli 122 represents a learning event i.e an altered “decision” of whether to respond or not to a given 123 stimulus, as opposed to a motor defect affecting the ability to sustain robust reactions over 124 multiple stimuli. 125

Short nights restore consolidated sleep and mechanosensory habituation in 127 

Neurocalcin knockout flies 128 

Hippocalcin, the mammalian homologue of Neurocalcin, promotes synaptic plasticity and 129 memory in mice 35-39 , and cognitive development in humans 40,41 . Thus, we sought to test 130 whether Neurocalcin facilitates habituation through its effect on night sleep, or via sleep-131 independent processes potentially linked to memory. 132 

We previously documented that night sleep loss in Neurocalcin mutant flies was 133 particularly prominent under long night, winter-like, conditions 9, suggesting that night length 134 can modulate sleep loss in Neurocalcin mutant flies. Interestingly, increasing sleep drive by 135 restricting the period of consolidated night sleep is utilised in cognitive behavioural therapy 136 for insomnia, and has similarly been shown to rescue sleep loss in a variety of Drosophila 137 sleep mutants 10 . Control and Neurocalcin knockout flies were raised under standard 12 h light: 12 h 141 dark conditions then, after 3-5 days of adulthood, were shifted to three days of long (8 h 142 light: 16 h dark) or short (16 h light: 8 h dark) night conditions, with sleep quantified on the 143 third day of each light: dark cycle. Consistent with prior results 9, we confirmed reduced 144 nighttime but not daytime sleep in Neurocalcin knockout flies under long night conditions 145 (Fig. 3A, C and Figure S3B-C), and found that this decrease was associated with 146 fragmentated sleep, characterised by a significant increase in the number of sleep bouts and 147 a concomitant reduction in sleep bout duration (Fig. 3D, E). In contrast, under short night 148 conditions, overall night sleep (Fig. 3B, C) and the degree of sleep consolidation (Fig. 3D, E) 149 were not significantly different between Neurocalcin knockout flies and controls. Day sleep 150 was also slightly increased in Neurocalcin knockout flies under short night conditions, likely 151 due to reduced locomotor activity towards lights-off (Fig. 3B, Figure S3B-C). Thus, changing 152 night length provides a means to tune night sleep quality on-demand in Neurocalcin 153 knockout flies, allowing us to disentangle sleep-dependent versus -independent functions of 154 Neurocalcin. 155 

Taking advantage of this finding, we quantified mechanosensory habituation in 156 control and Neurocalcin knockout flies entrained to short or long night conditions (Fig. 3F). 157 Strikingly, while mechanosensory habituation was reduced in Neurocalcin knockout flies 158 compared to controls under long night conditions (Fig. 3F), this difference was abolished 159 under short night conditions (Fig. 3G). These data strongly suggest that Neurocalcin 160 regulates habituation directly through its effect on night sleep. 161

How might night duration modulate mechanosensory habituation occurring after 171 nighttime sleep? Prior studies have shown that sleep deprivation (SD) in adult flies impairs 172 synaptic downscaling, resulting in increased levels of the presynaptic active zone protein 173 Bruchpilot 43 that can be visualised through immuno-fluorescent microscopy. Prolonged (16 174 h) SD was initially shown to increase presynaptic Bruchpilot expression by ~ 75% across 175 

Drosophila brain neuropil regions 44 . More recently, 12 h night-specific SD was shown to 176 modulate Bruchpilot to a more limited degree, enhancing Bruchpilot expression in cholinergic 177 neurons by ~ 25%, and in GABAergic neurons by ~ 10% 45 . Loss of the Drosophila 178 Insomniac protein, which reduces overall day and night sleep by ~ 65% in male flies 32 , also 179 leads to a 40-50% increase in presynaptic Bruchpilot expression 46 .180 

Extending our analysis in the context of the synaptic homeostasis hypothesis 5 and 181 the above studies, we investigated whether altering night length was sufficient to modify 182 presynaptic Bruchpilot expression in control flies, and if so, whether loss of Neurocalcin 183 affected this relationship. We immuno-labelled synaptic Bruchpilot in isolated brains of 184 control and Neurocalcin knockout flies following three days of entrainment to short or long 185 night conditions, then quantified fluorescent intensity of synaptic Bruchpilot through confocal 186 microscopy, normalising fluorescence to a control DNA marker (DAPI, see Methods). 187 

Consistent with the relative subtlety of this manipulation compared to mechanical 188 sleep deprivation 44,45 or loss of Insomniac 46 , we observed a trend towards a small increase 189 (~ 16%) in Bruchpilot throughout the brain neuropil region of control flies subjected to short 190 versus long nights, which was non-significant (Figure S4C). However, in the neuropil-rich 191 antennal lobe regions, which process olfactory information in insects, we observed a 192 significant increase (~ 40%) in synaptic Bruchpilot levels in short versus long night 193 conditions (Fig. 4A, B).. Collectively, the above data support a model in which reduced night sleep in control 199 flies exposed to short nights perturbs synaptic downscaling in specific neuropil domains 200 compared to flies experiencing long nights. We hypothesise that such domains may include 201 circuits relevant to mechano-sensation and movement, where impaired downscaling could 202 limit the ability of habituation-promoting mechanisms (such as enhanced synaptic 203 transmission from local inhibitory neurons 23 ) to dampen functional connectivity between 204 mechanosensory neurons and downstream motoneuron outputs. The improvement of sleep 205 quality in Neurocalcin mutants exposed to short nights (Fig. 3A, C-E) breaks the normal 206 correlation between night length and synaptic Bruchpilot expression (Fig. 4), leading to 207 improved rather than impaired mechanosensory habituation in short compared to long night 208 conditions (Figure S4). 209 

Further studies will be required to test this model – in particular, by identifying 210 mechanosensory and/or pre-motor circuits in which synaptic output is modulated by 211 habituation-promoting mechanisms. Intriguingly, recent work has shown that continuous or 212 repetitive mechanosensory stimulation, akin to the gentle rocking of a human baby, 213 increases daytime sleep in Drosophila through a habituation-like mechanism 47 . In concert 214 with our findings, these data suggest an intertwined and potentially bi-directional relationship 215 between mechano-sensation, sleep, and habituation, across both day-night and seasonal 216 cycles. Finally, we note that sleep and habituation are dysregulated in a variety of human 217 neurodevelopmental disorders 48-52 and corresponding animal models 49,53-57 . Since 218 habituation promotes attention to environmental novelties that form the substrates of 219 memory-related coincidence detection mechanisms 58 , our work has implications for 220 understanding the common co-morbidity of sleep, habituation, and memory defects in these 221 clinically important conditions. 222

MATERIALS AND METHODS 243 

Experimental model and subject details 244 

Experimental models were fruit flies of the species Drosophila melanogaster . Genotypes 245 used were iso31 (an isogenized strain of w1118 flies and a kind gift from Prof. Kyunghee Koh, 246 Thomas Jefferson University), w1118 ;+; Nca KO1 /TM2, and w1118 ;+; Nca KO2 /TM2 9 To control for 247 genetic background, these lines were outcrossed into the iso31 background for 5 248 generations. Flies were raised and maintained on standard fly food under 12 h: 12 h light-249 dark cycles (12L: 12D) at a constant 25°C. Experiments took place at 25°C, and unless 250 otherwise indicated, in 12L: 12D conditions. Experiments were conducted on male flies. 251 

252 

Habituation protocol 253 

Drosophila ARousal Tracking (DART) analysis was performed as described previously 1.254 Briefly, five- to seven-day old male flies were individually loaded into glass behavioural tubes 255 (Trikinetics inc., MA, USA) containing 4% sucrose and 2% agar, and left for 24 h to 256 acclimatise in the indicated L:D conditions. The mechanical stimulus consisted of a 257 mechanical vibration in the form of a train of five 200 ms pulses separated by 800 ms 258 intervals, set at the DART system’s maximum intensity, which elicited a robust movement 259 response. The habituation protocol consisted of 20 mechanical stimuli delivered at ten-260 minute intervals, beginning at Zeitgeber time 3 (ZT3). Videos were taken using a USB 261 webcam (Logitech). Speed (mm / s) was analysed by the DART system from absolute 262 position, and binned in one min intervals. Flies were excluded from analysis if they exceeded 263 an average of 1 mm / s speed in the five one-minute bins preceding the first stimulus. 264 Response to the stimulus was defined as the mean speed (mm / s) in the one-minute bin 265 following the stimulus. Speed was normalised within genotype to the mean of the response 266 to the first stimulus. To derive the % of flies responding to stimuli, a “response” was 267 distinguished from background movement by the (arbitrary) threshold of a change in mean 268 speed between the one-minute bins preceding and following a stimulus of >10% of the mean 269 response to the first stimulus. 270

A distinct automated Drosophila tracking system, the Zantiks MWP unit (Zantiks Ltd., 271 Cambridge, UK) was used to generate an equivalent habituation protocol. 5-7 day old male 272 iso31 control flies were loaded into individual wells filled with 4% sucrose and 2% agar in a 273 48-well plate, and left to acclimatise for 24 h in a 12 h light: 12 h dark cycle at 25°C. 274 

Drosophila were loaded into the Zantiks unit at ZT0, and the habituation protocol 275 commenced at ZT3. The habituation protocol again consisted of 20 mechanical stimuli 276 delivered at 10 min intervals, each stimulus consisting of a single 1 s pulse set at medium 277 strength, generated by the Zantiks mechanical stimulation stand. Distance travelled is 278 automatically tracked by the Zantiks system, and total distance travelled was binned in 1 min 279 intervals. Scripts used for the habituation protocol on the Zantiks MWP are available at 280 github.com/abigaildwilson/zanscripts. 281 

282 

Sleep analysis 283 

Sleep analysis was performed using the Drosophila Activity Monitor 5 system (DAM5H, 284 Trikinetics inc., MA, USA), which improves on the fidelity of the earlier DAM2 system by 285 using 15 infrared beams per tube, rather than a single one, to track movement 2. As above, 286 three- to five-day old male flies were loaded in behavioural tubes, and were left for two full 287 days to acclimatise at the relevant light cycle. On the third day the number of beam breaks 288 was measured using the Drosophila Activity Monitor. Sleep was defined using the standard 289 definition of > 5 mins of inactivity, which has been shown to correlate with postural changes, 290 increased arousal threshold and altered brain activity patterns 2. Sleep bout number and 291 duration was analysed using the open-source software suite phaseR 292 (https://github.com/abhilashlakshman/phaseR/tree/main ) 3.293
