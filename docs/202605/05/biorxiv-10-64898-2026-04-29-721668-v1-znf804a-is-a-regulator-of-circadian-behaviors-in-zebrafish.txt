Abstract 11 

12 

Sleep disturbances are common among individuals with schizophrenia and can exacerbate 13 

disruptions in cognitive processes like learning and memory. Elucidating pharmacologically 14 

targetable molecular pathways perturbed by schizophrenia genes may uncover new treatment 15 

avenues. Here, we investigated the relationship of the schizophrenia-associated gene znf804a with 16 

sleep and circadian pathways. Using multi-day behavior tracking, we showed that znf804a 17 

zebrafish mutants displayed changes in sleep and circadian behaviors when light cues were 18 

removed. Through bulk RNA sequencing of fish raised under normal light cycling and dark-only 19 

conditions, we identified altered gene expression in the core and auxiliary pathways controlling 20 

circadian rhythms. Expression of fbxl3a , which encodes a modulator of the core negative feedback 21 

regulator of the clock, decreased in a dose-dependent manner as znf804a mutant copy number 22 

increased. Further analysis also revealed shifts in the relative abundance of specific transcripts, 23 

including idh1, suggesting znf804a could influence transcript processing or stability. Together, 24 

these findings link a ZNF804A ortholog to sleep and circadian behaviors and identify the regulation 25 

of fbxl3a and transcript processing as candidate mechanisms through which this schizophrenia risk 26 

gene may influence circadian biology. 27 

28 

Introduction 29 

30 

Neuropsychiatric conditions, such as schizophrenia (SCZ), are a leading cause of disability, with 31 

SCZ alone affecting 1% of individuals worldwide 1. Sleep disturbances is a common symptom in 32 

individuals with SCZ and can cause major disruptions in everyday life 2 by affecting memory 33 

consolidation, attention, decision-making, and other cognitive processes 3,4 . SCZ is highly 34 

heritable, and identifying how SCZ-associated genes influence sleep may reveal molecular 35 

pathways that can be pharmacologically targeted to improve sleep and cognitive function. 36 

37 

ZNF804A is among the top genes associated with SCZ, first identified in a genome-wide 38 

association study (GWAS) 5, and further replicated in other cohort studies 6–9 . Several human 39 

studies have also linked a risk allele ( rs1344706 ) in the second intron of ZNF804A to brain 40 

anatomy 10–12 and connectivity 13–15 . The ZNF804A risk allele also has substantial effects on learning 41 

and memory. One study found that individuals with SCZ carrying the risk allele of ZNF804A 42 

performed worse on a visual memory task than those without 16 . Notably, the ZNF804A risk allele 43 

has been linked to changes in sleep neurophysiology and impacts the relationship between sleep 44 

and memory consolidation 17 . Taken together, these findings suggest that ZNF804A influences 45 

neural circuitry and sleep-dependent neural processes in individuals with SCZ. 46

Several in vitro and animal model studies have demonstrated that ZNF804A and its orthologs have 1

roles in cellular and molecular processes in the nervous system. At the circuit level, ZNF804A 2

regulates neurodevelopment and plasticity, including by altering the expression of genes linked to 3

cell adhesion and neural migration 18 and recruiting glutamatergic receptors 19 and translation 4

machinery to synapses 20,21 . However, its specific molecular and biochemical functions remain 5

unclear. Although it is predicted to contain a C2H2-type zinc finger domain commonly found in 6

transcription factors 22 , and one study found it binds to DNA to regulate transcription 23 , it has more 7

broadly been implicated in mRNA splicing and transcript processing. For example, ZNF804A has 8

been shown to regulate mitochondrial genes and genes regulating translation 20 , and it interacts with 9

RBFOX1, a clinically relevant mRNA splicing factor 24 . These findings point towards ZNF804A 10 

having roles in RNA processing and translation to coordinate neural development and circuit 11 

plasticity. 12 

13 

Links between sleep and ZNF804A in model organisms have not yet been established; however, 14 

effects on brain function and behavior have been reported. Mutant mice displayed impaired 15 

learning and memory in behavioral tasks and increased anxiety and depressive-like behaviors 25,26 ,16 

along with synaptic plasticity deficits in the hippocampus and glutamate/GABA imbalances 25 . One 17 

study also found sex-dependent differences in hippocampal LTP and neuromodulation, including 18 

serotonin and dopamine signaling 26 , in mutants. In zebrafish, znf804a regulates brain activity, with 19 

midbrain hypoactivity reported in znf804a mutants compared to siblings 27 , although no strong 20 

behavioral differences were observed under normal light-cycling conditions in larvae. Orthologs 21 

of ZNF804A therefore play important roles in the brain and behavior in humans and model 22 

organisms. Because sleep can affect anxiety-related behaviors and learning and memory, linking 23 

these ZNF804A orthologs to sleep may provide insights into other phenotypes observed in model 24 

organisms. 25 

26 

In this study, we aimed to understand the mechanisms underlying the znf804a mutant brain activity 27 

phenotypes 27 and how ZNF804A contributes to psychiatric disorders. Through a combination of 28 

behavioral and transcriptomic studies, we determined that loss of znf804a disrupts molecular 29 

pathways controlling circadian rhythms, leading to altered circadian behaviors. 30 

31 

Results 32 

33 

znf804a mutants display disrupted circadian activity 34 

35 

Given the relationship between ZNF804A and sleep in humans, we were interested in investigating 36 

circadian behaviors and pathways in znf804a zebrafish mutants. Preliminary bulk RNA sequencing 37 

(Figure S1 ) suggested that loss of znf804a reduced the expression of the circadian genes fbxl3a 38 

and per1b , which inspired us to further test circadian behaviors. We monitored 4 dpf larvae in our 39 

96-well behavior assay under four conditions: normal 14 hr light/10 hr dark cycles 27 , dark/dark 40 

cycles beginning at 10 PM (the start of their normal dark period), light/light cycles, and a light/dark 41 

experiment, hereafter referred to as light shift, where their light period was shifted to start at 3 AM 42 

and end at 5 PM after normal circadian entrainment. In normal light/dark cycles, we saw that there 43 

was a small shift in circadian activity in znf804a mutants compared to wild-type siblings ( Figure 44 

1A ). We repeated this experiment under dark/dark conditions, and observed a stronger shift in 45 

activity and decreases in overall activity ( Figure 1B ). These results were replicated in two 46

The core molecular mechanisms controlling circadian rhythms consist of positive regulators ( clock 15 

and BMAL /arntl genes) and negative regulators ( per and cry genes). Additional mechanisms that 16 

modify this pathway include the REV-ERB pathway ( nr1d genes), retinoic orphan response genes 17 

(ror genes), and fbxl3 genes ( Figure 3A , Table S6 )28–31 . We next compared how znf804a 18 

mutations and exposure to light/dark impacted each of these elements from the RNA sequencing 19 

data. We found that genes from each of these molecular pathways were impacted in znf804a 20 

mutants ( Figure 3B ). In the core pathway, arntl1a , cry1bb , and per1a were dysregulated in 21 

znf804a mutants. Additionally, clocka , clockb , and arntl1a were significantly higher in mutant fish 22 

raised in the dark vs those compared in normal light conditions. Several genes were affected by 23 

light exposure, including cry1aa , cry1bb , per2 , and per3 , consistent with prior reports 32,33 . These 24 

genes may allow the znf804a mutants to maintain circadian rhythms in normal light/dark 25 

conditions ( Figure 1A ). In the REV-ERB pathway, nr1d1 , nr1d4a , and nr1d4b were dysregulated 26 

by both znf804a mutation and by exposure to light ( Figure 3B , Table S6 ). In the retinoic acid 27 

orphan response group of genes, only rorcb was disrupted in znf804a mutants. Light/dark 28 

conditions did not affect the expression of any of the rora , rorb , or rorc genes. We next compared 29 

the expression of cdkn1a, an E-box-rich gene influenced by circadian genes like the clock and 30 

arntl, across all four groups, and found its expression was dysregulated by both znf804a mutations 31 

and light/dark conditions. Thus, znf804a mutations appear to influence RNA expression of genes 32 

related to the canonical and auxiliary circadian pathways, which could explain the circadian 33 

phenotypes seen in the fish. 34

fbxl3a has a gene-dosage relationship with znf804a 1

2

One notable circadian gene whose expression was almost eliminated in znf804a mutants was 3

fbxl3a (Figure 4A ), which encodes a protein that regulates CRY stability 30 . A previous zebrafish 4

study found that loss of fbxl3a resulted in circadian drifting when larvae were removed from 5

normal light/dark cycles 31 , similar to our observed phenotype ( Figure 1B ). Heterozygous fish 6

exhibited an intermediate reduction in fbxl3a expression compared to the wild-type and 7

homozygous mutant siblings. To validate this relationship, we injected wild-type embryos with 8

two cocktails of guide RNAs and Cas9 mRNA targeting the protein-coding or regulatory region 9

of znf804a . We found significant or trend-level reductions in fbxl3a expression in these two 10 

independent crispant experiments ( Figure S3 ). Nr1d1 expression was also increased, confirming 11 

broad effects on circadian gene regulation when znf804a levels are reduced. We observed similar 12 

patterns for many of the individual fbxl3a transcripts ( Figure 4B ). Based on a comprehensively 13 

annotated transcriptome from the Lawson Lab 34 , the large TCONS_00125506 isoform and the 14 

larger of the protein-coding containing isoforms, TCONS_00125508, both exhibited a similar 15 

gene-dosage dependent reduction in expression across wild-type, heterozygous, and mutant fish 16 

as overall fbxl3a expression. This supports the global reduction in fbxl3a gene expression seen in 17 

the gene-level analysis. Transcript TCONS_00125510, the isoform containing only the protein 18 

coding region and no UTRs, was downregulated in the mutants and heterozygous fish compared 19 

to the wildtypes, which does seem to indicate that znf804a mutants and heterozygous fish have 20 

deficiencies in processing this short transcript. Transcript TCONS_00125507 and 09, which are 21 

lowly expressed, did not differ between znf804a mutants, heterozygous, and wild-type siblings 22 

(Figure 4C ). These results suggest that overall fbxl3a expression is reduced in a gene-dosage-23 

dependent manner by znf804a loss, and that even in heterozygous fish, transcript processing of 24 

fbxl3a may be altered. 25

Transcript abundance shifts are observed in several genes, including genes linked to sleep 27 

and circadian pathway modulation 28 

29 

While the exact function of znf804a is unknown, znf804a has been highly implicated in RNA 30 

processing 20,24 . Further analysis of individual transcripts in our RNA sequencing data revealed 31 

multiple transcripts within a gene, in which one transcript was upregulated while another was 32 

downregulated ( Table S7 ). These shifts may reveal differences in transcript processing. In the fish 33 

raised in normal light conditions, 12 genes showed shifts in transcript abundance in znf804a 34 

mutants. Most of these pairs of isoforms had minor differences, generally less than 100-200 bps 35 

differences in total length, with several of the longer sequences having 5´ modifications. However, 36 

4 of the genes – ctnnd2b, caska, RAMP1, and idh1 – exhibited shifts involving substantially larger 37 

differences in isoforms. Notably, in idh1 , the long transcript idh1 -201/ TCONS_00123632 was not 38 

detected in any of the wild-type fish, but was present in all mutant samples and 3 of the 4 39 

heterozygous samples. Correspondingly, an intermediate transcript of 3490 base pairs not 40 

annotated on ENSEMBL was elevated in the wildtypes compared to the mutants. The smaller idh1 -41 

202 transcript was unaffected ( Figure 5A-C ). caska and ctnnd2b also exhibited similar shifts in 42 

expression of one transcript to another in the mutants compared to wildtypes ( Figure 5D ). These 43 

results suggest that znf804a may influence transcript processing or stability. 44

Discussion 1

2

In this study, for the first time in model organisms to our knowledge, we demonstrate a link 3

between the SCZ risk gene ortholog znf804a and circadian rhythms. Behaviorally, zebrafish with 4

mutations in znf804a exhibited disrupted circadian behaviors when light cues were removed. At 5

the molecular level, znf804a mutants exhibited disruptions in circadian pathways both under 6

normal lighting conditions and in the dark. These changes were widespread across all of the core 7

and auxiliary circadian pathways. These two lines of evidence align with findings that ZNF804A 8

impacts sleep in humans 17 . While uncertainties regarding ZNF804A’s exact function remain due 9

to its ambiguous protein structure and biomolecular interactions, we suggest two hypotheses for 10 

how znf804a impacts circadian pathways: 11 

12 

One hypothesis for how znf804a might influence circadian regulation is that Znf804a is directly 13 

acting on fbxl3a , which encodes an auxiliary regulator of circadian rhythms. The expression of 14 

fbxl3a is massively reduced in a gene-dosage-dependent manner, and analysis of individual 15 

transcripts revealed that several followed this pattern ( Figure 4 ). The loss of fbxl3a without 16 

upregulation of any annotated fbxl3a transcripts suggests that fbxl3a is either not being transcribed 17 

or precursor transcripts are not being processed and are eliminated via nonsense-mediated decay. 18 

The loss of fbxl3a would reduce the regulation of CRY through ubiquitination, which could cause 19 

a shift in cycling of circadian genes and shift circadian activity, which is seen in both znf804a and 20 

fbxl3a mutants in the absence of circadian cues 27 .21 

22 

A second, non-mutually exclusive hypothesis is that znf804a indirectly influences circadian genes 23 

either through direct transcript processing of other genes or by impacting the development or 24 

function of neurons in circadian circuits. There were four genes in which two transcripts of a single 25 

gene with substantial differences, ranging from hundreds to thousands of base pairs, were shifted 26 

in opposing directions. Although speculative, this and similar transcript-level changes could 27 

contribute to disruptions of circadian pathways and genes, especially given the links between 28 

CASK and CTNND2 in sleep regulation 35,36 and IDH1 ’s regulation of circadian pathways through 29 

Smad signalling 37 . While it is difficult to infer whether Znf804a is directly interacting with these 30 

gene products, the shift in transcripts seen in these genes, coupled with znf804a ’s proposed roles 31 

in RNA splicing, suggests that znf804a could be responsible for these imbalances. These transcript 32 

imbalances could have downstream effects on circadian pathways and genes, such as on fbxl3a ,33 

and further support that Znf804a could also be directly regulating fbxl3a transcripts. Finally, 34 

although we cannot rule out the possibility of developmental circuit effects in homozygous 35 

mutants, we suggest that this cause is unlikely because 1) there are no morphological changes 36 

observed in the mutant brain 27 , 2) the transcriptomics did not indicate any aberrant neuron types 37 

through GSEA analysis 38 (Figure S4 , Table S8 ), 3) znf804a is broadly expressed throughout the 38 

entire brain, making it less likely to affect only specific cell types 39 , and 4) isoform-level 39 

differences were observed in the heterozygous compared to the wild-type, even in the absence of 40 

strong heterozygous transcriptomic changes ( Tables S9-S12 ). 41 

42 

Additional studies elucidating how the exact functions of znf804a will provide further clarity of 43 

how znf804a impacts circadian genes. There is currently not a resolved protein structure or 44 

AlphFold model 40 , which makes it unclear whether znf804a is directly binding to RNA with its 45 

zinc finger domain or acts as a scaffold. To establish more causal relationships between znf804a 46

The prevalence of sleep disturbances in individuals with SCZ and the interconnectedness of sleep 8

and cognitive behaviors like learning and memory necessitate a better understanding of how SCZ 9

risk genes modulate sleep and circadian rhythms. Our results highlight the utility of model 10 

organisms to bridge SCZ genes and perturbations in sleep-related molecular pathways/behaviors, 11 

which generated testable hypotheses and identified pathways that can be prioritized in drug 12 

discovery. Further studies in model organisms can examine how ZNF804A ortholog mutations 13 

disrupt sleep architecture to determine if ZNF804A has conserved roles in sleep-related 14 

neurophysiological processes. Further, assessing how sleep disturbances in Znf804a/znf804a 15 

mutants affect learning and memory could further bridge findings in model organisms to findings 16 

in humans. 17

Materials and Methods 19 

Zebrafish Husbandry 20 

Zebrafish experiments were approved by the UMass Chan Institutional Animal Care and Use 21 

Committee (IACUC protocols IPROTO202300000053). All animals were maintained on a 14 h/10 22 

h light/dark cycle at 28°C before experiments. The process for generating the mutants used in this 23 

study is described in Thyme et al. 2019, and they were of the EK background. Larval fish were 24 

grown in 100 mm petri dishes at a density of 50 fish/dish. Debris was removed before experiments, 25

Larval Behavior 28 

Larval behavior assays were performed and analyzed as described 43 , with stimulus response 29 

paradigms removed. An IR lamp emitting 960 nm wavelength light (past the visible spectrum for 30 

zebrafish) and a Grasshopper GigE camera with 50 mm fixed focal length lens and an IR filter was 31 

used for tracking, and the light level was controlled through a white LED panel. For the larval 32 

experiments, the LED panel was controlled from a command file and was either turned off at 10 33 

PM and turned on at 8 AM (14 hr light/10 hr dark), turned off at 10 PM and remained off for the 34 

duration of the experiment (Dark condition), remained on for the duration of the experiment (Light 35 

condition), or was shifted to turn on at 3 AM and turn off at 5 PM. Tracking was performed using 36 

LabVIEW. Behavioral analysis code is available at: 37
