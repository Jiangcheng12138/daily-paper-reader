Abstract Aim : Aerobic metabolism supplies ~90% of the ATP for neural activity. In frogs, activity has large aerobic needs typical of an average vertebrate, but surprisingly, can shift to using only glycolysis upon emergence from hibernation. We hypothesized that hibernation triggers a global reduction in the aerobic cost of neural function. 

Methods : We simultaneously measured activity of the brainstem respiratory network via motor nerves and tissue oxygen partial pressure (pO 2) in vitro from control and hibernated bullfrogs (4 weeks cold submergence; 4°C). To identify which functions differentially consume O2, we sequentially blocked activity and various cellular processes requiring activity-independent ion regulation and used the resulting tissue pO 2 change ( ΔpO 2) as an index of O 2 consumed. We further assessed how activity varies as a function of tissue pO 2 and how O 2 consumption varies across network activity levels. 

Results : Despite similar network activity levels, we provide three lines of evidence that hibernation reduces its aerobic requirement. First, hibernators consume less oxygen for baseline activity. Second, network output remains stable from baseline to anoxia, while moderate hypoxia disrupts controls. Finally, accelerating activity does not enhance oxygen consumption as in controls, but aerobic metabolism ultimately increases during seizure-like activity. 

Conclusion : Hibernating frogs reduce aerobic needs for sustaining physiological levels of neural activity, revealing how they overcome the challenge of restarting motor circuits on the background of hypoxia during emergence from hibernation. More broadly,

INTRODUCTION 1Brain function is energetically expensive 1,2 . At the circuit level, synaptic 2transmission and the restoration of ion gradients following action potentials have high 3ATP demands, the majority of which is supplied by oxidative phosphorylation 3,4 . Given 4the ATP yield of aerobic respiration (30-32 moles ATP/1 mole glucose) compared to 5glycolysis (2 moles of ATP/1 mole of glucose), neural activity and oxygen consumption 6are tightly coupled, whereby increases in activity drive aerobic metabolism to sustain 7neuronal performance 5–7 . This relationship is especially critical, as disruptions to aerobic 8metabolism impair neural output and lead to neurological disease 8. The requirement for 9aerobic respiration is conserved across most vertebrate nervous systems and has 10 shaped prevailing views that sustained neural computation requires oxygen 11 consumption 9.12 Despite the view that neural activity’s large energy demands remain fixed, the 13 American bullfrog ( Aquarana catesbeiana ) provides a unique opportunity to address 14 how neural circuits can enter states that operate with far less energy. Under normal 15 circumstances frog circuits exhibit typical metabolic demands and rely heavily on 16 oxidative metabolism 10,11 . However, activity of brainstem motor circuits that generate 17 breathing improve function during anoxia from ~10 minutes to upwards of 4 hours under 18 otherwise identical experimental conditions after frogs emerge from hibernation 12,13 .19 This represents a striking display of metabolic plasticity, as this network has high 20 metabolic demands, with rhythm generating circuits that use glutamatergic synaptic 21 transmission, which is thought to incur large energy costs, and motor pools with 22 neurons that fire action potentials at rates >100 Hz during rhythmic bursting 14–16 . While low pO 2 does not present a problem 25 due to low metabolic rates and decreased need for many behaviors in the cold, the 26 problem is much more apparent upon emergence. Indeed, since neural activity typically 27 has oxygen demands, frogs emerging from hibernation must resume neural activity 28 under conditions when pO 2 is expected to be similarly low 18 , which would be difficult if 29 they did not become able to function with a lower requirement for aerobic ATP 30 production. 31 Mechanistically, there are two non-mutually exclusive possibilities that could 32 explain how hibernation alters the brainstem to function with less O 2: increasing ATP 33 supply during hypoxia and/or reducing the aerobic metabolism required to sustain 34 activity. First, while glycolysis produces ~15-fold less ATP than oxidative 35 phosphorylation, hypoxia can increase glycolysis through the Pasteur effect. Thus, 36 hibernation may lead to cellular changes that bolster flux through glycolysis 19 to sustain 37 neural activity without oxygen. On the other hand, hibernation may reduce the 38 dependence of neural activity on aerobic metabolism making while maintaining similar 39 network output. While there are several ways neural circuits could in principle enhance 40 their energy efficiency 20–22 , hibernation reduces the Ca 2+ permeability of NMDA-41 glutamate receptors and enhances receptor desensitization 23 , which is likely to reduce 42 the energetic burden of ion regulation at synapses. Resolving these possibilities may 43 provide new insight into how neural circuits can shift between states with vastly different 44 aerobic requirements while maintaining similar function, as well as leading to new ideas 45 for improving metabolic resilience in the brain. 46

DISCUSSION 154 Owing to the high ATP yield of aerobic respiration, increases in oxidative 155 metabolism are thought to be essential for meeting the energy costs incurred by neural 156 activity, from flies to mammals 6,29 . Energy limitations in neurological disorders have 157 prompted interest in animal adaptations as sources of novel mechanisms for improving 158 metabolic robustness in neural systems 18,30–33 . In frogs, hibernation transforms 159 brainstem circuits to function during lack of oxygen by altering synapses such that 160 glycolysis supports activity as a lone ATP source 12,13 . While impressive, we now show 161 that neural activity can be uncoupled from its support by aerobic metabolism, even 162 when oxygen is readily available. These results introduce that circuits with a seemingly 163 obligate requirement for aerobic metabolism can enter a low-cost state that achieves 164 the same functional output over a wide range of energetic challenges, including hypoxia, 165 elevated activity, and pathological hyperexcitability, all while consuming less O 2.166 Based on the cellular processes addressed, neural activity, activity-independent 167 Na + /K + ATPase turnover, and additional housekeeping processes that are activity-168 independent are the main contributors of O 2 consumption in the brainstem, with minimal 169 input from spontaneous excitatory postsynaptic currents and the V-ATPase involved in 170 filling vesicles with neurotransmitter (Figure 1F, Supplemental Figure 2). Despite similar 171 levels of neural activity, hibernator brainstems exhibited a reduction in O 2 consumption, 172 driven by less O2 consumed by neural activity and less spent on other activity-173 independent processes. While our results point to a lower cost of activity, they do not 174 allow us to address which aspects of network activity decrease energetic needs. 175 Synaptic transmission incurs a greater metabolic cost than action potentials in active 176

MATERIALS AND METHODS 250 

Experimental model 251 All procedures were approved by the Animal Care and Use Committee at the University 252 of Missouri (protocol #39264). Adult female American bullfrogs ( Aquarana catesbeiana ,253 ~100 g; n = 51) were obtained from Rana Ranch (Twin Falls, ID, USA) and housed in 254 20-gallon tanks containing dechlorinated, aerated water at room temperature on a 12-h 255 light/dark cycle. Frogs had access to wet and dry platforms, were fed weekly, along with 256 daily water checks and weekly water changes. Control animals were acclimated for ≥1257 week before experiments. A subset of frogs was subjected to a simulated hibernation 258 protocol. Food was withheld and water temperature was decreased from room 259 temperature to 4°C over 10 days in a temperature-controlled chamber. Once at 4°C, a 260 perforated screen was placed below the water surface to enforce continuous 261 submergence while allowing gas exchange at the air-water interface, and frogs were 262 maintained for 4 weeks before experimentation. Water pO 2 was approximately 155 263 mmHg in both control (~22°C) and hibernation (4°C) tanks, consistent with air-264 equilibrated water. Brainstem–Spinal Cord Preparation 270 Brainstem–spinal cord preparations were obtained as described previously 49 . Frogs 271 were anesthetized with isoflurane (~1 ml/L; v/v) until the toe-pinch reflex was no longer 272 present, then rapidly decapitated with a guillotine. The head was submerged in ice-cold 273 bullfrog artificial cerebrospinal fluid (aCSF; in mM: 104 NaCl, 4 KCl, 1.4 MgCl 2, 7.5 274 glucose, 40 NaHCO 3, 2.5 CaCl 2, and 1 NaH 2PO 4; bubbled with 98.5% O 2/ 1.5% CO 2;275 pH 7.85). The forebrain was removed, and the brainstem–spinal cord preparation 276 (including the midbrain, brainstem, cut caudal to the hypoglossal nerve roots) was 277 isolated with nerve roots intact and transferred to a recording chamber continuously 278 superfused with oxygenated aCSF. Chamber temperature was maintained at 22°C 279 using a Warner Instruments CL-100 bipolar temperature controller with the thermistor 280 placed adjacent to the tissue. Motor output was recorded from the vagus nerve (cranial 281 nerve X) using glass suction electrodes. Vagus nerve recordings provide a reliable 282 index of the overall motor pattern generated by the respiratory network and has been 283 widely used to assess circuit function in this and similar preparations 15,50,51 . CONCLUSION 413 Our findings reveal that neural circuits in the vertebrate brain can undergo a 414 fundamental reorganization of the relationship between activity and O 2 consumption. By 415 reducing the aerobic requirement for neural activity, the frog brainstem maintains stable 416 network output under metabolic conditions that normally induce severe perturbations. 417 The relevance of this reduced aerobic cost is likely for emergence from hibernation, 418 when frogs must restart the neural circuits that drive breathing before the first breath is 419 taken, at which point brain oxygenation has not yet been restored by lung ventilation. A 420 central element of this shift appears to be the enhanced efficiency of network function, 421 which allows hibernators to generate normal output with less ongoing aerobic 422 metabolism. These results raise the question of how neural circuits can transition 423 between vastly different aerobic requirements while maintaining similar functional output. 424 Understanding these mechanisms may be particularly relevant for neurological 425 disorders such as Alzheimer's and Parkinson's disease, where impaired aerobic 426 metabolism compromises circuit function 8.
