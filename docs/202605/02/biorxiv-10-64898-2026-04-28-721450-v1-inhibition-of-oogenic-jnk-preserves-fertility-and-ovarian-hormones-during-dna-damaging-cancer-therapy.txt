Abstract 24 

Primary ovarian insufficiency (POI) and related infertility, early menopause, and 25 

endocrine disorders due to hormonal deficiency are major side effects in young female 26 

cancer patients undergoing cancer therapy. Current strategies preserving the fertility 27 

and hormonal functions of the ovary remain imperfect due to concerns of feasibility, 28 

efficacy, or safety. Herein, we identified c-Jun N-terminal kinase (JNK) as a pivotal 29 

regulator of the DNA damage response (DDR) signaling in oocytes of primordial 30 

follicles in response to DNA-damaging cancer therapy. Using pharmacological JNK 31 

inhibition and a genetically modified mouse model with oocyte-specific JNK deletion, 32 

together with histological, bioinformatic, and molecular approaches, we demonstrated 33 

that JNK inhibition prevented chemotherapy-induced oocyte apoptosis and POI, and 34 

preserved long-term reproductive cycles and fertility. Mechanistically, JNK was 35 

activated in response to chemotherapy-induced DNA damage in oocytes of primordial 36 

follicles, causing activation of transcription factor TAp63 α and subsequent oocyte 37 

apoptosis, ultimately resulting in diminished ovarian reserve and POI. A more clinically 38 

relevant breast cancer-bearing mouse model revealed that JNK inhibition preserved the 39 

ovarian reserve without compromising anti-cancer efficacy of chemotherapy. Together, 40 

our study identifies oocyte-intrinsic JNK as a promising target for developing ovarian 41 

protectants and safeguarding reproductive health and fertility in young female cancer 42 

survivors. 43

Introduction 48 

Advances in cancer survival rates have greatly increased the awareness of the 49 

side effects of cancer therapy and long-term quality of life in cancer survivors, including 50 

primary ovarian insufficiency (POI) in childhood, adolescent, and young adult female 51 

cancer patients (1-3). Chemotherapy, along with irradiation, is gonadotoxic to the ovary, 52 

dramatically heightening the risks of POI and related delay or absence of puberty, 53 

infertility, early menopause, and endocrine disorders due to hormonal deficiency (4, 5). 54 

Women with POI also face other serious health consequences, including depression, 55 

osteoporosis, autoimmune disorders, heart disease, and increased mortality (6, 7). 56 

57 

The ovary contains follicles at various stages as its functional unit. Primordial 58 

follicles, the earliest stage of follicles in the ovary, consist of a central oocyte 59 

surrounded by a single flattened layer of granulosa cells. Primordial follicles are finite 60 

and non-renewable after birth, establishing a woman’s ovarian reserve that determines 61 

her reproductive lifespan. Depletion of the ovarian reserve leads to menopause. 62 

Oocytes within primordial follicles are particularly vulnerable to genotoxic insults and 63 

rely on a DNA damage response (DDR) signaling pathway to promptly detect and repair 64 

DNA lesions, or trigger apoptosis if the damage is irreparable (8-11). This highly 65 

evolved DDR ensures that only oocytes with a largely intact genome progress to 66 

ovulation and fertilization, preventing the transmission of genetic errors to the next 67 

generations. Most anti-cancer agents exert their effects by inducing DNA damage in 68 

highly proliferating cancer cells (12). However, these agents inadvertently cause DNA 69

Current strategies preserving the ovarian reserve, fertility, and hormonal 73 

functions in young female cancer patients include oocyte/embryo cryopreservation, 74 

ovarian tissue cryopreservation (OTC), or ovarian suppression (13). These methods, 75 

however, remain imperfect due to concerns of feasibility, efficacy, or safety. Oocyte or 76 

embryo cryopreservation requires ovarian stimulation, which does not protect the 77 

ovarian reserve, delays cancer therapy, and is not feasible for prepubescent girls. OTC 78 

necessitates surgical removal of ovarian tissue, resulting in the loss of hormonal support 79 

of the ovary; subsequent ovarian tissue transplantation may reintroduce malignant cells 80 

(14). Ovarian suppression with gonadotropin-releasing hormone (GnRH) agonist 81 

remains controversial due to inconsistent efficacy and incomplete protection of the 82 

ovarian reserve (15, 16). 83 

84 

Developing ovarian protectants has emerged as a promising strategy for 85 

protecting and preserving the ovarian reserve in young female cancer patients. Such 86

fertility and hormonal functions of the ovary (17, 18). Modulating key molecules within 89 

the DDR signaling pathway, such as the DNA damage-sensing kinases ATM or ATR, 90 

checkpoint kinases CHEK1 and CHEK2 (19), transcription factor TAp63 α (20), and pro-91 

apoptosis proteins NOXA and PUMA (10, 21), has been shown to prevent oocyte 92

Discussion 349 

DNA-damaging cancer therapy activates DDR signaling pathway in the oocytes 350 

of primordial follicles, leading to apoptosis, depletion of ovarian reserve, and POI. 351 

Previous studies, including our own, demonstrated that inhibition of several key DDR 352 

molecules prevents oocyte apoptosis induced by gonadotoxic cancer therapy (10, 19-353 

21). However, most of the DDR components are largely shared between oocytes and 354 

cancer cells, and, therefore, targeting these molecules raises concerns of interfering 355 

with the anti-cancer efficacy and/or compromising genome stability in somatic tissues. 356 

Here, we established JNK, as a non-canonical genotoxic sensing kinase, integrating 357 

cancer therapy-induced DNA damage to the activation of TAp63 α and apoptosis in 358 

oocytes of primordial follicles, and JNK inhibition preserves the ovarian reserve without 359 

impairing anti-cancer efficacy. 360 

361 

Although all three chemotherapeutic agents used here induce DNA damage, 362 

their mechanisms differ. DOXO acts as a topoisomerase II (TOP2) inhibitor, blocking 363 

the resealing of DSBs during DNA replication (36). CDDP and the active metabolite of 364 

CPA generate intra- and inter-strand DNA crosslinks (37, 38). In oocytes of primordial 365 

follicles, DOXO activates ATM, which phosphorylates CHK2; together with casein 366 

kinase 1 (CK1), CHK2 promotes the hyper-phosphorylation of TAp63 α, converting it 367 

from an inactive dimer to an active tetramer, triggering oocyte apoptosis (23, 39, 40). 368

CDDP activates both ATR/CHK1 and ATM/CHK2 pathways to promote TAp63 α-369
