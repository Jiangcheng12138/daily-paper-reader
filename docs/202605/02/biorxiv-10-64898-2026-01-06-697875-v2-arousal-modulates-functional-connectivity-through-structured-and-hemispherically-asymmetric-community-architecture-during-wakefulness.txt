Abstract 23 Arousal fluctuates continuously during wakefulness, yet how these 24 moment-to-moment variations shape large-scale functional connectivity (FC) remains 25 unclear. Here, we combined 7T fMRI with concurrent pupillometry to quantify, for 26 every functional connection, how time-varying FC covaries with spontaneous arousal 27 in the awake human brain. Rather than exerting a uniform influence across the 28 connectome, arousal organized FC into a low-dimensional set of seven connectivity 29 communities, each defined by characteristic network compositions. These 30 communities exhibited systematic hemispheric asymmetries, specifically identifying a 31 "left-hemisphere centripetal architecture" where the left hemisphere serves as a 32 structural sink for the asymmetric convergence of arousal-modulated signals. 33 Importantly, hemispheric asymmetry did not arise from global shifts in connectivity 34 strength, but instead reflected structured spatial heterogeneity embedded within 35 community architecture. This modular and asymmetric organization was highly 36 preserved during naturalistic movie watching, indicating that arousal-related 37 modulation of FC reflects intrinsic principles that generalize across awake cognitive 38 contexts. Together, these findings demonstrate that moment-to-moment arousal 39 fluctuations shape large-scale FC through structured, hemispherically asymmetric 40 network organization during wakefulness. 41 42 

Key words: Arousal; time-varying functional connectivity; modular organization; 43 hemispheric lateralization; network hierarchy; spatial heterogeneity 44 45

# Introduction 46 Arousal is a core dimension of brain state that shapes perception, attention, and the 47 coordination of large-scale neural systems. Across major transitions—such as 48 wakefulness to sleep, anesthesia, or disorders of consciousness—substantial 49 reorganization of functional connectivity (FC) has been consistently observed (Chow 50 et al., 2013; Tagliazucchi et al., 2016; Demertzi et al., 2019; Banks et al., 2020; 51 Damaraju et al., 2020; Huang et al., 2020; Jang et al., 2024). These state-based findings 52 demonstrate that arousal fundamentally influences whole-brain communication 53 patterns. However, such approaches typically contrast discrete arousal states and 54 therefore provide limited insight into how moment-to-moment fluctuations in arousal 55 within the awake brain influence the organization of functional connectivity. 56 Increasing evidence shows that arousal varies continuously even during stable 57 wakefulness, including during resting-state fMRI and ongoing cognitive engagement. 58 These spontaneous fluctuations, often indexed by pupil diameter (Reimer et al., 2014; 59 McGinley et al., 2015; Joshi & Gold, 2020), modulate neural gain, sensory responses, 60 and behavioral performance. Yet despite their ubiquity and behavioral relevance, it 61 remains largely unknown how fine-grained arousal variations are expressed across the 62 functional connectome. Prior work has primarily examined regional signal amplitude 63 or isolated networks (Yellin et al., 2015; Schneider et al., 2016; Breeden et al., 2017; 64 Podvalny et al., 2021; Sobczak et al., 2021; Lloyd et al., 2023), thereby leaving 65 unresolved the fundamental question of whether the awake brain’s FC is uniformly 66 sensitive to arousal or whether arousal instead imprints structured spatial patterns 67 across functional networks. 68 A parallel question is whether arousal-modulated connectivity patterns show 69 hemispheric asymmetry, given longstanding evidence for lateralized arousal, vigilance, 70

Studies of unihemispheric sleep and lateralized arousal 71 dynamics in animals (Rattenborg et al., 2000; Lyamin et al., 2016; Mascetti, Gian 72 Gastone, 2016; Reicher et al., 2021; Fenk et al., 2023; Libourel et al., 2023), 73 asymmetries in human EEG-based vigilance (Tamaki et al., 2016), and 74 right-lateralized alerting functions in attention (Heilman & Abell, 1980; Sturm & 75 Willmes, 2001; Shulman et al., 2010; Corbetta & Shulman, 2011) all suggest that 76 arousal may modulate left and right hemispheric systems differently. Nevertheless, 77 these observations have yet to be linked to the organization of whole-brain functional 78 interactions, and it remains unknown whether such asymmetries manifest at the level 79 of large-scale FC, and whether they reflect organized connectivity patterns rather than 80 non-specific global effects. 81 To address these questions, we combined high-field fMRI with concurrent 82 pupillometry to quantify, for every functional connection, how its connectivity 83 covaries with spontaneous arousal fluctuations during wakefulness. This edgewise 84 measure of arousal–time varying functional connectivity (tvFC) coupling provides a 85 comprehensive map of where in the connectome arousal leaves its strongest imprint, 86 without imposing predefined states or regional assumptions. Using this framework, 87 we first test whether arousal sensitivity is spatially homogeneous or segregates into 88 distinct sets of connections with similar coupling profiles. We next assess whether 89 these spatially organized arousal-modulated patterns show systematic hemispheric 90 asymmetry, with particular emphasis on attentional systems that show known 91 lateralization. Finally, we evaluate the cross-context stability of this organizational 92 structure by comparing resting state and naturalistic movie watching in the same 93 participants. Together, these analyses delineate how moment-to-moment arousal 94 fluctuations shape large-scale functional architecture in the awake human brain. 95

Arousal–tvFC coupling reveals seven distinct connectivity 118 

communities 119 For each functional connection, we quantified how strongly its time-varying 120 connectivity covaried with moment-to-moment arousal, producing an edgewise 121 arousal–tvFC coupling matrix per run. Across all participants and runs, these coupling 122 profiles showed clear structure: connections did not exhibit uniform arousal 123 sensitivity but instead formed separable groups. 124 Unsupervised clustering of edgewise coupling patterns identified seven stable 125 connectivity communities (Fig. 2A). This solution was consistently favored across a 126 broad range of cluster numbers, indicating that arousal–tvFC coupling is inherently 127 low-dimensional. The seven communities captured distinct patterns of arousal 128 sensitivity across the connectome. Projecting each community into canonical network 129 pairs space (Thomas Yeo et al., 2011) showed that these communities were not 130 random mixtures of connections. Instead, each displayed a characteristic distribution 131 across network pairs (Fig. 2B). Some communities were enriched in network pairs 132 linking heteromodal and unimodal systems (Mesulam, 1998), while others were 133 dominated by heteromodal–heteromodal (H-H) or unimodal–unimodal (U-U) network 134 pairs. Community participation entropy further highlighted this structure: 135 unimodal–unimodal connections showed low entropy, indicating a restricted 136 participation in a few communities, whereas H-H and heteromodal–unimodal (H-U) 137 connections showed significantly higher entropy, indicating more diverse engagement 138 across communities (F(2,88)=12.24, p = 4.38×10 -6 ). 139 Together, these results demonstrate that arousal does not uniformly modulate the 140 connectome but instead engages a small number of organized connectivity 141

Arousal-modulated community architecture exhibit systematic 162 

hemispheric asymmetry 163 We next asked whether these arousal-modulated communities express hemispheric 164 biases. Using integration and segregation indices derived from LL, RR, LR, and RL 165 edge categories, we quantified, for each community, whether arousal preferentially 166 modulated intra- versus inter-hemispheric connectivity and whether these effects 167 favored one hemisphere. 168 At the network-pair level, several pairs showed significant lateralization compared 169 with a spatial permutation null model (Fig. 3A–B). Importantly, lateralization was not 170 global: only specific network pairs within communities exhibited robust hemispheric 171 biases, while others remained symmetric. Some communities showed rightward 172 integration, indicating stronger arousal-related modulation of network pairs within the 173 right hemisphere or between the right hemisphere and the rest of the brain, whereas 174 others showed leftward segregation, reflecting preferential influence on 175 within-left-hemisphere interactions. 176

Arousal-tvFC coupling lateralization arises from spatial 264 

heterogeneity rather than mean shifts 265 In the preceding analyses, we mainly focused on the lateralization of the 266 organizational patterns of the decomposed communities at the network-pairs and 267 nodal levels. However, how the strength of the arousal–tvFC coupling is spatially 268 distributed and whether lateralization exists in this distribution remains elusive. We 269 therefore next examined the spatial distribution of arousal–tvFC coupling strength and 270 its lateralization properties. 271 To test whether hemispheric biases reflected simple shifts in mean arousal–tvFC 272 coupling strength, we compared mean integration and segregation across communities 273 and whole connectome. Although the inter-community difference for segregation was 274 statistically significant, the mean difference between communities was very small. 275 This indicates limited hemispheric imbalance. 276 Although the mean arousal modulation on FC showed no significant lateralization, 277 prior results suggested that its spatial pattern is highly community specific. We 278 therefore hypothesized that the key information lies in the spatial heterogeneity 279 (distribution gradient) of the modulation strength, not the overall strength. We 280 therefore quantified spatial heterogeneity by ranking network pairs along a281 “lateralization axis” (Yang et al., 2025) and computing the slope of integration or 282 segregation values along this axis. All communities showed slopes significantly 283 steeper than the whole-brain baseline (all p_FDR < 0.001; Fig. 5B), demonstrating 284 that lateralization arises from spatial heterogeneity—not uniform shifts. 285

# Discussion 346 In this study, we investigated how moment-to-moment fluctuations in arousal shape 347 large-scale functional connectivity in the awake human brain. By combining 348 high-field fMRI with concurrent pupillometry, we quantified arousal–tvFC coupling 349 at the level of individual edges and showed that arousal does not exert a uniform or 350 diffuse influence across the connectome. Instead, it modulates connectivity through a 351 set of seven distinct communities, each defined by characteristic network 352 compositions and hemispheric patterns. These findings demonstrate that fluctuations 353 in arousal, even within stable wakefulness, impose a structured and asymmetric 354 organization on whole-brain functional interactions. 355 Although arousal is often conceptualized as a global modulatory state, our results 356 show that its impact on tvFC is highly organized. Specifically, we show the presence 357 of low-dimensional, reproducible communities suggests that arousal modulates the 358 connectome through structured spatial patterns rather than homogeneous gain 359 modulation. We hypothesize that this structured macroscopic architecture reflects the 360 differentiated projection patterns of subcortical neuromodulatory systems, such as the 361 locus coeruleus–noradrenergic pathway (Aston-Jones & Cohen, 2005; Jordan, 2024) 362 and thalamus (Magnin et al., 2010; Lewis et al., 2015; Liu et al., 2018). The 368 hierarchical community pattern—where unimodal interactions cluster into fewer 369 patterns while heteromodal systems participate broadly—is compatible with theories 370 that place heteromodal cortex at the apex of large-scale integrative gradients 371 (Mesulam, 1998; Margulies et al., 2016). Together, these observations suggest that the 372 arousal-sensitive connectome reflects not merely regional susceptibility but an intrinsic 373 network-level architecture, in which state-dependent modulation is aligned with 374 established large-scale organizational principles. 375 A major goal of this work was to determine whether arousal imposes systematic 376 hemispheric asymmetry on FC. We found clear evidence that it does, but in a 377 community-specific rather than global manner. Certain communities showed 378 rightward integration, others leftward segregation, and others no significant 379 bias—indicating that hemispheric asymmetry emerges from the organization of 380 arousal-sensitive connectivity motifs rather than a single overarching hemispheric 381 dominance. This distributed asymmetry resonates with converging evidence across 382 species: unihemispheric vigilance in birds and marine mammals (Rattenborg et al., 383 2000; Lyamin et al., 2016; Mascetti, Gian Gastone, 2016; Reicher et al., 2021; Fenk et 384 al., 2023; Libourel et al., 2023), asymmetric EEG signatures related to vigilance in 385 humans (Tamaki et al., 2016), and classic findings of right-lateralized alerting and 386 reorienting functions (Heilman & Abell, 1980; Sturm & Willmes, 2001; Shulman et al., 387 2010; Corbetta & Shulman, 2011). This suggests that hemispheric specialization may 388 arise from distinct modes of arousal-modulated reconfiguration rather than fixed 389 structural asymmetries. 390

Instead, arousal-driven asymmetry 393 manifested as spatially heterogeneous gradients within communities. Arousal does not 394 uniformly shift connectivity toward one hemisphere; rather, it selectively amplifies 395 lateralization in specific connectivity motifs. This distributed, gradient-like pattern 396 complements recent work highlighting macroscale cortical gradients and manifold 397 structure as fundamental organizational principles (Margulies et al., 2016; Huntenburg 398 et al., 2018). We further found that communities with similar intrinsic topology 399 exhibited similar heterogeneity signatures, suggesting that baseline connectivity 400 architecture constrains how arousal shapes hemispheric interactions. This provides a 401 mechanistic explanation for why traditional hemisphere-level metrics often obscure 402 arousal-modulated asymmetries—these effects are expressed not as global biases but 403 as structured, topology-dependent gradients. 404 Another important observation is the stability of arousal–tvFC organization across 405 cognitive contexts. While the overall spatial layout of the seven-community 406 architecture showed moderate reorganization between resting-state and naturalistic 407 movie-watching, specific motifs—most notably communities 6 and 7—demonstrated 408 near-perfect correspondence across paradigms. This robustness aligns with prior 409 evidence that intrinsic connectivity organization persists across tasks (Cole et al., 2014; 410 Krienen et al., 2014) and that spontaneous fluctuations in arousal modulate cortical 411 dynamics even during rich sensory stimulation (Tanner et al., 2023). By demonstrating 412 that arousal-driven network modulation generalizes across both internally and 413 externally oriented states, our findings indicate that arousal acts as a stable organizing 414 axis of large-scale brain communication, rather than merely a background 415 physiological fluctuation. 416

First, 417 to ensure a mathematically rigorous assessment of hemispheric asymmetry, our 418 analysis was restricted to a symmetric cortical parcellation. Consequently, while we 419 demonstrate that arousal-modulated connectivity follows a structured macroscopic 420 architecture, we did not explicitly analyze the subcortical nuclei hypothesized to drive 421 these patterns. We hypothesize that the presence of these low-dimensional cortical 422 communities reflects coordinated motifs rather than a homogeneous gain modulation, 423 potentially mirroring the differentiated projection patterns of subcortical 424 neuromodulatory systems. For instance, the locus coeruleus–noradrenergic pathway 425 (Chandler et al., 2014; Schwarz & Luo, 2015) and thalamus (Hwang et al., 2017; 426 Shine, 2019; Müller et al., 2020; Shine et al., 2023) possess extensive yet non-uniform 427 projections that may anchor the community-specific and hemispherically asymmetric 428 patterns observed here. In the absence of direct subcortical-cortical integration in our 429 current framework, this link remains hypothetical. Future investigations incorporating 430 high-resolution subcortical data will be essential to empirically bridge the gap 431 between these large-scale cortical communities and the underlying physiological 432 scaffolding of the Ascending Reticular Activating System (ARAS). Second, while 433 pupillometry is a well-established and accessible proxy for central arousal (Joshi & 434 Gold, 2020), it remains an indirect peripheral marker. Integrating these findings with 435 concurrent EEG will be essential to provide a more granular, multi-modal 436 characterization of arousal states. Third, we utilized sliding-window FC to track 437 time-varying connectivity. Although this is a robust and widely validated technique, 438 employing alternative dynamic approaches with higher temporal resolution—such as 439 edge FC (Faskowitz et al., 2020) , multiplication of temporal derivatives (Shine et al., 440 2015) or dynamic conditional correlation (Lindquist et al., 2014)—may offer 441 complementary perspectives on the transient dynamics of arousal-modulated states. 442 Fourth, the generalizability of our approach to external cohorts warrants caution 443

# Methods and Materials 479 

Participants and datasets 480 We used data from the Human Connectome Project (HCP) 7T dataset, which includes 481 resting-state fMRI and naturalistic movie-watching runs with simultaneous eye 482 tracking (Van Essen et al., 2013). Across participants, up to four resting runs and four 483 movie runs were available, each approximately 15–16 minutes in length. All 484 procedures were approved by the Washington University Institutional Review Board, 485 and written informed consent was obtained from all participants. 486 

MRI acquisition and preprocessing 487 All imaging was collected on a Siemens 7T scanner (TR = 1 s, TE = 22.2 ms, voxel 488 size = 1.6 mm isotropic, multiband factor = 5, 900 volumes/run). We used the HCP 489 minimally preprocessed data (Glasser et al., 2013), followed by additional denoising: 490 linear detrending; regression of 24 head-motion parameters; regression of white 491 matter and CSF signals; band-pass filtering (0.01–0.1 Hz); scrubbing of frames with 492 FD > 0.2 mm; and interpolation across removed frames. 493

ROI parcellation 494 Analyses were performed using a 400-ROI symmetric parcellation with explicitly 495 matched left–right homologues (Yan et al., 2023). Vertexwise BOLD signals were 496 averaged within each ROI to obtain regional time series. All subsequent hemispheric 497 analyses relied on this explicit homotopic structure. 498 

Eye tracking preprocessing 499 Pupil diameter was extracted from the raw eye-tracking stream and cleaned following 500 established procedures (Gonzalez-Castillo et al., 2022): Removal of samples outside 501 MRI acquisition; detection of blinks and short missing segments (<1 s); linear 502 interpolation across missing segments; removal of brief physiologically implausible 503 excursions (<1 ms within long closures); smoothing with a 200 ms Hanning window; 504 down-sampling to 1 Hz to match the temporal resolution of the fMRI data. The 505 resulting time series served as a continuous arousal index for each run. 506 

Quality control 507 The final analyzed sample for the resting-state consisted of N = 139 healthy 508 participants (mean age = 29.1±3.5 years, 77 female). Runs were excluded if (a) more 509 than 20% of frames exceeded motion thresholds, (b) eye tracking did not cover the 510 full fMRI time series, or (c) more than 90% of samples were classified as eye closure. 511 After applying these criteria, 485 of the initial 723 scans were retained for analysis. 512 The same quality-control pipeline was applied to the movie-watching dataset, yielding 513 513 usable scans out of the original 725. After rigorous quality control, 139 514 participants (485 runs) were retained for the final analysis. Detailed information on 515 data retention and run distribution per participant is summarized in Figure S9. 516

Time-varying FC between each pair of ROIs was estimated using sliding-window 518 correlations: window length: 30 s; step size: 5 s. Within each window, Pearson 519 correlation coefficients were computed and Fisher-z transformed. This procedure 520 yielded a tvFC time series for each edge in each run. 521 

Arousal estimation from pupil size 522 Pupil data were processed using the same sliding-window parameters. The mean pupil 523 size within each window was taken as an index of moment-to-moment arousal level. 524 

Estimating arousal–tvFC coupling 525 For each functional connection, arousal–tvFC coupling was defined as the Pearson 526 correlation between its time-varying FC and the pupil-derived arousal fluctuations 527 across windows. Thus, each run produced a 400 × 400 symmetric matrix of coupling 528 values, later vectorized into edgewise features. 529 These matrices were concatenated across runs to form the dataset used for community 530 detection and all subsequent analyses. 531
