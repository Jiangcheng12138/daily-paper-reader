Abstract 11 

The development of minimally invasive multi-organ aging clocks, established through the 12 

deconvolution of plasma proteomics, has provided a convenient tool to assess the organ 13 

heterogeneity of aging. However, prior studies relied on bulk transcriptomic data for organ 14 

marker identification, which may lead to the potential misidentification of protein markers, 15 

and their research scope was largely confined to a few common diseases. To address 16 

these limitations, this study integrated multi-dimensional data to refine organ-enriched 17 

marker panels by incorporating organ-specific proteome information, and developed 18 

Proteome-Aware Organ Proxy Proteome Aging Clock (PAOPAC). PAOPAC exhibited 19 

decelerated biological age corresponding to improved physiological phenotypes across 20 

two independent external datasets, demonstrating its generalizability. We then leveraged 21 

PAOPAC to generate a comprehensive disease-aging landscape and to investigate the 22 

process of chronological and biological aging. Our analyses revealed that the majority of 23 

diseases are associated with an accelerated aging phenotype. 24

Introduction 26 

Accurately quantifying the biological aging process is highly dependent on reliable aging 27 

clocks. However, identifying clinically valuable aging biomarkers and constructing 28 

universally applicable aging clocks has long been a core unsolved problem in this field 1,2 

29 

and presents multiple methodological challenges. Firstly, regarding the choice of 30

We utilized proteomic data from the UKB 21 as the discovery dataset (Supplementary Fig. 85 

1a). Furthermore, we cross-examined three smaller public datasets based on the aptamer-86 

based SomaScan platform 16,22,23 (Methods, Fig. 1a and Supplementary Fig. 1a) for protein-87 

level and pathway-level consistency. 88

As noted earlier, organ enriched markers for current organ aging clocks were primarily 89 

derived from the GTEx dataset 14-16 , which reflects bulk RNA-level measurements 17 .90 

However, due to post-transcriptional, translational and post-translational regulation, protein 91 

abundance does not always correlate with RNA expression 18-20 . To address this 92 

discrepancy, we prepared three marker sets representing different molecular levels: the 93 

Human Protein Atlas (HPA) for bulk proteome, CellMarker 2.0 (CM2) for single-cell 94 

transcriptome, and the GTEx dataset for bulk transcriptome 17,24,25 (Methods and Fig. 1a). 95 

An initial analysis revealed that only a small proportion of organ markers used in existing 96 

clocks were consistent with HPA data, while at least 20% of proteins showed high 97 

expression in other organs (Supplementary Fig. 1b). But we found that markers derived 98 

solely from GTEx constitute approximately 20% of the final usable proteins in both 99 

platforms (Supplementary Fig. 2a). So, we decided to use this bulk-transcriptome data as 100 

a complementary resource after a stringent filter. Ultimately, we integrated a final marker 101 

list encompassing 15 tissues/organs and 5,954 proteins (Fig. 1b). 102 

To further validate these organ enriched markers are associated with the organ 103 

functions, we performed GO term enrichment analysis. The results indicated strong 104 

associations between the marker sets and relevant organ functions. For example, brain 105 

markers were enriched for terms such as modulation of chemical synaptic transmission, 106 

neurotransmitter transport, and regulation of membrane potential. Even for organs with 107 

relatively few markers, such as bone marrow, functionally relevant terms like myeloid cell 108 

homeostasis and erythrocyte homeostasis were significantly enriched (Fig. 1b). 109 

We next investigated proteins exhibiting age-dependent dynamics across platforms 110 

and traced their tissue origins. Linear age-associated proteins were identified by 111 

calculating Pearson correlation coefficients (PCC) between expression levels and age. We 112 

observed that ELN, SCARF2, DPT and SOST were positively correlated with age (Fig. 1c). 113 

Among these, ELN and SCARF2 are core components of a recently published proteomic 114 

aging clock 26 . Also, ELN and its fragment can reduce healthspan and lifespan 27 . DPT has 115 

been shown to ameliorate inflammation in adipose tissue 28 , and interacts with TGF-β, 116 

potentiating its effects. Notably, TGF-β is a key mediator of the senescence-associated 117

Construction of more accurate organ-proxy aging clocks 148 

To quantify the heterogeneity of aging rates across different organs, we constructed organ-149 

proxy aging clocks based on the plasma proteome, following the approach of previous 150 

studies 14-16 , but using the marker list identified in this work. We evaluated seven machine 151 

learning methods, among which LGBM and XGBoost demonstrated superior performance, 152 

particularly for organs with fewer markers (Fig. 3a). Due to its faster training speed (~32 153 

hours vs. ~50.5 hours for XGBoost), LGBM was selected as the final model for building the 154 

aging clocks. During model training, covariates were included for adjustment, and the 155 

Boruta algorithm was applied for feature selection (Methods). We ultimately developed one 156 

conventional aging clock trained on all plasma proteins and 14 organ-proxy clocks. On the 157 

held-out validation set (20% of the total data), 12 of the organ clocks achieved a Pearson 158 

correlation coefficient (PCC) above 0.4, with the exceptions being the Eye, Bone Marrow, 159 

and Oral clocks (Fig. 3b). 160 

Given that our Conventional clock was trained on the full proteome-consistent with 161 

previous studies-we further benchmarked its characteristics against prior models. Using 162 

Elastic Net (EN), Goeminne et al. 15 retained the largest number of features, followed by 163 

Oh et al. 14 , who utilized LASSO regression. In contrast, our model employed LGBM 164 

coupled with the Boruta algorithm for rigorous feature selection, resulting in the most 165 

parsimonious feature set. Notably, nearly all of the markers identified by our approach 166 

constituted a subset of those identified by the other two clocks (Supplementary Fig. 3a). 167 

This refined marker selection might have contributed to the enhanced generalizability to 168 

our approach. 169 

We next compared our organ-proxy aging clocks with previously published models. 170 

As the initial study by Oh et al . used the SomaScan platform 16 , making direct comparisons 171 

challenging, we focused on more recent work by Goeminne et al . and Oh et al .14,15 .172 

Although these studies also relied on the UKB proteomic data, the discrepancies in data 173 

splitting protocols make direct benchmark comparisons (e.g., MAE) problematic due to the 174 

unverifiable risk of data leakage. 175

However, a robust aging clock should also effectively capture variations in biological 176 

age, such as functional decline under disease conditions. To further examine the ability of 177 

these clocks to detect biological aging differences, we leveraged detailed ICD-10 178 

diagnostic records available in the UKB to evaluate associations between organ aging 179 

states and related diseases. 180 

First, we calculated the age-adjusted biological age difference for each organ-proxy 181 

clock (Organ-cAgeDiff, see Methods). Leveraging ICD-10 chapter codes, we mapped 182 

specific diseases to their corresponding target organs. The Conventional was excluded, 183 

because of its lacking of a specific anatomical target. Then, for both prevalent and incident 184 

cases, we employed Logistic or Cox proportional hazards regression models to determine 185 

the Odds Ratios (OR) or Hazard Ratios (HR) for each organ-proxy clocks in relation to 186 

these diseases. Within each disease category, the OR/HR values of all organs were ranked 187 

in descending order. A rank closer to 1 indicates that the corresponding organ-proxy clock 188 

possesses superior diagnostic or predictive power for diseases within that anatomical 189 

domain compared to other organ clocks, thereby validating its effectiveness as an organ-190 

level biological proxy. PAOPAC performed particularly well for Cardiovascular, 191 

Musculoskeletal, Digestive and Brain, while remaining competitive for Immune, Skin, 192 

Endocrine and Respiratory (Fig. 3c). Although PAOPAC included two novel organs: Eye 193 

and Bone marrow, they didn’t show good organ-proxy performance, same for the Urinary 194 

clock in comparison (Supplementary Fig. 3b). Thus, these three organs will be excluded in 195 

future research. 196

effects. We tested clocks using an external dataset comprising plasma samples from 43 198

treatment 41 . Notably, only the Respiratory clock in PAOPAC successfully detected a 200 

significant reduction in biological age within the treatment group compared to the placebo 201 

(Supplementary Fig. 3c). This shift likely reflects a meaningful improvement in pulmonary 202 

physiological integrity. Supporting this interpretation, we observed that reductions in 203

A comprehensive disease-aging relation landscape 211 

While previous studies have primarily focused on a limited set of common diseases-such 212 

as type 2 diabetes, heart failure, and Alzheimer’s disease--a comprehensive analysis of 213 

the relationship between disease and organ aging has been lacking. In this study, we 214 

therefore compared Organ-cAgeDiff between patients and healthy controls across 101 215 

prevalent disease groups and 104 incident disease groups. Our analysis reveals that most 216 

disease categories are not limited to the aging of a single organ but are associated with 217 

accelerated aging across multiple organ-proxies (OR/HR > 1, indicated by red shading in 218 

Fig. 4a). And, Organ-cAgeDiff exhibited a high degree of consistency between prevalent 219 

cases and incident risks (Fig. 4b). This suggests that the Organ-cAgeDiff not only reflects 220 

current pathological states but also possesses prospective predictive value for future 221 

disease onset. 222 

Given the pronounced changes in Brain-cAgeDiff observed across disease states (Fig. 223 

4a, b), we specifically examined diseases within the ‘Diseases of the nervous system’ 224 

chapter. The Cox proportional hazards regression results showed that each additional year 225 

of aging inferred from the Musculoskeletal, Brain, or Conventional clocks significantly 226 

elevated the risk of nervous system diseases (Fig. 4c). 227 

We further investigated the association between organ aging rates and all-cause 228 

mortality. Among the 12 organ-proxy clocks, the Conventional (HR = 1.14, 95% CI: 1.12-229 

1.17), Musculoskeletal (HR = 1.10, 95% CI: 1.09-1.11), and Brain (HR = 1.09, 95% CI: 230 

1.08-1.10) clocks exhibited the highest hazard ratios for mortality (Fig. 4d). To assess the 231 

cumulative impact of extreme aging phenotypes, individuals with an |Organ-cAgeDiff| > 2 232 

SD were categorized as having 'super-old' or 'super-young' organs. We observed a dose-233

Discussion 239 

This study establishes refined plasma proteome–based organ aging clocks by 240 

incorporating organ-specific proteome information, advancing beyond previous 241 

approaches that largely relied on bulk transcriptomic markers and focused on a narrow 242 

spectrum of diseases. By integrating multi-level molecular evidence and data across 243 

platforms, our clocks demonstrated enhanced biological interpretability, clinical relevance, 244 

and the ability to reveal systemic patterns of aging. In particular, our work extends the 245 

scope of prior proteomic aging studies by providing a comprehensive disease–aging 246 

landscape. 247 

Several limitations of this study warrant consideration. First, while we integrated multi-248 

omics datasets to identify organ-enriched markers, the representativeness of these 249 

proteins in plasma as proxies for their respective organs remains to be further refined. 250 

Second, although our study incorporated data from two major proteomic platforms (Olink 251 

and SomaScan), the resulting aging clocks are currently not directly interchangeable. The 252 

technical biases inherent in different affinity-based assays and the challenge of missing 253 

features remain significant hurdles for the clinical application of these models. Overcoming 254 

this limitation will require harmonization strategies or multi-platform training to ensure 255 

generalizability and translational utility. 256

Data quality control, curation, and preprocessing 276 

To account for measurement disagreements stemming from technical differences between 277 

platforms, z-score normalization and quality control were applied separately to the Olink 278 

UKB and SomaScan datasets before any cross-platform comparisons or ensemble 279 

modeling. 280 

For UKB cohort, data with an excessive proportion of missing values were first excluded. 281 

Specifically, any protein that was undetectable in more than 20% of the participants was 282 

classified as a high-missingness protein and removed from the dataset. Similarly, any 283 

individual with more than 20% of proteins undetected in their plasma sample was 284 

considered a low-quality sample and excluded. After this filtering process, data from 44,190 285 

volunteers and 2,911 proteins were retained for subsequent analysis. 286 

According to the Olink platform specifications, the provided measurements represent 287 

Normalized Protein Expression (NPX) values, which reflect relative protein abundance on 288 

a log 2 scale calibrated against internal controls. Since missing values indicate protein 289

Organ enriched marker identification 299 

We initially selected proteins classified as highly expressed in specific organs according to 300 

the HPA. Leveraging advances in single-cell technology, we then obtained cell-specific 301 

markers that were uniquely expressed in particular tissue from the CM2 database. 302 

Concurrently, we incorporated the GTEx dataset as a complementary resource at the bulk 303 

RNA level. Genes showing at least four-fold higher expression in one organ compared to 304 

any other organ were designated as organ-specific markers, by following a previously used 305 

definition 24 . For each candidate marker derived from any single dataset, it was definitively 306 

classified as a marker only if it was not associated with any other organ in both the HPA 307 

and CM2 databases. 308 

Identification of age-associated proteins 309 

For the initial identification of age-associated proteins, to ensure a balanced representation 310 

of age and sex across the study population, we selected a stratified subset of 6,200 311 

volunteers (100 males and 100 females for each year from 40 to 70). The sample size of 312 

100 per subgroup was determined based on the smallest demographic subgroup in the 313 

overall cohort (females aged 70, n=141), resulting in a sampling proportion of 314 

approximately 70%. This approach also had the practical benefit of reducing computational 315 

cost and processing time. 316 

For the identification of linearly age-associated proteins, the average protein expression 317 

level was first calculated for individuals at each age point. The PCC was then computed 318 

between the mean protein expression and age, with significance adjusted using the 319

Construction of PAOPAC 329 

For the UK Biobank cohort, the total of 44,190 volunteers were randomly divided into 330 

training and testing sets at an 8:2 ratio. Prior to model construction, all data were 331 

standardized. A conventional aging clock was built using all plasma proteins as initial input. 332 

In our work, we adopted a probabilistic inference strategy based on publicly available 333 

tissue-expression atlases: if a protein is highly and preferentially expressed in a particular 334 

organ, we assume that, when detected in plasma, it is most likely derived from that tissue. 335 

Organ-proxy aging clocks were trained using only the organ enriched marker proteins as 336 

input. Models for which the final feature set contained fewer than five proteins were 337 

excluded from further analysis. The LASSO, Ridge, and Elastic Net (EN) models were 338 

implemented using the glmnet (v4.1-8) and caret (v6.0-94) packages. A 5-fold cross-339 

validation was applied for each training round. For EN and related methods, 100 penalty 340 

coefficients (nlambda) were tested. The mean absolute error (MAE) was used as the loss 341 

function, and the model with the smallest MAE was selected. Partial Least Squares 342 

Regression (PLSR) and k-Nearest Neighbors (KNN) models were implemented using 343 

the scikit-learn (v1.5.1) package, with hyperparameter optimization performed via 344 

the hyperopt (v0.2.5) package. For PLSR, the number of components was selected from 345 

1 to 20 or the number of overlapping proteins between the organ markers and the platform-346 

detected proteome, whichever was smaller. For KNN, the number of neighbors (k) was 347 

optimized between 5 and 50. Five-fold cross-validation was used for both PLSR and KNN, 348

Calculation and adjustment of biological age 367 

The age predicted by the aging clocks is referred to as the biological age (BA). The 368 

difference between this BA and the chronological age (CA) is defined as the Age Difference 369 

(AgeDiff), which reflects an individual’s rate of aging. A higher AgeDiff indicates accelerated 370 

aging, while a lower value suggests decelerated aging. To correct for bias introduced during 371 

model training, locally estimated scatterplot smoothing (LOESS) regression was applied to 372 

model the relationship between AgeDiff and CA. The fitted values from this regression 373 

represent age-related prediction bias (AgeDiff hat). The corrected biological age (cBA) was 374 

obtained by subtracting AgeDiff hat from the BA. The corrected Age Diff (cAgeDiff) was 375 

then derived by subtracting the CA from cBA. Individuals with an absolute cAgeDiff greater 376 

than two standard deviations were classified as either ‘super-younger’ or ‘super-older’. 377
