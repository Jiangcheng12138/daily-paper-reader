> Abstract Objective.  To examine whether hypoxic burden is distinctly associated with deficits in gray matter pulsation and its coordination with CSF flow, which together support glymphatic activity, independent of respiratory effort-related arousals.
> Background.  OSA is a robust risk factor for multiple neurodegenerative conditions, including Alzheimer’s disease, and this association may be mediated in part by glymphatic activity. However, whether distinct pathological features of OSA, including hypoxic burden and sleep fragmentation, are differentially associated with brain pulsations and their coordination with CSF flow remains unknown.
> Methods.  Twenty-eight individuals with newly identified OSA and 8 non-OSA healthy controls completed either in-lab polysomnography or WatchPAT, providing estimates of time spent below 90% oxygen saturation (T90) and non-hypoxic respiratory effort-related arousals (RERAs). Participants also completed resting-state fMRI on a 7T scanner to quantify gray matter-CSF coupling and vasomotion-frequency (<0.1 Hz) regional pulsation amplitude. Associations between gray matter-CSF coupling and sleep measures were examined using hierarchical regression. Associations between regional pulsation amplitude and hypoxic burden were examined using Pearson correlations.
> Results.  Greater T90 significantly predicted weaker gray matter-CSF coupling after controlling for age, sex, race, and RERAs (Table 2; β=0.003,  p=0.01). Adding T90 also significantly improved model-explained variance in global gray matter-CSF coupling (Table 2; ∆R 2=0.18,  p=0.01). At the ROI level, higher T90 was associated with greater pulsation amplitude in the vasomotion frequency across multiple frontal, temporal, and superior parietal regions, but not the occipital cortex. Among ROIs with T90-linked elevations in pulsation amplitude, greater pulsation amplitude predicted weaker ROI-specific gray matter-CSF coupling in individuals with severe OSA (i.e., AHI4% ≥ 15), after adjustments for RERAs (β=0.0007, p=0.001).  This pattern differed significantly from individuals without severe OSA (Z=2.42,
> p=0.02) and from ROIs without T90-linked elevations in pulsation amplitude.

> Conclusions.  OSA is associated with deficits in fluid dynamics that support glymphatic function, with hypoxic burden, rather than respiratory effort-related sleep fragmentation, emerging as the primary driver. Greater hypoxic burden may weaken gray matter-CSF coupling by reducing compensatory CSF inflow in response to brain pulsations, representing a distinct pathway linking OSA to impaired CSF-mediated waste transport and neurodegenerative disorders.

> 1. Introduction
> Obstructive sleep apnea (OSA) is characterized by recurrent upper airway obstruction that results in intermittent hypoxemia and sleep fragmentation, and it is a disorder that affects nearly one billion adults worldwide. 1 OSA is a robust risk factor for multiple neurodegenerative conditions including Alzheimer’s disease and related dementias (ADRD), 2 and this association may be mediated in part by glymphatic activity. The glymphatic system facilitates the clearance of metabolic waste products - including amyloid beta (Aβ) - via bulk cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) flow through perivascular spaces. 3-8  A key driver of perivascular CSF flow is brain pulsation, defined as rhythmic expansion and contraction of the vasculature and surrounding tissue. 9,10  Brain pulsations in the vasomotion frequency range (<0.1 Hz), reflecting slow oscillations in vascular tone driven by smooth muscle activity, often show the greatest amplitude and have been found to exert the strongest effects on CSF transport, compared to pulsations in higher frequencies. 9,10  Because the brain must maintain constant intracranial volume, blood displacement during each pulsation cycle must be accompanied by reciprocal CSF movement into or out of the cranium, thereby generating the pulsatile fluid motion necessary for solute transport. 11
> Accordingly, vasomotion and CSF inflow have been shown to be tightly coupled in time, thereby facilitating solute transport, 10  while the decoupling of oscillations between brain pulsations and CSF inflow has been linked to ADRD pathology. 12-14  In OSA, this coordination between brain pulsations and CSF flow may be disrupted by hypoxic burden, which induces structural and functional alterations in cerebral arteries and may thereby impair cerebral vasomotion. 10,15-19  Supporting this hypothesis, prior work has shown that individuals with an OSA diagnosis exhibit weaker temporal coupling between CSF inflow and gray matter-wide pulsations in the vasomotion frequency range, with coupling strength decreasing as OSA severity increases, as measured by the apnea-hypopnea index using the 4% desaturation criterion (AHI4%). 20-22

> However, AHI4% reflects a composite of hypoxic burden and respiratory effort-related sleep fragmentation. Thus, the distinct contribution of hypoxic burden to brain pulsations and its coordination with CSF flow beyond non-hypoxic, respiratory effort-related sleep fragmentation, another key pathological feature of OSA, 1 still remains unclear. Gray matter pulsation-CSF flow dynamics may be differentially affected by hypoxic burden, often quantified as time spent under 90% oxygen saturation, and respiratory effort-related sleep fragmentation (RERAs) that do not induce hypoxemia, which can be quantified by the difference between all respiratory and arousal events with hypoxic events. 23,24
> Specifically, hypoxic burden may impair brain pulsations through inducing vascular injury, whereas sleep fragmentation may disrupt the rhythmic vasoconstriction and vasodilation of the cerebrovasculature associated with oscillations in norepinephrine tone. 8,10,25  In addition, another important gap concerns regional variations in the association between hypoxic burden and brain pulsations that support glymphatic activity. Regions with high metabolic demand, such as the frontotemporal cortex, may be especially vulnerable to hypoxic injury, which could translate into greater deficits in brain pulsations and their coordination with CSF flow. To address these gaps, this study first examined the associations of hypoxic burden and RERAs with the coupling strength between gray matter pulsations and CSF flow, placing particular emphasis on whether greater hypoxic burden is distinctively associated with reduced gray matter-CSF coupling independent of non-hypoxic sleep fragmentation. In addition, the study examined the association between hypoxic burden and ROI-specific gray matter pulsation amplitude. From a clinical perspective, these findings can help clarify whether interventions that preferentially reduce hypoxemia, improve sleep continuity, or target both processes are more likely to improve fluid dynamics that support glymphatic activity.

> 2.1. Sample
> This study examined thirty-six (36) individuals aged 55 to 75 years. The sample included 22 adults with a new clinical diagnosis of OSA recruited from the Mount Sinai Integrative Sleep Center and 14 community-dwelling, cognitively normal, healthy, older adults who completed in-lab polysomnography at the Mount Sinai School of Medicine. While the community-recruited subjects did not report any sleep complaints, OSA (AHI4% > 5/hour) was incidentally identified in 6 out of the 14 subjects, resulting in a final sample of 28 individuals with OSA and 8 OSA-free healthy controls. Exclusion criteria included having any self-identified cognitive complaints, any sleep disorders other than OSA, sleep duration of less than 5 hours or greater than 10 hours per night based on actigraphy, any significant neurological or psychiatric disorders, chronic usage of any psychoactive drugs aside from single selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors as antidepressants for a duration over 8 weeks, any unstable comorbid conditions, and metal in the body. All 36 participants met the study’s eligibility criteria.
> 2.2. Diagnostic sleep studies
> Participants completed either an in-laboratory polysomnography or a WatchPAT-based home sleep testing, a clinically validated method for assessing OSA that has shown excellent agreement with in-laboratory polysomnography. 26-28  The procedures for each modality are described below.
> 2.2.a. Polysomnography:  Fourteen participants recruited from the community completed an overnight, attended in-laboratory polysomnography at the Mount Sinai Integrative Sleep Center using Compumedics E-series (Melbourne, Australia). 29  Participants were connected to polysomnography and the lights off time were scheduled to approximate their self-reported habitual bedtime. Signal acquisition included electroencephalography, electro-oculography, electromyography, a nasal cannula, pressure transducer, oral thermistor, rib or abdomen impedance plethysmography, single-channel

All polysomnography recordings were scored by 1 or 2 registered polysomnographic technologists according to the American Academy of Sleep Medicine (AASM) standard criteria for sleep staging, arousals, and respiratory events. 29  Scored sleep studies were reviewed single expert for the consistency of scoring. Based on each participant’s scored sleep study, time spent under 90% oxygen saturation (henceforth referred to as T90) was defined as the cumulative duration in which peripheral oxygen saturation as measured by pulse oximetry remained below 90% (in minutes). Non-hypoxic sleep fragmentation was defined as respiratory effort-related arousals (RERAs). RERAs were calculated by subtracting the apnea-hypopnea index based on the 3% oxygen desaturation criterion (AHI3%) from AHI3%a. AHI3% includes all apneas, defined as an absence of airflow for ≥ 10 seconds, 29  and hypopneas, defined as a reduction in breathing amplitude by 30% or more for ≥ 10 seconds terminating in ≥ 3% decline in blood oxygen saturation or an arousal. 30,31  AHI3%a included AHI3% and event-related arousals. 30,31
> 2.2.b. WatchPAT Home Sleep Testing:  Twenty-two participants newly diagnosed with OSA recruited from the Mount Sinai Sleep Center wore WatchPAT ONEs (Zoll, Germany). 32  WatchPATs measure sleep duration based on a wrist actigraph, snoring and chest breathing excursions using a wireless thoracic sensor with a microphone, and oximetry and peripheral arterial tonometry (PAT) based on a finger probe. 32  Changes in PAT and movement were used to capture increases in sympathetic tone that are linked to respiratory effort-related arousals. 32  Automated respiratory event scoring algorithms were used to score the WatchPAT recordings to derive PAT-based measures of T90, respiratory disturbance index (henceforth referred to as PAT-derived RDI [pRDI], which includes the sum of total apneas, hypopneas, and RERAs), and AHI3%. RERAs for participants who wore WatchPATs were calculated by subtracting AHI3% from pRDI, following the AASM manual and prior research. 23,24  In addition to prior research showing excellent agreement in sleep and respiratory event metrics between in-lab polysomnography

> 2.3. 7T MRI Protocol
> Participants completed 9 to 15 minutes of resting-state functional MRI scan on a 7T scanner (Magnetom, Siemens, Erlangen, Germany) with a 32-channel head coil (repetition time [TR]=1.5 seconds, echo time [TE]=0.025, flip angle [FA]=55°, iPAT acceleration factor=3, bandwidth=33.921/pixel, 1.5 mm isotropic resolution). For the blood oxygen level dependent signal (BOLD) sequence, the bottom slice was aligned to the 4 th  ventricle to capture CSF inflow, consistent with prior research. 33  In addition, a whole-brain T1-weighted gradient echo (MP2RAGE) anatomical images were acquired (TR=4500 ms, TE=3.37 ms, TI1=1000s and 3200 ms, FA=4 and 5°, iPAT acceleration factor=3, bandwidth =130 Hz/pixel, 0.7 mm isotropic resolution).
> 2.3.a. Resting-state brain pulsation measures
> Processing steps for resting-state scans included deobliquing, motion-correcting, skull-stripping, spatial smoothing, grand mean-scaling, detrending of linear and quadratic trends, and registration to the structural scan. Using these processed resting-state scans in participants’ native space,  global  gray matter-CSF coupling was quantified as the cross-correlation of gray matter-wide average BOLD signal timeseries in the vasomotion frequency (i.e., <0.1Hz) with CSF signal timeseries captured at the 4 th
> ventricle at the same frequency. The gray matter-wide average BOLD timeseries were extracted by registering a gray matter mask to the processed resting-state fMRI scans in participants’ native space and averaging signals across the gray matter voxels. Average amplitudes were calculated for each ROI as defined by the Desikan-Killiany atlas. 34
> 2.4. Statistical analysis
> Sample characteristics were examined using means and standard deviations (SDs) for continuous variables and as counts and proportions for categorical variables. Differences in sample characteristics by OSA status were assessed using t-tests for continuous variables and chi-square tests for categorical variables. The primary analysis examined the variance in global gray matter-CSF coupling attributable to T90 using hierarchical linear regression. Here, gray matter-CSF coupling was modeled as a function of T90, non-hypoxic RERAs, age, sex, and race. Statistical significance of T90 and the incremental variance explained by its inclusion were evaluated using the change in R 2. Given that sleep was assessed using two different devices, we evaluated the sensitivity of the results to device type by additionally including device × T90 and device × RERA interaction terms in the model.

> 3. Results
> Sample characteristics can be found in Table 1. Mean age was 66.3 years (standard deviation [SD] = 5.5), 58.3% of participants were female, and 66.7% were White. Average Montreal Cognitive Assessment score was 26.9 (SD = 2.8), and mean body mass index was 30.3 (SD = 5.5). On average, participants spent 23.4 minutes with oxygen saturation below 90% (SD = 46.3), had an AHI4% of 26.3 events per hour (SD = 21.6), and experienced 2.1 RERAs per hour (SD = 2.1). Average sleep duration was 385.1 minutes (SD=63.3). The majority of characteristics were comparable between the two groups, although the OSA group had a lower proportion of female participants, a higher mean BMI, and greater OSA severity, as reflected by higher AHI4% and T90, but not RERAs. > 4. Discussion
> The primary motivation of the current study was to investigate whether two distinct pathological mechanisms of OSA, namely hypoxic burden and sleep fragmentation, are differentially associated with the drivers of glymphatic activity. We hypothesized that hypoxic burden would be distinctively associated with brain pulsations and their coordination with CSF flow, given that hypoxemia can induce vascular injury, disrupting rhythmic movements of the cerebrovasculature which induce bulk CSF flow. 10,35
> Supporting this hypothesis, greater hypoxic burden was robustly associated with weaker coupling between gray matter pulsations and CSF inflow, independent of non-hypoxic sleep fragmentation. Coordination between vasomotion and CSF flow has been shown to be a critical driver of CSF movement, CSF-ISF exchange, and solute transport within the glymphatic system. 10,35  Our study extends this literature by suggesting that hypoxic burden in OSA may play a unique role in impairing fluid dynamics that support glymphatic activity, which may in turn explain the elevated risk of ADRD in this population. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first study to disentangle the contribution of hypoxic burden to coordination between brain pulsations and CSF flow beyond that of non-hypoxic sleep fragmentation. Furthermore, this study found evidence suggesting that the association between greater hypoxic burden and weaker gray matter-CSF coupling may arise, at least in part, from reduced compensatory CSF inflow in response to brain pulsations. Specifically, ROI-level analyses were conducted to examine the association between hypoxic burden and region-specific gray matter pulsation amplitude in the vasomotion frequency range, which have been shown to facilitate bulk CSF movement necessary for solute transport. 9,35  Contrary to our expectations, greater hypoxic burden was associated with stronger

> gray matter pulsation amplitudes across multiple subregions of the frontal, temporal, and superior parietal cortices, but not in the occipital cortex. Particularly large effect sizes were found in the frontal ROIs, which are highly metabolically demanding, 36  and may have heightened vulnerability to hypoxic burden in OSA. Importantly, however, elevated pulsation amplitude in these regions did not appear to translate into stronger coupling with CSF inflow. Within the frontal, temporal, and parietal ROIs showing T90-linked elevations in pulsation amplitude, greater pulsation amplitude was not associated with stronger regional gray matter-CSF coupling among individuals with severe OSA. This pattern contrasts with the expected relationship under the Monro-Kellie doctrine, in which greater brain pulsations are tightly locked in time to larger-magnitude CSF movement to maintain constant intracranial volume. 11  Thus, the lack of stronger regional gray matter-CSF coupling despite elevated pulsation amplitude suggests insufficient compensatory CSF inflow in the ROIs with T90-linked pulsation increases. In contrast, among regions without T90-linked elevations in pulsation amplitude, greater pulsation amplitude predicted stronger regional gray matter-CSF coupling among both individuals with and without severe OSA, consistent with prior findings in healthy individuals and the Monro-Kellie doctrine. 9,11,35  Together, these findings suggest that hypoxic burden may increase regional pulsation amplitude while disrupting its coordination with CSF inflow. Hypoxic injury to the vasculature is known to promote vascular stiffening, 37  which may mediate these relationships. Future research is needed to directly test the underlying mechanisms of the observed findings. Finally, the association between hypoxic burden and ROI-specific brain pulsation amplitude showed a pattern of right-hemispheric predominance. > Respiratory events during sleep activate the sympathetic nervous system, increasing cortisol and physiological stress, all of which have been linked to right-lateralized involvement in central and autonomic stress regulation. 39,40  Our findings extend this work by suggesting that hypoxic burden may contribute to greater right-hemispheric abnormalities in brain pulsation dynamics, which may in turn contribute to deficits in glymphatic activity. This study has multiple strengths. First, it distinguishes the association of gray matter-CSF coupling with hypoxic burden from that with non-hypoxic sleep fragmentation using objective, validated sleep measures. Second, the sample included racially diverse individuals, improving generalizability to the population experiencing OSA. Third, MRI was conducted at 7T, providing improved resolution that can help enhance registration and gray matter segmentation. Fourth, the OSA group consists of individuals newly diagnosed with OSA, precluding potential confounding by OSA treatment. However, this study is not without limitations. First, gray matter pulsations and their temporal coupling to CSF flow were not assessed during sleep, when glymphatic activity is thought to be most active. 10,41,42
> Nonetheless, growing evidence suggests that the glymphatic system is also active during wakefulness, and wake-based measurements may still provide meaningful information about abnormalities in brain pulsation and CSF flow dynamics relevant to the glymphatic system among individuals with OSA. Second, sleep assessments were conducted using different modalities. However, prior studies, together with our supplementary data, show that WatchPAT demonstrates excellent agreement with in-lab polysomnography. 26-28  Third, our study included a modest sample size, and the findings should be validated in a larger cohort. > 4.1. Conclusions
> The current study shows that OSA is associated with deficits in fluid dynamics that support glymphatic function, with hypoxic burden, rather than respiratory effort-related sleep fragmentation, as the primary driver. This association between greater hypoxic burden and weaker gray matter-CSF coupling may arise, at least in part, from reduced compensatory CSF inflow in response to brain pulsations. OSA is a risk factor for AD and related dementias, and these findings suggest that hypoxic burden may represent a distinct pathway through which OSA contributes to impaired CSF-mediated waste transport and, in turn, ADRD.
