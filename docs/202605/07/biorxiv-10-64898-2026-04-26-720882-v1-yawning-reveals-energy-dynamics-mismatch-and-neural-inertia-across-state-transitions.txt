Abstract 37 

Yawning is a highly conserved behavior, yet its neural dynamics across arousal state transitions 38 

remain poorly understood. Classical reflex models fail to account for the precise timing of yawns 39 

during large -scale changes in brain network organization. Her e, we investigated the neural 40 

signatures of yawning using simultaneous electroencephalography (EEG), electromyography 41 

(EMG), and kinematic recordings in a beagle model during wakefulness and propofol anesthesia. 42 

Propofol -associated yawning exhibited marked temporal asymmetry, with 89.2% of events 43 

occurring during emergence from anesthesia. To resolve the fast neural dynamics surrounding 44 

these events, we applied empirical mode decomposition coupled with the Hilbert –Huang transform 45 

(EMD -HHT) analyses. Across induction, recovery, and wakefulness, yawning was consistently 46 

associated with a transient decoupling between local and global network dynamics: a brief increase 47 

in local γ -band power coincided with a drop in network flexibility, quantified by instantaneou s48 

frequency volatility; IFV). We termed this reproducible motif as "Energy -Dynamics Mismatch" 49 

(EDM). A machine learning classifier leveraging EDM features reliably decoded the brain state 50 

immediately preceding yawning. We further validated the presence of this signature in an 51 

independent human cohort during anesthesia induction. These findings indicate that yawning is not 52 

a passive reflex, but is tightly linked to moments of network instability during state transitions. From 53 

a computational perspective, EDG may reflect a transient "control effort" that preserves information 54 

integration in the presence of "neural inertia." This dynamic signature may provide a quantitative 55 

biomarker for monitoring arousal transitions brain -computer interface (BCI) interface st ate -alerting. 56 

Significance Statement 57 

Yawning is a ubiquitous and evolutionarily conserved behavior, yet its neural basis remain s elusive. 58 

Using high -resolution neural recordings in a cross -species model, we show that yawning is not a 59 

passive reflex, but re liably coincides with a distinct, transient brain -state signature. Specifically, 60 

yawning occurs when local high -frequency cortical activity briefly increases while global network 61 

flexibility decreases – a pattern we term an "Energy -Dynamics Mismatch" (EDM) . We propose that 62 

EDM reflects a compensatory neural process engaged during moments of network instability, 63 

consistent with overcoming neural inertia to preserve information integration. By identifying this 64 

conserved neural marker , this work advances the biological understanding of yawning and 65 

suggests a potential biomarker for detecting critical brain states in future BCI .66

Introduction 68 

69 

Yawning is an evolutionarily conserved physiological behavior observed across vertebrate species 70 

(1, 2) . It frequently occurs during transitions in brain state, particularly across the sleep -wake cycle 71 

(3) and changes in anesthesia depth (4) . Classic theories have primarily attributed yawning to brain 72 

thermoregulation (5) or airway homeostasis reflexes (6) . However, these accounts struggle to 73 

explain the remarkably precise timing of yawns during narrow windows of neuronal state transition. 74 

Contemporary neuroimaging research predominantly focuses on activation patterns of 75 

macroscopic brain regions (7, 8) . As a result, it lacks the temporal resolution necessary to resolve 76 

millisecond -scale neural processes that precede spontaneous physiological behaviors such as 77 

yawning. To overcome this , w e employed a propofol -induced anesthesia –emergence paradigm, 78 

which offers a well -controlled and highly reproducible model of transient brain -state transitions and 79 

graded levels of consciousness. Propofol -induced state transitions exhibit an asymmetric 80 

resistance between induction and recovery, known as 'neural inertia' (9, 10) . Despite its robust 81 

macroscopic characterization, it remains unclear how this macroscopic pharmacodynamic 82 

phenomenon maps onto microscopic brain network dynamics. In particu lar, whether the neuronal 83 

microstates preceding yawning reflect an active attempt by the brain to overcome this hysteretic 84 

resistance has not been empirically demonstrated. 85 

Here, we hypothesize that yawning reflect s latent neuronal computations that occur as the brain 86 

network negotiates transitions across different levels of consciousness. Cortical energy measures 87 

(e.g., γ power) (11) and network dynamic metrics (e.g., instantaneous frequency volatility, IFV) (12) 88

capacity to explore and switch between functional state s (13, 14) . IFV offers a quantitative measure 92

Accord ingly, we define IFV as a metric of "microscale neuronal variability," indirectly reflecting the 94

By integrating cordical energy and network flexibility within a unified observational framework, 96 

we i dentified a significant dissociation between these two elements in the microscopic time window 97 

preceding yawning across multiple arousal states. Specifically, cortical energy increased while 98 

network flexibility simultaneously decreased, indicating a transi ent internal conflict within the 99 

system. We term this phenomenon "Energy -Dynamics Mismatch" (EDM). This mismatch can be 100 

conceptualized as a form of neural "control effort" deployed in response to macro -state conflicts 101 

(17, 18) .102 

To test this theory, we developed a standardized anesthetic state transition model in beagle dogs 103 

and simultaneously recorded multimodal neurophysiological a nd high -precision kinematic signals. 104 

Compared with rodents, beagles possess more developed cortical architecture and network 105 

dynamics that more closely approximate those of humans. We observed a pronounced temporal 106 

asymmetry in propofol -induced yawning, wi th strong clustering during the anesthesia recovery 107 

period. Using high -resolution nonlinear time –frequency analysis, we characterized the underlying 108 

microscopic dynamics. Across all conditions, including deep anesthesia, delayed recovery, and 109 

normal wakefu lness, yawning was consistently preceded by an increase in local cortical γ power 110 

alongside a reduction in network flexibility, as measured by instantaneous frequency volatility (IFV). 111 

These findings indicate that EDM represents a universal microstate feat ure preceding yawning. 112 

Notably, this mismatch persisted even when the specific intrinsic mode function (IMF) associated 113 

with reduced flexibility shifted its center frequency to align with global brain rhythms, such as 114 

anesthesia -induced slowing (19) . We subsequently verified this dynamic decoupling pattern in an 115 

independent human cohort, demonstrating cross -species generalizability. 116 

Together, these findings redefine the neural precursor states associated with yawning. This 117 

process provides an objective, high -resolution quantitative framework for understanding the 118 

contextual and computational underpinnings of this evolutionarily conser ved behavior. Beyond 119 

mechanistic insight, EDM may serve as a novel biomarker of transitional brain states and offers a 120 

potential foundation for future applications, including BCI early warning systems based on 121 

microscopic dynamic tension. 122

Neural Oscillatory Reconfiguration and State -Space Trajectories in Propofol -Induced 167 

Anesthesia. We used a longitudinal, multi -cycle repeated -measures paradigm (n = 6; Fig. 2A ).168 

Following electrode implementation and recovery, each animal underwent three standardized 169 

anesthesia -emergence cycles, yielding 36 anesthesia recordings and 96 anesthesia -rel ated 170 

yawning events. This design ensured strong statistical power while minimizing the 171 

neuropharmacological impact of drug accumulation. 172 

Intravenous propofol infusion rapidly induced anesthesia, as evidenced by a marked reduction 173 

in electromyography (EMG) amplitude (Fig. 2B , top right). In the time -frequency domain, global 174 

brain oscillatory patterns were substantially reconfigured (Fig. 2B , bottom right). Low -frequency δ 175 

(1 –4 Hz) and α (8 –13 Hz) power increased throughout induction (0 –100 s), whereas high -frequency 176 

γ (30 –40 Hz) power decreased. These changes were uniform across cortical regions, remained 177 

stable during the maintenance phase, and gradually returned to waking levels during recovery. 178 

To better characterize the continuous evolution of brain state s, we constructed a179 

multidimensional neural state -space based on power spectral density across five canonical 180 

frequency bands. For trajectory quantification, wakefulness and loss of response (LOR) were 181 

defined as reference anchors (Fig. 2C ). Principal comp onent analysis (PCA) was used to project 182 

the original 80 -dimensional feature space onto a 3-dimensional subspace, revealing highly 183 

structured state trajectories. Anesthetic states converged markedly into a compact region near the 184 

LOR centroid, whereas wakeful states were more topologically distributed (Fig. 2D ). The empirical 185 

dataset provided topological su pport for both this "funnel -like" convergence and the nonlinear 186 

dynamics of state transitions (Fig. S1B ). Notably, although the recovery trajectory (green) 187 

eventually returned to the wakefulness region, it remained spatially distinct from the induction pat h188 

(blue). This reveals a pronounced state -space imbalance across the anesthesia -emergence 189 

process. Such hysteresis in network evolution trajectories provides a macro -electrophysiological 190 

representation of "neural inertia". 191

instantaneous neuronal state and the LOR centroid. The resulting distance time -series was 193 

subsequently fitted using generalized additive models (GAM) (Fig. 2E ). Three key observations 194

Unsupervised Learning -Based Deconstruction of Yawning Neural Phenotypes and State -204 

Manifold Mapping. To more finely dissect the micro -neural dynamics associated with yawning, we 205 

applied unsupervised spectral phenotypic classification. We extracted 64 -dimensional feature 206 

vectors from 1 6 EEG channels, comprising global absolute power in the δ (1 –4 Hz) and γ (30 –45 207 

Hz) bands, as well as their temporal changes before and after yawning. K -means clustering 208 

combined with UMAP dimensionality reduction revealed that the neurodynamic backgrounds of 209 

yawning segregated into four topologically distinct clusters (Clusters 0 –3; Fig. 3A). 210 

Average spectrograms across frontal, parietal, temporal, and occipital cortical regions revealed 211 

distinct spectral evolution patterns for each cluster (Fig. 3B ). Befo re and after yawning, Cluster 0, 212 

the most prevalent phenotype (53.8% of all events; Fig. 3C ), consistently exhibited low δ power 213 

and high -frequency γ activity. This pattern suggests occurrence in a state approximating the 214 

wakeful baseline during late recov ery. In contrast, Cluster 2 (10.8%) displayed a characteristic 215 

induction -phase collapse, marked by a simultaneous surge in δ power and a pronounced reduction 216 

in γ activity, indicative of a rapid descent into deep anesthesia. The opposite progression was 217 

ob served in Cluster 3 (15.1%), which showed decreasing δ power alongside recovery of γ activity, 218 

consistent with early recovery dynamics. Cluster 1 (20.4%) was dominated by high δ and low γ 219 

power, reflecting the macro -electrophysiological signature of deep a nesthesia. The occurrence of 220 

yawning under such pronounced suppression suggests a hidden micro -dynamic transition, implying 221 

that beneath an apparently stable inhibitory state, the cortex may undergo spontaneous 222 

reconfiguration. 223 

Mapping these four clusters into the 3D neural state manifold revealed that yawning events 224 

closely follow δ and γ topological organization. Cluster 2 aligned with the descending induction 225 

trajectory, while Cluster 1 localized to the manifold base within a deeply metastable inhibited region. 226 

Along the ascending recovery trajectory, Clusters 0 and 3 progressed upward toward wakefulness. 227 

The statistical stability of these distributions was confirmed using linear mixed -effects models 228 

(LMM). For absolute power measures, the model revealed a highly significant interaction between 229 

cortical regions and clusters (P < 0.001). In contrast, spatial interactions for changes in relative 230 

power (Δδ and Δγ) were either not significant or only marginally significant (P = 0.413 and P = 231 

0.063, respectivel y; Figs. 3E –H). Together, this distinct spectral segregation and geometric 232 

localization indicate that state -dependent yawning reflects more than a nonspecific physiological 233 

response activity. Instead, as the brain network transitions between states of cons ciousness, 234 

yawning appears to mark a specific and reproducible pattern of neurodynamic reconfiguration. 235

Wakefulness. We expanded our investigation to yawnin g during natural wakefulness to determine 318 

whether similar network mismatches emerge during normal physiological behavior. To exclude 319 

residual anesthetic effects, EEG recordings were initiated seven days after the last propofol dose 320 

(Fig. 6A ). During contin uous behavioral monitoring, we collected 100 instances of spontaneous 321 

yawning. Analysis revealed a significant increase in high -frequency cortical activity preceding 322 

yawning onset. Time -resolved examination showed that γ -band (30 –45 Hz) power remained 323 

elev ated above baseline prior to yawning, peaking at approximately − 10 s before gradually 324 

declining as the yawn itself approached (Fig. 6B ). We also calculated total γ energy over the 30 -325 

second pre -yawn interval, which demonstrated a highly significant increas e. Compared to the 326 

precisely matched non -yawning control periods, this change was significantly different (Fig. 6C , P 327 

< 0.001). 328 

In contrast, the instantaneous frequency volatility (IFV) decreased concurrently with rising high -329 

frequency power. This separati on of dynamics is clearly illustrated in the tracking plots (Fig. 6D ). 330 

Before a yawn, IFV of the first intrinsic mode function (IMF1 -IFV) remained consistently lower than 331 

baseline and lower than the more flexible oscillatory dynamics observed during contro l periods. As 332 

yawning onset approached, IMF1 -IFV dropped very quickly. Quantitative analysis confirmed a 333 

highly significant overall reduction in IMF1 -IFV across the entire pre -yawning window (Fig. 6E , P < 334 

0.001), reflecting a marked decrease in intrinsic local network flexibility. 335

Discussion 353 

354 

We investigated the spatiotemporal dynamics of yawning across different arousal states. Our 355 

results show that yawning is not a simple, uniform brain activity (20) . Instead, it consistently 356

coincides with a specific micro -dynamic decoupling process in the brain. In this process, local 357 

cortical energy increases rapidly (e .g ., high -frequency γ activity), while network flexibility 358

Propofol -induced yawning exhibited marked asymmetry on a macro -temporal scale. Specifically, 362 

89.2% of yawning events clustered during the recovery phase. In pharmacology, the difference in 363 

drug levels required to induce versus regain consciousness is referred to as "neural inertia" (21, 364 

22) . During recovery, residual anesthetic effects compete with endogenous arousal drive, 365 

prolonging the transition window (23) . The clustered yawning events observed during this phase 366 

likely reflect periods of intense macroscopic reorg anization as the system overcomes this neural 367 

inertia. 368 

Our micro -dynamic neural features, extracted using EMD -HHT , provide quantitative insight into 369 

these macro -state conflicts. Traditional EEG studies often associate yawning with increases in slow 370 

wave activity and suppression in the α -rhythm (24) . In contrast, EMD -HHT capture s quick, transient 371 

chang es by breaking down signals into intrinsic modes and estimating instantaneous frequency 372 

(25, 26) . Using this approach, we revealed p reviously hidden pre -yawning dynamics that remain 373 

consistent with established signatures of low arousal (27) . Specifically, we observed rapid 374 

increases in γ power and lower flexibility (lower IMF -IFV) in the brain just before a yawn starts. 375 

Previous studies linked γ-band activity and high energy needs of local neurons (28, 29) . The 376 

simultaneous increase in γ power and the reduction in flexibility may therefore reflect elevated 377 

energetic requirements for information processing (30, 31) , consistent with the theoretical concept 378 

of "control effort." Importantly, time -resolved trajectories (Fig. 5 and 6) demonstrate that these high -379 

frequency changes are not EMG artifacts. Cortical γ power consistently declined before the actual 380 

physical movement began, confirming that the elevated energy precedes motor execution. 381 

We further observed that the intrinsic modes (IMFs) carrying transient flexibility suppression 382 

exhibited a state -dependent frequency shift. Specifically, this "decoupling" moves to the highest 383 

frequency mode (IMF1) when th e animal is awake, but during anesthesia induction and recovery, 384 

the second high -frequency mode (IMF2) is more prominent. Interestingly, low -frequency waves 385 

have been shown to become the main part of brain activity when high -frequency activity is 386 

suppresse d (32, 33) . On the other hand, the bra in naturally has more activity and higher -frequency 387 

waves when it is very alert and awake (34, 35) . So, this clear shift in frequency bands shows how 388 

the network always adjusts its main wave mode under different levels of wakefulness. 389 

Together, these findings provide a new framework for understanding yawning as a conserved, 390 

cross -species behav ior. In contrast to traditional theories that frame yawning as a passive 391 

byproduct of sleepiness or thermoregulation (36) , our data indicate that the pre -yawning brain state 392 

reflects an active conf lict during large -scale state reorganization. Although yawning is classically 393 

associated with brainstem and hypothalamic structures such as the paraventricular nucleus (PVN) 394 

(37, 38) , transitions between arousal states involve whole -brain network dynamics (39, 40) . The 395 

destabilization and reorganization of cortical dynamics frequently precede or accompany the 396 

release of subcortical primal circuits (41, 42) . As local cortical regions exhibit increased energetic 397 

demand during periods of reduced global control (43) , dynamic decoupling emerges across 398 

hierarchical levels. Thus, yawning reflects a specific behavioral manifestation of constrained 399 

network flexibility rather than a simple reflex. 400 

Finding these hidden brain patterns was enabled by the pharmacologica l model and the use of 401 

rigorously matched baselines. Anesthetic manipulation amplified resistance during state 402 

transitions, facilitating detection of otherwise subtle dynamics (44, 45) . Importantly, EDM was 403 

replicated in both dogs and an independent human cohort, supporting its potential role as a 404 

conserved mammalian signatu re of state transition dynamics (46) . Clinically, conventional 405 

EEG ‑based monitoring tools that rely on slow ‑wave metrics often det ect state transitions only after 406 

substantial reorganization has occurred (47) . Our findings suggest that increases in local energy 407 

demand coupled with abrupt reductions in network flexibility (IMF ‑IFV) may precede macroscopic 408 

EEG changes, offering a potential early indicator of network instability. 409

Experimental Model and Data Acquisition. We performed longitudinal recordings of epidural 422 

EEG and intramuscular masseter EMG in six beagle dogs during propofol -induced state transitions. 423 

Yawning behavior was objectively identified using markerless kinemati c tracking (DeepLabCut). To 424

volatility (IFV). Pre -yawn microstates were classified using a Random Forest algorithm with cross -433 

validated feature importan ce. 434
