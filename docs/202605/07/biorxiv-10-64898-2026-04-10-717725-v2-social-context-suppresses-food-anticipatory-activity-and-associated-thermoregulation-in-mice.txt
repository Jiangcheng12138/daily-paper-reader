Thermoregulation in Mice 23Audrey I. Paik 1, Jacqueline R. Trzeciak 1, Cameron Harrington 1, and Andrew D. Steele 1*41Department of Biological Sciences, California State Polytechnic University Pomona; 5Pomona, CA; USA 6*Corresponding author adsteele@cpp.edu 7

Abstract 8Food anticipatory activity (FAA) is a robust behavioral output of food-entrained circadian 9rhythms, characterized by increased locomotor activity prior to scheduled feeding. 10 Despite the social nature of rodents, FAA is almost exclusively studied in singly housed 11 animals, leaving the influence of social context largely unexplored. Here, we used 12 implanted wireless devices to measure individual locomotor activity and subcutaneous 13 body temperature in group-housed mice and compared these measures to singly 14 housed controls. Social housing significantly suppressed FAA in both male and female 15 mice. In parallel, preprandial increases in body temperature were markedly attenuated 16 in group-housed animals. These findings demonstrate that FAA is a flexible, state-17 dependent behavior that reflects both circadian timing and energetic demand. Together, 18 these results identify social context as an important and underappreciated determinant 19 of food-entrained circadian biology. 20

Introduction 26 Circadian rhythms are intrinsic, self-sustaining oscillations with a period of 27 approximately 24 hours that regulate diverse physiological and behavioral processes, 28 including sleep, temperature regulation, metabolism, and activity. 1,2 These rhythms are 29 synchronized to the external environment by zeitgebers, or time cues, the most 30 prominent of which is the light–dark (LD) cycle. However, food availability is also a 31 potent zeitgeber, capable of entraining circadian rhythms in both central and peripheral 32 tissues, particularly under time-restricted feeding (TRF) or timed, calorie-restricted 33 conditions. 3,4 34 One of the most striking behavioral consequences of food-based entrainment is 35 food anticipatory activity (FAA), a circadian-controlled state characterized by a strong 36 increase in locomotor activity and a synchronized pre-prandial rise in core body 37 temperature that occurs in the hours preceding scheduled feeding. 5,6 FAA is the output 38 of the food-entrainable oscillator (FEO), a timing system that is traditionally studied by 39 feeding mice during the daytime when they are normally inactive. 7,8 Notably, FAA 40 manifests in both the light and dark cycles and possesses unique timing properties, 41 including specific limits of entrainment and independence from known core clock 42 components. 9-11 FAA persists even in constant darkness and in animals lacking a 43 functional suprachiasmatic nucleus (SCN), suggesting the presence of a separate and 44 potentially decentralized FEO network. 12-15 Unlike the relatively rigid hierarchy of light-45 based entrainment centered in the SCN, the system governing food anticipation 46 appears to be highly distributed across multiple brain regions, 12-14 potentially allowing 47

Discussion 239 Using implanted sensors to obtain individual-level activity and body temperature 240 measurements in group-housed animals, we show that social housing robustly 241 attenuates FAA and blunts preprandial increases in body temperature. This suppression 242 was observed in both sexes, with the strongest effect in male mice. These findings 243 identify social context as a powerful and underappreciated modulator of food-entrained 244 circadian behavior. 245 FAA has traditionally been viewed as a robust circadian phenomenon driven by a 246 FEO operating independently of the light-entrained suprachiasmatic nucleus. 5,6,9 247 However, nearly all prior studies examining FAA have been conducted in singly housed 248 animals implicitly assuming that FAA magnitude and expression are invariant across 249 social environments. 8 Our results challenge this assumption. Group housing 250 substantially reduced anticipatory locomotor activity without abolishing it, indicating that 251 while food-based entrainment persists, its behavioral expression is strongly shaped by 252 social context. 253

The interpretation of these findings is informed by prior work demonstrating that 254 locomotor and thermal components of FAA can be differentially regulated. For example, 255 preprandial increases in body temperature can be observed under conditions where 256 locomotor anticipatory activity is reduced or absent, and conversely, behavioral 257 anticipation can occur with attenuated temperature rhythms. 23,27,28 These observations 258 indicate that FAA is not an output of a single oscillator, but instead reflects the 259 coordinated activity of multiple food-entrainable oscillators that regulate distinct 260 physiological and behavioral processes. In this context, the parallel suppression of both 261 activity and temperature observed under social housing suggests a coordinated shift in 262 underlying physiological state rather than a simple attenuation of motor output. 263 A key insight from this study is that the effects of social housing on anticipatory 264 behavior are paralleled by changes in body temperature rhythms. Single-housed mice 265 exhibited robust preprandial increases in temperature, whereas group-housed mice 266 showed markedly attenuated temperature responses. Because body temperature is 267 tightly coupled with metabolic rate and energy expenditure, 29-31 these findings suggest 268 that FAA is not solely a behavioral readout of circadian timing, but also reflects 269 underlying physiological state. Consistent with this, preprandial increases in body 270 temperature are a well-established feature of FAA. 6,8,9 271 One plausible mechanism linking social housing to these effects is altered 272 thermoregulatory demand. Standard laboratory temperatures (~20–24 °C) fall below the 273 murine thermoneutral zone (~29–32 °C), placing singly housed mice under chronic cold 274 stress. 31,32 Under these conditions, animals must increase metabolic heat production to 275 maintain core temperature. 33 In contrast, group-housed mice benefit from social 276

Mouse strains and husbandry 367 

368 C57BL/6J mice (Jackson Laboratory, stock #000664) were used for all experiments. 369 Male and female mice were 11–14 weeks of age at the start of restricted feeding. 370 Animals were housed in a temperature-controlled vivarium (~22 °C) under a 12:12 h 371 light-dark cycle (lights on at zeitgeber time [ZT] 0, lights off at ZT12) with ad libitum 372 access to water. 373 Following recovery from surgery (see below), mice were assigned to either single 374 housing or group housing conditions. Each group-housed cage contained four mice (two 375 implanted and two non-implanted controls). Group-housed mice were maintained in 376 larger cages (46 × 19 × 15 cm) with four mice per cage as were singly housed mice 377 described in Figures 2 and 3. For the mice described in Figure 1, we compared single 378 housed mice in standard cages (26 x 18 x 12.5 cm) versus large cages. All mice were 379 acclimated to their housing conditions for approximately two weeks prior to the start of 380 restricted feeding. 381

Nanotag implantation surgery 388 Nanotags were obtained from Stoelting and decontaminated with ethylene oxide gas. 389 Mice were anesthetized using isoflurane anesthesia and then a nanotag was implanted 390 subcutaneously through a 2-cm incision on the dorsal midline. Once implanted, 391 incisions were stapled, and the mice were returned to individual housing for recovery, 392 receiving perioperative care in accordance with institutional guidelines. Statistical testing 406 407 All statistical analyses were performed using GraphPad Prism (version 10.0; GraphPad 408 Software). Data are presented as mean ± SEM. Statistical significance was defined a 409 priori as p < 0.05. For comparisons involving repeated measurements across time (e.g., 410 body weight, food intake, locomotor activity, and temperature across days or Zeitgeber 411 time), two-way ANOVA or mixed-effects models (restricted maximum likelihood) were 412 used, as appropriate. Mixed-effects models were applied in cases of missing values or 413 unequal sample sizes. Significant main effects or interactions were followed by Šídák’s 414 multiple comparisons tests. For analyses of circadian profiles (e.g., 24-hour activity and 415 temperature traces), two-way ANOVA with factors of time and housing condition (or 416 cage size) was used. For single time point comparisons between two groups, unpaired 417 two-tailed Student’s t-tests were used. Welch’s correction was applied when variances 418 were unequal. For categorical data (e.g., incidence of hypothermic events), Fisher’s 419 exact test was used. Where appropriate, repeated-measures two-way ANOVA was 420 used for longitudinal datasets with complete observations. For group-housed conditions, 421 individual animals were treated as the unit of analysis, as nanotag recordings enabled 422 continuous, individual-level measurements independent of cage-level aggregation. 423 424 425 426
