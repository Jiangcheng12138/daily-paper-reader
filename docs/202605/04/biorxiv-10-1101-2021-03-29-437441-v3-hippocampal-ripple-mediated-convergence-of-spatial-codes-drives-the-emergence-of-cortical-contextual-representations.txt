Abstract 37 

Hippocampal sharp -wave ripples (SWRs) and the associated replay of neuronal sequences are well -38 

established as the primary physiological substrates for memory consolidation. While the local 39 

transformations the y induce within the hippocampus are well -characterized, the ir  impact on the 40 

downstream  cortical representations  remain s unknown. Here, we identify a distinct functional class 41 

of neurons in the anterior cingulate cortex “spatial context cells” that emerge through the multi -day 42 

integration of discrete hippocampal inputs. Using  closed -loop  disruption  of SWRs  and  longitudinal 43 

calcium imaging, we demonstrate that formation of this global spatial framework depend s on SWR s44 

during offline periods. Whereas standard cortical place maps remain intact following SWR 45 

disruption, the emergence of spatial context cells is selectively abolished. These cells preferentially 46 

express c -Fos, identifying them as constituents of the cortical memory engram, and exhibit broad 47 

receptive fields that develop through an experience -dependent process of field convergence. 48 

Collectively, these findings establish SWR -mediated replay as a computational bridge transform ing 49 

high -resolution, fragmented hippocampal spatial codes into generalized, stable cortical schemas. 50 

This work reveals a bifurcated consolidation process, identifying SWR -dependent synaptic 51 

remodeling as a fundamental mechanism for the ontogeny of remote memory. 52

The stabilization of episodic memories relies on a systems -level dialogue between the 54 

hippocampus and the neocortex. Central to this process are hippocampal sharp -wave ripples 55 

(SWRs), high -frequency oscillations that occur during sleep and quiet wakefulness. These transient 56 

events are known to temporally compress and replay sequences  of neuronal firing  that  occurred 57 

during awake period s 1,2 , a mechanism theorized to be the essential driver for memory consolidation 58 

> 3,4

. While the local consequences of SWRs within the hippocampus have been  characterized 59 

extensively  5,6 , demonstrating that these events reshape local ensemble dynamics to refine internal 60 

spatial  representations 7, most  current models are limited by a hippocampal -centric perspective. 61 

Although we recognize SWRs as essential for long -term memory, the precise downstream 62 

mechanisms by which they broadcast and transform information to the neocortex , thereby shap ing 63 

cortical representation s, remain poorly understood. This gap in our knowledge, the lack of a 64 

cellular -level description of how hippocampal replay sculpts cortical circuitry, is a significant 65 

barrier to understanding systems consolidation. While prevailing theories posit that the neocortex 66 

synthesizes generalized environmental "schemas" from hippocampal experience  8,9 , it remains 67 

unclear whether cortical representations are merely passive reflections of hippocampal place fields 68 

or unique functional entities driven by SWR -dependent plasticity. Understanding how the discrete, 69 

high -resolution hippocampal "pixels" of space are transformed into the unified, large -scale 70 

contextual representations that characterize remote memories is essential to resolving how the 71 

cortex transcends its role as a passive repository to become the active orchestrator of remote, 72 

contextual memory. 73 

To address this, we employed a closed -loop system for the real -time disruption of  offline 74 

SWRs  during sleep and quiet wakefulness 3,6,10 , coupled with longitudinal calcium imaging in the 75 

anterior cingulate cortex (ACC), a region critical for long -term memory retrieval 11 . This identified a 76 

previously undescribed population of excitatory ACC neurons, termed “spatial context cells,” which 77 

failed to emerge when SWRs were disrupted. These neurons were characterized by exceptionally 78 

broad receptive fields that formed dynamically across the course of learning, in a timeline 79 

consistent with memory consolidation and were preferentially recruited into the  c-Fos -defined 80 

memory engram, establishing their role as a substrate for remote memory. Crucially, our 81 

experiments reveal a bifurcated consolidation process: while basic cortical spatial tuning is 82 

unaffected by SWR disruption, the emergence of spatial context cells is selectively abolished. These 83 

data indicate that SWRs function as a computational bridge, driving the synaptic convergence of 84 

fragmented hippocampal place codes into a generalized cortical contextual framework. These 85

Discussion 272 

Here we attempted to identify the downstream effect of SWR s that occur  during  offline 273 

period s by employing closed -loop disruption and  in vivo  Ca 2+  imaging. As a result,  we identif ied  a274 

previously undescribed class of neuron in the anterior cingulate cortex (ACC), which we term spatial 275 

context cells , as the specific class  of neurons  generated by SWRs . These neurons are defined by 276 

activity patterns that span extensive portions of a specific environment while remaining largely silent 277 

or remapping in others ( Fig. 1 –2). Unlike place cells in the hippocampus or ACC, spatial context 278 

cells exhibit markedly broader receptive fields, with recruitment increasing progressively with 279 

repeated environmental exposure. This experience -dependent emergence reflects a transformation of 280 

their activity: they transition from initially fragmented, multi -field, place -like firing to a stable, single 281 

field encompassing a majority of the familiar environment  (Fig.  2). Our results strongly support the 282 

idea that this neuronal subpopulation represents a cortical engram encoding specific contexts. 283 

Consistent with an engram function, these neurons are selectively enriched in the  c-Fos  labeled 284 

fraction after learning a novel task ( Fig.  4). Moreover, their emergence and refinement require 285 

experience and adhere to the established timeline for memory consolidation from the hippocampus 286 

to cortical structures  (Fig. 3 ).287

We observed no anatomical segregation between ACC place and spatial context cells, 288 

suggesting they coexist as a heterogeneous population.  While our  data  suggest  that spatial context 289 

cells are likely predetermined by intrinsic connectivity rather than random assignment , the  SWR 290 

disruption experiments ( Fig.  1 and 4 ) coupled with the pattern of spatial context cell development 291 

(Fig. 3 ) we observed, point to a mechanism  whereby  highly convergent input s from multiple dCA1 292 

place cells,  collectively  spanning a large area,  are  consolidate d as contextual  memory  trace s in ACC 293 

neuron s during SWR events  (Fig.  4f ). Given that  spatial context cells follow a distinct multi -day 294 

developmental trajectory where initially fragmented, multi -field activity converges into singular, 295 

global representations , o ur data suggest that prior to consolidation, hippocampal inputs to the ACC 296 

are largely subthreshold, reaching the firing threshold only at their peak intensities. This results in a 297 

sparse ACC firing pattern characterized by multiple, restricted receptive fields. During offline states, 298 

however, SWR -mediated replay induces LTP in ACC neurons where these inputs converge, boosting 299 

previously subthreshold signals to suprathreshold levels. This synaptic potentiation allows the ACC 300 

firing field to expand, integrating locations that were initially represented only by weak hippocampal 301 

inputs. While our model focuses on the hippocampal -ACC axis, we cannot exclude the possibility 302 

that this convergence occurs within an intermediary region bridging the two structures .303 

The coexistence and differential engagement of spatial codes in the hippocampus and ACC 304 

are of particular interest. The hippocampal map is locally activated, with small receptive fields 305 

tracking the animal’s position. While  SWRs  and  theta sequences briefly code non -local 306 

representations  25,26 , these remain limited to spatially constrained areas or well -defined trajectories. 307 

We found that the ACC contains an almost identical population of place cells , with similar field sizes, 308 

stability, and local activation during exploration , though a high proportion of these neurons also 309 

conjunctively code for head -direction and likely other sensory information. In contrast to these 310 

localized spatial maps, ACC spatial context cells exhibit a qualitatively different engagement pattern. 311 

Since their receptive field encompasses, on average, over half of the environment, coupled with a 312 

high degree of co -activation (>20% of the population active simultaneously), these neurons comprise 313 

a globally activated code for context, which contrasts sharply with the local hippocampal code from 314 

which they are likely derived ( Fig.  4).  Th is  high degree of co -activity and broad spatial coverage is 315 

not a computational artifact but rather a necessary functional property of  the  global contextual code 316 

generated by spatial context cells . Since the r etrieval of a consolidated memory trace requires the 317 

near -simultaneous activation of an ensemble representing the entire environment , the  high synchrony 318 

and expansive receptive fields  of spatial context cells are  optimized  to  provide this stable, non -319 

localized contextual signal. 320

Our findings establish that the formation of the ACC spatial representation is dependent on 321 

hippocampal activity, as silencing the hippocampus disrupts the emergence and stability of both ACC 322 

place and spatial context cells  (Fig.  3-4). What remains less clear is the precise anatomical route of 323 

this information transfer. While direct connectivity from the  ventral hippocampus (vCA1) to the ACC 324 

is anatomically supported  27 -31 , evidence for direct projections from dorsal hippocampus (dCA1)  is 325 

weak  32 -35 . This suggests an intermediate structure, such as the retrosplenial cortex (RSC)  36,37 , may 326 

be necessary to convey dCA1  spatial information  to the ACC. Given that the RSC contains spatially 327 

tuned, place -like neurons  36,38,39  and is ideally positioned to bridge these two structures, future studies 328 

targeting the RSC should clarify its role in sculpting the ACC spatial code. 329 

In summary, this study identifies a novel class of ACC neuron that houses a long -term 330 

memory trace of context  as the consequence of SWR/replay event s. As a population, these neurons 331 

develop and mature with experience in a time -frame consistent with memory consolidation. Their 332 

expression relies on information routed from the hippocampus, specifically during SWRs, which 333 

appear to transform precise spatial information into a more generalized, stable, and abstractive 334 

cortical contextual representation. While their role in remote memory recall is clear, it remains an 335 

open question whether these context -bearing neurons themselves represent a complete memory 336 

engram or if they form part of a broader network encoding multiple features of specific episodes. 337 

Future work will undoubtedly address this question and further explore their therapeutic potential for 338 

disorders characterized by maladaptive contextual associations. 339

Methods 532 

Animals 533 

We used TRE -G-CaMP7 -T2A -DsRed2 x CaMKIIα -tTA double -transgenic mice to co -express 534 

GCaMP7 and DsRed2 selectively in excitatory neurons  40 . This allowed for chronic, longitudinal 535 

observation of activity . In  a subset of  experiments, c -Fos -tTA mice  41 , were used for activity -536 

dependent  labelling  and chemogenetic manipulation. 537 

Subjects and housing 538 

All procedures were conducted in strict accordance with institutional guidelines and protocols 539 

approved by the RIKEN and Kyoto University Animal Experiment Committees.  Experiments 540 

utilized male mice (10 –24 weeks old) , which  were housed individually on a reversed 12 -hour light -541 

dark cycle (lights on at 6 PM, off at 6 AM). All behavioral and imaging experiments were 542 

performed during the animals' subjective dark phase (between 6 AM and 6 PM). 543 

Surgical procedures 544 

All surgical procedures were conducted under isoflurane anaesthesia (3% induction, 1.5% 545 

maintenance) with the animal secured in a stereotaxic frame (Kopf). 546 

Surgery for dorsal CA1 imaging:  Mice were fixed in the stereotaxic frame, and a custom 547 

stainless -steel headplate (25 mm length, 4 mm width, 1 mm thickness) was affixed to the skull with 548 

dental cement and three anchor screws. The headplate featured a circular opening (7 mm inner 549 

diameter, 10 mm outer diameter) offset 2.5 mm relative to the plate center. The center of this 550 

opening was positioned at 2 mm AP, 2 mm ML from Bregma, targeting the left hemisphere. Three 551 

to four days post -headplate implantation, a trephine drill was used to remove a circular section of 552 

skull centered at the target coordinates. The dura and overlying cortex above CA1 were slowly 553 

removed by aspiration using a 27 -gauge blunt needle, with exposed tissue continuously rinsed with 554 

cortex buffer (123 mM NaCl, 5 mM KCl, 10 mM glucose, 2 mM, 2 mM & 10 mM HEPES with a 555 

pH of 7.4). Finally, a metal guide tube was inserted into the cavity, positioned 200 -300 μm 556 

superficial to the CA1 cell body layer, and secured directly to the skull using dental acrylic. 557

four anchor screws were affixed to the skull with dental cement. Three to four days later, a circular 559 

craniotomy (2 mm diameter) was performed over the ACC.  The opening was centered on the 560 

midline at 1 mm AP from Bregma, targeting the ACC.  The dura overlying the implantation 561

Behavioural task and protocol 580 

Mice performed a spatial navigation task across nine consecutive days, with each daily session 581 

consisting of three 10 -minute epochs separated by 5 -minute intermissions ( Fig. 1 c). These consisted 582 

of context S (Square Track): A square track (50 cm per edge) with a wall blocking one corner. Mice 583 

ran back -and -forth between the two open ends to receive food rewards, provided upon reaching 584 

each end. Performance improved over days, characterized by increased  travelling distance ,585 

indicating familiarity  (Extended Data Fig.  1). Context O (Open Field): A 50 x 50 cm open arena 586 

featuring distinct wall patterns and a unique scent (0.5% acetic acid). Food rewards were randomly 587 

scattered within the arena to promote general navigation. Finally, context S': Mice were returned to 588 

the original square track (Context S). Mice were pre -exposed to the behavioral apparatus for 3 days 589 

which constituted the pretraining period. This consisted of one day in which mice could freely 590 

explore contexts S and O for 5 minutes. Followed by two days in which sucrose pellets (#1811251 591
