Abstract 1

The circadian system aligns behavior and physiology with the 24 -hour environmental cycl e2

through a distributed network of clocks including the master pacemaker in the 3

suprachiasmatic nucleus (SCN) and an autonomous retinal clock critical for local retinal 4

physiology and function. Although both clocks are entrained by light , they differ in their 5

photoreceptor input s and light sensitivity . The specific contributions and mechanisms by 6

which distinct photoreceptor pathways drive their photoentrainment , however, remain 7

incompletely understood. 8

In this study, we conducted a comp rehensive transcriptomic and integrative comparative 9

analysis of retinal and SCN circadian light responses using mouse models lacking specific 10 

photoreceptors or key components of signaling pathways . Under photopic conditions , we11 

found that each tissue displays distinct light -responsive transcriptional signatures across 12 

genotypes, yet both share d a conserved cluster of rod -driven immediate early -genes .13 

Strikingly , th e light -evoked transcriptional response was not sufficient to shift the phase of 14 

the SCN clock , in contrast to its robust phase -shifting effect on the retinal clock. 15 

Furthermore , by genetically disrupting rod/cone electrical coupling and pharmacological ly 16 

isolating rod pathways , we identif ied the OFF -cone bipolar cell circuit as both necessary 17 

and sufficient to mediate light -induced phase resetting of the retinal clock. Together, t hese 18 

findings delineate the specialized retinal circuitry that under lies circadian entrainment and 19 

reveal a fundamental divergence between retinal and SCN mechanisms of photic 20 

timekeeping .21

Introduction 23 

Living organisms rely on endogenous circadian clocks to synchronize their behavior 24 

and physiology with the environmental light/dark (LD) cycle. In mammals, the primary 25 

external time cue is environmental light, which can vary in intensity by up to a billion -fold 26 

between night and day [1] . Light is received by retinal photoreceptors, including rods, 27 

cones, and intrinsically photosensitive retinal ganglion cells (ipR GCs ), all of which 28 

contribute to non -image -forming responses such as the pupillary light reflex and the 29 

circadian photoentrainment of the master clo ck, located in the suprachiasmatic nuclei 30 

(SCN) of the hypothalamus [2 –10] . Be yond entraining the SCN, the mammalian retina 31 

harbors an autonomous circadian clock [11] that functions independently of SCN control 32 

[12] . The retinal clock orchestrates critical retinal functions [13] including photoreceptor 33 

disc shedding [14 –17] , melatonin and dopamine release [18,19] , photoreceptor electrical 34 

coupling [20 –22] , the amplitude of light response s [23,24] , circadian clock gene 35 

expression  [25,26] and visual processing [12] .36 

Although light -induced phase shifts of the SCN clock , driven by the rapid and 37 

transient induction of immediate -early genes such as Fos , are well characterized [27 –32] ,38 

direct comparative analyses of light -evoked responses between retinal and SCN clocks 39 

remain limited . Our previous work showed that the retina exhibits a markedly lo wer 40 

threshold for light -induced FOS expression , approximately 1 -2 log units below that of the 41 

SCN [33] . Intringuinly , phase shifting of the retinal clock requires substantially higher 42 

irradiance (~10 13 photons/cm 2/s [34] , than that required to elicit behavioral phase shifts 43 

(10 10 -10 11 photons/cm 2/s, [4,5,34 –36] ). We recently demonstrated that , under photopic 44 

conditions , rods, rather than middle -wavelength (MW) cones or melanospin, are essential 45 

for light -induced phase shifting of the mouse retinal clock but not SCN enterainment 46

The broad dynamic range of rod involvement across varying light intensities can be 49 

attributed to the engagement of ON and OFF pathways through three distinct rod -50 

mediated circuits, each characterized by different sensitivities [38] . The primary pathway 51 

is the most sensitive, operating near scotopic thres hold. It transmits light signals from rods 52 

to ON -type rod bipolar cells (ON -RBCs) expressing metabotropic glutamate receptor 6 53 

(mGluR6) [38,39] , which then synapses onto AII amacrine cells. These amacrine cells 54 

connect to ON -type cone bipolar cells (ON -CBCs) via gap junctions and to OFF -CBCs 55 

through inhibitory glycin ergic synapses, ultimately relay ing signals to ON and OFF retinal 56 

ganglion cells (RGCs), respectively. A secondary pathway, recruited at higher light levels, 57 

conveys rod signals directly to adjacent cones through  connexin 36 ( Cx36 ) gap junctions. 58 

When rod s hyperpolariz e, glutamate release ceases , leading to depolarization of OFF -59 

CBCs via ionotropic kainate/AMPA -type glutamate receptors. Conversely , ON -CBCs 60 

hyperpolarize through activation of mGluR6 receptors, resulting in reduced cationic 61 

conductance [40,41] . These CBCs then convey the signal to RGCs [42 –44] . The third and 62 

least characterized pathway involves direct synaptic rod input to OFF -CBCs [44 –47] .63 

Despite the delineation of these circuits , the specific rod pathway responsible for 64 

mediating light -induced phase shifts of the retinal clock remai ns unknown .65 

To isolate rod -specific transcriptomic signatures elicited by light in the SCN and 66 

retina, and to dissect the contribution of individual rod pathways to retinal clock phase 67 

shifting , we first investigated light -driven gene activation in isolated retinal layers and in 68 

the SCN of the same wild -type and “rod -only” mice . Transcriptomic profiling revealed 69 

tissue -specific light -responsive gene expression in both genotypes, along with a shared 70 

cluster of immediate early -genes. To further characterize these responses, we assesse d71
