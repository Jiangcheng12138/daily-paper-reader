Abstract 21 

Animals integrate environmental cues with internal physiological states through inter-organ 22 

communication to regulate reproduction. However, how environmental microbes are 23 

incorporated into these systemic pathways to control reproductive stem cell proliferation remains 24 

poorly understood. Here, we demonstrate that environmental microbes colonizing the gut 25 

promote stem cell–mediated oogenesis in Drosophila melanogaster by increasing germline stem 26 

cell (GSC) number. This process requires microbial activation of gut metabolic pathways, 27 

including glycolysis and the pentose phosphate pathway (PPP), linking microbial cues to 28 

organismal physiology. We further show that microbial cues modulate circadian clock gene 29 

expression in both the gut and brain, and that circadian gene activity in these tissues is required 30 

for microbe-induced increases in GSCs. These metabolic changes promote ecdysone and 31 

juvenile hormone signaling in ovarian somatic cells, and single-cell transcriptomic analyses 32 

further reveal cell type–specific metabolic and hormonal responses across germline and follicle 33 

cell populations. Together, our findings establish that microbe-dependent gut-mediated inter-34 

organ communication integrates metabolism, circadian gene expression, and endocrine 35 

signaling to regulate stem cell–mediated reproduction in Drosophila. 36

Introduction 40 

Germline stem cells (GSCs) are essential to Drosophila oogenesis, supplying the germ cells 41 

required for egg production and female fertility. The Drosophila ovary is among the most 42 

extensively characterized systems for dissecting stem cell regulation, providing fundamental 43 

insights into how specialized somatic niches coordinate stem cell maintenance, proliferation, and 44 

differentiation. GSCs reside at the anterior tip of the germarium, where somatic niche cells— 45 

including terminal filament cells (TF), cap cells (CC), and escort cells (EC)—support GSC self-46 

renewal and regulate the balance between stem cell maintenance and differentiation 1. Following 47 

asymmetric division, GSCs produce cystoblasts that undergo four rounds of incomplete 48 

cytokinesis to form 16-cell cysts, which develop into one oocyte and 15 nurse cells 1. These cysts 49 

are encapsulated by follicle cells, and the oocyte matures through the transfer of mRNAs, 50 

proteins, and organelles from nurse cells 2,3 .51 

GSC activity is finely tuned by systemic physiological cues. Nutrient-rich diets accelerate 52 

oogenesis and enhance GSC proliferation, whereas nutrient-poor conditions impair these 53 

processes 4-10 . These effects are mediated largely by insulin-like peptides (Dilps) produced from 54 

neuroendocrine cells in the brain and gut, which promote germline and follicle-cell proliferation 4,6 .55 

Mating provides an additional systemic trigger through neuroendocrine activation and ecdysone 56 

signaling to stimulate oogenesis 11-13 . The microbiome also shapes reproductive physiology by 57 

influencing nutrient intake, immunity, and metabolic homeostasis 14-20 . We previously 58 

demonstrated that environmentally acquired microbes enhance GSC proliferation and egg 59 

maturation via ecdysone- and juvenile hormone (JH)-dependent pathways, independently of 60 

insulin signaling 21 . Hormonal receptors for these pathways are expressed in germ cells and 61 

follicle cells, and their loss in the follicle cells impairs oogenesis 21-25 . Notably, microbe-induced 62 

GSC activation operates through somatic tissues and acts non–cell autonomously on GSC 63 

proliferation 21 . Thus, the Drosophila ovary provides a powerful model for dissecting how local 64 

somatic signals integrate with systemic cues to regulate stem cell behavior. These findings 65 

underscore the role of environmental cues in modulating GSC activity indirectly through inter-66 

organ communication rather than by acting directly on the germline. 67 

Inter-organ communication is a central mechanism through which environmental and 68 

physiological signals regulate reproductive function. In males, signals from the adjacent testis 69 

remodel intestinal carbohydrate metabolism via JAK–STAT signaling, generating a male-specific 70 

metabolic state that supports sperm development 26 . In females, dietary sugars modulate GSC 71 

proliferation through gut–germline communication. In addition, mating can trigger GSC 72 

proliferation through ovarian ecdysteroid production via the male-derived sex peptide 12 , while 73 

dietary glucose is converted to fructose in the hemolymph, which stimulates neuropeptide F 74

Microbes colonizing the gut can reshape host physiology by modulating energy balance, 78 

redox state, and biosynthetic capacity through diverse metabolites 28,29 . As a consequence, gut 79 

microbes influence nutrient utilization, immunity, and reproductive capacity across species 30-32 .80 

In mammals, perturbation of the paternal gut microbiota compromises sperm quality and 81 

placental development. These defects are reversed by restoring the microbiota prior to 82 

conception, thereby rescuing fetal development 33 . Microbe–brain communication is also well 83 

documented, as gut microbes alter neural activity, behavior, and mating preferences through 84 

metabolic and neuroendocrine pathways 34-36 . Together, these studies indicate that 85 

environmental microbes can function as regulators of inter-organ communication and are 86 

capable of modulating systemic metabolic and reproductive physiology. 87 

In parallel, host metabolic state and circadian timing are tightly interconnected. Core 88 

metabolic pathways such as glycolysis and gluconeogenesis exhibit circadian regulation 37 , while 89 

metabolic outputs, including NAD ⁺ levels and cellular redox status, feed back to the core clock 90 

machinery 38-43 . Disruption of this metabolic–temporal coupling can lead to physiological decline 91 

and metabolic dysfunction 40,44-47 . Taken together, these observations suggest that interactions 92 

with circadian clock machinery may represent an additional regulatory layer through which 93 

environmental microbes modulate host metabolic physiology. Despite extensive evidence that 94 

gut microbes regulate multiple host organ systems, the molecular mechanisms linking microbial 95 

cues from the gut and brain to ovarian function remain unclear. In particular, how these systemic 96 

signals are integrated to regulate reproductive stem cell behavior has yet to be defined. 97 

Here, we demonstrate that environmentally acquired microbes promote oogenesis by 98 

modulating inter-organ communication linking the gut, brain, and ovary in Drosophila 99 

melanogaster . Bulk transcriptomic analyses revealed that microbial exposure activates 100 

glycolysis, the pentose phosphate pathway (PPP), and circadian programs in the gut and brain, 101 

which in turn modulate ecdysone and juvenile hormone (JH) signaling ―key regulators of GSC 102 

homeostasis. Functional experiments further showed that gut-derived glycolytic and PPP 103 

activities, together with circadian clock regulators in both gut and brain, are required for microbe-104 

induced GSC proliferation. Single-cell transcriptomic profiling of the ovary uncovered stage- and 105 

cell type–specific transcriptional responses to microbial cues across germline and somatic 106 

lineages. Together, these findings define a microbe–gut–brain–ovary axis in which metabolic 107 

and circadian gene regulation converge to control reproductive stem cell activity. 108

Results 110 

Microbial cues induce tissue-specific transcriptional programs in the gut and 111 

brain 112 

We previously demonstrated that environmental microbes promote germline stem cell (GSC) 113 

proliferation and accelerate oogenesis in Drosophila 21 . To elucidate the molecular basis 114 

underlying this response, we employed a previously established exposure scheme in which 115 

donor flies were maintained in vials for 3–4 days, after which newly eclosed virgin females 116 

(“Acceptor”) were replaced into these vials for three days (Fig. 1A). Consistent with our prior 117 

observations, acceptor females exposed to microbe-rich (M+) vials exhibited a significant 118 

increase in GSC number compared with those maintained under microbe-poor (M-) conditions 21 .119 

To identify tissues responsive to microbial cues, we performed bulk RNA sequencing on 120 

dissected guts, brains, and ovaries from M+ and M– females (three biological replicates per 121 

condition; Fig. 1B). After quality control and alignment to the Drosophila melanogaster reference 122 

genome (BDGP ver. 6.32), differential gene expression analyses were performed independently 123 

for each tissue. 124 

Principal component analysis (PCA) of the top 500 differentially expressed genes revealed 125 

clear clustering of samples by tissue type (Fig. 1C), with the strongest segregation between M+ 126 

and M- conditions observed in the gut (Supplementary Fig. 1A). Heatmap analysis confirmed 127 

high reproducibility among biological replicates within each tissue (Supplementary Fig. 1B). 128 

Differential expression analysis (log 2 fold change ≥1.5, p < 0.01) showed that the gut exhibited 129 

the most pronounced transcriptional response to microbial exposure, with 201 genes 130 

upregulated and 165 genes downregulated under M+ conditions compared with M- conditions 131 

(Fig. 1D). The brain also showed substantial transcriptional changes, with 21 genes upregulated 132 

and 45 downregulated. In contrast, the ovary exhibited relatively few differentially expressed 133 

genes, with limited overlap across exposure durations. Comparing M+ to M− conditions, 18 134 

genes were upregulated and 19 were downregulated in ovaries after one day of exposure, 135 

whereas two genes were upregulated and 93 were downregulated after three days 136 

(Supplementary Fig. 1C). 137 

Because transcriptional differences in the ovary were modest and variable between one-138 

and three-day exposures (Supplementary Fig. 1B-E), we focused subsequent bulk 139 

transcriptomic analyses on the gut and brain. Comparison of these two tissues identified a small 140 

subset of genes that were consistently regulated under M+ conditions. Nine genes were 141 

commonly upregulated and four were commonly downregulated in both the gut and brain (Fig. 142 

1D). Notably, among the shared upregulated genes were circadian regulators ( vri , tim ) and a 143 

glycolytic and pentose phosphate pathway (PPP)-associated gene ( Mal-B2 ), whereas zw was 144

Gut metabolic pathways are required for microbe-induced GSC proliferation 153 

Bulk RNA-seq analyses identified PPP and glycolysis as candidate metabolic pathways 154 

responsive to microbial exposure in the gut. To determine whether these metabolic pathways 155 

are functionally required for microbial stimulation of GSC proliferation, we quantified GSC 156 

numbers following tissue-specific knockdown of PPP- and glycolysis-associated genes. Using 157 

the enteroendocrine cell (EE)-specific driver TKg-Gal4, which is expressed in the gut 11,48,49 ,158 

knockdown of key PPP enzymes ( Mal-B2, hex-A, zw, 6PGL, and pgd ) significantly suppressed 159 

the microbe-induced increase in GSC number (Fig. 2A). Similarly, knockdown of glycolytic genes 160 

(pgi, pfk, and pyk ) reduced GSC numbers under M+ conditions (Fig. 2B), indicating that both 161 

metabolic pathways are required in EEs for microbe-induced GSC proliferation. We next 162 

examined whether these metabolic pathways are required in another gut cell population. Using 163 

mex-1-Gal4 , which is expressed in enterocytes 48,49 , knockdown of PPP or glycolytic enzymes 164 

likewise abolished the microbe-dependent increase in GSC number (Fig. 2C,D). These results 165 

indicate that microbial regulation of gut metabolism in both EEs and enterocytes is essential for 166 

promoting GSC proliferation in the ovary. 167 

Previous studies reported that PPP activity is required for late oogenesis and egg 168 

production, whereas glycolysis appears largely dispensable at these stages 50 . To determine 169 

whether PPP activity also contributes to early germline regulation, we attenuated PPP gene 170 

expression in germ cells using Nanos (NN )-Gal4–driven RNA interference. Knockdown of Mal-171 

B2 , Hex-A , or Zw significantly suppressed the GSC increase normally observed under M+ 172 

conditions, reducing GSC numbers to levels comparable to M– controls (Supplementary Fig. 173 

2A). These findings indicate that PPP activity is required for late oogenesis and egg maturation, 174 

and that early germline proliferation depends on PPP function both within the ovary and through 175 

gut-derived signals. To validate transcriptional changes in these pathways, we quantified 176 

representative PPP and glycolytic genes by qPCR in guts from M+ and M– females. Consistent 177 

with the RNA-seq data, Mal-B2 and Zw were significantly upregulated in the gut under M+ 178 

conditions, and Pgi showed a modest increase (Fig. 2E). Other genes in these pathways 179

Microbes reshape intestinal stem cell differentiation and gut endocrine potential 186 

To determine where microbes localize after exposure to a microbe-rich environment, we 187 

quantified microbial titers in dissected guts and ovaries (Supplementary Fig. 3A). In both male 188 

and female non-mated donor flies, microbes were predominantly detected in the gut and were 189 

undetectable in the ovaries. A similar distribution was observed in recipient females maintained 190 

under M+ and M− conditions: microbial load was consistently confined to the gut and was 191 

markedly elevated under M+ conditions. No microbial signal was detected in the ovary under any 192 

condition, indicating that microbial effects are mediated indirectly via extra-ovarian tissues rather 193 

than by direct ovarian colonization. 194 

To assess how microbial exposure alters gut physiology, we examined intestinal stem cell 195 

(ISC) differentiation using established lineage markers and nuclear size criteria 51 . The esg -Gal4-196 

driven UAS-mCD8::GFP (esg>mCD8::GFP ) labels ISCs and progenitor populations, including 197 

enteroblasts (EBs) and pre–enteroendocrine cells (preEEs), whereas Prospero (Pros) marks 198 

preEEs and mature enteroendocrine cells (EEs). Accordingly, ISCs and EBs were GFP-positive 199 

(GFP ⁺) but Pros-negative (Pros ⁻), preEEs were GFP ⁺/Pros ⁺, EEs were GFP ⁻/Pros ⁺, and 200 

polyploid enterocytes were GFP ⁻/Pros ⁻ with large nuclei (Fig. 3A). 201 

Microbial exposure altered specific branches of the ISC lineage (Fig. 3B). The proportion 202 

of ISCs/EBs increased by ~40% under M+ conditions relative to M− controls (M+, 38.6 ± 9.6%; 203 

M−, 27.9 ± 8.8%; P < 0.005), and preEEs increased by ~2.5-fold (M+, 7.2 ± 5.2%; M−, 2.7 ± 204 

4.1%; P < 0.005). In contrast, the proportion of mature EEs did not differ significantly between 205 

conditions. Notably, the fraction of enterocytes was reduced by ~30% in the M+ gut (M+, 38.8 ± 206 

10.3%; M−, 54.5 ± 10.0%; P < 0.001). Together, these results demonstrate that microbial 207 

exposure reshapes ISC differentiation, favoring early EE-lineage progenitors while reducing the 208 

relative abundance of mature enterocytes. This shift in the proportions of intestinal cell types 209 

suggests that microbial cues remodel gut physiological output, potentially influencing endocrine 210 

and metabolic signaling to distal organs. 211

To determine whether transcriptional changes in gut PPP and glycolysis are reflected at the 214 

metabolic level, we performed comprehensive profiling of anionic and cationic metabolites in the 215 

gut, where microbes localize and intestinal cell differentiation is altered under microbe-rich 216 

conditions. Principal component analysis (PCA) revealed clear separation between gut samples 217 

from M+ and M− females (four biological replicates per condition), indicating clear microbe-218 

dependent metabolic remodeling (Supplementary Fig. 3B,C). Despite substantial variability 219 

among individual metabolites, central carbon metabolism displayed a coherent and reproducible 220 

shift under M+ conditions. Notably, PPP-associated metabolites showed a selective increase, 221 

whereas glycolysis and the tricarboxylic acid (TCA) cycle showed an overall reduction 222 

(Supplementary Fig. 4A–E). Consistent with increased PPP flux, the PPP intermediate 223 

sedoheptulose-7-phosphate (S7P) and the major PPP output NADPH were significantly elevated 224 

in M+ guts (Supplementary Fig. 4A,B,F, Supplementary Fig. 5A,D). 225 

In contrast, NADH levels and several glycolytic intermediates tended to be reduced in M+ 226 

guts, although these changes did not reach statistical significance (Supplementary Fig. 4C,F, 227 

Supplementary Fig. 5D). While a large fraction of both anionic and cationic metabolites showed 228 

an overall downward trend under M+ conditions (Supplementary Fig. 3C, Supplementary Fig. 229 

4A,B,F–H, Supplementary Fig. 5A–D), this global decrease contrasted with the selective 230 

upregulation of PPP-associated metabolites, indicating that specific metabolic pathways are 231 

affected by microbial activity rather than a uniform suppression of metabolism. Although steady-232 

state levels of glycolytic intermediates were not elevated in M+ guts, functional genetic analyses 233 

indicate that glycolytic activity is nevertheless required for microbe-induced GSC proliferation 234 

(Fig. 2B,D). In addition, riboflavin consistently exhibited an upward trend in M+ guts, mirroring its 235 

behavior in ovaries (Supplementary Fig. 4F, Supplementary Fig. 5D). This observation is 236 

consistent with previous reports that microbial exposure enhances mitochondrial function to 237 

support ATP production and oogenesis 20,21 .238

Circadian genes in the gut and brain are required for microbe-induced GSC 240 

proliferation 241 

We next examined whether circadian clock gene activity provides an additional regulatory layer 242 

that modulates the metabolic–endocrine axis in response to microbial exposure. Bulk RNA-seq 243 

analysis revealed that multiple core circadian clock genes were upregulated in both the gut and 244 

brain under microbe-rich conditions (Fig. 1E,F; Supplementary Table 1). To assess the 245 

functional relevance of this activation, we disrupted circadian gene expression in these tissues 246 

and quantified GSC numbers. Knockout or knockdown of core circadian regulators ( per, tim, or 247 

vri ) in enterocytes using mex-1-Gal4 abolished the microbe-induced increase in GSC number 248

Because circadian programs can be differentially regulated across tissues and are not 256

tim , per , and vri was observed in heads from the same animals, whereas cyc and clock were 261

Because metabolic pathways such as PPP and glycolysis can influence circadian 264 

oscillations through redox-sensitive mechanisms 39,42,43,55,56 , we next asked whether gut-derived 265 

metabolic activity contributes to circadian clock gene regulation in the brain. Knockdown of PPP-266

types contribute differently to gut–brain circadian signaling. Together, these results demonstrate 271 

that microbial activation of circadian rhythm programs in the gut is required for GSC proliferation 272 

and that gut metabolic activity contributes to circadian clock gene regulation in the brain. These 273 

findings suggest a functional link between peripheral metabolic pathways, regulation of circadian 274 

clock genes, and microbe-induced control of germline stem cell activity. 275

Discussion 389 

Animals must integrate fluctuating environmental inputs into stable reproductive decisions to 390 

ensure species continuity. Drosophila acquire microbes from environmental sources such as 391 

dietary substrates, frass, and conspecific contact 15,65 . Here, we propose that environmental 392 

microbes promote Drosophila GSC proliferation through gut-mediated inter-organ 393 

communication. Through integrated transcriptomics, genetic manipulation, metabolomics, and 394 

ovarian single-cell profiling, we demonstrate that microbial exposure remodels gut metabolism, 395 

couples this metabolic state to gut–brain circadian clock regulation, and potentiates endocrine 396 

signaling in ovarian somatic cells to support GSC proliferation. Microbial exposure elicits the 397 

strongest transcriptional and metabolic responses in the gut, where PPP and glycolysis are 398 

functionally required for microbe-induced GSC proliferation. The modest changes in steady-state 399 

metabolite levels under M+ conditions are consistent with regulation at the level of metabolic flux 400 

rather than metabolite accumulation. Microbial cues modulate circadian gene expression in both 401 

the gut and brain, and disruption of gut metabolic activity altered clock-gene expression in the 402 

head, indicating that peripheral metabolic activity influences central circadian gene regulation. 403 

These results define a multi-layered mechanism by which microbial exposure links gut 404 

metabolism, circadian coordination, and ovarian endocrine signaling to germline stem cell 405 

proliferation. 406 

The gut functions as a metabolic sensor that translates microbial signals into systemic 407 

reproductive outputs coordinated with organismal metabolic state. These responses integrate 408 

both intrinsic metabolic programs and extrinsic environmental inputs. In Drosophila , previous 409 

studies have shown that intrinsic activation of the PPP in the germline supports egg maturation 410 

and can influence organismal metabolism by modulating fat-body–derived satiety factors such 411 

as fit , thereby influencing nutrient preference through brain-integrated metabolic cues 50,66 . In 412 

contrast, our findings reveal a microbe-dependent, gut-mediated mechanism in which gut 413 

glycolysis and PPP are required for microbe-induced GSC proliferation. Suppressing either 414 

pathway in enteroendocrine cells or enterocytes abolished the microbe-induced GSC increase, 415 

demonstrating that the gut functions as a metabolic sensor and transducer of microbial signals 416 

that promotes germline proliferation. These extrinsic metabolic inputs act alongside cell-417 

autonomous metabolic requirements in the germline, together supporting GSC 418 

proliferation(Supplementary Fig. 2A, Supplementary Fig. 7C) 67 . This hormonal activation can 419 

enhance BMP/pMad signaling within the niche, thereby driving GSC increase through a gut– 420 

ovary inter-organ communication axis. 421 

These features distinguish microbial cues from dietary perturbations that act through more 422 

uniform metabolic and insulin-dependent mechanisms. Unlike high-sugar diets, which impair 423

GSC proliferation and disrupt ISC-to-enterocyte differentiation via insulin dysregulation and 424 

oxidative stress pathways 51 , microbial cues promote GSC proliferation and remodel gut cellular 425 

composition, including increased ISC activity and a shift toward EE-lineage progenitors (Fig. 3), 426 

potentially enhancing the production of endocrine signaling to the ovary and brain (Fig. 2A, B, 427 

Fig. 4E,F, Fig. 5). This interpretation aligns with previous reports that microbial conditions 428 

influence serotonin production in an EE cell–dependent manner 68 . Our scRNA-seq analyses 429 

further support this distinction: under M+ conditions, insulin pathway activity was attenuated, as 430 

indicated by foxo downregulation, whereas the juvenile hormone-binding protein Jhbp5 was 431 

upregulated, consistent with enhanced endocrine output rather than insulin-driven growth (Fig. 432 

7H,I and Supplementary Fig. 7B,H). Together, these findings indicate that microbial cues 433 

engage metabolic and hormonal pathways that are distinct from those activated by dietary 434 

sugar, enabling coordinated and tissue-specific effects on stem cell proliferation and 435 

differentiation. 436 

Glycolysis and the PPP provide both energetic and biosynthetic substrates required for 437 

endocrine cell activity, thereby facilitating the production of ecdysone and JH within the ovarian 438 

niche (Fig. 5), indicating that gut-derived metabolic cues act as extrinsic regulators of hormonal 439 

signaling. These observations raise the question of how gut metabolic activity is regulated at the 440 

biochemical level. Notably, steady-state levels of glycolytic intermediates were not elevated in 441 

M+ guts (Supplementary Fig. 4C-E), despite genetic and functional evidence showing that 442 

glycolytic activity is required for microbe-induced GSC proliferation. This discrepancy is 443 

consistent with the flux-controlled nature of glycolysis, in which pathway activity is not 444 

necessarily reflected by metabolite abundance 69-71 . Glycolytic flux can supply glucose-6-445 

phosphate to the PPP, supporting NADPH production and biosynthetic capacity without global 446 

accumulation of glycolytic intermediates 72-75 . In line with this model, metabolomic profiling 447 

revealed selective elevation of PPP-associated metabolites, including NADPH and S7P, under 448 

microbe-rich conditions, despite an overall reduction in many other metabolites (Supplementary 449 

Fig. 4A–F, Supplementary Fig. 5A,D). These observations indicate that microbial cues regulate 450 

gut metabolism via pathway flux rather than influencing steady-state metabolite levels. 451 

Reproductive regulation extends beyond metabolic activation and involves temporal 452 

coordination across tissues. Microbial cues play a role in circadian clock regulation in both the 453 

gut and brain, adding a temporal regulatory layer to the metabolic control of oogenesis. Under 454 

M+ conditions, multiple core clock genes, including tim , per , and vri , were upregulated in both 455 

tissues (Fig. 4C,D). Functional perturbation of these genes in gut and brain suppressed the 456 

microbe-induced GSC increase (Fig. 4A,B), demonstrating regulatory coupling between 457 

circadian clock gene expression and reproductive output. Previous studies have shown that 458

NADPH rhythms 55,76 . In addition, glycolysis and PPP can directly influence circadian oscillations 460 

through redox- and metabolite-sensitive mechanisms that link cellular energy state to clock 461 

function within the canonical transcription–translation feedback loop 39,42,43,56,77-79 . Consistent with 462 

these models, gut-specific knockdown of glycolytic and PPP enzymes reduced circadian gene 463 

expression in the head (Fig. 4E,F), demonstrating that peripheral metabolic activity can influence 464 

central temporal regulation. Microbe-derived metabolites function as dynamic regulators that 465 

interact with host metabolic and circadian pathways 80 . Accordingly, microbial cues engage host 466 

circadian regulatory networks to reprogram metabolic pathways in a time-of-day–dependent 467 

manner, thereby maintaining appropriate carbohydrate flux and metabolic plasticity 81-83 . In 468 

addition, the gut microbiome contributes to circadian regulation of host metabolism by 469 

modulating metabolite oscillations in a clock- and diet-dependent manner, highlighting microbes 470 

as active components of temporal metabolic control 54 . Recent work has also identified circadian 471 

clock–associated neuroendocrine pathways that regulate oogenesis 84 . Our findings are in line 472 

with these observations and suggest an additional, metabolism-linked upstream layer of 473 

regulation mediated by the gut. Together, these findings suggest that microbial modulation of gut 474 

metabolism may coordinate circadian programs across the gut–brain axis, thereby promoting 475 

germline stem cell activity and supporting oogenesis. 476 

At the level of ovarian output, endocrine signaling in ovarian somatic cells represents a key 477 

step linking systemic cues to niche activity. To understand how systemically generated signals 478 

are decoded within the ovary, we leveraged single-cell transcriptomic analyses to resolve cell-479 

and stage-specific responses to microbial cues. Our single-cell analysis provides further 480 

resolution into how these extrinsic cues are interpreted within the germline. Previous studies 481 

have shown that germline metabolic requirements and stress responses are dynamically 482 

reprogrammed across differentiation, enabling selective engagement of pathways such as 483 

glycolysis or the PPP in a stage-dependent manner 50,67 . In this context, microbial cues appear to 484 

be integrated through these pre-existing metabolic states, resulting in differential and stage-485 

specific transcriptional outputs rather than uniform activation across the lineage (Fig. 7C-G; 486 

Supplementary Fig. 7D). In contrast, somatic niche lineages —including FSCs, ECs, and 487 

TF/CCs—displayed stable transcriptional profiles across conditions. This observation is 488 

consistent with the established role of ovarian niche cells in providing relatively stable signaling 489 

that modulates germline behavior primarily through localized signaling rather than large-scale 490 

transcriptional responses 85-87 . Consistent with classical models in which germline stem cell 491 

identity is maintained by niche-derived signals and ECs provide a differentiation niche for the 492 

early germline 88-90 , single-cell atlases of the Drosophila ovary exhibit pronounced state-493

Collectively, our findings establish a mechanistic model (Fig. 7J) in which environmental 499 

microbes coordinate reproductive physiology through inter-organ communication. In this model, 500 

microbial colonization of the gut activates glycolysis and PPP, elevating NADPH production and 501 

anabolic intermediates that support systemic metabolic and endocrine outputs. These gut-502 

derived metabolic cues potentiate ecdysone and JH signaling in ovarian somatic cells, thereby 503 

promoting GSC proliferation. In parallel, microbial modulation of gut metabolism alters circadian 504 

clock gene expression along the gut–brain axis, potentially allowing metabolic flux to be 505 

coordinated with hormonal output. Future studies will be required to identify the specific 506 

microbial metabolites that initiate these systemic responses and whether changes in circadian 507 

rhythms accompany changes in circadian clock gene upregulation. Together, these findings 508 

reveal how environmental microbes shape reproductive physiology by linking metabolic state, 509 

circadian gene expression, and endocrine signaling across tissues. 510

Drosophila strains 513

Drosophila microbe-sensitization assays 517 

Microbe-sensitization assay are performed as previously described 21 . To generate microbe-rich 518 

(M+) conditions, five male pupae were placed into fresh food vials. After 3-4 days, the males 519 

were removed and replaced with five female pupae. For microbe-poor (M-) conditions, no male 520 

pupae were introduced into the vials. Female pupae were cultured for three days prior to the 521 

evaluation of oogenesis. For each experiment, three M+ and three M− vials were prepared. 522 

Germline stem cell (GSC) numbers were analyzed using a chi-square test (**** P ≤ 0.001, *** P ≤523 

0.005, ** P ≤ 0.01, * P ≤ 0.05, n.s., nonsignificant ( P > 0.05)). 524

Analysis for RNA-seq data 533 

Raw 150 bp-paired-end RNA-seq reads in FASTQ format were quality-checked and trimmed 534 

using fastp (ver. 0.20.1) with default parameters unless otherwise specified. Adapter sequences 535 

were automatically detected, and low-quality bases were trimmed from both read ends. Quality-536 

trimmed paired-end reads were aligned to the Drosophila melanogaster reference genome 537 

(BDGP ver.6.32, FlyBase) using STAR (ver. 2.5.2b) with the default parameters unless 538 

otherwise specified. Genome index was pre-built from the reference FASTA files and the 539 

corresponding gene annotations (GTF) files. The resulting BAM files were sorted and indexed 540 

using SAMtools (ver. 1.10) and used for downstream quantification with featureCounts 541 

(ver.2.0.0). Gene-level read counts were generated using the featureCounts from the Subread 542 

package in paired-end mode, with exon features and gene-level summarization. 543 

The resulting count matrix was used for differential expression analysis with DESeq2 (ver. 544 

1.44.0) in R (ver. 4.4.0) 91 . Raw counts were combined with sample metadata (tissue type and 545 

experimental condition) and converted into a DESeqDataSet object using 546 

DESeqDataSetFromMatrix. Counts were normalized and variance-stabilized using the variance 547 

stabilizing transformation (VST). Differential expression analysis was performed using a negative 548 

binomial generalized linear model including condition and batch effects in the design formula. 549 

Differential expression between the M+ and M− conditions was assessed using Wald tests. Log ₂550 

fold changes were shrunk with the apeglm method to improve effect size estimation for lowly 551 

expressed genes. P values were adjusted for multiple testing using the Benjamini–Hochberg 552 

procedure, and genes with an adjusted P value < 0.05 were considered significant. 553

axis and −log ₁₀ (P value) plotted on the y-axis. Vertical dashed lines indicate the fold-change 561 

threshold (log ₂FC = 1.5). Selected genes of interest were highlighted in red. 562
