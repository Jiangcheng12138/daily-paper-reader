INTRODUCTION 37 

Secretion is a primary function of the liver. Grams of albumin are synthesized by 38 

the human liver and secreted into the blood each day 1. The liver supplies the circulation 39 

with carrier proteins, apolipoproteins, coagulation factors, complement factors, and 40 

hormones 2, many of which are clinical biomarkers. Proteins secreted by the liver, termed 41 

hepatokines, mediate autocrine 3 and paracrine signaling 4 within the liver as well as 42 

endocrine signaling that underlies inter-organ crosstalk 5. Hepatokines contribute 43 

significantly to the pathophysiology of metabolic disease, and as such, are potential 44 

therapeutic targets. 45 

The liver is a temporally dynamic organ, as many of its primary functions exhibit a 46 

circadian rhythm. The mouse liver circadian proteome is enriched for well-characterized 47 

secreted proteins 6,7 and a recent study showed that at the transcript and protein level, the 48 

clock protein BMAL1 specifically targets and regulates proteins annotated as secreted 8.49 

In addition, certain hepatokines are regulated by the molecular clock in the liver or 50 

mediate circadian functions, including RBP4 9 and FGF21 10 , two hepatokines implicated 51 

in metabolic disease. 52 

Secreted proteins are involved in intercellular and intertissue circadian regulation. 53 

An active classical secretory pathway is required for the generation of a robust population-54

The link between circadian regulation and protein secretion may be especially 64

Functionally characterizing the full repertoire of liver secreted proteins and their 73

The hepatocyte clock regulates hepatic protein secretion 219 

Time-dependent secretion may involve temporal regulation stemming from the 220 

molecular clock. We measured hepatic mRNAs for a set of ten time-dependent secreted 221 

proteins and found extensive differential expression in hepatocyte-specific Bmal1 222 

knockout mice ( Bmal1 hep-/-) (Supplementary Fig. 4a). Altered expression was appreciable 223 

at the protein level; for example, FGB was upregulated at several diurnal timepoints, 224

Endostatin tunes adipocyte metabolism to the inactive, fasting phase 332 

Next, we sought to determine if hepatocyte Bmal1 influences circulating 333 

abundance of endostatin which could, in turn, affect distal tissues via endocrine signaling. 334 

In WT mice, serum endostatin was time-of-day-dependent, with a small peak at ZT8 (Fig. 335 

5a). This peak was not observed in Bmal1 hep-/- mice, which exhibited elevated serum 336 

endostatin at the dark to light transition (ZT0). 337 

To interrogate time-dependent endocrine effects of endostatin, adult 15-week-old 338 

male and female mice were administered tail vein injections of 100 ug recombinant 339 

human endostatin or vehicle control at ZT6 or ZT18, near the natural peak and trough of 340 

serum endostatin, respectively (Fig. 5b). Based on the ~3 h half-life of recombinant 341 

endostatin 43 , tissue was collected 2 h post-injection. Food was removed during this 2 hour 342 

period to control for acute food intake. Blood glucose was not significantly altered by 343 

vehicle or endostatin compared to pre-injection values (Supplementary Fig. 8a,b). 344 

Analysis of epididymal white adipose tissue (eWAT), a previously identified target of 345 

endostatin, revealed that endostatin lowered the expression of several genes that are 346 

critical to the metabolic activities of adipocytes, including the Pparg2 isoform that 347 

regulates adipogenesis 44 (Pparg2 , Srebf1 , Lipe , Mgll , Fabp4 ). These effects were 348

DISCUSSION 376 

In this study, we identified hepatokines regulated by time-of-day, the hepatocyte 377 

clock, and sex, involved in various functions. We further characterized the temporal 378 

secretion mechanism and temporal functional effect of one prominent hepatokine, 379 

COL18A1/endostatin, finding that heightened fasting phase secretion of endostatin acts 380 

on adipocytes to lower mitochondrial respiration and promote lipolysis. Altogether, our 381 

results implicate the temporal regulation of hepatokines in systemic homeostasis. 382 

Previous studies show that secreted proteins are enriched in the liver circadian 383 

proteome at specific times of day 6,7 . Proteins comprising the secretory machinery in the 384 

ER, Golgi, and vesicles exhibit a rhythm that peaks roughly around the night-to-day 385 

transition. Here we found that Bmal1 hep-/- liver secretes less albumin, and that Bmal1 hep-/-

386 

mice and mice exposed to chronic jet lag exhibit a drop in total serum protein abundance 387 

during the inactive, fasting phase near ZT8. Albumin and a few other liver-secreted 388 

proteins account for most of the total protein content of the blood 1. Therefore, timing of 389 

hepatic secretion may be critical to stabilize total serum protein abundance over the 390 

course of a day. For all proteins, we found a weak correlation between liver protein 391 

abundance and secretion according to time-of-day, but a moderate correlation according 392 

to disruption of Bmal1 . Thus, the effect of clock disruption on the hepatic proteome can 393 

be observed in the secretome to a considerable degree. It is unlikely that one rhythm 394 

dictates the timing of all secreted proteins given the various control mechanisms that 395

Evidence also supports a role for feeding rhythms as a key component of circadian 399 

control of secreted proteins. In humans, mistimed eating and sleeping induced by 400

ambulatory counts). Data from a 2-week period at the end of the paradigm was analyzed 875
