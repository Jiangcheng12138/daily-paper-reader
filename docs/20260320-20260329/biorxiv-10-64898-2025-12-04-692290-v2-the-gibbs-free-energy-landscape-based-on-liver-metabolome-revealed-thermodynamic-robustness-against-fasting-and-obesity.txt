SUMMARY 23 

Mammalian liver metabolism undergoes  a substantial  shift during fasting. Thermodynamic 24 

principles impose fundamental constraints on metabolism , and  the Gibbs free energy change 25 

of reaction  (Δ𝑟 𝐺 ′) indicates  the  reaction’s direction and distance from equilibrium. However, 26 

Δ𝑟 𝐺 ′ landscape s in intact mammalian organs  remain  largely uncharacterized.  Here, we 27 

mapped  Δ𝑟 𝐺 ′ profile  of  glucose metabolism  in mouse liver  during fasting, using 28 

experimentally measured absolute metabolite concentrations  and a newly  developed 29 

computational method,  GLEAM . We found that despite  large  metabolite  fluctuations  during 30 

fasting , the corresponding  Δ𝑟 𝐺 ′s remained  robust , even for  reactions  revers ing  the  direction 31 

between  glycolysis and gluconeogenesis.  A remarkable thermodynamic robustness  is found 32 

in  maintaining  potential  candidates of  rate -limiting  step s during  fasting.  This  robustness  is 33 

achieved by expending  substantial  cost s for  enzyme  expression s, contributing to efficient 34 

switching  from  glycolysis  to  gluconeogenesis . Furthermore , obese m ouse  liver also showed 35 

thermodynamic robustness despite  obesity -induced metabolic disruption . Our framework 36 

provide d a n ovel  thermodynamic perspective on  intact  organ  metabolis m, demonstrating that 37 

the liver  robust ly  maintains thermodynamic  characteristics  favorable  for metabolic  control 38 

by buffering metabolite concentration  differences .39

INTRODUCTION 43 

Blood glucose homeostasis is  crucial  in mammals  for  providing stable  energy sources  to 44 

organs and maintaining health.  During fasting , t he liver plays a central role in maintaining 45 

blood glucose homeostasis by shifting its metabolism from glycolysis to gluconeogenesis  1–

46 

> 5

. This metabolic shift is a  highly  regulated process that involves  the  directional  revers al  of 47 

metabolic reactions and fluctuations in  the concentrations of numerous metabolites  6–8.48 

Obesity induces metabolic dysregulation in the liver  7–12 , leading to impaired glucose 49 

homeostasis  13 –17  and the development of further diseases , including type 2 diabetes  9,18 –20 .50 

51

zero when the reaction reaches equilibrium. Therefore,  Δ𝑟 𝐺 ′ indicates the reaction distance 55 

from equilibrium.  Δ𝑟 𝐺 ′ can be calculated as the difference between the Gibbs free energy 56
