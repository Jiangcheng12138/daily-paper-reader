Abstract: 28 Brown adipose tissue (BAT) exhibits exceptional metabolic plasticity, rapidly increasing energy 29 expenditure to sustain thermogenesis during cold exposure. This high metabolic activity imposes 30 substantial demands on cellular systems, requiring robust adaptive mechanisms to maintain 31 homeostasis and prevent cellular stress. Yet, the pathways that support and coordinate these 32 adaptive responses in brown adipocytes remain incompletely understood. Here, we identify a cold-33 induced adaptive program in BAT characterized by the formation of endoplasmic reticulum-34 plasma membrane (ER-PM) contact sites and the activation of store-operated calcium entry 35 (SOCE), which is essential for maintaining brown adipocyte health during thermogenic activation. 36 Cold exposure enhances ER-PM contacts and upregulates the expression of STIM and Orai 37 proteins, key mediators of SOCE. Loss of STIM in brown adipocytes disrupts intracellular Ca²⁺ 38 homeostasis and induces aberrant aggregation of ER membranes. STIM deficiency also impairs 39 cold-induced mitochondrial fission resulting in hyperfused mitochondria with reduced oxidative 40 capacity, independently of UCP1 abundance. Importantly, mice lacking STIM in BAT exhibit 41 impaired lipid oxidation, are cold intolerant and develop exacerbated peripheral insulin resistance 42 when challenged with a high-fat diet. Together, these findings identify ER-PM remodeling and 43 STIM-mediated SOCE as a central regulator that links organelle architecture to brown adipocyte 44 function and contributes to whole-body metabolic homeostasis. 45

Introduction: 47 

Brown adipose tissue (BAT) exhibits a unique ability to promote futile nutrient oxidation and 48 convert chemical energy into heat, making it a primary site of non-shivering thermogenesis 1,2 . The 49 thermogenic capacity of brown adipocytes is largely attributed to their high mitochondrial content, 50 uncoupling oxidative phosphorylation from ATP synthesis through UCP1 and the activation of 51 futile cycles, including creatine and lipid cycling 3–7 . To maintain high oxidative activity, brown 52 adipocytes rely on both intracellular and circulating lipids, glucose and branched-chain amino 53 acids, thereby affecting systemic fuel levels 4,6,7 . BAT activity also impacts systemic metabolism 54 by secreting a range of proteins, lipids, and metabolites that exert peripheral metabolic effects 8. In 55 a recent large-scale study, 18 F-FDG PET scans revealed that individuals with detectable BAT had 56 a lower prevalence of cardiometabolic diseases 9,10 . Conversely, reduced thermogenic capacity of 57 adipocytes with age plays a key role in driving chronic diseases 11 . Thus, activation of BAT 58 metabolic activity poses a potentially important therapeutic target for metabolic diseases. 59 

60 

Most research in the BAT field has focused on mechanisms that regulate mitochondrial metabolism 61 and uncoupled respiration through UCP1 1,5,12 . However, organelles do not function in isolation. 62 Emerging studies have revealed that other organelles, particularly the endoplasmic reticulum (ER), 63 are also critical for maintaining the thermogenic capacity of BAT. For instance, cold exposure 64 activates the ER-associated transcription factor NFE2L1, effecting proteasome activity and 65 promoting protein quality control 13 . Disruption of this response leads to accumulation of 66 ubiquitinated mitochondrial proteins, resulting in BAT dysfunction 13. Similarly, recent studies 67 have shown that inhibition of ER-associated protein degradation leads to cold intolerance 14 , and 68 activation of PERK, a core component of the ER unfolded protein response, to regulate 69 mitochondrial protein import and cristae remodeling in response to cold 15 . In addition, a futile Ca 2+ 

70 cycling in the ER mediated by SERCA has been shown to induce thermogenesis independently of 71 UCP1 16,17 . Thus, ER-related proteins and processes are critical to sustaining mitochondria 72 homeostasis and metabolic activity of brown adipocytes. 73 74 In most cell types the ER is highly abundant, forming extensive physical contacts with all other 75 organelles and the plasma membrane (PM) to coordinate the exchange of lipids, ions and 76 metabolites 18,19 . Remodeling of ER inter-organelle physical communication is a central aspect of 77

4the ER's adaptation in response to environmental changes, including nutritional challenges 19–21 .78 However, in brown adipocytes, the ER appears relatively sparse compared to the abundant 79 mitochondria that dominate the cellular landscape 22 . How the ER is spatially organized in these 80 cells and whether dynamic remodeling of ER structure and contact sites is required to support BAT 81 functional adaptation to shifting metabolic demands remains poorly understood. This gap in 82 knowledge is partly due to the technical challenges associated with high-resolution ultrastructural 83 imaging of BAT, which has a uniquely complex subcellular architecture and is rich in lipid content. 84 85 Here, we show that cold exposure induces extensive remodeling of ER-PM contact sites in brown 86 adipocytes, facilitating a previously unrecognized Ca 2+ signaling mechanism in BAT via STIM-87 mediated store-operated Ca 2+ entry (SOCE). While oscillations in cytosolic Ca²⁺ has been shown 88 to modulate adrenergic signaling in brown adipocytes 23,24 , our findings identify SOCE at ER-PM 89 contact sites as a distinct and essential mode of Ca²⁺ regulation required for remodeling of 90 mitochondrial and ER structure and function during thermogenic activation. Importantly, loss of 91 STIM-mediated SOCE in BAT uncouples adrenergic signaling from organelle dynamics, leading 92 to impaired thermogenic capacity, cold intolerance, and insulin resistance during obesity. 93

Cold exposure leads to ER-PM contact site remodeling as a previously unrecognized feature 96 

of BAT activation. 97 

To investigate how ER architecture adapts to thermogenic activation in vivo, we performed high-98 resolution 2D transmission electron microscopy (TEM) and 3D volume EM through Focused-Ion-99 Beam-Scanning Electron Microscopy (FIB-SEM) of murine brown adipose tissue. ER, 100 mitochondria, lipid droplet, and nuclei were identified by manual labeling and automated 101 segmentation using 3D-UNet-convolutional neuronal network models 25 . In mice housed at room 102 temperature (RT), 2D TEM images depicted scarce ER either dispersed throughout the cytosol or 103 associated with mitochondria and lipid droplets ( Fig. 1A) . However, 3D reconstruction showed 104 that the ER forms a network of membranes interspersed between the lipid droplets, mitochondria 105 and other organelles ( Fig. 1B-C, video 1 ). Distinct from other cells like hepatocytes 25 , brown 106 adipocytes lacked parallelled stacks of rough ER sheets ( Fig. 1B, C ). The relative abundance of 107 ER was around ~1.5% of the cellular volume, whereas mitochondria and lipid droplets occupied 108 about 20% and 38%, respectively ( Fig. 1D ). 109 110 We then compared the subcellular organization of the ER and mitochondria in BAT isolated from 111 mice housed at RT or exposed to cold (4°C) for 12 hours. Consistent with previous reports 26 , cold 112 exposure induced mitochondrial fission, resulting in rounder organelles with increased circularity 113 and reduced aspect ratio compared to the more elongated mitochondria present in BAT from mice 114 at RT ( Extended Fig. 1A-D ). In cold-exposed BAT, the ER maintained its organization as a 115 continuous network. However, in this condition, a significant fraction of ER membranes were 116 redistributed toward cortical regions of the cell ( Fig. 1E, F, video 2,3 ). Cold exposure also led to 117 a marked increase in ER-PM contacts (~18nm apart) in brown adipocytes (indicated in red in 118 

Fig. 1G, video 4, 5 ). Manual annotation of TEM images confirmed that ER-PM contact sites were 119 rarely observed in mice housed at RT but became abundant in response to cold exposure ( Fig. 1H; 120 

Extended Fig. 1E-F ). In some cold-exposed cells, the ER formed tri-way junctions involving 121 simultaneous contacts with the PM and mitochondria ( Fig. 1H; Extended Fig. 1F ). We further 122 found that cold exposure not only reorganized ER-PM contacts, but also significantly increased 123 ER-mitochondria interactions, as revealed by quantitative analysis ( Fig. 1I ), suggesting that in 124 BAT, cold exposure significantly remodels organellar network interactions. 125

6Cold-induced thermogenesis is driven by adrenergic signaling in response to norepinephrine 126 (NE) 27,28 . To test whether adrenergic signaling in general drives ER remodeling toward the plasma 127 membrane, we treated differentiated brown adipocytes with NE and measured ER-PM contact site 128 abundance using MAPPER, a fluorescent reporter composed of an N-terminal ER-targeting signal, 129 a transmembrane domain anchoring it to the ER, and a C-terminal polybasic motif that binds 130 negatively charged PM phospholipids at a ~25 nm spacing 29 (Extended Fig. 1G ). Cold exposure leads to upregulation of STIM and Orai at ER-PM contact sites. 138 

ER-PM contact sites are key regulators of intracellular Ca 2+ homeostasis through store-operated 139 Ca 2+ entry (SOCE) 18,30,31 . Given the prominent expansion of ER-PM contacts during cold exposure 140 and the central role of Ca 2+ signaling in brown adipocyte function 16,23,24,32 , we hypothesized that 141 cold-induced ER remodeling allows SOCE activation to support Ca²⁺ homeostasis in conditions of 142 increased thermogenic demand. 143 SOCE is mediated by the transmembrane ER protein and Ca 2+ sensor STIM and the PM-localized 144 Orai Ca 2+ channels. Under resting conditions, STIM is distributed throughout the ER membrane 145 and bound to Ca 2+ . Upon ER luminal Ca 2+ depletion, STIM oligomerizes and translocates to ER-146 PM contact sites, where it interacts with Orai to promote Ca 2+ influx from the extracellular space 147 into the cytosol. This imported Ca 2+ is then taken up into the ER to replenish Ca 2+ stores and also 148 coordinate Ca 2+ signaling in the cytosol 18,30,31 . Two STIM isoforms (STIM1 and STIM2) and three 149 Orai isoforms (Orai1-3) are expressed with tissue-specific patterns. Our analysis revealed that 150 STIM1 is the main isoform expressed in adipose tissues and is enriched in BAT compared to other 151 adipose depots ( Extended Fig 2A-B ). Orai1 and Orai3 are expressed in adipose tissues with Orai3 152 expression being higher in BAT compared to other depots, while Orai2 is not detected in any 153 adipose depot ( Extended Fig 2C ). To our knowledge, this represents the first systematic 154 characterization of STIM and Orai isoform expression across adipose depots, providing a 155 molecular framework for understanding how SOCE is configured in thermogenic versus non-156

STIM is down regulated in obesity and STIM1/2 deficient mice show metabolic 341 

dysregulation. 342 

To determine the potential translational relevance of these findings, we examined the role of 343 STIM1/2 in adipocytes in the context of obesity. Although BAT activation exerts broad metabolic 344 benefits under physiological conditions 4,6,7 , chronic obesity is associated with reduced BAT mass 345 and activity in both mice and humans 42,43 . To investigate whether altered STIM-mediated SOCE 346 is involved in BAT dysfunction in obesity, we determined STIM1 and STIM2 expression in BAT 347 from mice fed chow or a high-fat diet (HFD) for 16 weeks. As shown in Fig. 6A , STIM1 and 348 STIM2 expression were significantly lower in BAT from HFD-fed obese mice compared to lean 349 controls. To determine the impact of STIM deficiency on systemic metabolism in the context of 350 obesity, we fed STIM1/2 flox and STIM1/2 AdpKO mice with HFD and examined how this intervention 351 affected systemic metabolic parameters. STIM1/2 flox and STIM1/2 AdpKO gained body weight at a 352 similar rate over the course of the HFD ( Fig. 6B ). However, when challenged with an insulin 353 tolerance test, STIM1/2 AdpKO mice showed significantly higher insulin resistance compared to 354 controls ( Fig. 6C ). Increased insulin resistance was also observed by oral glucose tolerance test, 355 since to maintain similar glucose excursion rates, STIM1/2 AdpKO mice released significantly higher 356 levels of insulin ( Fig. 6D, E ). Discussion: 370 371 Remodeling of ER contact sites is increasingly recognized as a key regulator of cellular 372 metabolism, facilitating adaptation to environmental changes through the regulation of 373 intracellular Ca 2+ transport, lipid exchange, organelle dynamics, and other mechanisms 18,19 .374 However, most studies have relied on immortalized cell lines, limiting our understanding of how 375 these structures function in specialized tissues such as adipose tissue in a physiological context. 376 Our previous work characterized the 3D structural organization of ER in liver tissue across fasting, 377 fed, and obese states, revealing that ER morphology and its inter-organelle interactions are highly 378 sensitive to metabolic conditions. This dynamic remodeling is disrupted in obesity, impairing 379 tissue's ability to adapt to metabolic demands 20,25 .380 381 In this study, we leveraged high-resolution 3D volume EM and TEM to resolve organelle 382 morphology in BAT, providing the first high resolution 3D ultrastructural characterization of ER 383 morphology in this tissue. Our findings reveal that dynamic remodeling of ER-PM contact sites 384 during cold exposure is essential for precise cytosolic and inter-organellar Ca 2+ handling, which 385 in turn sustains signaling pathways that regulate substrate utilization and organelle structure and 386 dynamics ( Extended Fig.7 ). These mechanisms are central for enabling brown adipocytes to meet 387 the elevated metabolic demands imposed by cold exposure and contribute to insulin sensitivity in 388 the context of obesity. 389 390 Intracellular Ca 2+ signaling is tightly controlled in space and time, coordinating a wide range of 391 cellular responses. In brown adipocytes, activation of Ca 2+ influx through the plasma membrane 392 L-type voltage-gated Ca 2+ channels in response to adrenergic stimulation leads to rise in cytosolic 393 Ca 2+ and activate downstream PKA signaling 24,44,45 . In this study, we demonstrate that 394 intracellular Ca 2+ regulation in brown adipocyte response to adrenergic stimulation is more 395 complex than previously anticipated. Adrenergic signaling induces ER Ca 2+ release which 396 subsequently activates SOCE, resulting in a sustained elevation of cytosolic Ca 2+ levels, followed 397 by ER Ca 2+ replenishment. Disruption of this regulatory system markedly alters the kinetics of 398 cytosolic Ca²⁺ signaling, reduces steady-state ER Ca²⁺ storage, and limits Ca²⁺ uptake by 399 mitochondria. These changes impair lipid mobilization and compromise mitochondrial structure 400 and function, at least in part by disrupting the proper activation of kinase signaling pathways, 401
