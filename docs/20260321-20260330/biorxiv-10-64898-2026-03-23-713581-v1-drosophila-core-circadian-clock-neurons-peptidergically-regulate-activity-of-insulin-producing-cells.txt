13 Abstract 

14 Central pacemaker neurons use a combination of external stimuli and neuropeptide 15 signaling to synchronize molecular oscillations leading to circadian behaviors. The clock 16 network structure and signaling between these pacemaker neuron groups have been 17 well described, but how these pacemakers communicate with specific brain output 18 regions remains poorly understood. Here, we identified how “core” clock neurons in 19 Drosophila , the ventrolateral neurons (LNvs), signal to the proto-hypothalamic region, 20 the pars intercerebralis (PI). Previously thought to communicate with the PI only 21 indirectly, we provide evidence to show that LNvs functionally modulate insulin-22 producing cells (IPCs) of the PI in a time-of-day-dependent manner. This functional 23 connectivity relies on neuropeptidergic signaling of two classical clock neuropeptides: 24 pigment dispersing factor (PDF) and short Neuropeptide F (sNPF). Connectomic 25 analysis does not identify any direct synaptic inputs from clock neurons to IPCs. Small 26 ventrolateral clock neurons, which secrete both PDF and sNPF are 15-20 m away from 27 IPCs, suggesting that volume transmission across these distances may be possible in 28 the fly dorsal protocerebrum. Peptide application with functional imaging of IPCs 29 provides insight into how these two neuropeptides may act synergistically via their 30 receptors to signal to IPCs. Our findings indicate that LNvs can signal directly to IPCs 31 by volume transmission and also form indirect multisynaptic circuits with IPCs, which 32 may model more broadly how circadian clock peptides communicate with other clock 33 output regions.

34 Author Summary 

35 Circadian clocks in the brains of animals from flies to humans allow animals to 36 anticipate daily environmental changes and temporally coordinate internal processes. 37 The central clock in the brain serves as a master pacemaker that sets the pace of 38 circadian clocks in all other tissues. Fruit flies have been an essential model system for 39 discovering the genetic and cellular underpinnings of circadian rhythms, with work in 40 flies being awarded the 2017 Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine. Brain clocks in flies 41 and mammals use classical synaptic communication and neuromodulatory peptides to 42 signal between the neurons that make up the brain clock. Our study asks whether the 43 neuropeptides that signal between clock neurons also signal from clock to non-clock 44 neurons. We found that the signature fly clock neuropeptide pigment dispersing factor 45 signals outside the brain clock to insulin producing cells of the brain. There are no 46 synaptic connections from clock neurons to insulin producing cells, instead signaling 47 occurs by diffusion of peptide signals across tens of microns, termed volume 48 transmission. Our findings point to the possibility that neuropeptide volume transmission 49 may be a general feature not only of intra-clock signaling but also of brain clock output 50 signaling to non-clock neurons.

51 Introduction 

52 Circadian behavior and physiology are regulated by a central brain pacemaker that 53 provides entrainment signals to the molecular clocks of downstream cells and tissues. 54 Across species, the brain clock is structured as a network of molecular oscillators that 55 together serve to synchronize circadian oscillation within the brain clock and provide 56 robust outputs [1,2]. Work in model organisms has served to elucidate the brain clock 57 network structure, while less is known about how these multiple oscillators coordinate 58 signals to output regions. 59 60 The Drosophila brain clock network consists of ~240 neurons that express the cell-61 autonomous clock transcription-translation feedback loop [3,4]. Although fly clock 62 neurons are distributed throughout the central brain, rather than in a single nucleus as 63 in mammals, the essential clock circuit structure is conserved from flies to humans [5– 64 7]. Neuropeptides are crucial signaling molecules within brain clocks. In flies, the 65 signature clock neuropeptide is pigment dispersing factor (PDF) which is produced by 66 the small and large ventrolateral neurons (s- and lLNvs), except for the 5 th sLNv which 67 is PDF negative [8]. PDF serves to coordinate clock neuron groups by adjusting the 68 period and phase of the molecular clock and regulating cellular excitability [9–13]. 69 sLNvs co-express short neuropeptide F (sNPF) and glycine, while a subset of lLNvs co-70 express neuropeptide F (NPF) [14–16]. sLNvs are morning active, and release 71 neuropeptides into the dorsal protocerebrum, signaling to dorsolateral (LNd) and dorsal 72 clock neurons (DN) [11,17–19]. LNd neurons are evening active and consist of four 73 distinct subgroups identifiable by transcriptomic and connectomic differences [16,19,20].

258 259 Brain circadian clocks across phylogeny consist of highly interconnected networks of 260 peptidergic cells. This interconnectivity allows the central clock to maintain robust 261 timekeeping in the face of changing internal and external states. However, central 262 clocks must also convey time of day information outside the clock to other brain regions, 263 a process termed clock output. A central question in the field is whether clock output 264 requires synaptic transmission, or also repurposes some of the same neuropeptides 265 used for intra-clock communication for output signaling. Here we address this in the 266 Drosophila pars intercerebralis, which has been described as a circadian output hub, 267 with known modulation by DN1 and LNd clock neurons [27–29,31]. We demonstrate 268 that the “core” LNv clock neurons also provide input to insulin producing cells of the PI 269 via the classical clock neuropeptides PDF and sNPF. Functional imaging experiments 270 confirmed that stimulation of LNvs provides time-of-day dependent input to IPCs, with 271 strong morning activation in both male and female flies (Fig 1). Stimulation of LNvs in 272 the presence of TTX eliminated the GCaMP response of most IPCs, however a small 273 subset still shows an increased response, suggesting that there is direct LNv input to 274 some IPCs (Fig 1). Functional imaging found that DH44 + neurons do not respond to LNv 275 stimulation (Fig 2). We further find that the IPC response to PDF depends on the 276 presence of not only PDFR but also sNPFR (Figs 3-5). Our findings also highlight the

345 Our findings highlight the importance of in vivo functional experiments to identify 346 neuromodulatory signaling by volume transmission and to probe for sparse or variable 347 synaptic connections not detectable in connectomic datasets. Our functional 348 connectivity, peptide application, and GPCR CRISPR knockout studies suggest that 349 most IPCs respond to LNv activation, likely via bulk neurotransmission. A small subset 350 may form synaptic connections, but no synaptic connections are identified in male or 351 female fly brain connectome datasets (Fig 6). Our TTX experiments (Fig 1) do not rule 352 out the possibility of morning-specific synaptic connections from LNvs to IPC that are 353 not detectable in the available connectomes. Some fine neurites in the connectome are 354 not visible or not yet traced in public datasets, which can result in underestimation of 355 sparse synaptic connections detected experimentally. For example, prior synaptically 356 targeted GRASP identifies sparse connections from CRY - LNd clock neurons to IPCs, 357 which are not found in connectomes [29]. Additionally, recent Tango-seq identified 358 sparse synaptic connections from sLNvs to DN1ps, also not detectable in connectomes 359 [65]. Additionally, sLNvs have daily remodeling of the dorsal axon terminals projecting 360 toward the IPCs not captured by a single connectome [50,66]. However, the plastic 361 sLNv sites have been shown to primarily provide input to the clock, rather than output, 362 so this remodeling may be irrelevant to LNv signaling to IPCs [67]. LNv input to IPCs is 363 morning-specific, further work will be required to determine the molecular mechanisms 364 underlying this temporal specificity. 365 366 Given the lack of synaptic connections from LNvs to IPCs, we turned to proximity 367 analysis to estimate the possibility of peptidergic communication.
