AB STRACT 27 

Duration of nightly rest  is a trait that  varies  between individuals , influenced  by a comple x28 

interplay between  multiple genetic and  environmental factors . The  central circadian clock  that 29 

orchestrates  daily rhythm in  behavior/sleep  resides in the suprachiasmatic nucleus (SCN). 30 

Yet , how  the SCN  encodes  the “length” of  the  circadian rest phase (ρ)  remains  an open 31 

question . Here we demonstrate  that the unique G -protein -subtype  Gz contributes to this 32 

process by sculp ting  the waveform of  the  circadian cAMP -PKA activity rhythm  with in the 33 

SCN.  Genetic deletion and subsequent rescue of  Gz expression reversibly alter ed  the ρ 34 

duration , shifting it from ~10 h in  Gz +/+  mice to ~7.5 h in  Gz –/– mice, accompanied by 35 

proportional  changes in  peak  cAMP -PKA  activity duration and transcriptome  remodelin g in 36 

the SCN . Notably , i ntra -SCN cAMP activation  led to  behavioral rest , with  Gz specifically 37 

shap ing  this  response  without affecting 24 -h rhythmicity . These findings suggest that  the SCN 38 

is  not  merely a  rhythm -generator , but actively  allocat es  the  ρ-rest  period  via  Gz signaling .39

INTRODUCTION 41 

The amount of time  spent for rest or sleep  per night  differs between  individuals  and between 42 

animal species — multiple genetic and environmental /social  factors influence this trait 1-3. For 43 

example,  some  people sleep  for  5 hours  at night in a consolidated block , while others sleep 8 44 

hours ; and  people keep the  same  patterns,  from day to day, with 2 4-h rhythmicity . This  implies 45 

that periodicity and rest  duration are  likely  separate traits.  However , we know very little about 46 

the mechanism governing the  “allocation ” of rest  time  duration  in the 24 -hour  cycle,  compared 47 

to  the  well -elucidated circadian clock mechanism responsible for  generating  24 -h rhythmi city. 48 

In mammals,  the  suprachiasmatic  nucleus  (SCN)  ha s been defined as  the  central clock for  daily 49 

rhythms in  behavior and  physiology 4,5 . The SCN  generates  robust  24 -h rhythmicity  through  a50 

mechanism involving cell -autonomous transcriptional feedback  control  of  clock genes as 51 

well as inter -cellular coupling  via cell -to -cell communication 6, whi ch  ultimately  confer 24 -h52 

rhythmicity  on  gene expression  as well as  cell signaling , metabolism , and  ne uronal  firing  rate 53 

in  the SCN 6-8. Nevertheless,  the mechanism  by  which the SCN  allocates  circadian  rest  timing 54 

and duration  in  each  24 -hour  cycle  remain s largely unknown . The manners in which rest  timing 55 

and  durati on  are expressed at the molecular level in the SCN  are not fully understood. 56 

57 

The unique G -protein subclass  Gz (also initially referred to as  Gx) is an evolutionarily conserved 58 

vertebrate  G-protein that belongs to the  family  of canonical G i/o  (re f9,10 ). Similar to G i1, G i2,59 

and G i3, G z inhibits the activity of adenylyl cyclases and thereby reduces cAMP synthesis 11 .60 

However,  Gz is not just an “ersatz”  Gi. As its name suggest s,  Gz displays a range of unique 61 

properties that differentiate it from other  Gi/o  members. First, in contrast to the ubiquitous 62 

expression of  Gi1/2/3 , tissue distribution of  Gz is  rather s pecific  to brain 12,13  inc luding  the  SCN 14 .63 

Second ly , the G i/o  inhibitor , pertussis toxin (PTX) , does not inhibit G z. PTX catalyzes ADP 64 

ribosylation at a  specific  cysteine residue  conserved in  Gi/o . The  absence  of this residue  in 65 

Gz makes  it  resi stant  to PTX, posing  hurdle s in  pharmacological  approach  to  this protein 15 .66 

Thirdly, and equally as important as the first two,  Gz bears relatively low GTPase activity 16 .67

Antiphasic  24 -hour  coordination of  SCN PKA  activity  and  behavio r in  freely moving  mice 94 

The SCN cAMP signal fluctuate s in a circadian fashion in in -vitro cultured SCN slices 23,24 . In 95 

the present study, we monitored the downstream PKA activity oscillation, in vivo.  To determine 96 

the precise temporal relationship between the day/night rest –activity profiles and in vivo SCN 97 

circadian  PKA ac tivity rhythms over multiple days, we used fiber photometry ( Fig. 1a , sche -98 

matic cartoon).  In  this experiment,  we used a genetically encoded  FRET -based  PKA biosensor ,99 

AKAR3EV  (ref 25 ) for monitoring PKA activity in  vivo  and we  employed infrared video  tracking 100 

system  (ref 26 ) to simultaneously monitor animal daily behavior ( Fig.  1a ). We conducted long -term 101 

recording, initially under a regular 12 -h light  : 12 -h dark cycle (LD) condition and then consta nt 102 

dark (DD) condition, followed by a light pulse stimulation in animal’s subjective night ( Fig. 103 

1b ). Notably,  real -time monitoring revealed consistently anti -phasic relationships between in 104 

vivo FRET activity in the SCN and organismal spontaneous behavior al activity , under LD -105 

entrainment condition, DD free -running condition, and even after a light pulse -induced phase 106 

shift of the rhythms (see double -plotted temporal profiles of circadian FRET activity in  green 107 

and behavioral activity in  blue , as well as ho rizontal serial temporal profiles in  Fig. 1b ). This 108 

antiphasic temporal organization of PKA activation and behavior is consistent with previous 109 

observations from time -series cAMP enzyme -immunoassay 27  as well as cAM P signal -related 110 

immunohistochemistry 28,29 , but is more precise than these assays in terms of time resolution 111 

(every  5 min) and monitoring length (e.g.  16  days in  Fig. 1b ; see also  Supplementary  Fig. 1 ).112

DISCUSSION 357 

Daily  profiles of rest –activity behavior are  expressed  as an output of the master clock residing 358 

in the SCN.  Besides providing periodicity ( τ) to the organism, t he SCN  has a role in directing 359 

timing and duration  of  behavior  – “active ( α)”  versus “rest ( ρ)”  – of  the organism.  However ,360 

contrasting  to the extensive research on the r hythm -generation mechanism,  the manners in 361 

which daily  temporal  patterns  of  ρ (or  α) are  expressed  at  the molecular level  in  the SCN  in vivo 362 

have  remained  unexplored . By combination of  animal  behavior monitoring and  long -term  fiber 363 

photometry -based  real -time  tracing  of  PKA activity  in freely  moving  mice , we revealed  tha t364 

the  circadian PKA activity  that the SCN displayed in vivo  occur red  synchronously  with  ρ. Of 365 

note , the  unique  G-protein subclass  Gz contributes  to this process  as a key regulator for cAMP -366 

PKA  signaling in the SCN . The ge netic deletion and subsequent resto ration of  Gz expression 367 

reversibly alter  the circadian rest ( ρ) bout length, shifting it from  ~10 h in  Gz +/+  mice to 7.5 h 368 

in  Gz –/– mice, accompanied by proportional changes in the PKA acrophase length in the SCN. 369 

This reversibility only affects the lengt h of  ρ and peak duration of SCN cAMP -PKA activity 370 

rhythm, without affecting  circadian period ( τ).  Thus, G z is uniquely suited in the central clock 371 

“output” system governing  ρ: O ur data  show  th at G z contributes to  the definition of  circadian 372 

rest  period  of  the organism  by  sculp ting  the  waveform  of  circadian  cAMP -PKA  activity  rhythm 373 

in the SCN  (s ee  our model summary in  Supplementary Fig.  4).374

We  took advantage of dual real -time monitoring of intra -SCN activity and behavior . This 413

How do our findings relate to research on sleep regulation?  –It has been proposed that two key 426 

processes underlie the regulation of sleep, one represented by homeostatic sleep regulation and 427 

the other by circadian control on sleep timing (see refs 39 -41 ).  In  this paradigm, the role that we 428 

identified for Gz in the SCN  — namely,  the  determination of the time and duration of consolidated 429 

circadian rest by Gz — can be ascribed to the circadian process. However,  we should mention 430 

that  Gz is not necessary for either rhyth m-generation or perio d-determination; our data  suggest 431 

that  Gz plays a role in a unique niche within the circadian process as it only affects the size 432 

of ρ (output shaping : Supplementary Fig.  4). It is worth noting that  the tota l sleep  time per day 433 

of  Gz–/– mice  is equivalent to that of wildtype  mice  (Fig.  3b ), indicating that  homeostatic sleep 434 

regulation is  not impaired in these  mice.  Gz–/– mice exhibit  increased sleep time in the  middle 435 

of  α (Fig.  3c), suggesting a compensatory siesta rest in these mice.  These data reinforce the 436 

idea that  Gz contributes  to the circadian process  without compromising sleep homeostasis. 437 

A potential functional crosstalk  bet ween the process es  of  circadia n ρ length  regulation and 438 

homeostatic sleep regulation is an intriguing possibility that remains to be studied. 439

Supplementary Fig. 4 | A model depicting a role for G z in circadian clock system. 

G z is not necessary for either rhythm generation or circadian period determination. Rather G z has a specific contribution to output shaping. Specifically, G z contributes to shaping the acrophase of circadian cAMP-PKA activity rhythm in the SCN and thereby defines the time and duration of circadian rest timing ( ρ) of the organism. The shape of output rhythm govering ρ can be reversibly modified by G z.
