# Abstract 1

To survive and reproduce, living organisms need to maintain an efficient balance be-2

tween energy intake and energy expenditure. Changes in environmental conditions 3

can disrupt previously efficient energy allocation strategies, and organisms are required 4

to change their behaviour, physiology, or morphology to cope with the new environ-5

ment. However, how multiple phenotypic traits interact with one another and with 6

environmental conditions to shape energy allocation remains poorly understood. To 7

better understand this type of phenotype-environment interactions, we develop a pre-8

dictive framework, grounded in energetic and biophysical principles that allows us to 9

make predictions on how metabolic rate and movement speed should change in response 10 

to environmental temperature and resource supply, differentiating between short-term, 11 

acute exposure to novel conditions and longer-term exposure that allows acclimation 12 

or adaptation. We tested these predictions by exposing axenic populations of the ciliate 13 

Tetrahymena pyriformis to different combinations of temperature and resource availability. 14 

We measured population growth, cell size, respiration, and movement. Acute increases 15 

in temperature led to higher movement speeds and respiration rates, consistent with 16 

expectations from physical scaling relationships such as the Boltzmann-Arrhenius equa-17 

tion and the viscous drag acting on movement. However, by around 3.5 days after the 18 

introduction of Tetrahymena into a novel environment, all measured traits shifted toward 19 

values closer to those of the original environment. These changes likely reflect pheno-20 

typic acclimation responses that restored a more efficient energy allocation under the 21 

new conditions. Changes in cell size played a key role in this process, by simultaneously 22 

affecting multiple phenotypic traits, including metabolic rate and the energetic costs of 23 

movement. In small microbial consumers like Tetrahymena , body size can change rapidly, 24 

relative to ecological and seasonal timescales. Changes in body size can therefore be ef-25 

fectively leveraged - alongside physiological and biochemical regulations – to cope with 26 

environmental changes. 27

# Introduction 28 

To survive and reproduce, living organisms need to obtain energy from their environ-29 

ment, and to allocate it efficiently to competing processes of growth, movement, and 30 

reproduction (Alexander, 1996). Different strategies of energy allocation manifest, in 31 

practice, as different phenotypes: having a larger or smaller body size, or a different 32 

growth rate and movement patterns, for example. When the environment changes, the 33 

change can affect the organisms directly (e.g. by increasing the metabolic rate when tem-34 

perature rises) and indirectly (e.g. if resources become scarce). As a consequence, energy 35 

allocation strategies that used to be efficient in the original environment may become in-36 

efficient, and organisms are required to adapt to the new environment by changing their 37 

behaviour, or their phenotype, or they will incur a fitness cost. 38 

An extensive body of scientific literature has identified general patterns of phenotypic 39 

variation in relation to environmental variables such as temperature and the availability 40 

of resources. These empirically observed patterns are described in terms of ‘rules’ of 41 

phenotypic change such as the temperature-size rule – the observation that ectotherms 42 

grow to a larger adult size when they develop in a colder environment (Atkinson and 43 

Sibly, 1997; Angilletta Jr et al., 2004; Walters and Hassall, 2006), or the relation between 44 

metabolic rate, environmental temperature and body size observed across different taxa 45 

(Gillooly et al., 2001; Brown et al., 2004). Phenotypic traits such as the movement and 46 

the behaviour of organisms are also modulated in consistent ways by environmental 47 

variables, with temperature, again, playing an important role (e.g. Gibert et al., 2016; 48 

Abram et al., 2017). 49 

Understanding all these general patterns of phenotypic change is important because 50 

they not only affect the survival of individual organisms, but also ecological variables: 51 

population growth rate (DeLong and Hanson, 2009), carrying capacity (Bernhardt et al., 52 

2018), and predation risk (Gibert et al., 2017), with cascading effects beyond a single 53

However, it is also important to understand the ultimate causes, and particularly the 62 

adaptive value, of commonly observed phenotypic changes in relation to the environ-63

of phenotypic change: if a phenotypic shift in a particular direction brings an adaptive 65 

benefit – we could argue – this is the kind of shift that is more likely to be produced and 66 

to persist in a population on the long-term, whether mediated by plasticity or by evolu-67 

tion. Phenomena that appear to be maladaptive, like non-adaptive reaction norms could 68 

then be interpreted in terms of limited previous selective pressure when organisms are 69 

exposed to environmental conditions outside the range of those historically experienced 70 

(Ghalambor et al., 2007). Transient phenotypic changes that are different over short and 71 

long timescales (Dupont et al., 2024; DeLong et al., 2017) can also be interpreted in rela-72 

tion to adaptive value benchmarks, for instance in terms of mechanistic constraints that 73 

could prevent the implementation of plastic adaptive responses over short timescales, 74 

such as temporal lags for genetic expression and morphological changes. 75

strategies are ‘optimal’ for a given set of environmental parameters, has been provided 77 

by optimisation theory. Optimisation theory has been used to make predictions about a 78 

number of different phenotypic traits, ranging from the size and shape of an organism 79 

(Cooper and Denny, 1997; West et al., 2001), to the arrangement of its constituent units, 80

In the present work, we adopt a related framework, which involves considering the 91 

energetic costs and benefits of different phenotypic and ‘behavioural’ traits explicitly, to 92 

study how a model microorganism, the ciliate Tetrahymena pyriformis (henceforth Tetrahy-93 

mena ) responds to changes in environmental conditions characterised by differences in 94 

temperature and concentration of food. We make predictions on the direction of change 95 

of phenotypic traits (i.e. whether they are expected to increase or decrease) for four main 96 

traits: rate of population increase, cell size (expressed as cell volume), respiration (as a 97 

proxy for metabolic rate), and movement speed. While the first three of these phenotypic 98 

traits have been extensively considered in the scientific literature on thermal adaptation, 99 

movement speed is a ‘behavioural’ trait that hasn’t yet systematically and quantitatively 100 

been put in relation with the other three. 101 

We test these predictions empirically, by measuring how the phenotypic traits of 102 

Tetrahymena change under different culture conditions and different timescales. We in-103 

terpret our results based on the idea that the physical parameters of the environment af-104 

fect the organisms directly (for example, through the effect of temperature on metabolic 105 

rate) and in so doing they alter the energetic costs and benefits of different phenotypic 106 

traits and behaviours. For example, water viscosity affects the cost of movement, and 107

Prediction 3: Short-term changes of movement speed and metabolic rate 236 

are unlikely to be sustainable in the long-term. 237 

The changes of metabolic rate and movement speed with environmental temperature 238 

predicted above reflect simple physico-chemical changes in the rate of metabolic reac-239 

tions, and in the physical forces acting on the microorganism from the environment 240 

(such as the drag) but they are unlikely to reflect efficient energy allocation strategies for 241 

the fitness of the individual organism. 242 

For example, an increase of movement speed at high temperature, and the associated 243 

increase of metabolic power P allocated to movement (as in eq. 1) is only beneficial if it 244 

is also matched by a corresponding increase in energy intake from feeding F. However, 245 

this is unlikely to be the case because the energetic gains from feeding are expected to 246 

increase more slowly than the energetic costs of movement. In simple encounter-based 247 

models, the volume searched for food – and thus the attack rate – scales at most linearly 248 

with speed (at best, F ∝ U in eq. 4), while the energetic cost of movement increases 249 

faster than speed ( P ∝ U2). 250 

This mismatch between feeding energy acquisition rate and power consumption of 251 

movement is robust to the details of model formulation: any mechanism for which 252 

feeding increases sub-quadratically with speed will lead to the same qualitative outcome. 253

Consideration: Changes in cell volume and morphology can modulate 307 

metabolic rate and movement speed 308 

Phenotypic changes that affect the total metabolic rate of an organism, and the alloca-309 

tion of metabolic energy to maintenance, movement, growth, and reproduction, could 310 

involve changes in its physiology, e.g. biochemical regulations related to enzyme struc-311 

ture, changes in the concentration of regulatory intracellular molecules, density of mito-312 

chondria etc. (see e.g. Angilletta, 2009 for a review). However, also morphological traits 313 

such as cell volume and elongation can affect metabolic rate and movement speed. We 314 

see this in equation 2, where metabolic rate scales with cell volume and in equation 3, 315 

where power dissipation is dependent on parameters s which is itself proportional to cell 316 

radius. For this reason, we can expect that modulations of metabolic rate and movement 317 

speed can be mediated by changes in cell volume and shape, in addition to changes in 318 

physiology. 319

Table 1: Summary of theoretical predictions and factors to be controlled. 

Prediction Timescale Explanation 

(1) High temperature will lead to higher metabolic rate and faster population growth, if resources are not limiting. Both short and long timescales. The rate of metabolic reactions increases with temperature. (2) Movement speed will increase with temperature, but the increase will be described by a lower activation energy than metabolic rate. Short timescales only. At low Reynolds numbers, movement speed scales as the square root of the metabolic power invested for movement. (3) Under prolonged exposure to stable temperature and resources, movement speed should revert to less extreme values than under short-term exposure. Medium and long timescales. Increases or decreases of movement speed induced by temperature may be suboptimal; optimal speed is determined instead by resource acquisition rate and swimming power costs. 

Factors to be controlled Reason 

Does acclimation affect metabolic rate through physiological up- and down-regulation? Or through body size changes? 

Per capita metabolic rate can be modulated by regulating metabolic pathways, but it is also affected by body size: larger organisms consume proportionally more energy. What is the contribution of these non-exclusive mechanisms? Does acclimation affect movement speed through physiological regulations, e.g. by increasing ciliary activity? Movement speed can also be affected by morphological changes in body size and shape; for example, larger organisms are likely to move faster. Effects of temperature on oxygen availability. Temperature-dependent oxygen availability – rather than feeding resources – could limit maximum cell size and population growth rates. Different locomotion modes – swimming and crawling – could affect the relation between energy expenditure and movement speed. Theoretical arguments supporting predictions (2) and (3) above are based on organisms acting their forces on the water and do not necessarily apply to crawling organisms. Instantaneous changes associated with temperature ‘shocks’. Over very short timescales, the physiology and movement of microorganisms could be altered by the change of environmental conditions , rather than by environmental conditions themselves. 16

# Materials and Methods 329 

Cell cultures 330 

Axenic cultures of a clonal strain of Tetrahymena pyriformis , strain 1630/1W, were ob-331 

tained from CCAP ( http://www.ccap.ac.uk ) and cultured at 20 °C in a Proteose pep-332 

tone - yeast extract medium (20 g Proteose peptone + 2.5 g yeast extract in 1 L deionised 333 

water). Throughout, we will refer to this ‘standard’ medium composition as the 100 % 334 

concentration, to distinguish it from a 50 % medium (with half the concentration of Pro-335 

teose peptone and yeast extract) and a 200 % medium, with double concentration of 336 

yeast and peptone. 337 

Design of the experiment 338 

Cells were initially maintained in the exponential growth phase at 20 °C and 100 % 339 

medium concentration for approximately 50 days (under constant temperature and in 340 

the absence of light) to acclimate to our laboratory conditions in preparation for the 341 

experiment. At the beginning of the experiment, cultures were split across 9 different 342 

treatments (3 temperature conditions × 3 medium concentrations), and were exposed to 343 

these stable growth conditions for at least three weeks (corresponding to a minimum of 344 

20 generations), in four replicates for each condition (see fig. 2 for a schematic diagram of 345 

the experiment and Appendix section 1 for a detailed description of the culture protocol). 346 

We chose a treatment duration of at least 3 weeks because previous studies had shown 347 

that after such duration Tetrahymena develop detectable phenotypic changes (Fussmann 348 

et al., 2017). Throughout the entire study, exponential growth was maintained by adopt-349 

ing a serial transfer regime, which involved the transfer of approximately 1 mL of culture 350 

into approximately 24 mL of fresh growth medium every 1-4 days, as required to keep 351 

population densities within the exponential growth range (details in the Appendix, sec-352

Population growth 366 

Population densities were estimated at the time of each subculture into fresh medium 367 

(i.e. every 1 to 4 days) by counting cells under the microscope. Assuming exponen-368 

tial growth throughout the experiment, the per capita growth rate of the population (or 369 

‘growth rate’, for brevity), expressed in number of generations per day, was calculated as 370 

log 2(N(t)/N(0))/d, where N(0) is the density at the beginning of a subculture, N(t) is the 371 

final density of the subculture, and d is the length of the subculture period in number of 372 

days. Note that we use the logarithm base 2, rather than the natural logarithm, because 373 

in the case of organisms that reproduce by binary divisions like Tetrahymena this has an 374 

intuitive interpretation as the number of generations per day (Reiss and Schmid-Araya, 375 

2010). 376

Video tracking and movement analysis 391 

We used a custom-made software written in Python (available at https://github.com/ 392 

pernafrost/Tetrahymena ) to extract trajectories and measurements of cell volume and 393 

cell morphology directly from the videos. 394 

The instantaneous movement speed of each tracked particle was measured from the 395 

trajectories as the displacement per unit time of the centre of mass of the particle, mea-396 

sured over a timescale of 1/10 of a second (3 frames). We took the median of all the 397 

instantaneous speed values along the trajectory as the individual speed of the particle. 398 

We excluded from the analysis particles that did not move and cells with an unnaturally 399 

small size that might have been incorrectly detected in the image. Unless otherwise spec-400 

ified, in the analyses and in the figures presented here we focus on the top 20 % fastest 401 

moving cells within each tested experimental condition, to avoid bias from temporarily 402 

immobilised cells. (Further details of the video tracking and movement analysis are pro-403 

vided in the Appendix, section 1.3, including an explanation for the choice of focusing 404 

the analysis on the fastest cells). 405

Feeding rate 410 

One additional variable that we consider in interpreting our results in terms of the en-411 

ergy budget of Tetrahymena is its feeding rate. We do not measure the feeding rate of 412 

Tetrahymena directly in our experiments, although we have indirect evidence about feed-413 

ing from other experimental variables such as population growth rate. We model feeding 414 

rate based on a Holling type II functional response function in which movement speed 415

# Discussion 613 

Based on well-established bio-physical principles and energetic considerations, we aimed 614 

to predict patterns of phenotypic changes that could be observed in unicellular con-615 

sumers over short and long timescales. Our main focus was in relation to patterns of 616 

movement speed. 617 

Short-term effects of environmental conditions 618 

We assumed that, over short-term, transient exposure to novel environmental conditions, 619 

the direct effects of temperature and resource availability dominate over physiological 620 

regulations and morphological phenotypic changes. This assumption allowed us to pre-621 

dict that the movement speed of swimming ciliates should have a lower activation energy 622 

than their metabolism, due to the quadratic scaling of power consumption with move-623 

ment speed for swimmers in a viscous medium (prediction 2). The thermal response 624 

curves experimentally measured on Tetrahymena broadly confirmed this prediction, as 625 

the values of activation energy measured for movement speed under a short-term, tran-626 

sient exposure were lower than those measured for respiration (a proxy for metabolic 627 

power consumption): when environmental temperature increased, respiration increased 628 

disproportionately more than swimming speed. Previous studies have also found rel-629 

atively low values of activation energy for short-term movement-speed responses in 630 

ciliates. For example, Glaser (1924) reported speed values for paramecia that correspond 631 

to an activation energy of approximately 0.37 eV, similar to our results. 632 

Nevertheless, we note that the empirical values of activation energy measured for 633 

Tetrahymena indicate a greater difference than theoretically predicted, with activation 634 

energies for short-term movement speed at 42 % on average of the activation energies 635 

for respiration, against a theoretical prediction of approximately 50 %. Such a discrep-636 

ancy can most likely be ascribed to noise in the data. It could also be due to additional 637

Conclusion 753 

Our study highlights the multiple changes associated with the response of microorgan-754 

isms to changes in their environment. Short-term, acute responses to changes in the 755 

environment are characterised by rapid and marked changes in metabolic activity and 756 

movement speed. These short-term changes mainly reflect simple physico-chemical pro-757 

cesses, such as the effects of temperature on the rates of metabolic reactions (Brown et al., 758 

2004; Arroyo et al., 2022) and the temperature dependence of water viscosity (Beveridge 759 

et al., 2010). Yet, movement and respiration are not affected in the same way by changes 760 

of temperature. This likely introduces a mismatch between energy intake and energy ex-761 

penditure that imposes behavioural and physiological adjustments. In our experiments, 762 

the medium- and long-term exposure to a new environment resulted in the mitigation 763 

of acute responses, with both metabolic rate and movement speed converging towards 764 

similar values, independently of the environment in which cells were cultured. These 765 

adjustments were largely, but not entirely, mediated by changes in cell size. 766 

An energy allocation framework can help make sense of empirically observed pat-767 

terns of phenotypic change. In future, the framework could be further refined and 768

2.11 Impact of cell size on movement speed 1336 

Larger organisms move typically faster than smaller ones. They also have a faster per 1337 

capita metabolic rate, simply because of their size. The observation that, in our exper-1338 

iments, Tetrahymena cells changed their volume rapidly and substantially, depending 1339 

on the experimental conditions, begs for the question: to what extent do the observed 1340 

changes in cell volume alone account for the other observed changes in metabolic rate 1341 

and movement speed? 1342 

To better understand the possible relation between cell volume and speed, we can 1343 

consider the example of an isogeometric unicellular organism (an organism that could 1344 

have different sizes but which preserves the same shape): its metabolic rate – and total 1345 

available power – would scale as the volume of the cell, or as the third power of the 1346 

linear dimension (assuming a linear scaling between metabolic rate and cell volume in 1347 

unicellular eukaryotes; DeLong et al., 2010), and this metabolic power would have a 1348 

positive impact on speed; the viscous drag, having a negative impact on speed, would 1349 

scale as the linear dimension (e.g. eq. 3, expanded in Appendix eq. 13). If we further 1350 

assume that a constant fraction of metabolic energy is allocated to movement, then speed 1351 

U depends on the invested power P as P = sU 2 (eq. 3). Here, P is proportional to V and 1352 

s is proportional to the linear cell dimension ( s ∝ V1/3 ). Combining these relationships 1353 

leads to the prediction: U ∝ Vα with α = 1/3. (If, instead of assuming a linear scaling of 1354 

metabolic rate with cell volume we assumed a 3/4 power scaling (as in Kleiber’s law), 1355 

the same reasoning would lead to a predicted relationship between movement speed 1356 

and cell volume as U ∝ Vα, with α = 5/24). 1357 

Based on these scaling arguments, we could predict that a change by a factor 2 in 1358 

the cell volume would translate into a factor 2 1/3 ≈ 1.26 in the change of speed (or 1359 

25/24 ≈ 1.16 if we assumed a 3/4 power scaling of metabolic rate with cell volume). 1360 

Inevitably, this isogeometric unicellular organism is only a very crude approximation 1361 

of our Tetrahymena . Among the limitations of this approximation we could mention the 1362
