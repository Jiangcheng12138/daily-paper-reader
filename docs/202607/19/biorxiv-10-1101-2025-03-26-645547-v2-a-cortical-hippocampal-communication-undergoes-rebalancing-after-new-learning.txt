Abstract 8

The brain’s ability to consolidate a wide range of memories while maintaining their distinctiveness 9

across experiences remains poorly understood. Sharp -wave ripples, neural oscillations that occur 10 

predominantly within CA1 of the hippocampus during immobility and sleep , have been shown to 11 

play a critical role in the consolidation process. More recently, evidence has uncovered functional 12 

heterogeneity of pyramidal neurons within distinct sublayers of CA1 that display unique properties 13 

during ripples , potentially contributing to memory specificity. Despite this, it remains unclear 14 

exactly how ripples shift the activity of CA1 neuron al population s to accommodate the 15 

consolidation of specific memories and how sublayer differences manifest. Here , w e studied 16 

interactions between the anterior cingulate cortex (ACC) and CA1 neurons during ripples and 17 

discovered a reorganization of their communication following learning. Specifically, using a 18 

generalized linear model decoder, we demonstrated the pre -existence of ACC -to -CA1 19 

communication, which is weakened during post -training sleep following learning , suggesting that 20 

ACC activity reallocates the contribution of CA1 neurons during memory formation . Interestingly, 21 

the reorganization appeared unique for a subset of CA1 superficial (CA1sup) neurons that were 22 

task inactive , whereas communication between the ACC and CA1 deep neurons remained largely 23 

stable across pre - and post -training sleep . Consistent with this s ublayer -selective reorganization ,24 

we found that optogenetic stimulations of the ACC preferentially suppressed CA1sup neurons 25 

while activating a unique subset of CA1 interneurons . Overall, th ese findings highlight an26 

important role of the ACC in rebalancing CA1 neuron al population s’ contribution in learning and 27 

memory consolidation .28

Introduction 29 

Memor ies are not static, rather they are gradually consolidated into long -term traces 30 

across days , week s, or longer (Klinzing et al., 2019; Squire et al., 2015) . At any given moment, 31 

we are processing and consolidating an array of differing experiences. The ability to preserve 32 

distinctiveness across these various experiences is essential for memory consolidation . This 33 

requires a delicate balance between the mechanisms that facilitate the reactivation and 34 

trans formation of new memories alongside those which regulate consolidation to prevent the 35 

intermingling of distinct experiences. While much attention has been focused on uncover ing the 36 

principles that guide the reactivation and consolidation of memories, less is known about the 37 

mechanism s that regulate these process es and govern memory specificity .38 

During sleep, sharp -wave ripples, neural oscillations predominantly within the CA1 of the 39 

hippocampus, facilitate hippocampal reactivations and accompan y cortical reactivations of 40 

memory -related neurons, a process referred to as replay (Buzsáki, 2015; Buzsáki et al., 1992; 41 

Davidson et al., 2009; Diba & Buzsáki, 2007; Foster & Wilson, 2006; Lee & Wilson, 2002) . Replay 42 

has been shown to be essential for memory consolidation as disruption or enhancement of ripples 43 

impairs or improve s memory , respectively (Ego -Stengel & Wilson, 2010; Fernández -Ruiz et al., 44 

2019; Girardeau et al., 2009; Gridchyn et al., 2020; Jadhav et al., 2012; Wang et al., 2015) .45 

Recent ly, evidence has uncovered functional and anatomical heterogeneity in CA1 neurons 46 

based on their position within the pyramidal layer: the superficial (CA1sup) and deep (CA1deep) 47 

sub layer s (Danielson et al., 2016; Geiller et al., 2017; Hall & Wang, 2023; Harvey et al., 2023; 48 

Mizuseki et al., 2011; Sharif et al., 2021) . CA1sup neurons have more stable firing rates and 49 

higher spatial acuity (Danielson et al., 2016; Harvey et al., 2023; Mizuseki et al., 2011) , w hereas 50 

CA1deep neurons respond more to enriched environments, sensory landmarks, and reward 51 

(Danielson et al., 2016; Geiller et al., 2017; Harvey et al., 2023; Sharif et al., 2021) . During replay 52 

events CA1sup neurons show increased activ ity after spatial learnin g, whereas CA1deep neurons 53 

show decreased activity (Berndt et al., 2023; Harvey et al., 2023) . Together , ripples and the 54 

recruitment of distinct subpopulations within CA1 drive new memory formation . Despite this 55 

understanding , the mechanisms which regulate their activity to balance consolidation across 56 

experiences remain unclear .57 

Emerging evidence has demonstrated the importance of suppression within the 58 

hippocampal network to balance neuronal excitability surrounding learning (Gava et al., 2024; 59 

Karaba et al., 2024; Norimoto et al., 2018) . During pre -learning sleep, ripples depotentiate 60 

synapses which , if perturbed, impair s learning (Norimoto et al., 2018) . Similarly, after learning, 61 

ripples facilitate the depotentiation of memory -unrelated neurons (Norimoto et al., 2018) .62

Additionally , during post -learning sleep, hippocampal neurons initially display heighte ne d firing 63 

rates before ultimately return ing to baseline (Giri et al., 2019; Wilson & McNaughton, 1994) . Prior 64 

studies have demonstrated that reestablishing baseline is necessary for learning , as excessive 65 

neuron al activity and synchrony impairs learning (Gava et al., 2024; Karaba et al., 2024) .66 

Suppression of the hippocampus , then, appears to play a key role in down regulating synapses ,67 

preventing memory saturation , and promoting memory flexibility (Balduzzi & Tononi, 2013) .68 

Interestingly, CA1sup neurons appear to be the most sensitive to this process (Gava et al., 2024) .69 

Following repetitive learning, CA1sup neurons become highly synchronized and coactive , which 70 

impairs future memory formation (Gava et al., 2024) . Correspondingly , optogenetic inhibition of 71 

CA1sup, but not CA1deep neurons, after learning decouples and decreases neuron al activity 72 

which reduc es rigidity and resto res new memory formatio n (Gava et al., 2024) . Still , exactly how 73 

facilitation and suppression are balanced throughout cons olidation , and whether cortical inputs 74 

play a role in modifying hippocampal activity in this process , remains poorly understood. 75 

Here, we employ in vivo electrophysiology recording across contextual fear conditioning 76 

(CFC) and sleep to understand how communication between the anterior cingulate cortex (ACC) 77 

and hippocampal CA 1 are modified following learning . Previous studies ha ve shown that the ACC 78 

displays increased activity immediately preceding CA1 ripples and is instrumental in CFC and 79 

memory consolidation (Einarsson & Nader, 2012; Frankland et al., 2004; Wang & Ikemoto, 2016) .80 

We uncovered a novel, sublayer specific, line of communication between ACC and CA1 81 

surrounding ripples with ACC preferentially communicating with CA1sup neurons . This 82 

communication rebalances following learning, suggesting a potential role in memory 83 

consolidation. 84

Our GLM analysis generated a PG score for each CA1 neuron, enabling us to examine 116

among CA1 neurons would suggest that learning weakens existing ACC→CA1 communication 121

top quartile following learning, whereas transitions between top and bottom quartiles were rare .129

Discussion 250 

Balancing network excitability with suppression after learning has long been thought to be 251 

essential for memory formation, with sleep serving a key homeostatic role (Liu et al., 2010; Tononi 252 

& Cirelli, 2003; Tononi & Cirelli, 2006; Watson et al., 2016) Excessive excitability and overly 253 

strengthened synapses can hinder neuronal flexibility and saturate the capacity for learning 254 

(Balduzzi & Tononi, 2013; Gava et al., 2024; Norimoto et al., 2018). Additionally, underregulated 255 

excitability may pathologically link multiple memory traces, leading to memory overgeneralization 256 

(Jinde et al., 2012; Kim et al., 2021) . Therefore, regulatory mechanisms that rebalance the 257 

excitability of neurons to enable neuronal flexibility are essential for memory formation (Gava et 258 

al., 2024; Karaba et al., 2024) . CA1sup neurons have been shown to be particularly susceptible 259 

to memory rigidity (Gava et al., 2024; Hall & Wang, 2023) . While activation of these neurons is 260 

necessary for the encoding of new memories, their regulation and timely suppression appear 261 

equally important to evoke flexibility and enable de novo consolidation within the hippocampal 262

Learning selectively weakens ACC coupling with task -inactive CA 1sup neurons. 271 

We implemented GLM machine learning to decode CA1 neuronal firing rates during 272 

ripples based off the spiking rates of large ACC neuronal populations preceding ripples 273 

(Rothschild et al., 2017) . The underlying principles of this analysis posit that if ACC activity 274 

preceding ripples influences subsequent activity in CA1 during ripples, the specific activity pattern 275 

of ACC neurons (predictor cells) should hold relevancy over the outcome of CA1 neurons 276 

(predicted cells). We found this to be true across all sleep sessions and neuron types, with real 277 

ACC spiking data showing significantly higher prediction gain compared to shuffled data, 278 

indicating an information flow from ACC to CA1 surrounding ripples. Interestingly, prediction gain 279 

was weakened following learning specifically for task -inactive CA1sup neurons, suggesting a 280 

selective reorganization of communication with CA1sup neurons according to their recruitment 281 

during training. However, questions remain over exactly how the ACC influences CA1sup activity 282 

and why this information flow rebalances after learning. 283 

One possible explanation is that ACC →CA1sup communication before training may reflect 284 

encoding of past experiences. Research has demonstrated that the ACC has a limited role in 285 

consolidation immediately after learning (Junyu Liu et al., 2024) . Instead, memories become ACC -286 

dependent beginning typically two days after learning (Einarsson & Nader, 2012; Frankland et al., 287 

2004; Junyu Liu et al., 2024) . It is possible that heightened pre -exist ing communication between 288 

ACC and CA1sup is reflective of consolidating past experiences. If true, decreased 289 

commu nication between regions may be due to the network’s shift towards prioritizing the 290 

encoding/consolidating of the new experience and thus the role of the ACC to CA1, consolidating 291 

past memories, is reduced (Diekelmann & Born, 2010; Huelin Gorriz et al., 2023; Wilson & 292 

McNaughton, 1994) . From a broader perspective, ACC→CA1 could function as part of default 293 

mode network activity, and learning induces network shift toward the newly acquired memory 294 

(Buckner et al., 2008; Raichle, 2015) .295

Alternatively, ACC→CA1 communication may contribute to the homeostatic downscaling 296 

of memory -unrelated synapses during sleep . E vidence finds that slow -wave sleep is strongly 297 

linked to downscaling of non -learning related neuron activity (Gulati et al., 2017; Liu et al., 2010; 298 

Tononi & Cirelli, 2003; Tononi & Cirelli, 2006; Watson et al., 2016) . Slow -wave sleep ripples in 299 

particular depotentiate memory unrelated synapses (Gulati et al., 2017; Norimoto et al., 2018) . In 300 

our study, we find that the learning -related reduction in communication between ACC and CA1 301 

was selective for task -inactive neurons. Therefore, ACC →CA1sup communication may not simply 302 

weaken following learning but rather becomes selectively disengaged from task -inactive neurons, 303 

enabling homeostatic downscaling while preserving behaviorally relevant synapses (Liu et al., 304 

2010; Norimoto et al., 2018; Tononi & Cirelli, 2003; Tononi & Cirelli, 2006; Watson et al., 2016) .305 

Interestingly , behavioral recruitment alone cannot account for the observed remodeling of 306 

ACC→CA1 communication, as CA1sup and CA1deep neurons did not display significant 307 

differences in task -related activity (Figure 2 —figure supplemental 2). Instead, these learning 308 

related changes of task inactive neurons appear sublayer specific. 309 

A potential mechanism supporting this selectivity is suggested by our optogenetic findings, 310 

which revealed preferential suppression of CA1sup neurons following ACC stimulations. ACC -311 

mediated inhibition may contribute to the gradual disengagement of subsets of CA1sup neurons 312 

overtime as memories transition toward long -term storage (Karaba et al., 2024; Raven & Aton, 313 

2021) . Such a mechanism would align with ACC’s known role in systems consolidation and be 314 

particularly useful in the regulation of CA1sup neurons as they have been shown to be susceptible 315 

to memory rigidity and overgeneralization (Einarsson & Nader, 2012; Frankland et al., 2004; Gava 316 

et al., 2024; Junyu Liu et al., 2024) . Future experiments casually manipulating this communication 317 

are needed to determine whether ACC activity directly influences the downregulation of CA1sup 318 

activity during training, or whether these regions are correlated partners whose activity is 319 

ultimately mediated by an external brain region. 320 

As research investigating CA1 heterogeneity continues to gain attention, functional 321 

distinctions across different behaviors have emerged (Hall & Wang, 2023) . Previous research has 322 

found that CA1deep neurons are more involved in reward and sensory landmark encoding, while 323 

CA1sup neurons are more involved in context and spatial encoding (Danielson et al., 2016; Geiller 324 

et al., 2017; Harvey et al., 2023) . Moreover , CA1deep place cells are more likely to remap in 325 

response to local cue changes than CA1sup place cells (Esparza et al., 2025) . There have been 326 

conflicting reports over which sublayer is more involved in learning and replay and which neurons 327 

are more plastic or rigid in response to learning (Berndt et al., 2023; Geiller et al., 2017; Grosmark 328 

& Buzsáki, 2016; Hall & Wang, 2023; Harvey et al., 2023) . We speculate that it may depend on 329

experiments utilizing appetitive learning paradigms, rather than the aversive contextual fear 357

Alternatively,  ACC →CA1 communication may be mediated by multiple intermediate 402 

structures (Behzadi et al., 1990; Oh et al., 2014; Shi et al., 2022; Souza et al., 2022) . The ACC 403

which project directly to CA1 (Oh et al., 2014; Shi et al., 2022) . The RE has a known role in 405

Ramanathan et al., 2018; Ratigan et al., 2023; Silva et al., 2021; Xu & Südhof, 2013) . Interestingly, 407

modulate CA1 activity through the MnR, potentially suppressing PV interneuron and pyramidal 417 

neuron activity via local inhibitory circuits, thereby contributing to the regulation of hippocampal 418 

oscillations and memory consolidation (Huang et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2015) . Ultimately, future 419
