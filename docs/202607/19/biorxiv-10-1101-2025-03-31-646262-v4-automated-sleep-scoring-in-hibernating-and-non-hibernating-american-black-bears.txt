# Abstract 31 

Hibernating bears show remarkable metabolic suppression. Their decline in core body 32 

temperature (T b) is moderate (from 38°C to 30-35°C), but their metabolism declines as much as 33 

75%. To understand the role of sleep in this hypometabolic state, we recorded biotelemetrically 34 

EEG, EOG and EMG data over 3500 days from 16 captive American black bears in and out of 35 

hibernation under semi-natural conditions. This data set is too large to score manually for Wake, 36 

REM- and NREM sleep, so we tested two machine learning classifiers: (1) Somnotate trained 37 

on multiple one-day recordings, and (2) Somnivore, trained on a small subset from each 38 

recording. As automated scoring methods have not been applied to hibernating species before, 39 

a major concern is the effect changing brain temperature has on the EEG and on the machine 40 

learning based detection. Therefore, we selected reference data using consensus by 3 manual 41 

sleep scorers from each of 6 bears, two one-day recordings at the highest and lowest body 42 

temperatures during hibernation when T b was oscillating in multiday cycles, and a non-43 

hibernating one-day recording in summer. Somnotate results were excellent when trained 44 

separately for hibernating and non-hibernating data. Training Somnotate separately for high and 45 

low T b within hibernation did not improve results further. Sleep times in hibernation were about 46 

2x that in summer for both automated scores and manual scores (p<0.0001). There were no 47 

significant differences in occupancy of vigilance states between automated and manual scores 48 

in hibernation (p>0.05), but a small overestimate of sleep time in summer (p<0.05). Both 49 

applications yielded F-measures against manual scores in the 0.90-0.98 range. Outliers in the 50 

0.67-0.88 range were correlated between the two applications, indicating that specific files are 51 

more challenging to annotate. We conclude that both applications have accuracies approaching 52 

that of manual scorers when trained on high quality data. 53

# Introduction 55 

Bears are an unexplored and interesting model for studying therapeutic hypometabolism and 56 

the role of sleep in that adaptation. In the hypometabolic state of hibernation black bears have 57 

been found to suppress metabolic rate to 25% of normal basal rates, while their core body 58 

temperatures (T b) on average decrease only 5.5°C during hibernation. But, their T bs do not stay 59 

at a stable level, but vary in multiday body temperature cycles in the range of 30-35°C [1, 2]. 60 

Animal studies typically classify sleep into stages of non-REM sleep (NREM), rapid eye 61 

movement sleep (REM) and Wake [3], with NREM characterized by domination of medium to 62 

large amplitude delta waves caused by synchronization of neuronal firing across cortex, REM 63 

dominated by low amplitude alpha waves and presence of bipolar EOG spikes and lack of EMG 64 

activity, and Wake with irregular high-frequency, low-amplitude EEG patterns and random EMG 65 

activity. Studies in marine mammals have also defined a stage of Drowsiness [4, 5]. Human 66 

studies additionally classify NREM further into sleep stages N1, N2 and N3 according to the 67 

American Academy for Sleep Medicine (AASM) standards [6, 7]. With the exception of certain 68 

hibernating dwarf lemur species [8], EEG based sleep studies have previously only been 69 

published for smaller hibernators that go into torpor through a period of sleep but remain at too 70 

low temperatures during deep torpor to record brain activity that can be used for classification of 71 

sleep stages. Thus sleep stages can typically only be classified in these hibernators during the 72 

episodic arousal episodes when animals return to euthermic levels of T b for one or two days [9, 73 

10, 11, 12]. While functionality of the increased deltawave at early arousal caused much 74 

controversy among these studies as to its function or even if it can be classified as sleep, in 75 

contrast, bears in hibernation do not show torpor-arousal cycles and remain at high enough 76 

body temperatures to be responsive to disturbance at any time, and because their brain 77 

temperatures do not drop below the low 30 °C [1, 2], their vigilance states can be scored 78 

throughout hibernation. 79

Presumed without data, bears are often said to be “asleep” during hibernation, but in this 81 

context it refers to the hibernating state (“winter dormancy”). No sleep studies based on brain 82 

wave activity existed in a large hibernator before we started collecting a very extensive (>3500 83 

days) repository of polysomnographic data in American black bears ( Ursus americanus ) kept in 84 

outdoor enclosures in Fairbanks, Alaska. Using telemetry implants we recorded global cortical 85 

EEG, EOG, EMG, ECG, T b, breathing and respirometry (while dens were closed) and in some 86 

of the recordings blood pressure, using telemetry implants. In contrast to small hibernators, 87 

there is need to analyze sleep architecture though the whole 5-6 month hibernation period and 88 

transitions in and out of the hibernation season to understand role of sleep in the 89 

hypometabolism of hibernating bears. Through the years of our study, our polysomnographic 90 

recordings produced too much data to score manually, and at the time data were collected 91 

reliable methods to automatically score these data in their entirety were not available. A broad 92 

range of machine learning techniques have become available for sleep classification more 93 

recently, and a number of them have been reviewed [13, 14, 15, 16]. The majority of the studies 94 

aimed at verifying machine learning based sleep classification have been performed on human 95 

data and standard laboratory animals, e.g. mice and rats. Previous studies have not verified 96 

machine learning based sleep classification of EEG recordings from hibernating animals. Thus, 97 

before any automatic scoring technique is applied to analyze these data on a larger scale, the 98 

integrity of the analysis must be verified. The variations in T b of hibernators has the potential to 99 

affect the frequency distribution of the EEG signals [17], and since machine learning techniques 100 

are typically based on a first step of convolution to extract the frequency distributions of the 101 

signals [14], there could be potential problems with correct detection if the machine learning 102 

routines are trained on data recorded at different T b‘s than occurred during the analyzed 103 

recording. For the present study we selected two different machine learning based automatic 104 

sleep qualifiers based on their previous successful verifications on animal data: (1) open source 105

Somnotate, a probabilistic classifier based on a combination of linear discriminant analysis with 106 

a hidden Markov model that has previously been tested on mouse data [18], and (2) Somnivore, 107 

which applies proprietary assisted machine learning algorithms that have been successfully 108 

tested on a wide range of human data and animal data from mice, rats and pigeons [19]. The 109 

purpose of the present study is to evaluate the integrity of automated sleep scoring using these 110 

two applications on data from American black bears ( Ursus americanus ) in and out of 111 

hibernation. 112 

113 

Due to the dissimilarity in how the two programs were designed, it was not feasible and we did 114 

not intend to perform a comparison between the two qualifiers on terms of similar training and 115 

testing conditions. While both programs can perform batch scoring on multiple files after 116 

training, Somnivore was primarily designed to be trained on a minimal amount of data from a 117 

one day recording to be tested (assisted machine learning) and currently cannot easily be 118 

trained on data from multiple files. Somnotate is able to accumulate training data from an 119 

unlimited number of files and multiple animals to generate machine learning models that provide 120 

robustness against incursion of incorrect data in the training data set and can be applied across 121 

multiple animals. For testing integrity and robustness of training models, Somnotate can 122 

automate use of holdout testing so that each file to be tested is excluded from the training data 123 

[18] and the results can then be compared to the non-holdout training model. Holdout testing 124 

would typically not be used with Somnivore because of its assisted machine learning design. 125 

We trained and tested the two applications on a reference data set that was selected from each 126 

of 6 bears, a one day recording in mid hibernation with a T b at the peak of a multiday body 127 

temperature cycle [2], a one day recording in mid-hibernation near the trough of a body 128 

temperature cycle, and a one day recording in a non-hibernating state either in later summer or 129 

after full recovery from hibernation in early summer. 130

# Material and methods 133 

## Reference data set 134 

The analyzed data were collected for monitoring purposes during a previous study, and the 135 

procedures for animal care and implantation of the telemetry transmitters are described by 136 

Tøien et al. [2]. These experiments were approved by the Institutional Animal Care and Use 137 

Committee at University of Alaska Fairbanks (IACUC nos. 05-56 and 08-64). Sleep related data 138 

from this data collection have not been published previously. Briefly, American black bears 139 

(Ursus americanos, 62-93 kg) were captured in Alaska by Alaska Department of Fish and Game 140 

(ADF&G) during May–July, transferred to Fairbanks and held individually in an outdoor facility 141 

near the Institute of Arctic Biology in Fairbanks. The bears had been scheduled to be removed 142 

from the wild by ADF&G due to bear–human conflicts and would have been euthanized if not 143 

used by the project. In late August to late September in each year of the study, bears were 144 

immobilized with 8 mg/kg Telazol administered with a pole syringe and brought into surgery. 145 

Under isoflurane anesthesia and using aseptic protocol, the bears were implanted with 146 

telemetry devices (model T28F-14B, Konigsberg Instruments Inc., Pasadena, CA 91107) for 147 

measurement of T b, EKG, and deltoid muscle EMG. Using the same devices, global cortical 148 

EEG was recorded with a single differential pair of electrodes diagonally 1 cm from the skulls 149 

midline seam and 2 cm in front of and behind the bregma. The electrodes were stainless steel 150 

bone screws that penetrated into the extradural space and were insulated with acrylic cement. A 151 

single pair of EOG electrodes was implanted subcutaneously above the eyebrows. Some of the 152 

bears were also implanted with a blood pressure transducer in the caudal aorta. During 153

We selected from each of 6 bears three 24 h recordings: from mid-hibernation with a T b at the 183

Scorers #1 and #3 had previous publication records in sleep research that included manual 187 

scoring of EEG data from mammals [20, 21, 22, 23]. Scorer #2 recorded all the data to be 188 

analyzed and had the longest experience with sleep data from hibernating bears, including the 189 

real-time observation of polysomnograms with video monitoring. None of these scorers were 190 

authors of the software that was used. As common in animal studies, we scored data into 191 

stages of NREM, REM, and Wake using 10 s epochs, and generally mapped what would be 192

transition stage of Drowsiness with intermittent medium amplitude slow waves during 196 

inspirations occupying less than 50% of an epoch. However, we found difficulty in obtaining 197 

consistency among scorers, and it did not show consistent differences in and out of hibernation. 198

## Machine learning training 210 

Due to the differences in design of the software, training differed between Somnivore and 211 

Somnotate. For training Somnivore we used 100 epochs of NREM, REM, and Wake randomly 212 

selected by the software from each file to be tested as per the design of the software. After an 213 

initial assessment we used only the EEG and EOG channels for training as the recorded EMG 214 

from the deltoid muscle showed very little activity in bears during hibernation, possibly due to 215 

the typical curled postures of the bears [1] that causes stretching of the neck muscles. We 216 

applied a scoring rule of minimum consistency of 3 epochs for each of NREM, REM and Wake, 217 

which was necessary to approximate the sensitivity to changes in signal frequencies during 218 

transitions between states. Score Forcing that was an available option was not used to prevent 219 

Wake to REM transitions as it was found that spurious qualifications of short periods of Wake in 220 

the NREM to REM transition could affect what was correct detection of REM periods. 221 

222 

Somnotate was trained on multiple complete one day recordings using the EEG and EOG 223 

channels. We used a time resolution of 10s (same as the epoch length), which was found to be 224 

a good compromise to prevent too high sensitivity to state changes during transition states. For 225 

initial assessment we used multiple training data sets. In non-holdout mode we used all the 226 

available data in a training data set, while in holdout mode we trained Somnotate with all the 227 

files in the training data set except the specific file to be tested. We created training models: a) 228 

based on a training data set with all the reference data, b) separate training data sets for 229

## Statistical analysis 234 

The metric used for detailed evaluation of algorithm generalization accuracy versus manual 235 

scoring for both applications was F-measures calculated after import of scoring results into 236 

Somnivore. It is one of the most stringent metrics available, as both high sensitivity and high 237 

precision are required for high F-measure [19, 24]. Data are given as means ± standard 238 

deviation. Further statistical analysis was performed in Sigmaplot ver. 11 and Excel. For testing 239 

of statistical significance between different tests on the same sets of animals we used two-tailed 240 

paired t-tests, otherwise two-tailed equal variance or unequal variance t-tests as found 241 

appropriate after testing the data distributions. Differences were considered significant at 242 

p<0.05. 243

# Discussion 408 

Comparing the use of two different automated scoring applications, this study is the first to score 409 

vigilance state recordings from a non-human large animal model that includes hibernation and 410 

non-hibernation. A major concern with the data from hibernators is the frequency shifts in the 411 

EEG signal with decreases in brain temperature [17] that could cause problems for correct 412 

designations of vigilance states by the automated scoring algorithms. The drop in T b of 413 

hibernating black bears is more moderate, less than 8°C [1], than it is in smaller hibernators 414 

where it can exceed 30°C. It is not known if those changes as well as the multi-day body 415 

temperature cycles during the hibernation season [2] could prevent correct detection of vigilance 416 

state by the machine learning algorithms. In addition, it was not known if the nature of sleep 417 

> .CC-BY-NC-ND 4.0 International license available under a was not certified by peer review) is the author/funder, who has granted bioRxiv a license to display the preprint in perpetuity. It is made The copyright holder for this preprint (which this version posted July 14, 2026. ;https://doi.org/10.1101/2025.03.31.646262 doi: bioRxiv preprint

during hibernation differs in other qualities from that during summer in ways that could 418 

compromise integrity of the automated scoring. Our results show that with proper use of training 419 

models the automated scoring with the two tested machine learning based qualifiers show 420 

remarkably similar performance and can approximate the performance of manual scorers. 421 

422 

The use of consensus between 3 manual scorers in the present study helped reduce 423 

uncertainties due to subjective judgment of the manual reference scores. As shown by 424 

Brodersen et al. [18] a significant increase in accuracy of automatic scores was obtained 425 

already when increasing the number of manual scorers from 1 to 3. As applied, the assisted 426 

machine learning model of Somnivore [19] was unproblematic with respect to variability in the 427 

data as it had the advantage of training on data very similar to that being analyzed by using a 428 

limited number of epochs from each file to be analyzed as training data. Our protocol of using 429 

100 epochs per vigilance state, which is above the number that was found to provide optimal 430 

performance on human data and near optimal in rodents [19], only requires manual scoring of 431 

3.5% of the total data to be automatically scored. Our initial testing showed relatively little 432 

dependence of accuracy on which training channels were used. While it was optimal to train on 433 

EEG and EOG channels in hibernation, it appears that the most important information during 434 

training is extracted from the EEG channel. Thus, only a 1.3% decrease in overall accuracy is 435 

predicted if an EOG channel is not available in the recording (Fig 3). It was not surprising that 436 

training Somnivore on data recorded from a different bear than the analyzed file (cross-animal 437 

training, Fig 4) caused a profound decrease in overall accuracy for the hibernation data, as it 438 

included both the effects of a different animal and T b and is beyond the design of Somnivore. 439 

Training Somnivore on files from the same animal at a different T b during hibernation also 440 

caused a marked decrease in overall accuracy. Thus, for hibernating animals Somnivore is best 441 

used with assisted machine learning as designed. 442

After training Somnotate on 6 one-day recordings of mouse data, it was shown to be highly 444 

robust and tolerated as much as 50% randomly mislabeled epochs added to the training data 445 

before accuracy decreased [18]. For our bear data we found that overall accuracy was 446 

decreased if both hibernation and summer data were used for training and applied to summer 447 

data, while we did not find a substantial drop in accuracy when the same training was applied to 448 

hibernation data (Fig 5). This may partly reflect that our reference data set consisted of two 449 

times as much data from hibernating bears as non-hibernating bears, but could also be due to 450 

other characteristics of the signals that differ too much between hibernation and summer. The 451 

finding of a high accuracy on hibernation data with training models based on both data from 452 

high and low T b during hibernation is consistent with the previously observed robustness of 453 

Somnotate on mice data [18]. The ability to use a single training model within hibernation in 454 

bears is an important finding, as it otherwise would have been difficult to define which training 455 

model should be selected when T b is varying though a continuum during multi-day body 456 

temperature cycles. 457 

458 

The overall accuracy of automatic scores of Somnotate with separate training models for 459 

hibernation and summer was only slightly lower or at the level of agreement of single manual 460 

scores with the manual consensus scores (92.0-93.5% vs 94.5% Fig 5 and Table 1, 461 

respectively). The use of consensus scores for training likely helps avoid outliers in the training 462 

data due to subjective judgment and carries over to improve the automated scores. Our results 463 

compare favorably with published results on machine learning based automated sleep scoring 464 

that had overall accuracies in the 80-98% range for human data [13, 14, 15, 16]. The detailed 465 

F-measures also indicate excellent ability to distinguish between vigilance states for both for 466 

Somnotate and Somnivore with F-measures mostly in the 0.90-0.98 range. The two applications 467 

performed remarkably similarly with a tendency for lower average F-measures and more spread 468 

in F-measures for REM in summer (Fig 7). The lower occupancy of REM in summer (Fig. 8) 469

resulted in average F-measures of 0.97, while tests of two other applications on the same 471 

mouse data set had F-measures of 0.94 and 0.73 respectively [18]. Somnivore tested on rodent 472

with both Somnotate and Somnivore in our study, while NREM had the highest average F-475 

measure of 0.94-0.95 (Fig. 7). 476 

477 

The total distribution of vigilance states based on automated scores of the reference data set 478 

show for the first time evidence that bears sleep about 2x as long during hibernation compared 479 

to summer (Fig 8). The functional physiological base for this increase in NREM and REM 480 

compared to summer will require a much deeper analysis than offered by our limited analysis 481 

here. However it is unlikely that bears share functional similarities with the increase in delta 482 

waves reported during early arousals in smaller hibernators [9,10], where the resolution of that 483 

SWA is resolved without sleep [11, 12, 25]. This elevated SWA after arousal from low T b in 484 

smaller hibernators may be related to synaptic loss during deep torpor that occurs linearly with a 485 

decrease in brain temperature and could be due to a decreased release or responsiveness to 486 

wake-promoting factors or to the slow regrowth of exitatory synapses in the cortex and thalamus 487 

[26]. These sleep changes observed in deep hibernators have limited translational relevance to 488 

hypometabolic states at brain temperatures closer to normal as in bears (30-35°C) [2] where T b489 

is high enough to allow cortical brain activity to be recorded continuously and loss of synaptic 490 

connectivity is unlikely to occur. 491

The results are a snapshot of two days during the coldest period during mid hibernation when 493 

sleep patterns could have been affected by shivering. Thus, scoring extended periods during 494 

hibernation in more bears is needed to better understand the time courses of sleep times and 495

These results are remarkably similar for Somnivore and Somnotate and did not differ 497 

significantly from the manual consensus scores during hibernation (Fig 8). However, there was 498 

a small but significant overestimate of sleep time and underestimate of wake for the summer 499 

data compared to the manual consensus scores. The summer data might present some 500 

challenges in that the patterns were more fragmented, with bears drifting in an out of 501 

drowsiness and sleep. This could perhaps cause a cumulative effect if automatically scored 502 

sleep periods extend one or a few more epochs during each transition than they do in the 503 

manually scored recordings. The summer recordings were also less clean with presence of 504 

artifacts as bears moved around including EMG infusion into the EOG and EEG recordings. 505 

Also, there were more problems with telemetry signal dropouts in summer recordings, although 506 

both programs have strategies to detect and exclude these epochs. 507 

508 

Problematic files with lower F-measures tended to be outliers in both Somnivore and Somnotate 509 

(Fig 9). It is likely that some data files have less well-defined states than others making the 510 

automatic scoring more difficult. An alternative or additional explanation is that these 511 

problematic files were also difficult for the manual scorers resulting in lower quality manual 512 

consensus scores, and that the automatic scoring picked up features not recognized by the 513 

manual scorers, resulting in the automatic scores deviating from the manual scores. 514 

515 

A particular potential difficulty is the correct detection of transition states. As pointed out, the 516 

entry into NREM in hibernating bears could be challenging to qualify due to the respiration-517 

related periodic appearance of slow waves (example Fig 2). However, it did not seem to affect 518 

reliable detection as NREM had the highest F-measures (Fig 7). Scoring of transition states of 519 

NREM was generally similar between the automatic scores and the consensus manual scores 520 

(examples in Fig 6), and misalignment of a few epochs would have minimal effect on the overall 521

patterns with frequent transitions. Automatic detection occasionally failed to detect correct 523 

transitions as determined by the manual scorers, particularly with respect to transitions into 524 

REM. This was particularly the case with Somnivore. However the incorrectly detected periods 525 

of Wake before REM were quite short and likely did not contribute much to overall occupancy of 526 

vigilance states. The sensitivity of the scoring software to state changes caused by short term 527 

frequency changes in the EEG is modulated by the configuration. For Somnotate we used a 528 

sampling interval of 10 s, and for Somnivore a scoring rule of minimum score consistency of 3 529 

epochs was required to allow a state change for all three states. These are settings used to 530 

align scoring rules to the conventions used to manually score the data, but do not necessarily 531 

reflect the short term underlying frequency changes. They might compensate for uncertainty in 532 

the determinations that otherwise would cause flipping of states back and forth and not 533 

considered state changes by manual scorers. However if the required minimum score 534 

consistency in Somnivore were set too high or the defined sampling interval in Somnotate was 535 

set too long, the responsiveness to state changes could become too slow. So definition of these 536 

parameters are compromises, but can possibly be fined tuned. For the manual scores we 537 

decided to not base scores on pre-conceived conventions about allowed state changes, i. e. not 538 

excluding Wake to REM transitions as making a priori assumptions may not be well founded 539 

when scoring a new species and in a hibernating state. We did detect some unusual Wake to 540 

REM transitions in the bears, but detailed anaysis of more data would be needed to draw any 541 

conclusions on validity and origins. Caution should be used and not just reliance on automatic 542 

scores to make conclusions about presence of unusual transitions as exemplified in Fig 6. 543 

Somnotate has the additional ability, not explored in the results presented here, to flag epochs 544 

with low probability for the state determination [18] that could be useful in finding transition 545 

states that need manual inspection. 546

To conclude we have for the first time shown that automatic sleep scoring of a large mammal 548 

comparing hibernation and non-hibernation states can be done with trained machine learning 549 

models. Both Somnotate and Somnivore have overall accuracies approaching the best in the 550 

literature based on automatic vs. manual consensus scores. We now have the tools to 551 

determine distribution of vigilance states in the data set of 3500 days of polysomnographic 552 

recording from hibernating and non-hibernating black bears using automated batch scoring. We 553 

think one should keep an open mind on the possibility of unusual vigilance state transitions in a 554 

new species and in unexplored states, but we suggest that conclusions about problematic 555 

transitions should be determined by manual inspection that can be assisted by software 556 

support. 557
