# Abstract 

Deep brain stimulation has demonstrated its therapeutic potential in modulating pathological oscillations associated with Parkinson’s disease and epilepsy. However, its efficacy in treating disrupted theta-gamma phase-amplitude coupling seen in memory-related disorders, such as Alzheimer’s disease, remains poorly understood. While recent studies have targeted the entorhinal-hippocampal circuit, results remain inconsistent. This discrepancy stems from a lack of mechanistic understanding regarding how stimulation protocols affect this circuit. In this work, we present a reduced multicompartment model of the hippocampal CA1 area that reproduces theta-nested gamma oscillations characteristic of healthy neural activity during memory performance. The model comprises pyramidal, basket and OLM cells with simplified morphologies. We also incorporated CA3-to-CA1 axonal projections, providing a foundational framework for studying how stimulation-induced recruitment of afferent pathways modulates CA1 dynamics. By balancing computational efficiency with anatomical accuracy, our model enables systematic investigation of the effects of electrode placement and orientation, as well as stimulation amplitude and frequency on CA1 neural activity. We demonstrate that the excitatory response in CA1 is primarily driven by the recruitment of Schaffer collateral projections. Overall, this work provides a computationally efficient template for exploring diverse stimulation configurations and could be expanded for developing neuromodulatory strategies to restore physiological network dynamics. 

# Author summary 

Deep brain stimulation has shown success in treating Parkinson’s disease by suppressing abnormal neural activity responsible for movement disorders. However, when applied to memory-related pathologies, such as Alzheimer’s disease, the therapeutic outcomes remain unpredictable, ranging from cognitive improvement to impairment. This discrepancy highlights a critical gap in our understanding of how stimulation protocols interact with neural dynamics of the targeted circuits. To address this, we developed a computationally efficient model of the hippocampus, which is involved in memory processes, in order to understand how deep brain stimulation might influence its activity. # Introduction 1

The hippocampus plays a central role in episodic memory by processing and integrating 2

information originating from distributed neocortical regions [1, 2]. Information 3

processing and communication is supported by neural oscillations, which coordinate the 4

activity of neurons across hippocampal areas. In particular, theta (4-12 Hz) and gamma 5

(30-100 Hz) rhythms are known to play a role in memory-related operations, including 6

encoding and retrieval of information [3, 4]. Notably, phase-amplitude coupling (PAC) of 7

theta and gamma oscillations, during which the phase of theta oscillations modulates 8

the amplitude of gamma oscillations, has been observed in memory tasks [5, 6]. The 9

strength of theta-gamma PAC is positively correlated with the efficacy of encoding and 10 

retrieval, as well as overall mnemonic performance [7–9]. However, these oscillatory 11 

patterns are vulnerable to neurodegeneration. An alteration of theta-gamma coupling is 12 

observed in the hippocampus during the early stage of Alzheimer’s disease [10–13], 13 

making theta-gamma coupling a potential early biomarker of the disease. 14 

Given this fundamental role of rhythmic synchrony, interventions that aim at 15 

restoring these oscillatory patterns have gained significant interest for their therapeutic 16 

potential. In particular, the effects of deep brain stimulation (DBS) of the 17 

entorhinal-hippocampal circuit have been tested in epileptic patients across various 18 

studies [14 –21], leading to inconsistent findings as highlighted by recent reviews [22 – 25]. 19 

Stimulation of entorhinal cortex or hippocampus has yielded results ranging from 20 

enhancement [14–16] and impairment [17, 18] to no significant effect on memory 21 

performance [19]. This discrepancy in results may originate from variations in 22 

stimulation protocols. For instance, Titiz and colleagues [15] emphasized the importance 23 

of precise anatomical targeting, while Merkow and colleagues [18] demonstrated that 24 

effects are time-dependent. Furthermore, recent works have suggested that closed-loop 25 

stimulation could improve memory performance, whereas random stimulation may 26 

impair it [20, 21]. These variations in parameters, including spatial, temporal, and 27 

frequency-based characteristics, present a vast array of stimulation configurations that 28 

must be systematically investigated to optimize therapeutic efficacy. 29 

Computational modeling provides an essential methodology for elucidating the 30 

underlying mechanisms of neural oscillations and evaluating the impact of 31 

neuromodulation on these dynamics. Models span a wide spectrum of biological realism, 32 

ranging from abstract mathematical mean-field representations [26, 27] to highly 33 

detailed conductance-based models that incorporate complex neuronal 34 

morphology [28, 29]. While the latter offer high fidelity, their computational demands 35 

can be prohibitive. Other approaches reduce complexity by simplifying or even omitting 36 

dendritic morphology [30 – 32]. Despite these differences in scale, these models converge 37 

on the emergence of gamma oscillations from recurrent excitatory-inhibitory 38 

interactions or within interconnected inhibitory networks. The origin of theta 39 

rhythmicity, however, differs across models. Some models impose theta as an external 40 

drive to induce theta-gamma coupling [26, 30, 32], whereas in others, theta emerges 41 

endogenously from network dynamics [27, 28]. Although Vardalakis and colleagues [32] 42 

have begun investigating the effects of neuromodulation on theta-gamma coupling, they 43 

modeled the electrical stimulation as an intracellular current applied equally to neurons 44 

in the targeted area of stimulation. This simplification neglects the fact that the effects 45

Computational models specifically addressing hippocampal DBS remain sparse and 49

to high computational costs [35, 36]. For instance, Bingham and colleagues [35], as well 51 

as Farzad and colleagues [36], developed computational models of the dentate gyrus 52 

comprised of granule cells with explicit inputs from the perforant path. By 53 

reconstructing the anatomy of the dentate gyrus from a mouse atlas, they accurately 54

network activation. However, while these high-fidelity models allow for precise 56 

prediction, they are computationally demanding and do not account for the effects of 57 

stimulation on network oscillations. In this work, we present a computationally efficient 58 

model to study the impact of extracellular stimulation on theta-gamma oscillations 59 

within a model of the human CA1 network. While simpler models treating the electrode 60 

as an ideal point source have been successfully applied to the nerve fibers [37 – 39], to our 61 

knowledge, such an approach has not yet been implemented to study hippocampal 62 

theta-gamma dynamics. 63 

To enable systematic investigation of various stimulation protocols, we aim at 64 

designing a reduced model of CA1 that offers the same functional and morphological 65 

properties as a large-scale model. However, this reduction is far from being a linear 66 

process regarding population size, parameters and morphological properties. More 67 

precisely, the reduced model must exhibit known properties (theta-gamma dynamics) 68 

while offering the possibility of investigating extracellular stimulation in a reasonable 69 

time. Extracellular stimulation produces a gradient of extracellular potential along the 70 

neuron. These spatial differences in extracellular voltage induce an axial current flow 71 

within the intracellular space. Such effects cannot be captured by single-compartment 72 

neuron models. Furthermore, different parts of a neuron respond differently to electrical 73 

stimulation, highlighting the need for multicompartment neuron models to accurately 74 

represent extracellular stimulation [33, 34]. While the hippocampus exhibits a vast 75 

diversity of interneurons [40, 41], previous studies have suggested the contribution of 76 

both parvalbumin-expressing (PV+) basket cells and oriens lacunosum moleculare 77 

(OLM) cells in theta-gamma PAC [29, 42, 43]. Therefore, our network focuses on reduced 78 

models of basket, OLM and pyramidal cells. As stimulation preferably activates axonal 79 

terminals projecting to a region rather than the local cells [44, 45], we explicitly 80 

represent Schaffer collaterals projecting from the CA3 area of the hippocampus to CA1 81 

as myelinated axons [46, 47]. Our reduced model exhibits theta-nested gamma 82 

oscillations while remaining computationally tractable on a portable computer. 83 

Using this model, we systematically investigated how variations in stimulation 84 

amplitude, frequency, and electrode configuration affect the network activity. In 85 

addition, we explored the contribution of Schaffer collaterals to the network’s response 86 

by comparing stimulation-induced dynamics in both intact and collateral-ablated 87 

models. Our results underscore the importance of electrode location and orientation on 88 

cell recruitment. Furthermore, we demonstrated that Schaffer collaterals exert a 89 

modulatory effect on the network as stimulation induces an increase of activity in their 90 

presence, whereas a partial decrease in activity is observed in their absence. 91

## Development and validation of the model 93 

To investigate the effects of extracellular electrical stimulation of CA1, we first built a 94 

multicompartment network model of a hippocampal slice that can generate theta-nested 95 

gamma oscillations (see Material and methods for details). Our network model is made 96 

of pyramidal, basket and OLM cells. Axonal trajectories of Schaffer collaterals emerging 97 

from CA3 and synapsing onto CA1 pyramidal neurons were also represented. To 98 

facilitate the placement of individual cells, their connections based on distance, and the 99 

generation of collateral trajectories, we simplified the topology of the hippocampal 100 

formation slice into an S-shape, allowing us to develop an intrinsic coordinate system 101 

(Fig. 1). 102

Synaptic weight tuning within CA1 enables the generation of gamma 103 

rhythms 104 

Assuming that gamma oscillations emerge from the interactions between excitatory and 105 

inhibitory neurons, we first tuned the synaptic weights of the CA1 network in the 106 

absence of Schaffer collaterals to ensure it would sustain activity in the gamma range 107 

(see Fig. 2). An intracellular ramp current with an amplitude increasing from 0 to 1 108 

nanoampere (nA) was injected into the soma of the pyramidal cells. Starting with 109 

initial synaptic weights adapted from [32], we performed several 5-second simulations 110 

during which the excitatory synaptic weights ( wEOLM and wEBC ) were scaled by a 111 

factor kE ranging from 0.1 to 2, and the inhibitory synaptic weights ( wIE ) by a factor 112 

kI ranging from 0.1 to 1. The selected parameters, kE = 2 and kI = 0 .1, allowed us to 113 

achieve an oscillation frequency of at least 30 Hz in all cell populations under high 114 

intracellular stimulation), as depicted by the heatmaps in Fig. 2B, as well as the 115 

spectrograms in Fig. 2D. We also chose these values so that we would observe a firing 116 

rate that was 2.5 times faster in the basket population than in the pyramidal population 117 

(Fig. 2C), which is in accordance with the fact that basket cells have a higher discharge 118 

rate than pyramidal cells [48]. 119

Schaffer collaterals inputs tuning enable theta-gamma oscillations 141 

Once all the CA1 neuronal populations were able to produce oscillations in the gamma 142 

frequencies, we added the Schaffer collaterals. 6-Hz oscillatory intracellular currents 143 

were injected into the first node of each Schaffer collateral to simulate endogenous theta 144 

rhythms emerging from the CA3 area and propagating to the CA1 area. We optimized 145 

the amplitude of these oscillatory inputs, as well as the synaptic weights, wSCA , from 146 

Schaffer collaterals to pyramidal cells (see Fig. 3). Several 5-second simulations were 147 

performed during which the input amplitude, kamp , were varied from 0.02 to 2 nA. The 148 

synaptic weights wSCA were scaled by a factor kSCA ranging from 2 to 4, starting from 149 

a value adapted from the same value as pyramidal-to-basket synaptic weight obtained 150 

after optimization. Heatmaps representing the mean oscillatory frequency of pyramidal 151 

and basket populations within theta phases were built (Fig. 3B). From these heatmaps, 152 

we determined the parameters that induced the strongest gamma activity in both 153 

populations. The selected parameters, kamp = 0 .1 nA and kSCA = 2 .9, resulted in a 154 

mean oscillatory frequency above 50 Hz in both pyramidal and basket populations 155 

nested within the phases of the ongoing theta (Fig. 3B and C). 156

## Single cell response to extracellular stimulation 157 

After setting up the model and tuning its parameters such as it exhibits theta-nested 158 

gamma oscillations, we sought to study the effects of extracellular electrical stimulation, 159 

first on single-cell activity and then on network dynamics. We first aimed to confirm the 160 

influence of electrode location and stimulation polarity on the different cell types 161 

individually (Fig. 4). To this end, for each cell type, we performed several 350ms 162 

simulations during which a 1ms single-pulse stimulation was applied to the extracellular 163 

space surrounding an individual neuron. The electrode was positioned at various 164 

locations in the x-y plane, with an increment of 50 μm in both x and y directions. We 165 

tested different stimulation amplitudes, ranging from 0.01 mA to 10 mA in steps of 0.01 166 

mA, and recorded the smallest amplitude that elicited a somatic action potential 167 

(stimulation amplitude threshold). 168

## Population response to extracellular stimulation 197 

Our main objective was then to study the influence of various stimulation parameters 198 

on the network oscillatory activity. 199 

Stimulation amplitude, electrode position and orientation 200 

We first analyzed the effects of stimulation amplitude and electrode configuration 201 

(Figs. 5, 6). To this end, we performed several 5-second simulations in which a 2-second 202 

50 Hz biphasic extracellular stimulation pulse train was applied to the network. We 203 

tested different electrode configurations, including monopolar and bipolar arrangements. 204 

For each configuration, we explored a wide range of stimulation amplitudes, from 0.5 205 

mA to 6 mA in 0.5 mA increments, and quantified for each neuronal type the 206 

proportion of neurons that significantly increased or decreased their firing rate during 207 

the stimulation period compared to their baseline activity. The simulation results 208 

showed that the network activity increased with the stimulation amplitude across all 209 

stimulation arrangements. We also observed that for both monopolar (Fig. 5) and 210 

bipolar (Fig. 6) configurations, stimulation applied to the inner curvature of the 211 

hippocampal CA1 region induced stronger network excitation than stimulation on the 212 

outer curvature. For instance, in the case of monopolar stimulation at 3 mA, around 213 

65% of pyramidal cells increased their activity when the electrode was placed on the 214 

outer curvature of CA1 (Fig. 5B1) against ∼90% when the electrode was placed on the 215 

inner curvature (Fig. 5B2). For a stimulation amplitude of 6 mA, ∼85% of pyramidal 216 

cells increased their activity when the electrode was placed on the outer curvature, 217 

while the percentage reached 100% when placed on the inner curvature. This difference 218 

in recruitment depending on electrode placement was more pronounced for bipolar 219 

orthogonal stimulation with at most ∼65% of pyramidal cells increasing their activity 220 

when the electrode was positioned on the outer curvature (Fig. 6A1), against 100% 221 

when positioned on the inner curvature (Fig. 6A3). 222

# Discussion 277 

## Highlights 278 

In this work, we have developed a reduced multicompartment network model of CA1 279 

that exhibits theta-nested gamma oscillations. Our model incorporates 280 

multicompartment neurons and explicit axonal afferents to meaningfully simulate the 281 

effects of extracellular stimulation of the CA1 area [33, 34, 50, 51]. Simplifications in the 282 

neuronal anatomy, as well as the number of neurons and connectivity, enable 283 

computationally tractable simulations on a portable computer. This balance between 284 

accuracy and efficiency allows for the systematic testing of various stimulation 285 

configurations within a reasonable timeframe. We believe this work provides a template 286 

that can be used to gain intuition regarding the effects of other stimulation 287 

configurations, the effects of stimulation on pathologies, or even a foundation for 288 

building customized reduced models. 289 

In order to build a CA1 model that generates theta-gamma oscillations, we initially 290 

applied the scaling method described by Romaro and colleagues [52] to downscale the 291 

network implemented by Vardalakis and colleagues [32]. However, this approach failed 292 

to reproduce gamma oscillations. This is likely due to the increase in neuronal 293 

morphological complexity, which probably altered the intrinsic excitability of the cells. 294 

Furthermore, our reduced network size may have reached the limits of the scaling 295 

methodology used by Romaro and colleagues. While they suggest compensating the loss 296 

of synaptic input by direct current, we found this approach computationally prohibitive. 297 

Instead, we iteratively adjusted the synaptic weights in the CA1 network in the absence 298 

of Schaffer collaterals until gamma oscillations emerged (see Fig. 2). Then, we added 299 

the Schaffer collaterals to the network in order to introduce theta input. We optimized 300 

both theta amplitude and the synaptic weight from the collaterals to the CA1 network 301 

in order to get strong theta-gamma oscillations (see Fig. 3). 302 

We investigated the response of individual cells to extracellular stimulation. Our 303 

results demonstrate that for all cell types, the stimulation amplitude threshold increases 304 

with electrode-to-neuron distance, regardless of stimulation polarity. Moreover, neuronal 305 

morphology modulates these thresholds, as the amplitude threshold depends on the 306 

location of the electrode alongside the different neuronal elements. The stimulation 307 

polarity also influences the threshold profiles. All these results are in line with previous 308 

computational work of McIntyre and Grill [53]. Additionally, our simulations validate 309 

that axonal fibers exhibit higher sensitivity to extracellular stimulation than cell 310 

bodies [34]. As shown in Figure 4, Schaffer collaterals exhibit smaller thresholds 311 

compared to other cell types. Given the scarcity of studies analyzing cell-type-specific 312 

responses to extracellular stimulation, our study provides predictions, particularly for 313 

the interneuron populations. However, because most neuron models are calibrated for 314 

intracellular stimulation, their predicted response to extracellular stimulation should be 315 

interpreted with caution. Finally, the grey regions in the first panel of Figure 4B denote 316 

electrode locations where action potentials failed to initiate despite increasing the 317 

stimulation amplitude. The observed effect may result from numerical instability arising 318

We also examined the network response to stimulation by comparing conditions with 321 

and without Schaffer collateral inputs (Fig. 8). Our results demonstrate that the 322 

presence of Schaffer collaterals enhances the activity of pyramidal cells, whereas their 323 

removal attenuates their response. This observation supports the hypothesis that DBS 324 

acts indirectly on local neurons by recruiting afferent pathways [51, 54, 55]. Specifically, 325 

the excitation of Schaffer collaterals in response to DBS serves to drive the activity of 326 

the underlying pyramidal cell population. In the absence of Schaffer collaterals, DBS 327 

acts directly on the targeted brain area. It has been hypothesized that DBS exerts 328 

direct inhibition on local cells, either by depolarization block, or by activating 329 

GABAergic pathways [54, 56]. In our work, GABAergic afferent pathways were not 330 

represented. Therefore, the decrease of activity in pyramidal neurons could originate 331 

from depolarization block or from the direct recruitment of local interneurons which 332 

subsequently inhibit the pyramidal population. 333 

## Limitations 334 

Since our model serves as an initial template for building computationally efficient yet 335 

accurate models, only the most crucial mechanisms involved in theta-nested gamma 336 

oscillations and extracellular stimulation have been implemented. Therefore, many 337 

assumptions and simplifications had to be made, and further improvements could be 338 

implemented. For instance, theta oscillations are provided as input to the network. 339 

Numerous studies have suggested that theta oscillations in the CA1 area of the 340 

hippocampus originate from inputs from neighboring regions [31, 57 –59]. Specifically, it 341 

is thought that theta emerges from the interplay between inputs from CA3 Schaffer 342 

collaterals, entorhinal cortex perforant path and medial septum GABAergic projections. 343 

Although theta oscillations in CA1 can be entrained by extrinsic inputs, local cells can 344 

also be responsible for generating theta. For example, Colgin [58] suggests that theta 345 

can emerge from local interactions between pyramidal and inhibitory neurons. Some 346 

computational studies have also investigated the emergence of theta rhythms within 347 

CA1 and found that theta can be generated endogenously within the network [27, 28]. 348 

Bezaire and colleagues [28] have suggested that the interconnection between several 349 

pyramidal neurons is sufficient to generate theta oscillations within the network. 350 

However, given the scale of our network and the fact that we did not account for 351 

pyramidal-to-pyramidal connections, this theta-generation mechanism could not be 352 

implemented in our model. Furthermore, Sengupta and colleagues [27] have proposed 353 

that theta emerges from the interactions between pyramidal and bistratified cells. For 354 

simplification purposes, we did not implement all known interneuron types in CA1, such 355 

as bistratified cells. Therefore, we could not test this theory either. 356 

Another limitation is the exclusion of CA1 input pathways besides the Schaffer 357 

collaterals. CA1 pyramidal cells also receive excitatory inputs from the entorhinal 358 

cortex through the perforant path. The medial septum also provides inhibitory inputs 359 

to CA1. Since the effects of DBS primarily depend on the activated pathways [55], 360 

modeling projections from both the entorhinal cortex and the medial septum could yield 361 

a more complex and accurate response to stimulation. Furthermore, as these different 362 

pathways do not innervate the same layers of CA1, electrode placement and orientation 363 

could have a more significant impact on the network’s response to stimulation. In 364 

addition, the Schaffer collaterals in our model maintain a constant diameter and do not 365 

include branching points. However, it is well-established that hippocampal axonal 366 

projections taper and branch. These features significantly impact how they are 367 

recruited during stimulation [36]. While incorporating these morphological 368 

characteristics would increase the computational cost of our model, doing so would be 369

In addition, while this study focused on the acute effects of DBS, we did not account 372 

for activity-dependent plasticity mechanisms operating at different timescales. At short 373 

timescales, stimulation of afferent fibers triggers action potentials that propagate to the 374 

axonal terminals where neurotransmitters are released into the synaptic cleft. However, 375 

during high-frequency DBS, the rate at which action potentials are generated outpaces 376 

the biological mechanisms responsible for vesicle replenishment. This leads to synaptic 377 

depression. Consequently, the postsynaptic neurons fail to reach threshold, attenuating 378 

signal transmission [51, 60, 61]. Incorporating these short-time plasticity mechanisms 379 

could substantially alter how the network responds to sustained high-frequency 380 

stimulation. Furthermore, chronic DBS is known to induce persistent changes in neural 381 

circuits through long-term plasticity mechanisms. In particular, repeated stimulation 382 

may reshape connectivity through spike-timing-dependent plasticity. Integrating such 383 

mechanisms is essential to capturing how stimulation affects the network beyond the 384 

stimulation period and for investigating whether DBS can induce durable network 385 

reorganization. Finally, while this study tested the effects of stimulation in a healthy 386 

network, it would also be interesting to investigate whether stimulation can restore 387 

theta-gamma PAC in pathological networks and whether plasticity mechanisms 388 

contribute to the recovery and maintenance of physiological oscillatory dynamics. 389 

## Perspectives 390 

Future work could focus on expanding the model to encompass the entire hippocampal 391 

formation while explicitly modeling the different pathways. Such an expansion would 392 

allow for the investigation of various stimulation targets and their effects on 393 

downstream hippocampal regions, as well as on global theta-gamma oscillations within 394 

the hippocampus. Given that closed-loop stimulation represents a highly promising 395 

approach, future studies should focus on implementing these protocols to evaluate their 396 

impact on oscillatory dynamics. Furthermore, by introducing pathological state in the 397 

network, we could determine the optimal stimulation strategies for restoring 398 

theta-gamma coupling. Another essential addition would be to incorporate mechanisms 399 

of synaptic plasticity in order to evaluate the effect of extracellular stimulation after the 400 

end of the stimulus. 401

## Model architecture 510 

Number of neurons 511 

The number of principal neurons present in the CA1 area in the human brain is on the 512 

order of 14 million [64]. However, to mitigate the computational cost, we reduced the 513 

number of neurons used in our model, while keeping the ratios between the different cell 514 

types. For instance, interneurons are known to represent about 8-10% of the neurons in 515 

hippocampal regions. Furthermore, PV+ basket cells constitute around 14.4% of the 516 

interneurons in hippocampal regions, while OLM cells make up for about 4.3% of 517 

them [65], which can be approximated to a ratio of 3:1 between basket cells and OLM 518 

cells. A population of 100 pyramidal cells was therefore used to allow the emergence of 519 

stable collective dynamics while maintaining a computationally tractable model. We 520 

modeled 9 basket cells and 3 OLM cells to keep the ratio between the cell types. 521 

Furthermore, a minimum of 3 OLM cells was required to achieve synaptic coverage 522 

across the entire CA1 area (see the section below on neuronal connections). 523 

We also wanted to represent explicit projections of Schaffer collaterals, which are 524 

axonal projections from CA3 pyramidal cells to the CA1 area. There are about 2.8 525 

million pyramidal cells in the CA3 area of the human brain [64]. We approximated the 526 

ratio between CA1 and CA3 pyramidal cells to 4:1, thus modeling 26 Schaffer collaterals. 527 

The number of neurons represented for each cell type is summarized in Table 5. 528 

Geometry of the model 529 

The hippocampal formation has a curved shape, with excitatory neurons projecting 530 

along its proximo-distal axis. The entorhinal cortex lies at the most distal end of this 531 

axis and the dentate gyrus forms the proximal end (see Fig. 1C). To accurately 532 

represent the trajectory of Schaffer collaterals from CA3 to CA1, as well as the overall 533 

organization of CA1, we used an intrinsic coordinate system ( t, u) in our model, where 534 

the t-axis corresponds to the curvilinear axis and the u-axis represents the radial, 535 

superficial-to-deep axis (from stratum oriens to stratum lacunosum moleculare as seen 536 

in Fig. 1D). 537

a generalized S-shape, extracted from the Hippocampus book [66]. This shape has the 539 

advantage of connecting every region one after the other, allowing the t-axis to remain 540 

continuous. As a result, spatial manipulations and distance computations become more 541 

straightforward. For example, the curved structure can be easily transformed into a 542 

flattened representation for better interpretability. We manually defined (using 543 

Inkscape) two cubic Bézier curves joined end to end, following the generalized S-shape. 544

Neuron placement 581 

Neuron placement was assigned using the intrinsic coordinate system ( t, u) described in 582 

the previous subsection. The u-coordinate of each CA1 neuron’s soma was constrained 583 

by the anatomical boundaries of its respective layer: OLM cells were placed within the 584 

stratum oriens, while pyramidal and basket cells soma were restricted to the stratum 585 

pyramidale (see Fig. 1D). Due to the relatively small number of inhibitory neurons, 586 

their t-coordinates were chosen deterministically to ensure even spacing along the 587 

curvilinear axis. This was achieved by dividing the transverse length of the CA1 area by 588 

the number of neurons plus one, thereby avoiding placement directly on the region’s 589 

boundaries. On the other hand, the t-coordinates of pyramidal neuron somata were 590 

assigned randomly using a uniform distribution along the curvilinear axis of the CA1 591 

region. To ensure functional inhibitory connectivity, an additional constraint was 592 

applied: each pyramidal neuron had to lie within 470 μm of a basket cell, guaranteeing 593 

that it received inhibitory input from at least one basket cell (see next section). 594 

To place the Schaffer collaterals, we first determined the coordinates of the first node 595 

for each collateral. As with the CA1 pyramidal neurons, the u-coordinates were sampled 596 

from a uniform distribution within the stratum pyramidale. The t-coordinates were 597 

randomly assigned using a uniform distribution along the curvilinear axis of the CA3 598 

region. Next, we assigned a length to each collateral ( LSCA ). To do this, we divided the 599 

CA3 region into two equal parts: a proximal CA3 half, which borders the dentate gyrus, 600 

and a distal half, which borders the CA1 region. Similarly, the CA1 region was divided 601 

into two halves: a proximal CA1 half, adjacent to the CA3 area, and a distal CA1 half, 602 

which borders the subiculum. Schaffer collaterals arising from pyramidal cells in the 603 

proximal CA3 region project to distal portions of the CA1 area, near to the subiculum. 604 

Conversely, collaterals originating from distal CA3 neurons terminate in proximal CA1, 605 

adjacent to the CA3 area [66]. Therefore, the length of each collateral can be calculated 606 

as follows: 607 

LSCA = Dx + rSCA × LCA 1, (50) where Dx is the intrinsic distance of the collateral’s first node to the border between 608 

CA3 and CA1 regions; rSCA is a random number between 0.5 and 1 if the first node is 609 

located in the proximal CA3, and between 0 and 0.5 otherwise; LCA 1 is the curvilinear 610 

length of the CA1 area. Given the coordinates of the first node and the total length of 611 

the collateral, we generated the trajectory of each collateral using Bézier curves, as 612 

described in the previous section. Unlike the modeled CA1 neurons, which had a fixed 613 

topology and a fixed number of compartments, the number of compartments of each 614 

Schaffer collateral depends on its length. In our model, this number ranges from 45 to 615 

760 compartments per collateral. However, the length of each compartment was kept 616 

the same as in [38]. 617

Neuronal connectivity 618 

Pyramidal neurons form synapses on the soma of both basket and OLM cells. There are 619 

few connections from pyramidal to pyramidal neurons in the CA1 area. On average, one 620 

pyramidal neuron connects to 1% of pyramidal neurons [67]. We compared simulations 621 

in which pyramidal neurons synapsed onto exactly one other pyramidal neuron with 622 

simulations that excluded pyramidal-to-pyramidal connections, and found no significant 623 

differences in network behaviour. Therefore, no pyramidal-to-pyramidal connection was 624 

included in the final model. 625 

As for the synaptic connections included in the model, basket cells synapse on the 626 

soma of pyramidal neurons and other basket cells. OLM cells inhibit the apical tuft of 627 

the pyramidal neurons, which corresponds to the distal apical dendrites located in the 628 

stratum lacunosum moleculare. In our model, this region is represented by a list of 6 629 

dendritic sections. For each OLM-to-Pyramidal connection, one section was randomly 630 

selected from this list as the synapse target. Pyramidal cells also receive excitatory 631 

input from the Schaffer collaterals onto their apical trunk, modeled as 3 dendritic 632 

sections located in the stratum radiatum (see Fig. 1B). One section was randomly 633 

selected as the target for each Schaffer collateral input. 634 

The connectivity in our model is distance-based, meaning that neurons form 635 

synapses with other neurons in close proximity. The Hippocampome.org [68] database 636 

provides the mean axonal length, which we will refer to as ”synaptic distance”, for each 637 

cell type. To account for anatomical scale differences between mouse and human brains, 638 

we scaled the database values by multiplying them by a factor of 1.5. Next, we created 639 

histograms to visualize the distances between each cell type and their potential targets 640 

in our model, and overlaid the synaptic distances on top of these histograms in order to 641 

validate this approach. This revealed that no basket cell was within synaptic distance of 642 

another basket cell, likely due to the small number of basket cells in the model. To 643 

address this, we increased their synaptic distance as to get basket-to-basket connections. 644 

Similarly, we increased the synaptic distance for pyramidal neurons to enhance the 645 

pyramidal-to-OLM connections, so as to get all the OLM cells receive connections from 646 

the network. Finally, each cell was connected to any other cell that was both within its 647

stimulation span if its mean spike count during stimulation was higher than its mean 808

count during stimulation was smaller than its mean spike count minus standard 811 

deviation during baseline activity. We computed the number of neurons that had an 812 

increase or a decrease in activity for each neuronal population and we divided it by the 813
