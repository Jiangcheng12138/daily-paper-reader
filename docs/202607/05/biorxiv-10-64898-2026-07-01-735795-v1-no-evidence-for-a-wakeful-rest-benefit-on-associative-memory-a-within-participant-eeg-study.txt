# ABSTRACT 15 

16 

Wakeful rest after learning has been proposed to facilitate memory consolidation compared 17 

to engaging in a distraction task, with prior EEG studies linking slow oscillation power during 18 

rest to better memory performance. However, replication attempts have yielded mixed results. 19 

We investigated whether 10 minutes of wakeful rest would enhance associative memory 20 

performance relative to 10 minutes of a hippocampus -dependent auditory short -term memory 21 

distraction task, using a within -participant design with con tinuous EEG recording. We 22 

employed both a replication -inspired analytical approach, closely modeled on prior work, and 23 

a data -specific approach adapted to our dataset. Contrary to our hypotheses, we found no 24 

advantage of rest over distraction on associativ e memory performance. We did, however, 25 

observe an order effect: performance was better for the second learning than the first, and this 26 

improvement was more pronounced when rest was performed second compared to first . At 27 

the neurophysiological level, neith er slow oscillation nor alpha power during the post -learning 28 

period correlated with memory performance, regardless of analytical pipeline, although cross -29 

over analyses revealed that the choice of EEG reference influenced the direction of some 30 

correlations. At the phenomenological level, self -reported mental activity during rest and 31 

distraction, as well as trait daydreaming frequency, were not related to memory outcomes, 32 

despite the two conditions inducing distinct subjective cognitive states. Together, thes e33 

findings do not support a robust benefit of post -learning wakeful rest over a hippocampus -34 

dependent distraction task for associative memory, nor do they replicate prior EEG correlates 35 

of consolidation. We discuss methodological factors, including task -le arning effects in within -36 

participant designs, the coarseness of averaged spectral power measures, and variability in 37 

EEG preprocessing pipelines, that may contribute to inconsistencies across the literature, and 38 

we call for greater standardization and tran sparency in future studies. 39

## 1 Introduction 45 

46 

A growing body of research suggests that short periods of wakeful rest after learning can 47 

facilitate memory retention, compared to engaging in distraction tasks . This effect has been 48 

observed across diverse memory tasks (e.g., stories (e.g., Dewar et al., 2012 ; Brokaw et al., 2016 ;49 

Sacripante et al., 2019 ; Evans et al., 2021) , word lists (e.g., Craig et al., 2014 ; Dewar et al., 2014 ;50 

Millar and Balota, 2022 ; Craig and Greer, 2024) , translations (e.g., Mercer, 2015 ; Wang et al., 51 

2021) , picture -word pairs (King and Nicosia, 2022) ) and distraction tasks (e.g., spot -the -difference 52 

(e.g.,  Dewar et al., 2012, 2014; Craig et al., 2014, 2016; Sacripante et al., 2019; Martini et al., 53 

2020; Evans et al., 2021) , Snood game  (Brokaw et al., 2016; Humiston and Wamsley, 2018; Wang 54 

et al., 2021) . In most studies, resting after learning leads to better recall and less forgetting than 55 

being engaged in a distraction task . In a recent systematic review and meta -analysis based on 63 56 

experiments Weng et al. (2025) report ed a significant effect of wakeful rest on memory 57 

consolidation with a medium effect size.  Ho wever, these findings are not always consistent accross 58 

studies, and a non negligeable amount of them did not find any beneficial effect of rest over 59 

distracting task on memory performances (e.g., Fatania and Mercer, 2017; Varma et al., 2017, 60 

2018, 2019; Martini et al., 2018, 2020; Humiston et al., 2019; Tucker et al., 2020; Millar and Balota, 61 

2022) . For instance, Brokaw et al.  (2016)  initially found that 15 minutes of post -learning rest 62 

reduced the decline in correct recall and decreased false recall of short stories compared to 15 63 

minut es spent playing the Snood game after learning . However, the same team later failed to 64 

replicate these behavioral results  (Humiston et al., 2019) . Additionnal ly, i n their follow -up meta -65 

analysis of 11 studies investigating the same effect, they found a moderate effect size  (Humiston 66 

et al., 2019) . They conc luded that while wakeful rest appears beneficial for memory consolidation, 67 

the effect may be smaller than previously believed .68 

To understand the se discrepencies, several lines of research investigated the factors 69 

influencing the benefits of wakeful rest on memory consolidation . In an opinion paper,  Martini and 70 

Sachse (2020)  identified the nature of the distraction task as a modulating factor . Indeed , Varma 71 

et al. (2017 , 2018 , 2019)  conducted a series of eight experiments using various versions of the N -72 

back task , a wo rking memory paradigm characterized by minimal semantic content and 73 

negligeable media l temporal lobe involvement , to systematically manipulate distraction task 74 

difficult y, memory content (word -picture pairs and faces) and retrieval type (free recall and 75 

recognition). Across all conditions, t hey found no advantage of wakeful rest over the N -back task 76 

on memory performances , suggesting that distraction tasks with limited hippocampal involvement 77 

may still be compatible with memory consolidation.  Addressing the role of attentional engagement 78 

rather than hippocampal involvement,  Craig and Greer (2024) showed that post -encoding 79 

engagem ent, and not attentional load (i.e., amount of cognitive resources require d by a task ),80 

disrupts consolidation:  using two difficulties of a Sustained Attention to Response Task (SART) as 81 

distractor, they showed that  memory scores were higher after rest than after distraction task s, with 82 

no difference between low - and high -attentional load conditions. Dewar et al. (2007) reported 83 

equivalent memory impairments across distracting tasks wit h substantially different content. Their 84 

findings showed that interference effects occur regardless of whether the distracting task shares 85 

content with the memory task, indicating that content overlap is not a necessary condition for 86 

memory interference. Accordingly, when the goal is to examine memory consolidation during 87 

wakeful rest rather than content -specific interference, distraction tasks should be designed to 88 

minimize overlap with the memory material. Altogether , t hese findings suggest that for a distraction 89 

task to impair memory consolidation through the interruption of active consolidation processes , it 90 

should be hippocampus -dependent , capture attention , though not necessarily be difficult , and it 91 

should be dissimilar to the learning task . Otherwise, any observed disruption may stem from 92 

classical retroactive interference .93

Another factor proposed to potentially influence the wakeful rest effect was t he amount of 94 

mindwandering and daydreaming du ring wakeful rest . Indeed,  Craig et al. (2014) showed that 95 

autobiographical thinking cued by sounds , but not listening passively to meaningless sounds, 96 

resulted in lower memory performances compared to rest (see also Varma et al., 2018) . In the 97 

same line, Brokaw et al. (2016) studied the mental activities associated with memory 98 

improvements by asking the percentage of time engaged in different mental activities during the 99 

post -learning conditions (i.e., autobiographical thinking, thinking about the memory task, 100 

meditating, slee ping, thinking about what I am doing). They did not find any link between mental 101 

activities during rest and memory performance. However, participants exhibit ed better memory 102 

performances when, during distraction , they spend less time thinking about the dis traction task, 103 

and more time thinking about their past, current or future life, or meditating. To further study the 104 

potential modulatory role of daydreaming and mindwandering on memory consolidation during 105 

wakeful rest , Humiston et al. (2019 ) extended this approach by assessing these tendencies as 106 

stable individual traits in everyday life, using validated psychometric instruments: the Daydreaming 107 

Frequency Scale (DFS , Stawarczyk et al., 2012) , measuring dispositional daydreaming, and the 108 

Mindfulness Attention Awareness Scale (MAAS , Brown and Ryan, 2003) , measuring trait 109 

mindfulness and meditative tendencies.  Contrary to the ir hypothesis , they  reported that 110 

participants with high propensity of daydreaming and mindwandering showed less memory 111 

improvement s when learning i s followed by rest . This observation opened a new idea in the field: 112 

wakeful rest is not sufficient for memory consolidation , and one have to be offline not only from a 113 

task to perform, but  also from spontaneous thoughts. In this view, wakeful rest could be beneficial 114 

for memory consolidation by  increasing the probability of experiencing offline states (i.e., strong 115 

decrease of externally generated cognition coupled with a strong decrease in internally generated 116 

thoughts), o r mind blank. 117 

Mechanistically, r est is believed to create a state that s upports early memory consolidation, 118 

possibly through processes similar to those active during sleep, such as slow oscillations 119 

(Wamsley, 2022) . To our knowledge, Brokaw et al. (2016) were the first to use 120 

electroencepha lography (EEG) to explore this idea, showing that slow oscillation (SO, 0.3 –1 Hz) 121 

power during post -learning rest positively correlated with memory performance s. They also 122 

showed that alpha (8 -12 Hz) power during rest was negatively correlated with memory 123 

performances . However, these finding s have not been replicated . In a follow -up study, the same 124 

team investigated whether brief offline states occurring during a post -learning task could promote 125 

memory consolidation. To do so, they operationalized two distinct states: "online" states ,126 

characterized by external attentional focus , reduced alpha and SO power , increased pupil 127 

diameter , and faster reaction time s, were associated with active task engagement ; c onversely, 128 

"offline" states , characterized by increased alpha/SO power, reduced pupil diameter, task -129 

unrelated thoughts , and s lower reaction time s, were hypothesized to be conducive to memory 130 

consolidation. They found that the proportion of time spent in offline states during a post -learning 131 

SART significantly predicted subsequent memory performance for a short story. Importantly , this 132 

study did not replicate Brokaw et al. 's (2016) findings , in that SO power alone during the SART did 133 

not correlate with memory performances, and it did not directly compare rest versus distraction 134 

foll owing learning. 135 

In this study, we aimed to replicate the findings from Brokaw et al. (2016) using different 136 

memory and distraction tasks , to assess the generalizability of the EEG findings . We compared 137 

memory performances to an associative memory task after 10 minutes of wakeful rest or 10 138 

minutes of a n auditive short term memory task as distractor with continuous EEG recording in a 139 

within -participant design . The choice of the distraction t ask has been made following the literature 140 

recommendation of hippocampal engagement (Borderie et al., 2024) . We hypothesized that (1) 141 

memory performances after rest would be better tha n memory performances after distracti on , (2) 142 

memory performance s after rest would be positively correlated with SO power , and negatively 143

2.2.2 Associative memory task 199 

The memory task itself was adapted from Guinet et al., (2026) . It has been coded in Python 200 

(version 3.1 0.19) using Psycho py (version 2023.1.3) . The overall structure of the task was as 201 

follows: Encoding 1, followed by Initial Retrieval 1, then the first post -learning condition (either Rest 202 

or the Distraction task). Thi s was followed by Encoding 2, Initial Retrieval 2, the alternate post -203 

learning condition (i.e., Rest if Distraction was done first, Distraction if Rest was done first) , and 204 

finally the Surprise Delay Retrieval (Figure 1A) . The associative memory task laste d approximately 205 

one hour, from Encoding 1 to the Surprise Delay Retrieval .206 

Encoding 207 

Each Encoding session (Encoding 1, Encoding 2) was composed of 2 5 trials. A trial consisted 208 

in a 1-second presentation of a fixation cross , followed by a 5 -second presentation of a landscape -209 

object association . Participants had 5 seconds to rate the likelihood of the association on a discrete 210 

scale from 0 (very unlikely) to 3 (very likely) (Figure 1B , left ). Participants were instructed to 211 

remember as many associations as possible for the retrieval test that followed. An encod ing 212 

session last ed approximately five minutes. 213 

Initial Retrieval 214 

Each Initial Retrieval session (Initial Retrieval 1, Initial Retrieval 2) was composed of 2 5 trials. 215 

A trial consisted of the presentation of one of the landscape pictures presented during the 216 

Encoding , for 5 seconds. Then, participants were given up to 15 seconds to type the name of the 217 

previously associated item using a keyboard. If they were unable to re trieve the item ’s name, they 218 

were instructed to enter “0.” A trial ended when the participant s pressed the spacebar to val idate 219 

their answer or when the 15 -second time limit elapsed (Figure 1B , right ). Order of presentation of 220 

the pictures of landscape was random. A retrieval session lasted nine minutes maximum. 221 

Surp rise Delayed Retrieval 222 

The Surprise Delay Retrieval session was composed of 50 trials similar to the Initial Retrieval 223 

sessions, and where t he 50 landscapes from Encoding 1 and Encoding 2 were presented in a 224 

randomi zed order. The surprise delayed retrieval lasted 18 minutes maximum. 225

Only EEG data from post -learning conditions (Rest and Distraction) have been analyzed for 338

artifacts locally . ICA was first computed on signals filtered between 1 and 100 Hz using the Infomax 350 

method from the MNE package (version 1.0.2 , Gramfort et al., 2013) . Ocular and cardiac artifacts 351

cardiac artifacts, based on electrocardiogram recordings. These artifacts were then corrected 353

(CWT -MSSA). The CWT -MSSA algorithm involved : 1) Localizing artifacts using wavelet -based 355 

methods ; 2) Classifying and correcting blink and cardiac artifacts using Singular Spectrum Analysi s356 

or applying a rectification procedure for saccades ; 3) Projecti ng the corrected components back to 357 

propagate the correction across all channels. Muscular artifacts were detected using a sliding Root 358

EEG data were processed using two distinct approaches. First, in replication -inspired 366 

analyses, to replicate the negative correlation between memory performance and slow oscillation 367

For replication -inspired analyses , since Brokaw et al. (2016) recorded EEG signals with 370

to reliably estimate the PSD of slow oscillations — since a 0.3 Hz oscillation has a period of 383

## 4 Discussio n623 

Our goal was to test the beneficial effect of short periods of wakeful rest after learning for 624 

memory retention, compared to engaging in distraction tasks , and the potential role of slow brain 625 

oscillations in this consolidation process during rest . We used a within -participant design to assess 626 

memory performance in an associative memory task follow ed either by 10 minutes of wakeful rest 627 

or by a 10 -minute distraction task, both accompanied by continuous EEG recording. Our analytical 628 

approach combined a replication -inspired method , closely aligned with Brokaw et al. (2016) , and 629 

a data -specific method, tailored to our specific dataset. Contrary to our hypothesis, we found no 630 

beneficial effect of wakeful rest on memory performances, compared to Distract ion condition. 631 

However, we found an advantage of the second learning compared to the first learning, and even 632 

more when considering Rest than Distraction condition. On the neurophysiological level, we found 633 

no relationship between EEG slow oscillations or alpha power, and memory performances. On the 634 

phenomenological level, we also found no significant relationship between mental activity during 635 

Rest and Distraction, daydreaming frequency, and memory performances. 636 

4.1 No replication of the wakeful rest effect o n memory performances 637 

The present study found no beneficial effect of post -learning wakeful rest on memory 638 

performance compared to a distraction task. We propose a set of methodological and theoretical 639 

explanations that, taken together, highlight the complexity of studying cons olidation during 640

While rest had been demonstrated to be beneficial for memory consolidation in several studies, 642 

(e.g.,  Gottselig et al., 2004; Dewar et al., 2012, 2014; Craig et al., 2014, 2016; Mercer, 2015; 643 

Brokaw et al. , 2016 ; Schlichting and Bäuml, 2017; Sacripante et al., 2019; Evans et al., 2021; 644 

Wang et al., 2021; King and Nicosia, 2022; Craig and Greer, 2024) , and reported in a recent 645 

systematic review and meta -analysis based on 63 experiments with a medium effect size 646 

(Hedges’s g = 0.448, 95% CI [0.339, 0.557], p < .001 ; Weng et al., 2025)  our result is consistent 647 

to a subset of studies that also failed to replicate the wakeful rest benefit.  Varma et al., (2017) 648 

attributed their negative result to the nature of their distraction task (i.e., N -back task) arguing it did 649 

not engage the hippocampal , thought to interfere with consolidation . Although our distraction task 650 

is hippocampo -dependant  (Borderie e t al., 2024) , it may rely on different hippocampal subsystems 651 

than those engaged during the associative encoding phase , potentially limiting its interference. 652 

Humiston et al. (2019) , who failed to replicate the behavioral findings from  Brokaw et al. (2016) 653 

using an identical protocol , invoked an insuficient sample size or sampling vari ability . Finally, 654 

studies attempting to extend the wakeful rest paradigm to ecological settings have consistenly 655 

found no ben efit  (Brandmark et al., 2020; Leetham et al., 2024; Seban et al., 2026) , underscoring 656 

the difficulty of translating laboratory findings to real -world situations .657 

A central issue in wakeful rest research is that the con dition itself is poorly standardized. 658 

Participants left to rest wakefully do not share a common cognitive or mental state: some engage 659 

in passive visual exploration, others ruminate on personal concerns, and others struggle against 660 

drowsiness. To a lesser extent during the distraction task , even though the state of the participants 661 

was constrained by the task , they still experience d fluctuations in their attentional engagement and 662 

free thoughts that were not monitored online. These variable inner experiences could substantially 663 

modulate consolidation processes, contributing to the large inter -individual variability observed in 664 

memory performance across studies. This suggests that the post -learning condition may be a poor 665 

proxy for the underlying ne ural processes relevant to awake consolidation. 666 

In the present study, we attempted to characterize mental activity during both conditions via 667 

post -experiment questionnaire , like it has been generally done in previous studies . Participants 668 

reported spen di ng significantly more time engaged in autobiographical thoughts during the Rest 669 

condition , and more time thinking about the task they were doing during the Distraction condition 670 

confirming that the two post -learning conditions induced significantly distinct cognitive states. 671 

However, we found no significant correlation between self -reported mental activity and memory 672 

performance. This absence of a relationship aligns with the mixed results in the existing literature: 673 

Brokaw et al. (2016) reported a positive correlation between mindwandering and memory 674 

performance during Rest, Humiston et al. (2019) foun d a negative correlation, Wamsley and 675 

Summer (2020) observed associations that depended on the type of memory task, and Wang et 676 

al. (2021) found no relationship. The inconsistency across studies suggests that the link between 677 

subjective men tal states and consolidation is neither direct nor robust, and may depend on 678 

unmeasured moderating variables. More broadly, and consistent with concerns raised in prior 679 

studies, retrospective estimation of the time spent in different mental states across a 10 -minute 680 

window is inherently difficult and subject to systematic distortion, limiting the precision and 681 

reliability of these self -reports. Future studies may benefit from using experience sampling or 682 

continuous ratings to capture mental activity in a mo re temporally resolved manner. In this regard, 683 

more precise analyses incorporating physiological and behavioral indices of attentional 684 

engagement , such as pupillometry or response time variability alongside subjective ratings of 685 

attentional focus ( Wamsley and Summer, 2020; Wamsley et al., 2023) , may be necessary to 686 

uncover the subtle dynamics underlying consolidation during wakefu lness .687

emphasizing the importance of a within -participant design, despite the risk of a task -learning effect. 689 

Indeed, the scores for Global Memory Change ranged from minimum -50 to maximum 9.09 in the 690 

Rest condition and from minimum -43. 75 to maximum 10.53 in the Distraction condition. This 691 

strong disparity can of course be due to normal inter -participant variability. However, based on 692 

participants oral accounts during the final interview, the encoding strategy used by participants 693

4.2 Absence of EEG correlates of memory consolidation 708 

We found no significant correlation between EEG activity during post -learning conditions a nd 709 

subsequent memory performances. Surprisingly, in our data, relative SO power was higher during 710 

the Distraction condition than during Rest (Table 2). While this difference was modest, it raises 711 

questions about the specificity of SO power as a predictor o f memory consolidation and suggests 712 

that absolute power levels may not straightforwardly reflect the quality of consolidation processes .713 

This discrepancy aligns with mixed findings in the literature: while some studies (e.g., Brokaw et 714 

al., 2016; Bencze et al., 2025) report a link between SO power during rest and long -term memory, 715 

others, like Wang et al. (2021) , found no such correlation across multiple frequency bands, despite 716 

better memory outcomes for rest and nap conditions compared to distraction. Several factors may 717 

explain these inconsistencies, such as protocol differences ( i.e., memo ry task s), retention delays 718 

or measurement approach (immediate recall test and average of spectral power in  Brokaw et al. ,719 

2016 , Wang et al., 2021 and our study ; recall test after 7 days , and change in spectral power from 720 

a pre -learning baseline to the post -learning rest period in  Bencze et al., 2025 ). However, we also 721 

note a more fundamental issue: averaging spectral power across an ent ire 10 -minute rest epoch 722 

and correlating it with a single memory score may be a too coarse approach to capture the subtle 723 

and transient neurophysiological dynamics that support consolidation. 724 

According to the two -stage model of memory (Buzsáki, 1989) , new experiences are initially 725 

encoded in parallel across both the hippocampus a nd neocortical networks . The hippocampus 726 

subsequently reactivates these memory traces during offline states , most notably during sleep , but 727 

potentially also during wakeful rest (Mednick et al., 2011; Wamsley, 2022) , orchestrating their 728 

replay in the neocortex. This iterative reactivation is thought to underlie the stabilization of initially 729 

fragile memory traces into durable long -term representations . Critically , the hippocampal -730 

neocortical dialogue is believed to rely on precise coupling between fast and slow oscillatory 731 

rhythms (Schreiner and Staudigl, 2020) , SO acting as the primary pacemak er. During SO, cortical 732 

networks alternate between hyperpolarized down and depolarized up states, each lasting from 0.2 733 

to 1 second . Hippocampal ripples and thalamic spindles consistently align with SO up states during 734 

slow -wave sleep (SWS), giving rise to a fine -tuned cross -frequency coupling widely recognized as 735 

an important electrophysiological substrate of memory consolidation (Clemens et al., 2007; 736 

Staresina et al., 2015; Keeble et al., 2025) .737 

While this mechanism is well -established during SWS , the functional role of SO during 738 

wakefulness remains considerably less clear . Although SOs have been obser ved in humans during 739 

wakefulness (Demanuele et al., 2010) , the oscillatory landscape of the waking brain differs 740 

markedly from that of SWS, suggesting that th e consolidation mechanisms at play may be partially 741 

distinct (Wamsley, 2022) . Scalp -recorded SO power during brief rest periods may provide only an 742 

indirect and potentially incomplete index of the consolidation processes of interest. Recent work 743

4.3 The need for methodological transparency 753 

Our analytical approach combined a replication -inspired method , closely aligned with Brokaw 754 

et al. (2016) , and a data -specific method, tailore d to our specific dataset. At the behavioral level, 755 

results were highly consistent across analyses, regardless of whether Global Memory Change or 756 

Relative Memory Change was used as the memory score, and whether linear models (replication -757 

inspired approach) or trial -by -trial GLMMs (data -specific approach) were employed. This 758 

convergence across methods strengthens confidence in the robustness of our behavioral findings. 759 

At the neurophysiological level, however, notable differences emerged between the two 760 

anal ytical pipelines. Neither the replication -inspired analysis ( Global Memory Change , mastoid -761 

like reference, 6 channels, 4 -second Hanning windows for PSD computation) nor the data -specific 762 

analysis (Relative Memory Change , average reference, 62 channels, 20 -second Hanning 763 

windows) yielded significant correlations between SO power and memory performance. Yet, 764 

differences in effect sizes and p-values were observed between pipelines . To disentangle the 765 

respective contributions of the memory score and the EEG pre processing parameters in these 766 

discrepancies , we conducted exploratory cross -over analyses to explore the impact of reverting 767 

the two memory scores , as well as window duration for PSD computation, and the offline reference 768 

on the correlation between memory performances and SO power. In short, while reverting the 769 

memory scores and the window duration did not seem to change the correlations values, the offline 770 

reference change almost reverts the direction of some correlations though it did not reach the 771 

thres hold of statistical significance. Further details on these analyses are available in the 772 

supplementary file (Supplementary Figure 8, 9 and 10). 773 

Taken together, these observations highlight a broader issue in the field: the considerable 774 

variability in neur ophysiological analysis pipelines across studies, often accompanied by 775 

insufficient reporting and justification of parameter choices. 776 

4.4 Order effects interpretation 777 

To our knowledge, only a minority of within -participant studies have explicitly tested for order 778 

effects. Evans et al. (2021 ) found no significant effect of Order nor interaction between Condition 779 

and Order on memory performances. Brokaw et al. (2016) and Martini et al. (2018) , just as our 780 

results showed, both reported better memory performance when Rest was completed s econd 781 

rather than first . Notably , in both studies , memory performance in the Distraction condition did not 782 

improve when that condition was presented second . Brokaw et al. (2016) attributed this pattern to 783 

a facilitatory effect of distraction on subsequent new encoding, while Martini et al. (2018) argued 784 

that distraction interferes with the consolidation of material learned prior to the rest period . We785 

propose an alternative interpretation : the observed order effect may primarily reflect a task -learning 786 

effect, whereby participants became more familiar with the associative memory paradigm and 787 

developed mo re effective encoding strategies during the second attempt . The apparent advantage 788 

of Rest when presented second may not stem from condition -specific consolidation dynamics, but 789 

rather from the interplay between task familiarity (associative memory task is easier when 790 

performed the second time tha n the first) and emotional state (e.g., confidence of anxiety -related 791 

thoughts , may be less present during Distraction due to task focus ).792

A critical interpretive challenge in within -participant designs is the difficulty of dissociating a 793 

post -encoding consolidation effect from a learning -related improvement already present at the time 794 

of encoding. To address this, we examined initial retrieval performance alone, prior to any post -795 

learning condit ion, and found that the performance advantage for the second learning was already 796 

evident at this stage. This result constitutes an additional argument in favor of a task -order learning 797 

effect, independent of any consolidation process. To our knowledge, su ch a learning effect has not 798 

been explicitly reported in wakeful rest memory literature using within -participant designs, though 799 

its absence from published reports may reflect a lack of systematic investigation rather than a n800 

absence of the phenomenon. Thi s raises a broader methodological concern: within -participant 801 

designs, while statistically efficient and well -suited to control for individual differences, may be 802 

particularly vulnerable to order effects when the target effect is of small to moderate magni tude. In 803 

such cases, task learning may obscure, dilute, or interact with the consolidation effect of interest, 804 

complicating the interpretation of condition differences. 805 

4.5 Conclusion 806 

The present study failed to find a significant advantage of post -learning R est over Distraction 807 

on associative memory performance, nor any relationship between post -learning SO or alpha 808 

power, mental activities, and subsequent memory performances . Although our aim was to 809 

conceptually replicate and assess the generalizability of the behavioral and EEG findings of 810 

Brokaw et al. (2016), several methodological differences may account for the discrepancy, 811 

including the use of an associative memory task with cued retrieval rather than story recall with 812 

free recall, a hippocampus -depende nt auditory distractor task, and a delayed final retrieval test 813 

administered after both conditions to preserve the surprise effect and limit intentional rehearsal. 814 

Despite these differences, our protocol followed widely used procedures in the wakeful rest 815 

literature and was grounded in prior evidence. Our cross -analysis comparisons further highlight 816 

that small -to -moderate effects are particularly sensitive to EEG preprocessing decisions , notably 817 

the reference choice and PSD estimation window , underscoring the importance of methodological 818 

transparency in this literature. Whether the absence of a wakeful rest benefit reflects differences 819 

between paradigms, insufficient sensitivity of broadband SO power as a consolidation index, or 820 

uncontrolled inter -individual factors such as encoding strategy, remains an open question. Fut ure 821 

work should prioritize more precise neurophysiological measures, systematic reporting of order 822 

effects, and greater standardization of analytical pipelines to build a more cumulative science of 823 

offline memory consolidation. 824
