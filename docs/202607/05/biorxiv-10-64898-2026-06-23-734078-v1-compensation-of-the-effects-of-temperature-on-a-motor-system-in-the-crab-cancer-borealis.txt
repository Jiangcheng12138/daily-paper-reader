Abstract 22 

Rising ocean temperatures challenge ectothermic animals to maintain essential 23 

behaviors such as movement and feeding. We asked how a complete 24 

neuromuscular pathway preserves function when every component process 25 

responds differently to warming. In the pyloric system of the crab Cancer borealis ,26 

we simultaneously recorded motor nerve activity, muscle membrane potential, and 27 

contraction. Warming preserved rhythmic nerve activity and excitatory junctional 28 

potentials, but contraction declined and failed first. Fixed low-frequency 29 

stimulation, mimicking cold-temperature motor output, resulted in reduced 30 

contraction at warm temperatures, whereas higher-frequency stimulation, 31 

mimicking warm-temperature motor output, partially restored contraction. Warming 32 

hyperpolarized muscle fibers, moving them farther from contraction threshold, but 33 

also reduced input resistance, which together limited over-excitability. However, 34 

high-potassium stimulation revealed that the muscle contractile machinery 35 

remained functional. Thus, warming acts differently across levels, and overlapping 36 

compensatory mechanisms help preserve neuromuscular function across a wide 37 

range of temperatures. 38 

39 

Significance statement 40 

Cold-blooded animals that live in climates with significant seasonal changes in 41 

ambient temperature must have myriad mechanisms to function over a wide range 42 

of environmental conditions. We explore the effects of temperature at multiple 43 

levels of organization within the stomatogastric system of the crab, Cancer 44 

borealis . We find a series of compensatory mechanisms that cooperatively help 45 

maintain stable function despite the fact that the motor patterns, neuromuscular 46 

junctions and muscle functions are all differently temperature dependent. 47

Introduction 49 

Crustaceans, such as lobsters and crabs, are poikilotherms and some species 50 

inhabit environments with large daily and seasonal temperature variations. In their 51 

New England Atlantic Ocean habitat, the crab Cancer borealis experiences 52 

temperatures ranging from ~3°C to ~25°C (Marder et al. , 2015; ‘NOAA’, 2026). 53 

Despite temperature's profound influence on neuronal and muscular components, 54 

these animals maintain life-critical processes across this range. Understanding 55 

how temperature compensation cooperates across different organizational levels 56 

presents an intriguing challenge. Specifically, we ask whether temperature 57 

changes affect motor pattern production, the synapse between the motor neuron 58 

and the muscles they innervate, and nerve-evoked muscle movement 59

Temperature affects both passive and active properties of neuromuscular 62 

junctions across a wide range of species, both vertebrates and invertebrates. 63 

Much work has been done in species like the frog ( Rana pipiens ) (Katz and Miledi, 64 

1965) , crayfish ( Astacus leptodactylus, Procambarus clarkii ) (Harri and Florey, 65 

1977; Fischer and Florey, 1981; White, 1983), crabs ( Carcinus maenas , Cancer 66 

pagurus , Ocypode ceratophthalma ) (Florey and Hoyle, 1976; Stephens and 67 

Atwood, 1983; Philip J. Stephens, 1985; Hyde et al. , 2015), barnacles (Dipolo and 68 

Latorre, 1972), rat diaphragm (Ward, Crowley and Johns, 1972; Head, 1983), 69 

drosophila (Ormerod, Scibelli and Littleton, 2022). In general, warming shortens 70 

synaptic delay and often reduces the amplitude and temporal summation of the 71 

excitatory junctional potentials (EJPs). Warming can also increase miniature end-72 

plate potential frequency (Ward, Crowley and Johns, 1972). Warming alters 73 

passive membrane properties. It commonly hyperpolarizes the resting membrane 74 

potential, decreases input resistance, reducing synaptic integration (Fischer and 75 

Florey, 1981; Jacquerie et al. , 2026). At the mechanical level, warming weakens 76 

nerve-evoked contractions (Foldes et al. , 1978; Fischer and Florey, 1981; Thuma 77 

et al. , 2013). The excitation-contraction threshold in the crayfish opener muscle is 78 

relatively temperature independent (Fischer and Florey, 1981). Together, these 79 

studies indicate that warming can challenge neuromuscular systems through 80 

combined effects on synaptic transmission, membrane properties, and force 81 

production. 82 

Many studies have also examined the effects of temperature on motor 83 

pattern generation. The stomatogastric nervous system of crabs and lobsters, 84 

particularly the pyloric circuit involved in food filtering has been extensively 85 

studied. In this system, warming generally increases pyloric rhythm frequency 86 

while preserving the relative phase relationships of the motor pattern over a broad 87 

permissive temperature range. At higher temperatures, however, the rhythm 88 

becomes irregular or fails, a phenomenon often referred to as a “crash” (Tang et 89 

al. , 2010, 2012; Marder et al. , 2015). In Cancer borealis , pyloric rhythm frequency 90 

approximately doubles between 11°C and 21°C (Tang et al. , 2010, 2012; Pereira 91 

et al. , 2014; Soofi et al. , 2014; Marder et al. , 2015; Städele, Heigele and Stein, 92 

2015; Haddad and Marder, 2018; Kushinsky, Morozova and Marder, 2019; Powell 93 

et al. , 2021; Städele and Stein, 2022; Hampton, Kedia and Marder, 2024; 94 

Schapiro et al. , 2024). These observations raise a central question: how does the 95 

neuromuscular system respond when motor pattern frequency increases with 96 

temperature? 97

the ventilatory central pattern generator of the locust Locusta migratoria 99

Motor pattern generation and neuromuscular transmission have usually 104

mechanisms emerge across levels of organization. The closest related studies are 107 

those on the crustacean heart (Worden et al. , 2006; Kushinsky, Morozova and 108 

Marder, 2019), where warming increases beat frequency while contraction 109 

amplitude eventually declines, and the work of Thuma and colleagues on the 110

fails before the pyloric rhythm itself as temperature rises (Thuma et al. , 2013). 112 

Here, we study both the pyloric motor pattern and its target muscles. This work 113 

reveals how cooperative changes across the motor circuit, neuromuscular 114 

synapse, and muscle help sustain motor output during temperature challenge. 115

To isolate the contribution of motor input parameters from the temperature-220 

dependent changes in network activity, we severed the lvn and stimulated it with 221 

controlled burst trains while varying saline temperature independently. Each train 222 

consisted of 20 bursts of 8 spikes, a spike number chosen to match the mean LP 223 

burst size observed in our recordings (Figure 1C) and consistent with values 224 

reported by Tang et al. (2012). We designed four stimulus patterns, “LP 6”, “LP 225 

11”, “LP 16”, and “LP 21”, to mimic the LP neuron activity recorded at 6, 11, 16, 226 

and 21°C, respectively, varying both pyloric frequency (0.5 to 2 Hz) and intraburst 227 

spike frequency (20 to 60 Hz) (Figure 3A, top). 228 

At a fixed stimulation pattern, muscle force depended strongly on 229 

temperature, with contraction amplitude decreasing at elevated temperatures 230 

(Figure 3A, by columns; LME main effect of temperature, p = 9.82 × 10 -5 , n = 7 231 

animals) .This is consistent with the temperature-dependent decline in contraction 232 

force reported by Thuma et al. (2013) for the p1 muscle of Panulirus interruptus 233 

under controlled nerve stimulation. 234 

At a fixed temperature, stimulation pattern also affected the maximum force 235 

reached during the 20-burst train (Figure 3A, by rows; LME main effect of 236 

stimulation pattern, p = 0.039). The fastest pattern (LP 21) generally produced 237 

greater force than the slowest (LP 6) at every temperature tested. 238 

At low temperatures (6°C, 11°C), high-frequency stimulation caused 239 

temporal summation of individual contractions: the tonic amplitude, defined as the 240 

minimum contraction to which the muscle relaxes between neuron bursts, 241 

increased, while the phasic amplitude, defined as the oscillation amplitude riding 242 

on this tonic baseline, decreased. This was clearly visible for LP 21 at 6°C (raw 243 

trace, Figure 3A) and it is consistent with the frequency-dependent contraction 244 

components described by Morris and Hooper (1997, 1998). This reduction of the 245 

phasic amplitude at cold temperature for a high frequency pattern is shown in 246 

Figure 3C for 7 animals, and demonstrates that the muscle cannot relax fast 247 

enough to follow high frequency discharge. Figure 3D supports that relaxation 248 

time constant considerably decreases with temperature, from 182 ms at 6°C to 57 249 

ms at 21°C. 250

Warming reduces the effectiveness of EJPs at the neuromuscular junction 266 

During STG-evoked motor activity, contractions failed at high temperature while 267 

EJPs were still present, suggesting that the loss of mechanical output was not 268 

simply caused by the disappearance of synaptic input. To test how temperature 269 

affects neuromuscular transmission under controlled conditions, we cut the nerve 270 

and stimulated the lvn with paired pulses from 1, 5, 10, 20, 30, and 40 Hz across 271 

temperatures (11°C, 16°C, 21°C, and 26°C). 272 

Paired-pulse stimulation increased EJP amplitude at all temperatures and 273 

frequencies (Figure 4A-B). EJP 2 was significantly smaller at 21°C than at 11°C 274 

across all stimulation frequencies (Holm-corrected p ≤ 0.024 for all frequencies), 275 

showing that warming reduced the enhancement of the synaptic response 276 

produced by the second stimulus. At 26°C, spontaneous EJPs were observed, 277 

indicating spontaneous nerve discharge at this temperature. 278 

A linear mixed-effects model confirmed a strong main effect of stimulation 279 

frequency on EJP 2 amplitude (p = 7.6 × 10 -15 ), a significant main effect of 280 

temperature (p = 0.015), and no significant frequency × temperature interaction (p 281 

= 0.75). The absence of a significant interaction indicates that the frequency-282 

dependent facilitation of EJP 2 is preserved in shape across temperatures -283 

warming shifts the amplitude curve downward uniformly rather than selectively 284 

abolishing high-frequency enhancement. Even at 21°C, high-frequency stimulation 285 

still produced larger EJPs than low-frequency stimulation (40 Hz vs 1 Hz at 21°C: 286 

Holm-corrected p = 5.5 × 10 -8 ). However, this frequency-dependent compensation 287 

was incomplete: the second EJP at 40 Hz and 21°C was not significantly different 288 

from the second EJP at 10 Hz or 20 Hz at 11°C (Holm-corrected p = 0.64 and p = 289 

0.18, respectively). 290

= 10 fibers, n = 6 animals, Figure 4C), moving the membrane farther from the 295 

muscle excitation threshold. Input resistance calculated at steady-state also 296 

decreased with temperature (Friedman test, p = 0.0074, n = 4 animals, Figure 4D), 297

Contractile machinery remains functional at high temperatures 315 

The loss of nerve-evoked contractions at high temperatures is partly explained by 316 

a decrease in the efficacy of neuromuscular transmission. However, the 317 

temperature sensitivity of the contractile machinery of the muscle itself is unknown 318 

and may also contribute to the loss of nerve-evoked contractions. To address this, 319 

we depolarized the muscles by applying saline solution with 10x the standard 320 

concentration of potassium for 40 s to elicit contractures at 11, 16, 21, and 26°C 321 

(Figure 5A). 322 

We measured the peak force produced during these contractures (n = 10 323 

animals, Figure 5B). Temperature had a significant effect on contracture amplitude 324 

(Friedman test, p = 0.00023). Contracture amplitude increased moderately at 16°C 325 

compared to 11°C (Holm-corrected 0.0059). At 21°C and 26°C, contracture 326 

amplitude was unchanged compared to 11°C (for 21°C to 11°C, Holm-corrected p 327 

= 0.85; for 26°C to 11°C, Holm-corrected p = 0.21). Thus, while temperature 328 

influenced the amplitude of high potassium evoked contractures, the muscles 329 

were still capable of contracting at 26°C. In contrast with the semi-intact 330 

preparations used for STG-evoked recording, by 20°C, 3 out of 7 preparations had 331 

lost contractions, while the remaining 4 had lost the ability to produce contractions 332 

by 23°C. 333 

Taken together, this suggests that the contractile machinery of the muscles 334 

remains functional at warm temperatures at which other crucial components of the 335 

neuromuscular system have failed. 336

Discussion 338 

Living systems face a fundamental physical problem: every protein, every ion 339 

channel, every membrane, and every biochemical reaction responds to 340 

temperature, yet the organism must maintain coordinated function across a wide 341 

range of environmental conditions. The components that together produce 342 

behavior - from the neurons that generate a motor pattern, to the synapses that 343 

relay commands, to the muscles that execute movement - each carry their own 344 

individual thermal sensitivity. What is remarkable is that evolution has produced 345 

systems capable of sustaining coherent output despite these differences. Rather 346 

than making all parts equally robust, or equally temperature sensitive, evolution 347 

appears to have built systems in which compensatory changes at one level offset 348 

limitations at another, and in which the architecture of the pathway itself distributes 349 

the thermal challenge across multiple components working in concert. 350 

Understanding which components are most vulnerable, which provide 351 

compensation, and how the system as a whole sustains performance is one of the 352 

deep questions in physiology (Robertson and Money, 2012; Marder et al. , 2015; 353 

Marder and Rue, 2021). Thermal resilience is also necessary in the human 354 

nervous system, where heat stress, fever, and neurological disease all challenge 355 

the coordinated operation of neurons, synapses, and muscles (Kim and Connors, 356 

2012; Wang et al. , 2014; Thompson, 2015; Peters et al. , 2016; Christogianni et al. ,357 

2018; Mavashov et al. , 2023). Gaining insight, as we do here, into how 358 

temperature sensitivity and compensation operate across levels of organization 359 

should illuminate temperature resilience in both healthy and neurologically 360 

compromised humans. 361 

We addressed this challenge in the neuromuscular transform of C.borealis .362 

While previous work has studied the effect of temperature on individual 363 

components of motor systems at different levels of organization independently, we 364 

looked at them in the interacting system. Our data suggest that, in the 365 

transformation from motor pattern generation to movement, the neuromuscular 366 

junction is one of the least heat resilient components of this system. The decline in 367 

muscle function with warming is likely explained by multiple interacting factors: the 368 

hyperpolarization of the muscle fibers (Jacquerie et al. , 2026), which moves them 369 

farther from the threshold for excitation–contraction coupling, the reduction in EJP 370 

amplitude and decay time constant, leading to the reduction in synaptic 371 

integration, which limits temporal summation and decreases the depolarization 372 

produced by each motor neuron spike; and, at extreme temperatures, the failure of 373 

the nerve itself, consistent with previous results in related systems (Tang et al. ,374 

2010; Haddad and Marder, 2018; Schapiro et al. , 2024; Carrier et al. , 2026). 375 

Together, these effects indicate that temperature does not act on a single isolated 376

Muscle fiber hyperpolarization is likely caused by the activation of specific 379

process with molecular data showing that C. borealis expresses two two-pore 381

temperature-dependent opening of K2P channels, leading to hyperpolarization 387 

and decreases in input resistance, may be advantageous. Because motor nerve 388 

activity nearly doubles as temperature increases (Tang et al. , 2010), the 389 

hyperpolarization of the muscle fibers may act as a protective mechanism, 390 

preventing excessive depolarization and over-contractability when high-frequency 391 

input reaches the muscle. Jointly, reduced input resistance decreases the time 392 

constant of EJPs and synaptic integration, which may act to prevent excessive 393 

depolarization by reducing summation between spikes (Philip J Stephens, 1985). 394 

This interpretation is supported by the observation that muscle contractility 395 

evoked by high-potassium application appears less temperature-sensitive than 396 

contractions elicited by nerve stimulation or contractions when the ganglion 397 

remains attached to its target muscles. This suggests that the contractile 398 

machinery itself may retain substantial function across temperature, whereas 399 

synaptic transmission, muscle excitability, or presynaptic release may impose 400 

stronger limits on movement. The neuromuscular junction may therefore represent 401 

a critical site where temperature-dependent compensation can succeed or fail. 402 

More work will be needed to determine how warming affects the presynaptic 403 

terminal, transmitter release, synaptic reliability, and the ability of neuromodulators 404 

to stabilize neuromuscular output. 405 

In their natural environment, C.borealis experience seasonal temperatures 406 

that can approach or exceed 20°C. Our experiments, performed in animals 407 

acclimated to 11°C, suggest that this mid-range acclimation is not sufficient to 408 

preserve robust function across the full thermal range of C.borealis experience in 409 

the ocean. This implies that long-term acclimation, neuromodulation, or both are 410 

likely required to extend the physiological operating range of the system (Blundon, 411 

1989; Chung and Webster, 2004; Zhurov and Brezina, 2005; Hamilton et al. ,412 

2007; Jury and Watson, 2013; Thuma et al. , 2013; Lewis and Ayers, 2014). 413 

Consistent with this idea, Kedia et al. showed that acclimation can shift the 414 

operating range of Homarus americanus (Kedia et al. , 2026) and that C.borealis 415 

network behavior is also influenced by acclimation and ocean experience (Tang et 416

In addition to cellular and molecular processes, behavioral strategies can 425

Many foundational studies on temperature and neuromuscular physiology 435 

were performed between 1950 to 1990 (Harri and Florey, 1977; Fischer and 436 

Florey, 1981; Stephens and Atwood, 1982, 1983; Philip J. Stephens, 1985). This 437 

earlier work established important principles, and, in many cases, our results 438 

validate its major conclusions. Then the field moved on. Between roughly 1990 439 

and 2020, studies relevant to the effects of temperature and other environmental 440 

conditions on neuromuscular junction and muscle excitability in non-model 441 

organisms have largely disappeared from the literature. Today, we can return to 442 

some of these questions in the context of the challenges posed by altered 443 

environmental factors. What was missing then - and what we now have - is the 444 

ability to move from the phenomenological to the mechanistic. We can sequence 445 

genomes, identify ion channel families, relate molecular expression to cellular 446 

physiology, and connect single-channel biophysics to whole-system behavior. 447 

The urgency of this work extends beyond a single crustacean species or a 448 

neuromuscular preparation. As environmental temperatures rise at unprecedented 449

cellular and biophysical mechanisms that determine whether a nervous system, a 452 

synapse, or a muscle will remain functional under thermal stress. If neuroscience 453

fundamental physiology in ecologically relevant organisms, such as marine 455
