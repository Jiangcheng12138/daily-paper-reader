Abstract 51 

S-palmitoylation modulates the activity of many cardiac ion channels, yet the upstream 52 

signals that control this post-translational modification (PTM) are poorly defined. Here 53 

we identify a phosphorylation -dependent mechanism that governs splice variant-54 

specific S -palmitoylation of the potassium channel Kv4.3. Using Acyl -RAC in native 55 

tissue and heterologous cells, we map palmitoylation to C546/547 in the intrinsically 56 

disordered Kv4.3 C -terminal tail. The short Kv4.3 splice variant Kv4.3S is ~2.5-3 -fold more 57 

palmitoylated than the long splice variant Kv4.3L, and systematic mutagenesis localises 58 

the dominant inhibitory determinant of Kv4.3 S-palmitoylation to residues 488-498 within 59 

the Kv4.3L -specific splice insert. KChIP2.1 promotes accumulation of a post-60 

translationally modified Kv4.3 species that is selectively S-palmitoylated, and nanobody -61 

targeted dephosphorylation removes this species and reduces Kv4.3 S-palmitoylation. 62 

Phos -tag electrophoresis and C -terminal truncation mapping identify S538 as the 63 

principal phosphorylation site enabling Kv4.3 S-palmitoylation; mutation of S538 64 

markedly reduces formation of the palmitoylation -competent species. TurboID 65 

proximity -labelling and biochemical assays indicate that phosphorylation enhances 66 

recruitment of Kv4.3 to zDHHC5. Acute kinase inhibition rapidly eliminates 67 

phosphorylation but only gradually reduces palmitoylation, revealing temporal 68 

uncoupling between these PTMs. Functionally, non -palmitoylatable Kv4.3S exhibits 69 

larger peak currents, faster inactivation, and a left -shifted activation curve, consistent 70 

with palmitoylation limiting channel function and modulating gating transitions. Together, 71 

these findings identify phosphorylation of S538 as a priming modification that licenses 72 

Kv4.3 S-palmitoylation at C546/547, explain splice -variant differences in S-73 

palmitoylation, and define a PTM cascade that tunes Kv4.3 channel behaviour. 74

Introduction 76 

Coordinated cardiac electrical activity is essential for normal cardiac function; electrical 77 

instability is a hallmark of many cardiac pathologies. 1, 2 The cardiac action potential (AP) 78 

reflects the tightly controlled activity of inward depolarising and outward repolarising 79 

currents across the cardiomyocyte plasma membrane. 3-6 Cardiac potassium channels, 80 

responsible for regulating cardiac repolarisation and resting membrane potential, are 81 

the most functionally diverse family of cardiac ion channels. Broadly, they can be divided 82 

into three categories: voltage-gated, inward rectifying and background potassium 83 

currents. 5, 7 Over 40 different voltage-sensitive (K v) channels are expressed across 84 

multiple tissues in both excitable and non-excitable cells. Generally, these K v channels 85 

consist of four pore forming a-subunits and multiple b-subunits with a range of 86 

interacting accessory subunits that fine tune channel function. K v channels can either be 87 

homo-oligomers or hetero-oligomers. 5, 8 

88 

During phase 1 of the cardiac AP the transient outward current (I to ) transiently repolarises 89 

ventricular myocytes, regulating AP plateau voltage, morphology and duration. I to is 90 

composed of two currents, I to1 and I to2 . I to1 carries a potassium current that activates and 91 

inactivates rapidly and can be divided into fast (I tof , formed by Kv4.2/4.3) and slow (I tos ,92 

formed by Kv1.4/1.7/3.4). 9, 10 Kv4.3 is a member of the Kv4 (Shal-related) family that 93 

mediates outward A-type potassium currents. 5, 8, 11 The expression of I tof and I tos differs 94 

across species, giving rise to the different AP waveforms. 12-14 

95 

Kv4.3 contains four pore forming a-subunits, with each subunit containing six 96 

transmembrane domains (S1-S6) and intracellular N and C termini. The N-terminal 97 

tetramerization (T1) domain is required for assembly of the four a-subunits. 15-17 The 98 

intracellular C-terminal tail of Kv4.3 is largely unstructured. 16, 18 Various accessory 99 

proteins interact with and regulate the activity of Kv4.3, including the KCNEs, DPPs, 100 

SAP97, and KChIPs. 19-26 For example, KChIPs interact with both termini of Kv4.3 to 101 

stabilise the complex. 16 Co-expression with KChIP2 significantly increases Kv4.3 cell 102 

surface expression, slows I to activation and enhances I to current amplitude. 11, 16, 24, 27-30 

103 

KChIP2 expression is downregulated in heart failure (HF) and this has been suggested to 104 

contribute to changes in both calcium handling and repolarisation. 31 

105 

Unique to Kv4.3 within the Kv4 family is the presence of an additional exon which is 106 

included in ~50% of transcripts in the heart. Compared to the short variant Kv4.3S and 107 

other Kv4 family members, the alternatively spliced long variant Kv4.3L gains a 19 amino 108 

acid region (resides 488-506) within the S6 proximal unstructured cytosolic C-tail. 32, 33 

109 

The current density, gating kinetics and voltage dependence of homomeric Kv4.3L and 110 

Kv4.3S recorded in murine fibroblasts at room temperature are indistinguishable. 34 

111 

However, Kv4.3L contains a consensus phosphorylation site, T504, which is 112 

phosphorylated by protein kinase C (PKC). PKC both reduces peak Kv4.3-mediated 113 

currents in mammalian cells, 34 and differentially modulates closed-state inactivation of 114 

Kv4.3L in Xenopus oocytes. 35 Expression of the spliced forms remodels differently during 115 

the evolution of heart failure. Kv4.3L expression is upregulated ~33% and Kv4.3S is 116 

downregulated ~75% in the failing heart. This highlights the need for a greater 117 

understanding of the splice variants as potential therapeutic targets in heart failure. 36 

118 

Lipid modifications, particularly those involving the attachment of a fatty acid, are the 119 

most common type of post-translational modification (PTM). These lipid modifications 120

contribute to the stereochemical diversity of proteins and can be reversible or 121 

irreversible. S-palmitoylation, sometimes referred to as S-acylation or palmitoylation, 122 

refers to the reversible attachment of a C16 fatty acid, typically palmitic acid, to a 123 

cysteine residue via a thioester bond. S-palmitoylation reversibly anchors intracellular 124 

regions of proteins to membranes and can alter protein trafficking, protein-protein 125 

interactions and protein activity. 37-39 

126 

In cardiomyocytes, multiple ion channels and their regulatory proteins are S-127 

palmitoylated including HCN4, Nav1.5, the a1C-subunit of Cav1.2, NCX, KChIP2.1 and 128 

phospholemman. 40-47 Here, we show that Kv4.3 is S -palmitoylated at C546/547 within its 129 

cytoplasmic C-terminal tail and that the Kv4.3S splice variant is more heavily 130 

palmitoylated than Kv4.3L. By systematic mutagenesis, we identify residues within the 131 

N-terminal half of the 19 -amino -acid splice insert as primary determinants of this 132 

difference. We further demonstrate that phosphorylation promotes acquisition of a 133 

palmitoylation -competent Kv4.3 species, consistent with a model in which site -specific 134 

phosphorylation primes the Kv4.3 C -tail for S -palmitoylation at C546/547. Functionally, 135 

we show that loss of palmitoylation produces a gain -of -function phenotype 136 

characterised by increased current amplitude and altered gating kinetics, indicating that 137 

S-palmitoylation acts as a negative regulator of Kv4.3 activity. Together, these findings 138 

define a phosphorylation -dependent mechanism and cis -sequence elements that 139 

govern splice -variant-specific palmitoylation of Kv4.3. 140

Results 141 

Kv4.3 is S-palmitoylated at C546/547 142 

Kv4.3 and its homologues have been identified in several mammalian palmitoyl 143 

proteomes, 48-51 but to date no palmitoylation sites have been identified. We used acyl-144 

resin assisted capture (acyl-RAC) to assess Kv4.3 palmitoylation in mouse heart and 145 

brain tissue and in HEK293 cells transiently expressing Kv4.3L. Across all contexts, Kv4.3 146 

was robustly recovered in Acyl -RAC eluates, indicating S -palmitoylation under native 147 

and heterologous conditions (Figure 1A). Systematic cysteine substitution in HEK293 148 

cells mapped palmitoylation to C546/547 within the Kv4.3 intrinsically disordered C -149 

terminal tail. Deleting the N-terminal T1 domain also substantially reduced 150 

palmitoylation of full -length Kv4.3L, placing channel assembly upstream of a151 

palmitoylation -competent state (Figure 1B). This T1 dependence of Kv4.3 palmitoylation 152 

is consistent with the idea that proper tetramerization and/or a specific inter -domain 153 

juxtaposition is required to expose C546/547 to the acyltransferase active site. 17, 52 

154 

155 

Differential S-palmitoylation of Kv4.3 splice variants 156 

Unique to Kv4.3 within the Kv4 family is the presence of an additional spliced region. The 157 

alternatively spliced version, Kv4.3S, lacks a 19 amino acid region (residues 488-506, 158 

Kv4.3L numbering) within the S6 proximal cytosolic C-terminus compared to Kv4.3L 159 

(Figure 1C). We first confirmed that the same cysteines are palmitoylated in both splice 160 

variants (Figure 1D). Notably, Kv4.3S was significantly more S-palmitoylated than Kv4.3L 161 

(Figure 1E). Because phosphorylation and S-palmitoylation can antagonise each other, 162 

we first tested whether phosphorylation within the 19-residue spliced segment explains 163 

the difference. Mutations of two established phosphorylation sites in the alternatively 164 

spliced region, T504A or S499A, did not alter the S-palmitoylation of Kv4.3L to match that 165 

of Kv4.3S (Figure 1F). 19, 35 We conclude from these experiments that Kv4.3L is not less 166 

palmitoylated than Kv4.3S because of phosphorylation in the alternatively spliced region. 167 

To define sequence determinants of differential splice variant palmitoylation, we 168 

performed complementary perturbations: (i) alanine scanning across residues 488-506, 169 

(ii) stepwise truncations, (iii) insertion of sub-segments into Kv4.3S, and (iv) complete 170 

scrambling of the 19-residue splice site. All mutants were expressed in HEK293 cells, 171 

and their S-palmitoylation assessed via Acyl-RAC (Figure 1G). Alanine substitutions at 172 

GLS (488-490), YLV (491-493), and LL (497-498) but not SVRT (499-502) or STIK (503-506) 173 

each increased Kv4.3L palmitoylation. Alanine mutants of the DDP block (494-496), 174 

which matches a DXXLL endosomal targeting motif 53 expressed poorly. Kv4.3L 175 

truncations removing residues 488-493, 488-498, or 488-502 (but not 499-506) likewise 176 

enhanced palmitoylation (Figure 1H), localising the strongest negative influence on 177 

Kv4.3L palmitoylation to the N-terminal half of the splice site (488-498). Truncation 178 

mutants of the DDPLL block (494-498) expressed poorly. Introducing only GLSYLV (488-179 

493) or DDPLL (494-498) into Kv4.3S did not depress its palmitoylation (Figure 1I). A 180 

scrambled-sequence Kv4.3L variant expressed but was not detectably palmitoylated 181 

and accumulated to lower abundance than wild-type (Figure 1J). Together, these 182 

experiments indicate that residues 488-498 (GLSYLVDDPLL) act as a cis-determinant 183 

that reduces palmitoylation of Kv4.3L, with GLS, YLV, and LL contributing most. 184

KChIP2.1 enhances Kv4.3 palmitoylation without abolishing splice -variant differences 186 

Using a bicistronic Kv4.3-IRES-KChIP2.1 vector, we asked whether the auxiliary subunit 187 

modifies Kv4.3 palmitoylation. Co-expression with KChIP2.1 significantly increased 188 

palmitoylation relative to expression of both Kv4.3 splice variants alone, yet Kv4.3S 189 

remained more palmitoylated than Kv4.3L (Figure 1K). KChIP2.1 itself was palmitoylated 190 

but its palmitoylation was unchanged by co-expression with either Kv4.3 variant (Figure 191 

1K), indicating that KChIP2.1 promotes a palmitoylation-competent conformation / 192 

cellular location rather than being reciprocally modified by the pore-forming subunit. 193 

194 

A slower -migrating, palmitoylation -competent Kv4.3 species requires KChIP2.1 and N -195 

terminal architecture 196 

To test whether a specific Kv4.3 conformation is prerequisite for palmitoylation of the 197 

distal C-tail, we engineered a transmembrane-deleted mutant (TMD-del) containing the 198 

N-terminus, the linker to the first transmembrane domain, and the full C-terminus 199 

(Figure 2A). TMD-del was not palmitoylated when expressed alone. Strikingly, co-200 

expression with KChIP2.1 restored palmitoylation of TMD-del and yielded two Kv4.3 201 

bands on SDS-PAGE; only the slower migrating species was enriched by Acyl-RAC (Figure 202 

2B). The slower band was also observed for both TMD-del splice variants and a non-203 

palmitoylatable mutant co-expressed with KChIP2.1 (Figure 2C). Replacing KChIP2.1 204 

with KChIP2.2, or deleting the N-terminus or T1 region from Kv4.3, abolished the slower 205 

band (Figure 2D). Thus, KChIP2.1 and the Kv4.3 N-terminal/T1 architecture are necessary 206 

for formation of a slower-migrating species that is competent for palmitoylation, 207 

independent of splice form or cysteine palmitoylation state. 208 

209 

Targeted dephosphorylation collapses the slower species and reduces palmitoylation 210 

We hypothesised that the slower band represents a post-translationally modified Kv4.3 211 

species that favours palmitoylation, focusing first on phosphorylation as the ‘priming’ 212 

modification. Nanobodies are single chain antibodies in which the VHH protein binding 213 

domain can fold in the mammalian cytoplasm to form a small stable domain that binds 214 

its target with high affinity. Many PTMs rely on protein-protein interactions: enzyme-215 

nanobody chimeras are validated tools to deliver enzymes mediating PTMs to a 216 

nanobody’s target protein. 54 We therefore fused the GFP/YFP/CFP binding nanobody 217 

LaG-16 to the protein phosphatase PP2A and co-expressed this reagent with YFP-Kv4.3. 218 

Control experiments utilised a nanobody in which one amino acid in each of the three 219 

complementarity-determining regions was mutated to tryptophan. This ‘3W’ form 220 

displays a reduced ability to bind and deliver enzymes to YFP (Figure 2E). LaG-16-PP2A 221 

co-immunoprecipitated with YFP-Kv4.3S but not with untagged Kv4.3 (Figure 2F). In cells 222 

expressing LaG-16-PP2A, YFP-Kv4.3S collapsed from a doublet to a single, faster band 223 

and showed reduced palmitoylation relative to controls (Figure 2G). Untagged Kv4.3S 224 

palmitoylation decreased modestly with PP2A-3W (but not with LaG-16-PP2A), possibly 225 

indicating some off-target activity. These findings support the concept that 226 

phosphorylation generates the slower migrating species and promotes palmitoylation. 227

We set out to directly visualise Kv4.3 phosphorylation using Phos-tag electrophoresis. 228 

The Phos-tag ligand retards the progress of phosphorylated proteins on SDS PAGE, 229 

allowing them to be visualised by a mobility shift. Phos-tag SDS-PAGE resolved three 230 

Kv4.3 mobility states, consistent with multiple phosphorylation states. LaG-16-PP2A 231 

shifted YFP-Kv4.3S toward the unphosphorylated state relative to the two controls 232 

(Figure 2H). TMD-del truncations of both splice variants were more phosphorylated in the 233 

presence of KChIP2.1 (Figure 2I), and Acyl-RAC-enriched Kv4.3 fractions were selectively 234 

phosphorylated (Figure 2J). Taken together, these results suggest that the TMD del 235 

mutant becomes phosphorylated in the presence of KChIP2.1 and that the palmitoylated 236 

form of Kv4.3 is considerably more phosphorylated. The data are therefore consistent 237 

with the concept that phosphorylation is required for Kv4.3 palmitoylation. 238 

239 

Phosphorylation site mapping in Kv4.3 240 

We set out to identify the phosphorylation site in Kv4.3 that promotes its palmitoylation 241 

by further truncating the TMD-del mutant (Figure 3A). All mutants were co-expressed with 242 

KChIP2.1. Their interaction with KChIP2.1 was assessed by co-immunoprecipitation, 243 

and their phosphorylation by the presence of a slower migrating species on SDS PAGE. 244 

The phosphorylation-induced change in Kv4.3 mobility was lost when 130 amino acids 245 

were deleted but present when 100 amino acids were deleted from the Kv4.3 C tail, 246 

indicating the phosphorylation site(s) are within this region (Figure 3B). FLAG co-247 

immunoprecipitation confirmed that both the Δ100 and Δ130 deletion mutants still bind 248 

KChIP2.1 (Figure 3C). Further deletions (Figure 3D) and mutagenesis of candidate serine 249 

/ threonine residues in Kv4.3 to alanine (Figure 3E) identified S537 and S538 as the key 250 

residues responsible for altered Kv4.3 mobility on SDS PAGE. Alanine mutagenesis of 251 

only S538 was sufficient to substantially reduce Kv4.3 palmitoylation (Figure 3F), 252 

implying phosphorylation here is required for Kv4.3 palmitoylation. We therefore 253 

generated pseudophosphorylated (S to D) mutations of both S537 and S538 in TMD-Del 254 

Kv4.3 and evaluated whether the dependence of TMD-Del palmitoylation on co-255 

expressing KChIP2.1 was abrogated by positioning a negative charge in position 537 or 256 

538 (Figure 3G). Palmitoylation of TMD-Del remained KChIP2.1-dependent in these 257 

experiments and was impaired by mutation S538D. S to D mutagenesis of S537 but not 258 

S538 in full length Kv4.3 slightly enhanced its palmitoylation (Figure 3H), suggesting 259 

aspartate mutagenesis of S538 cannot fully substitute for phosphorylation in promoting 260 

Kv4.3 palmitoylation. Taken together, we conclude that phosphorylation of Kv4.3 S538 is 261 

required for palmitoylation of Kv4.3 and is promoted by the presence of KChIP2.1, but we 262 

do not rule out that phosphorylation at other sites may also enhance Kv4.3 263 

palmitoylation. 264

were labelled by briefly treating cells with biotin before lysis, and biotinylated proteins 270 

purified and identified using mass spectrometry. Among 691 proteins in proximity to 271 

Kv4.3 (Supplementary Table 1) were two zDHHC-PATs: the closely related enzymes 272

zDHHC5 and zDHHC8. We investigated the relationship between zDHHC5 and Kv4.3. 273 

Reciprocal co-immunoprecipitation experiments identified a physical interaction 274 

between zDHHC5 and Kv4.3S (Figure 4A). Re-expression of zDHHC5 in a zDHHC5 275 

knockout cell line increased Kv4.3S palmitoylation, suggesting a functional relationship 276 

between the two proteins (Figure 4B). Mutation of S537 and S538, the key 277 

phosphorylation site(s) required for Kv4.3 palmitoylation, reduced but did not abolish the 278 

physical interaction between Kv4.3S and zDHHC5 (Figure 4C), suggesting that 279 

phosphorylating Kv4.3 S538 promotes its recruitment by zDHHC5, but that other contact 280 

points exist between the two proteins. 281 

S-palmitoylation and phosphorylation are traditionally thought to oppose each other 282 

because they attract and repel proteins from membranes respectively. The AlphaFold 283 

predicted structure of zDHHC5 includes two positively charged pockets on the enzyme 284 

surface in the intracellular loop containing the catalytic site between transmembrane 285 

domains 2 and 3. This topology has recently been confirmed experimentally. 55 We 286 

hypothesised that these regions could conceivably interact with and position a 287 

phosphorylated region of a protein to present it to the zDHHC5 active site. We therefore 288 

used HPEPDOCK to examine whether a phosphorylated Kv4.3 peptide (residues 531-550, 289 

containing the S-palmitoylation and phosphorylation sites) would be appropriately 290 

positioned for palmitoylation if phosphorylation promoted its interaction with zDHHC5. 291 

Models that did not position the palmitoylated cysteines close enough to the active site 292 

for catalysis were rejected. Several modelling scenarios suggest that phosphorylation of 293 

S538 favours Kv4.3 interaction with zDHHC5, and that there is enough flexibility in the 294 

531-550 region of Kv4.3 to allow the S-palmitoylated cysteines C546/547 to be brought 295 

into proximity with the zDHHC5 active site (Figure 4D). 296 

We used serum starvation and kinase inhibitors to identify the kinase family responsible 297 

for phosphorylating Kv4.3 S538. A significant decrease in Kv4.3S S-palmitoylation was 298 

observed following both an overnight and a one-hour serum withdrawal (Figure 4E), 299 

suggesting that a serum-dependent kinase may be responsible for S538 phosphorylation. 300 

A significant decrease in Kv4.3S S-palmitoylation was also observed when cells were 301 

treated with staurosporine overnight (Figure 4F), but treating cells with staurosporine for 302 

1 hour caused no significant change in Kv4.3S S-palmitoylation. We examined the 303 

phosphorylation status of Kv4.3 using Phos-tag SDS-PAGE. This showed a304 

phosphorylated species in vehicle treated cells which was entirely absent from cells 305 

treated with staurosporine for 1 hour, indicating that Kv4.3 is no longer phosphorylated 306 

but remains S-palmitoylated (Figure 4G). We conclude from these experiments that 307 

kinase inhibition for 1 hour quickly prevents Kv4.3 phosphorylation, however Kv4.3S 308 

remains S-palmitoylated until the phosphorylation has been prevented for a longer 309 

period, in this case overnight. 310

Influence of palmitoylation on Kv4.3 function 312 

We tested whether palmitoylation at C546/547 modulates Kv4.3 channel function by 313 

comparing WT Kv4.3S with a non palmitoylatable C546/547AA mutant in HEK293 cell 314 

lines stably expressing Kv4.3S using whole-cell patch clamp. Different protocols were 315 

applied to evaluate the electrophysiological kinetics of Kv4.3 channels. 316

The voltage dependence of activation was assessed using a standard protocol shown in 317 

the inset of Figure 5. Relative to wild type Kv4.3S, C546/547AA produced larger peak 318 

currents which inactivated more rapidly, with a left shifted V 0.5 of activation and an 319 

increased slope factor (Figure 5Ai, 5Aii, 5B, 5C and 5D). The time-course of channel 320 

inactivation was evaluated by bi-exponential fitting of current elicited at +20 mV. The 321 

slow time constant of inactivation (τ s) was significantly smaller for C546/547AA than WT 322 

Kv4.3S, while the fast component of inactivation (τ f) was unaltered (Figure 5E). 323 

An atrial action potential (AP) waveform was applied under voltage clamp (AP clamp) to 324 

examine channel behaviour during a dynamic physiological waveform. With an atrial AP 325 

command, C546/547AA again showed an increased current amplitude (Figure 5F, 5G). 326 

Thus, the gain of function phenotype of the non-palmitoylatable mutant persists with a 327 

physiological waveform, consistent with palmitoylation acting as a negative modulator 328 

of Kv4.3S current magnitude. 329 

The voltage dependence of channel inactivation was studied with a protocol shown in 330

Discussion 394 

We identify a phosphorylation -dependent mechanism that licenses Kv4.3 S-395 

palmitoylation, and provide a molecular explanation for splice variant specific 396 

differences in lipid modification and channel function. Specifically, phosphorylation 397 

centred on S538 promotes formation of a palmitoylation -competent Kv4.3 species that 398 

depends on intact N -terminal/T1 architecture, is promoted by KChIP2.1, enabling S-399 

palmitoylation at C546/547. In parallel, we define a cis -acting inhibitory element within 400 

residues 488-498 of the Kv4.3L splice insert that suppresses palmitoylation and thereby 401 

explains the lower S-palmitoylation state of Kv4.3L relative to Kv4.3S. 402 

403 

Phosphorylation as a priming modification for S -palmitoylation 404 

Our data support a model in which phosphorylation precedes and enables S-405 

palmitoylation of Kv4.3. Multiple independent perturbations, including nanobody -406 

targeted PP2A recruitment, serum withdrawal, and broad kinase inhibition reduced 407 

Kv4.3 S-palmitoylation but in some cases only after a delay relative to loss of 408 

phosphorylation, indicating temporal uncoupling between a rapidly turning -over 409 

phosphorylation event and a slower turning-over lipid modification. This strongly 410 

suggests that phosphorylation acts upstream to generate a substrate competent for S-411 

palmitoylation rather than directly stabilising the S-palmitoylated state. The inability of 412 

S538D to fully substitute for phosphorylation indicates that authentic phosphate 413 

chemistry (rather than simply negative charge) is required for Kv4.3 S-palmitoylation. We 414 

suggest that a phospho-specific protein interaction or local hydrogen-bonding geometry, 415 

rather than purely electrostatic priming, underlies the requirement. 416 

This mechanism aligns with emerging precedents in cardiac signalling. Most notably, 417 

phospholemman phosphorylation at Ser68 enhances its palmitoylation by zDHHC5, 45, 47, 

418 60 demonstrating that phosphorylation can positively regulate S -palmitoylation by 419 

promoting substrate recognition or enzyme engagement. Our findings extend this 420 

principle to a voltage -gated potassium channel and suggest that phosphorylation -421 

dependent recruitment to zDHHC enzymes may represent a broader organising principle 422 

for dynamic regulation of membrane protein S-palmitoylation. 423 

Consistent with this, proximity labelling, co -immunoprecipitation and modelling 424 

collectively support a mechanism in which phosphorylated S538 enhances Kv4.3 425 

interaction with zDHHC5, positioning C546/547 for catalysis. The AlphaFold -guided 426 

docking analysis suggests that electrostatic interactions between phosphorylated Kv4.3 427 

residues and positively charged surfaces within the zDHHC5 catalytic loop could 428 

stabilise a productive orientation of the intrinsically disordered Kv4.3 C -terminal tail. 429 

Importantly, mutation of S538 dramatically reduced Kv4.3 S-palmitoylation, but reduced 430 

(and did not abolish) Kv4.3 interaction with zDHHC5, suggesting that phosphorylation 431 

helps position Kv4.3 for catalysis rather than creating a binary binding determinant. 432 

Notably, detectable Kv4.3 palmitoylation in zDHHC5 -deficient cells indicates that 433 

zDHHC5 is not the sole enzyme acting on Kv4.3, and that phosphorylation likely 434 

represents a shared upstream priming signal utilised by multiple zDHHC -PATs rather 435 

than a strictly enzyme -specific docking event. The larger effect of S538 mutation 436 

compared with zDHHC5 ablation further supports this interpretation. 437

KChIP2.1 promotes formation of a palmitoylation -competent state 439 

KChIP2.1 increased Kv4.3 palmitoylation without abolishing splice -dependent 440 

differences, indicating that it enhances access to the palmitoylation pathway rather than 441 

determining substrate specificity. Since palmitoylation levels were normalised to total 442 

Kv4.3 abundance, this effect cannot be explained simply by increased Kv4.3 expression. 443 

Instead, our data support a model in which KChIP2.1 facilitates formation of a 444 

conformation or subcellular context permissive for phosphorylation and subsequent 445 

palmitoylation. 446 

The influence of KChIP2.1 on Kv4.3 S-palmitoylation (but not KChIP2.2, which is not S-447 

palmitoylated and therefore cannot localise to membranes 61 ) and the dependence on 448 

intact N -terminal/T1 architecture suggest that assembly of the Kv4.3-KChIP complex is 449 

upstream of phosphorylation and palmitoylation competence. Given that KChIP2 is 450 

known to stabilise Kv4 complexes and enhance their surface expression, 61 one simple 451 

interpretation is that complex assembly promotes exposure of the Kv4.3 distal C -tail for 452 

modification by membrane -associated kinases and zDHHC enzymes, although direct 453 

evidence for trafficking -dependent mechanisms will require further study. 454 

455 

Functional consequences of palmitoylation 456 

Functionally, palmitoylation constrains Kv4.3 channel activity. Non -palmitoylatable 457 

Kv4.3S exhibited increased peak current, accelerated inactivation, left -shifted activation, 458 

and faster recovery, indicating that S-palmitoylation modification stabilises channel 459 

states associated with reduced availability and slower gating transitions. 460 

Phosphorylation sites previously identified in Kv4.3 (including PKC -sensitive T504 and 461 

CaMKII -dependent sites) are also located near the Kv4.3 S-palmitoylation sites and are 462 

known to regulate current amplitude and inactivation kinetics. 35, 57, 62-64 Taken together 463 

with our data, these findings support a model in which the Kv4.3 C -terminus acts as a 464 

regulatory hub integrating multiple PTMs, with phosphorylation and palmitoylation acting 465 

cooperatively rather than independently to shape channel behaviour. 466 

Despite these clear effects in heterologous electrophysiology, palmitoylation did not 467 

measurably alter AP duration or contractility parameters in hiPSC -derived 468 

cardiomyocytes under the conditions tested. Two factors likely contribute. First, 469 

expression of Kv4.3 itself is sufficient to accelerate early repolarisation, potentially 470 

masking more subtle modulatory effects of palmitoylation. Second, the non -471 

palmitoylatable mutant exhibited reduced steady -state expression, which may partially 472 

offset any increase in current magnitude. Together, these data suggest that 473 

palmitoylation fine -tunes Kv4.3 function rather than acting as a primary determinant of 474 

repolarisation in this model, and that its effects may be more evident under conditions 475 

of endogenous expression or pathological remodelling. We do not rule out the possibility 476 

that palmitoylation controls steady state expression of Kv4.3 in cardiac cells, but 477 

observed no impact of palmitoylation on Kv4.3 stability in engineered cell lines. 478

In the failing heart, Kv4.3 expression is reduced and splice variant abundance remodels 481 

such that Kv4.3L increases and Kv4.3S markedly decreases. 36 Given that Kv4.3L is less 482 

palmitoylated, this shift would be predicted to reduce overall Kv4.3 palmitoylation in 483 

heart failure. Since our data indicate that reduced palmitoylation enhances Kv4.3 current 484 

amplitude, such remodelling could partially counterbalance the loss of Kv4.3 expression, 485 

representing a compensatory mechanism to preserve repolarising current. 486 

More broadly, our findings challenge the conventional view that phosphorylation and S -487 

palmitoylation act antagonistically, instead supporting a model in which rapid 488 

phosphorylation events gate access to slower lipid modifications. This hierarchical PTM 489 

coupling may be particularly important for ion channels with intrinsically disordered 490 

regulatory domains, where conformational flexibility enables long -range communication 491 

between modification sites. 492 

493 

Limitations and future directions 494 

Several limitations should be considered. First, most mechanistic experiments were 495 

performed in HEK293 cells, which provide tractability but may not recapitulate 496 

cardiomyocyte -specific signalling environments. Second, the kinase responsible for 497 

S538 phosphorylation remains unidentified. Our data indicate that it is serum -498 

responsive and staurosporine -sensitive, and the positive impact of KChIP 2.1 but not 499 

KChIP2.2 on S-palmitoylation of the TMD-Del construct is consistent with a membrane -500 

associated kinase, but further work is required to define its identity and regulation. Third, 501 

reduced expression of certain mutants, particularly within the splice insert, limits 502 

interpretation and suggests additional roles for this region in protein stability or quality 503 

control. Fourth, although iPSC-derived cardiomyocytes provide a human, genetically 504 

tractable model system, their relative structural and electrophysiological immaturity 505 

may limit direct extrapolation of our findings to adult ventricular myocardium. 506 

507 

Conclusion 508 

We define a phosphorylation -dependent PTM cascade controlling Kv4.3 S-509 

palmitoylation, explain splice -variant differences via a discrete inhibitory sequence 510 

element, and demonstrate that S-palmitoylation modulates Kv4.3 channel gating. This 511 

work establishes Kv4.3 as a model for hierarchical PTM integration, in which 512 

phosphorylation primes S-palmitoylation to tune ion channel behaviour. 513

HiPSC-CMs were incubated in Tyrode’s solution (composition (in mM): 93 NaCl, 20 725

Biosciences) with an inverted Olympus microscope, 40x objective (0.6NA). Recordings 730 

were made at 10 kHz sampling rate for 10 seconds (typically 6-8 APs per recording). Three 731 

sites per well were selected for recording. AP recordings were analysed using CellOPTIQ 732 

software. The fluorescence traces were baseline subtracted, and the traces then filtered 733 

using a 15-pass, 3-point moving average (boxcar) filter. The consecutive APs from a single 734 

recording were ensemble-averaged, after aligning timepoints of the first derivative peaks, 735 

to generate a single AP trace per recording. Functional measurements reported: mean 736 

interval (interval between APs), AP rise time, APD 10 (time interval between 90% rise and 737 

10% decay of the AP), APD 50 (time interval between 50% rise and 50% decay of the AP), 738 

APD 90 (time interval between 10% rise and 90% decay of the AP). 739 

Brightfield video recordings of cell contractions were made using an Inverted Olympus 740 

microscope with a high-speed Hamamatsu camera, 40x objective (0.6NA), with a 741 

recording time of 10 seconds at 100 fps. Cell contractility recordings were analysed using 742 

the open source MUSCLEMOTION (MM) contractility algorithm. 75 This method measures 743 

the change in pixel intensity as movement generating a representative contractility trace 744 

for each recording. Functional measurements reported: Up 90 (time interval between the 745 

10% and 90% upstroke levels of contractility), Dn 90 (time interval between the 90% and 746 

10% levels of the relaxation phase), CD 10 (time interval between 90% rise and 10% decay 747 

of the contraction), CD 50 (time interval between 50% rise and 50% decay of the 748 

contraction), CD 90 (time interval between 10% rise and 90% decay of the contraction). 749
