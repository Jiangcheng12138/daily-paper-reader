Glaucoma is a group of eye diseases that affects 4 million people in the US and is one of 33 

the leading causes of vision loss due to damage to the eye’s optic nerve (ON) which is composed 34 

of axons from retinal ganglion cells (RGCs) that transmit visual information to the brain. Injury 35 

to the ON often triggers RGC death and subsequent loss of visual function. Despite its increasing 36 

prevalence worldwide, effective therapies for glaucoma remain elusive. Notably, the thirteen-37 

lined ground squirrel (TLGS) exhibits intrinsic neuroprotection during hibernation; however, 38 

reproducing this protective state pharmacologically has proven challenging. To elucidate the 39

Introduction: 56 

The precise pathophysiology of glaucoma remains incompletely understood, hampering 57 

the development of effective therapies. Several mechanisms have been proposed, including 58 

mechanical compression injury resulting from elevated intraocular pressure, ischemic damage to 59 

the retinal ganglion cell (RGC) axons, impaired ocular blood flow, genetic susceptibility, 60 

autoimmune-mediated axonal degeneration, and neurodegenerative processes that may originate 61 

in the brain. Together, these factors contribute to both primary and secondary neuronal 62 

degeneration. Given the devastating and irreversible nature of vision loss due to glaucoma, there 63 

is a critical need for innovative strategies to prevent RGC death, preserve visual function, and 64 

promote neural repair and recovery. 65 

One promising line of investigation arises from the study of mammalian hibernators, such 66 

as the thirteen-lined ground squirrel (TLGS), Ictidomys tridecemlineatus , which has evolved 67 

exceptional mechanisms of cellular preservation. During torpor, these animals profoundly 68 

suppress metabolic activity, reduce oxidative stress, and attenuate inflammation [1, 2]. 69 

Remarkably, they endure prolonged periods of hypothermia and ischemia - conditions that mimic 70 

aspects of glaucoma pathogenesis, including hypoxia, energy depletion, and oxidative stress – 71 

without sustaining irreversible neuronal damage. During deep hibernation, core body 72 

temperature (CBT) can fall from 37°C to as low as 4°C, yet they avoid the cellular damage 73 

typically associated with such conditions, making hibernators a powerful model for studying 74 

endogenous neuroprotective mechanisms. 75 

To explore this phenomenon, we employed a controlled ON crush (ONC) model to 76 

simulate axonal damage due to glaucoma [3, 4]. In awake TLGS, this injury induces axonal 77 

degeneration and progressive RGC loss. However, RGCs in hibernating ground squirrels exhibit 78

Comprehensive Metabolomic Profiling Reveals Injury-Associated Metabolic Shifts 132 

Using untargeted metabolomic analysis, we identified 218 metabolites in negative 133 

ionization mode and 230 metabolites in positive ionization mode. Together, these datasets 134 

provide comprehensive coverage of the retinal metabolite profile and reveal dynamic changes 135 

associated with ON injury in the retinas of hibernating and awake animals. 136

Negative Ionization Mode Metabolomics Reveals Neuroprotective Metabolic Adaptations 150 

MS data from the negative ionization mode were first analyzed by grouping the TLGS 151 

retina samples (biological replicates) according to the following experimental conditions: awake 152 

and hibernating. Hierarchal clustering analysis of the retinal metabolites in uninjured and injured 153 

TLGS revealed metabolites with significant changes in abundance (|log(Fold Change)|>1.5), 154 

identifying potential biomarkers of ON degeneration and candidate metabolites for 155 

neuroprotection [15]. 156 

One cluster, denoted as Group A (yellow), exhibited a significant number of altered 157 

amino acids across experimental groups ( Figure 4A ). In Awake squirrels, metabolites in this 158 

cluster began at elevated levels but declined following injury and remained suppressed. In 159 

contrast, the same metabolites in the Hibernating group also started at elevated levels, became 160 

slightly reduced immediately after injury, but returned to elevated levels at 7 days post-injury. 161 

Amino acids levels, in general, are known to fluctuate following ON injury due to disruptions in 162 

neurotransmitter release, impaired neuronal function, and oxidative stress associated with 163 

demands for more energy [16, 17]. The identification of key Krebs cycle intermediates such as 164 

isocitritic acid, citric acid, and malic acid - among significantly changing metabolites in Awake 165 

animals further supports the notion of altered energy production following injury. The increased 166 

utilization of these intermediates likely contributes to increased ATP production to meet the 167 

increased demand in response to injury. The excess ATP released from dying RGCs into the 168

TLGS retina samples from Awake and Hibernating groups. Hierarchical clustering of the 252

One cluster resembled Group A (yellow) from the negative ionization mode, 255

promising candidates for further investigation as protective and potentially therapeutic 274 

metabolites for retinal degenerative diseases, including glaucoma. 275

Pathway Analysis Reveals Hibernation Engages Amino Acid, Lipid, and Purine Pathways to 330 

Enhance Retinal Resilience 331 

Pathway analysis for each subgroup was conducted in the positive and negative 332 

ionization modes using the complete set of significant metabolites in our data against the KEGG 333 

metabolic pathways ( Figure 6 ). This analysis revealed a prominent enrichment in amino acid 334 

metabolism, as well as pathways related to the citric acid cycle, glycerophospholipid 335 

metabolism, and purine metabolism. During hibernation, metabolic activity is significantly 336 

depressed; however, these pathways provide alternative energy sources despite reduced oxygen 337 

and nutrient availability. Under metabolic stress, glycerophospholipids likely help maintain 338 

membrane fluidity and protect the central nervous system. Additionally, purine metabolism— 339 

including purine nucleosides such as adenosine, inosine, and guanosine—has been implicated in 340 

neuroprotection and is likely critical for regulating entry into and exit from torpor. Thus, 341 

hibernation triggers a cascade of adaptive responses that confer cytoprotection, which also serves 342

Our metabolomics analysis revealed metabolites previously associated with optic nerve 412 

crush injury in non-hibernating species. Prior studies in mice have shown that ATP levels 413 

moderately increase within the first 10 hours post-ONC, likely due to enhanced mitochondrial 414 

oxidative phosphorylation [17]. Inhibition of ATP production—by blocking either glycolysis or 415 

oxidative phosphorylation in that model—increased RGC death. Notably however, supplying 416 

exogenous ATP had no effect on RGC survival. Based on these findings, the authors 417 

hypothesized that limiting oxidative phosphorylation could promote RGC survival in the ONC 418 

model. Indeed, treatment with meclizine, a drug that shifts energy metabolism toward glycolysis, 419

Consistent with these findings, our metabolomics data show a reduction in TCA cycle 423

oxidation. Additionally, increased glyceric acid, a product of glycerol oxidation during 430

changes align with previous reports describing adaptive energy strategies in hibernating 432 

species.[57-59] 433 

A related metabolomic study examining lung preservation at 10°C identified loss of 434

membrane lipid content. While ceramide accumulation has been implicated in pathogenic 438 

processes under certain conditions (possibly influenced by whether the cells favor respiration or 439 

lipid metabolism), our data suggest a potentially protective role in the context of hibernation. 440 

This divergence may reflect differences in energy substrate utilization during torpor, where 441 

metabolic adaptations prioritize cellular survival under conditions of prolonged stress. 442

Optic Nerve Crush 985 

Squirrels were anesthetized using isoflurane gas, with induction at 5% and maintenance 986 

at 4% via a low-profile nose cone. Anesthetic gas was scavenged using an attached activated 987 

charcoal filter. Sterile surgical instruments were autoclaved prior to use and subsequently 988 

disinfected between animals by immersion in 70% ethanol followed by bead sterilization at 989 

approximately 270°C for 20 seconds. To prevent infection, topical antibiotic (polymyxin B-990 

neomycin-bacitracin) ophthalmic ointment was applied to the surgical eye. To prevent corneal 991 

dehydration during anesthesia, Systane® lubricating gel was applied to both eyes. Ketoprofen (5 992 

mg/kg, subcutaneous) was administered prior to recovery to provide preemptive analgesia, which 993 

helps reduce the severity and duration of postoperative pain. 994 

For hibernating animals, body temperature was maintained at approximately 4°C using 995 

an ice pack placed beneath the animal during the procedure. Animals were anesthetized with 996 

isoflurane (induction at 3%, and maintenance at 3%) to sustain the hibernation state and prevent 997 

arousal. Animals were kept under isoflurane anesthesia to maintain the hibernation state and 998 

prevent arousal. To minimize sensory stimulation, ambient lights were turned off, the room was 999 

isolated from external noise, and exposure to illumination from the surgical microscope was kept 1000 

to a minimum. To maintain body temperature during transfer and to minimize light, animals were 1001 

transferred to and from the hibernaculum in a foam shipping container equipped with ice packs. 1002 

In the fall preceding the hibernation season, animals were implanted subcutaneously with 1003 

temperature transmitters (TA-F40, Data Sciences International) or iButtons (AlphaMach), 1004 

enabling wireless body temperature monitoring to confirm that animals remained in torpor 1005 

following the surgical procedures [73]. 1006
