Abstract 1

Adaptations in animals occupying environments of extreme cold or heat offer unique 2

insights into thermoregulation. The antelope ground squirrel, a desert-dwelling rodent 3

situated within a clade of hibernators, provides a notable example of these 4

thermoregulatory extremes. As a non-hibernator closely related to hibernators, antelope 5

ground squirrels may represent a rare case of trait reversal, with a striking ability to 6

maintain core body temperatures exceeding 43°C while displaying poor tolerance to 7

prolonged cold. Here, we explore the genetic and phenotypic basis of these unique traits 8

by generating the first genome assembly for this species, which we use to conduct 9

comparative genomics across the squirrel family. We complement this with acute and 10 

chronic cold-exposure experiments coupled with transcriptomic profiling of thermogenic 11 

organs: brown adipose tissue, white adipose tissue, and skeletal muscle. Together, 12 

these findings reveal a shift away from non-shivering thermogenesis toward 13 

metabolically demanding shivering thermogenesis, a perilous strategy for sustained heat 14 

generation. 15 

> 16

Introduction 17 

The dynamic control of body temperature is a critical adaptation that enables mammals 18 

to survive across the full range of earth’s climates. Small-bodied hibernators exemplify 19 

thermoregulatory extremes by adjusting their body temperature from below zero (-2.9°C) 20 

to euthermia (37°C) within hours 1 . Within the squirrel family (Sciuridae), the tribe 21 

Marmotini contains 13 genera, most of which can dynamically regulate body temperature 22 

through hibernation or estivation 2 . A notable exception is the desert-dwelling genus 23 

Ammospermophilus (antelope ground squirrels), which remain active year-round and do 24 

not enter these hypometabolic states 3 . Instead, antelope ground squirrels demonstrate 25 

impressive heat tolerance, with an ability to sustain core body temperatures near 43°C 4 .26 

Intriguingly, these thermoregulatory patterns appear to have come at a cost: chronic cold 27 

exposure can be lethal, reflecting cold intolerance 5 . To date, the genomic and 28 

phenotypic signatures underpinning this unique thermal physiology remain unknown. 29

Results and Discussion 31 

To unravel the genetic determinants of thermal physiology in antelope ground squirrels, 32 

we generated the first genome for the genus Ammospermophilus (A. leucurus ; Figure 33 

1A) and the only genome for a non-hibernating member of the tribe Marmotini using 34 

long-read PacBio HiFi sequencing. The genome assembly exhibited high completeness 35 

(BUSCO Eukaryota: 94.1% complete) and strong contiguity (contig N50 = 31.58 Mb), 36 

placing it among the most contiguous marmotine genomes to date (Figure 1A and Data 37 

S1). To assess gene completeness more thoroughly, we used TOGA2, a tool that 38 

generates whole-genome alignments to a high-quality reference genome (human: hg38) 39 

to identify conserved one-to-one orthologs and assess gene intactness, absence, or 40 

disruption 6 . A. leucurus displayed the greatest gene intactness, containing fewer missing 41 

or disrupted genes than any other squirrel species (Figure 1C and Data S2). Leveraging 42 

sequence alignments of conserved Sciuridae orthologs, we conducted a phylogenomic 43 

analysis that recapitulated established non-genomic phylogenies, recovering A. leucurus 44 

as the sister group to all other hibernators (Figure 1B). 45 

> 46

physiology, we scanned all orthologous genes across Sciuridae to identify predicted 48 

gene-inactivating mutations specific to A. leucurus . This comparative approach revealed 49

UROC1 loss in this desert-dwelling species may reflect selection to increase or maintain 62 

urocanic acid levels. 63 

> 64

Despite the identification of predicted gene losses with potential physiological relevance 65 

in A. leucurus , we did not find a specific loss that directly explains its altered 66 

thermogenic phenotype. This suggests that subtle regulatory changes, such as altered 67 

gene expression programs in classical thermogenic tissues, may more directly shape A. 68 

leucurus thermal physiology. To test this, we exposed laboratory-reared A. leucurus to 69 

either acute (24h) or chronic (10 day) cold (6°C) followed by bulk RNA sequencing of 70 

tissues that regulate shivering (skeletal muscle) and non-shivering thermogenesis 71 

(brown and white adipose tissue) (Figure 1D). Importantly, during chronic cold exposure, 72 

we gradually decreased ambient temperature to allow acclimation and minimize 73 

hypothermia risk (see Supplemental Material). 74 

> 75

Brown adipose tissue (BAT), the main contributor to non-shivering thermogenesis 9 ,76 

demonstrated a rapid response following 24h cold, with 421 and 386 genes up- and 77 

down-regulated, respectively (Figure 1E and Data S4). While we observed increased 78 

gene expression of non-canonical thermogenic markers (futile creatine cycling: Ckb ;79 

futile lipid cycling: Gk ) and mitochondrial regulators ( Pgc1a , Nr4a2 , Slc22a5 ), most 80 

canonical thermogenic markers showed no change ( Ucp1 and Cidea) or even decreased 81 

expression ( Cox8b) (Figure 1H). This blunted thermogenic response persisted following 82 

10 days of cold exposure, with thermogenic markers Ucp1 , Prdm16 , Pgc1a , and Ckb 83 

either remaining unchanged, returning to baseline, or decreasing below baseline levels 84 

(Figure 1H). 85 

> 86

In marked contrast, subcutaneous white adipose tissue showed the strongest acute cold 87 

response, with 1,937 differentially expressed genes (DEGs) (Figure 1F and Data S4). 88 

These included increased expression of lipolytic genes Ces1 , Atgl , Lxra, Abhd5, and 89 

Mlycd (Figure 1I). Although the number of DEGs dropped by nearly half after 10 days of 90 

cold exposure, white adipose tissue maintained robust transcriptomic remodeling, 91 

shifting toward a lipogenic profile (e.g., increased Dgat1 and Fasn ) (Figure 1I). Notably, 92 

despite increased expression of some thermogenic markers (e.g., Cidea ), we did not 93 

detect Ucp1 expression in this depot (TPMs <1). Moreover, we observed no induction of 94 

genes previously linked to Ucp1 -independent thermogenesis, including creatine cycling 95 

(Ckb /Alpl ), calcium cycling ( Atp2a2 ), or lipid cycling ( Gk ). These data suggest that the 96 

pronounced cold-induced remodeling of white adipose tissue does not contribute to 97 

direct heat production. Thus, the two adipose depots considered to produce heat via 98

These data suggest shivering (muscular) thermogenesis may be the primary heat source 102 

in A. leucurus. Consistent with this, acute cold exposure increased expression of skeletal 103 

muscle genes involved in fatty-acid uptake and transport ( Cd36 , Fabp3 , Plin5 , Cpt2 ,104 

Ucp2 , and Mlycd ), supporting fatty-acid-fueled shivering (Figure 1G and 1J). Moreover, 105 

the most up-regulated gene was Hmox1 , a key sensor and regulator of cellular oxidative 106 

stress 10 . Notably, in contrast to brown and white adipose tissue, skeletal muscle 107 

transcriptional responses continued to increase after 10 days of cold. Despite this 108 

sustained response, gene set enrichment analysis (GSEA) of DEGs at 10 days showed 109 

strong negative enrichment of programs linked to extracellular matrix organization, 110 

blood-vessel morphogenesis, and locomotion, suggesting a limited capacity for the 111 

remodeling required to sustain prolonged shivering thermogenesis (Data S5). Taken 112 

together, these data indicate that A. leucurus compensates for blunted adipose 113 

thermogenesis by relying primarily on muscular shivering. However, skeletal muscle 114 

shows a limited capacity for the remodeling needed to sustain prolonged shivering 115 

thermogenesis, such as enhanced vascularization and extracellular matrix remodeling. 116 

This constrained adaptive response may explain impaired cold tolerance of A. leucurus 117 

compared to its hibernating marmotine relatives. More broadly, our results highlight how 118 

evolutionary shifts in thermogenic programs may explain how closely related mammals 119 

thrive or fail under distinct thermal demands. 120
