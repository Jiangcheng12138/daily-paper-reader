Winter survival in dormant animals depends on conserving finite energy reserves, yet winter 22 

temperatures fluctuate around shifting means. In ectotherms, metabolic rate increases 23 

exponentially with temperature, so thermal variability is expected to accelerate energy loss, 24 

with important consequences for overwinter survival and population persistence under 25 

climate change. However, it remains unclear whether dormant ectotherms can compensate 26 

physiologically for thermal variability. We overwintered Bombus impatiens queens under 27 

constant (2, 3, 4°C) or variable (2 ± 6°C or 4 ± 6°C) regimes for six weeks, then measured 28 

metabolic rates across a range of temperatures. The temperature dependence of metabolic rate 29 

shifted in response to thermal experience, but the direction of compensation depended on 30 

mean temperature: variability centered on 2°C elevated metabolic rate and increased thermal 31 

sensitivity relative to all other conditions, whereas variability centered on 4°C reduced 32 

metabolic rate and dampened thermal sensitivity relative to constant 4°C. We used these 33 

metabolic responses to simulate rates of lipid depletion and found that survival trajectories 34 

echoed physiological shifts: experiencing variability around 2°C would reduce subsequent 35 

survival time, whereas experiencing variability around 4°C would preserve subsequent 36 

survival even under variable future conditions. Thus, identical thermal variance produced 37 

opposite energetic outcomes depending on the mean temperature around which fluctuations 38 

occurred. Integrating both temperature means and variability is, therefore, essential for 39 

predicting overwintering survival in a changing world. 40

Introduction 45 

Temperature variability is a defining feature of natural environments. Daily highs and lows, 46 

seasonal cycles, and abrupt cold snaps or warm spells create dynamic thermal landscapes that 47 

strongly influence organismal physiology (1–7). For ectotherms, these fluctuations can be 48 

especially consequential because metabolic rate rises exponentially with temperature, such 49 

that repeated exposure to warmer temperatures disproportionately raises total energy 50 

expenditure (i.e., Jensen’s inequality or non-linear averaging; Denny, 2017; Jensen, 1906; 51 

Martin & Huey, 2008; Ruel & Ayres, 1999). As a result, fluctuating thermal regimes can 52 

increase mean metabolic rate relative to a constant regime at the same mean temperature (Fig. 53 

1A), accelerating depletion of energy stores available for growth, reproduction, and survival. 54 

However, some organisms can adjust their physiology in response to thermal variation 55 

through metabolic compensation, altering baseline metabolic demand and/or thermal 56 

sensitivity of metabolism in response to their thermal environment. Importantly, 57 

compensation is not unidirectional. In insects that remain active in cold environments, 58 

metabolic compensation may elevate metabolic capacity to sustain performance at low 59 

temperatures (12). During energetically constrained life stages, such as dormancy, 60 

compensation would be expected to reduce baseline metabolic demand or thermal sensitivity 61 

to conserve limited energy reserves (13). Although the energetic consequences of thermal 62 

variability are well documented in active insects – where fluctuations can reduce reproductive 63 

output (14, 15) – we know far less about whether dormant ectotherms can adjust their 64 

metabolism to buffer these costs, even though such responses could strongly influence 65 

overwinter survival and population persistence. 66 

Dormant ectotherms must survive winter without feeding and, therefore, rely on finite 67 

energy reserves (16). These energetic constraints are further complicated by the fact that 68 

winter temperatures are rarely constant. Even within thermally buffered refuges such as soil 69

Conversely, if dormant individuals retain the capacity for compensation, they may shift 74 

metabolic trajectories toward lower baseline demand or reduced thermal sensitivity, 75 

minimizing the energetic consequences of winter variability (19). 76 

Crucially, the energetic consequences of variability likely depend not only on the 77 

magnitude of temperature fluctuations but also on the mean temperature around which 78 

variability occurs and how frequently temperatures cross physiologically important 79 

thresholds (22). Variability centered on relatively warm temperatures will increase 80 

cumulative energetic demand but may also provide sufficient time at permissive temperatures 81 

to enable compensatory remodeling or recovery (23, 24). In contrast, if variability is centered 82 

on colder temperatures, even brief warming events may repeatedly trigger metabolically 83 

costly physiological transitions without allowing enough time for compensation, generating 84 

cumulative costs that are not reflected in mean temperature alone (21, 25). Thus, thermal 85 

regimes with identical degrees of variability but different mean temperatures can yield 86 

strikingly different energetic outcomes, with consequences for overwinter survival and 87 

subsequent seasonal success. 88

thermal variability shapes metabolic regulation during winter dormancy. As solitary 90 

overwintering individuals, queens must conserve finite lipid reserves while avoiding freezing 91 

injury to survive winter, and overwinter survival directly determines spring colony 92 

establishment and population persistence (20). Understanding how mean temperature and 93

We predicted that compensation would be strongest at higher mean temperatures, 108

stronger metabolic suppression than variability centered on 2°C (Fig. 1). If queens 113 

compensate physiologically for thermal variability, these metabolic shifts should influence 114 

energetic trajectories during dormancy. We therefore expected individuals that reduce 115 

metabolic rate or thermal sensitivity to retain energy reserves longer and show slower lipid 116

Minor variation in assay temperatures does not explain metabolic rate differences 177 

Because acclimation and soak temperatures during respirometry deviated slightly from the 178 

nominal assay temperatures, we tested whether these deviations explained variation in 179 

metabolic rate (Supplemental Fig. 5). Models including acclimation temperature showed 180 

weak effects for CO 2 production ( P = 0.092) and a significant interaction between 181 

acclimation temperature and assay temperature for O 2 consumption ( P = 0.001). However, 182 

acclimation temperature alone was not significant for O 2 consumption ( P = 0.46). Models 183 

incorporating soak temperature yielded similarly weak results. Although soak temperature 184 

was marginally associated with CO 2 production ( P = 0.049), the interaction with assay 185 

temperature was not significant ( P = 0.13), and no effects were detected for O 2 consumption 186 

(all P > 0.09). Inspection of batch-specific relationships revealed no consistent directional 187 

effect (Supplemental Fig. 6), suggesting that minor variation in acclimation and soak 188 

temperatures did not systematically influence metabolic rate. 189

Discussion 245 

Winter temperature variability is a defining feature of natural environments, yet the energetic 246 

consequences during dormancy of this variability remain unclear. Because metabolic rate 247 

increases exponentially with temperature, variability in temperature will elevate cumulative 248 

energy expenditure (8–11, 21). But animals may compensate metabolically. Our results show 249 

that due to differential metabolic compensation, the energetic consequences of winter 250 

temperature variability depend on the mean temperature around which fluctuations occur. 251 

Overwintering queens adjusted their metabolism in response to thermal experience, 252 

but the direction of this compensation depended on the mean temperature around which 253 

variability occurred: the same magnitude of fluctuation (± 6°C) produced contrasting 254 

energetic outcomes when centered on 2°C versus 4°C. Queens exposed to variability around 255 

2°C exhibited elevated baseline metabolic rates and greater temperature sensitivity of 256 

metabolism when metabolic rate was measured across multiple assay temperatures (Figs. 2-3; 257 

Table 1). Under these conditions, variability increased metabolic rates during warming and 258 

accelerated predicted lipid depletion, with predicted downstream effects on overwinter 259 

survival (Fig. 5A). In contrast, queens exposed to variability centered on 4°C showed reduced 260 

baseline metabolism and lower thermal sensitivity relative to constant 4°C treatments (Fig. 261 

3). This suppression corresponded to predicted survival trajectories that were similar to those 262 

of queens overwintered under constant 4°C conditions (Fig. 5B). Together, these results 263 

indicate that thermal variability can either amplify or reduce energetic demand depending on 264 

the mean temperature around which fluctuations occur. 265 

During dormancy, the primary physiological challenge is conserving finite energy 266 

reserves that must sustain individuals through prolonged periods without feeding. Diapausing 267 

insects, therefore, typically suppress metabolism to minimize energetic expenditure and 268

such suppression occurred under variability centered on 4°C, whereas variability centered on 272 

2°C was associated with elevated metabolic demand. 273 

Several physiological processes could contribute to the elevated metabolic demand 274 

observed under colder-centered variability. Repeated exposure to extreme low temperatures 275 

may impose physiological maintenance costs – such as maintaining ion homeostasis or 276 

repairing chill injury (25) – that could restrict metabolic suppression. Alternatively, repeated 277 

transitions across physiological thresholds may maintain metabolic flexibility in a manner 278 

similar to cold compensation observed in active ectotherms. Such responses are plausible 279 

because dormancy is not physiologically static. Insects can remodel tissues and reorganize 280 

metabolic pathways during diapause; for example, Colorado potato beetles reduce 281 

mitochondrial density in flight muscle during diapause to lower energetic demand (29). 282 

Endocrine pathways – including insulin signaling, adipokinetic hormone, and neuropeptide F 283 

– also regulate lipid mobilization and metabolic suppression during diapause (30–34). 284 

Although we did not examine these mechanisms directly, such processes provide plausible 285 

pathways through which thermal history could influence metabolic phenotype during 286 

dormancy. 287 

Differences in metabolic parameters among overwintering treatments remained 288 

detectable when queens were later assayed across the range of test temperatures, suggesting 289 

that early thermal exposure may influence subsequent metabolic responses. Autumn 290 

temperatures are often more variable than those of mid-winter (20, 35), raising the possibility 291 

that conditions early in diapause could influence energetic trajectories later in the 292 

overwintering period. In our lipid depletion simulations, queens exposed to variability 293

summer heat stress (36, 37), yet winter temperature variability may represent an additional 301 

source of energetic constraint. If early overwinter conditions increase baseline metabolism or 302 

amplify metabolic responses to subsequent warming events, queens may enter late winter or 303 

early spring with reduced energetic reserves. As climate change alters both seasonal means 304 

and patterns of temperature variability, understanding how variability interacts with 305 

physiological processes may improve predictions of overwinter survival. 306

Conclusion 308 

The energetic consequences of winter temperature variability depend on the mean 309 

temperature around which fluctuations occur. In our study, variability centered on colder 310 

mean temperatures was associated with increased metabolic demand and shorter predicted 311 

survival, whereas variability centered on warmer mean temperatures corresponded with 312 

greater metabolic suppression and more stable survival trajectories. These findings suggest 313 

that evaluating the effects of climate variability on overwintering ectotherms requires 314 

considering not only the magnitude of temperature fluctuations but also the mean thermal 315 

context in which variability occurs. 316

light-dark cycle, 63% RH; I36VL, PERCIVAL Scientific, Inc., Perry, IA, USA) and supplied 323

Experimental Setup 327 

We marked callow bumble bee queens (easily identified by their silvery pile and curled 328 

wings) with color-numeric tags within two days of eclosion to track age and returned them to 329 

their natal colonies. After marking a sufficient number of queens ( n = 120), we housed them 330 

in colony-specific boxes supplied with ad libitum nectar and returned them to incubators. We 331 

induced dormancy by gradually cooling incubators from 28°C to 4°C and under an 8:16 L:D 332 

cycle over seven days, followed by holding at 4°C in the dark for four more days. We then 333 

placed queens individually in 5-dram plastic vials (Thornton Plastics, Salt Lake City, UT, 334 

USA); each vial was connected via holes in the lid to a second 5-dram vial filled with 335 

dampened sphagnum moss to maintain humidity. Queens were haphazardly assigned to one 336 

of five temperature treatments, maintained in incubators: constant 2°C (2.01 ± 0.47°C), 337 

constant 3°C (3.0 ± 0.27°C), constant 4°C (4.02 ± 0.54°C), or two oscillating treatments. The 338 

first oscillating treatment ranged from -1 to 11°C (“4 ± 6°C”; 4.14 ± 5.74°C), while the 339 

second ranged from -5 to 7°C (“2 ± 6°C”; 1.77 ± 6.51°C; see Fig. 1C, Supplemental Figure 340 

3). 341

below 1600 ppm. At 11°C, we limited sealing time to 60 minutes to avoid exceeding these 372 

thresholds. Based on these trials, we sealed syringes for 90 minutes at -5, 2, and 4°C, and for 373 

60 minutes at 11°C. 374 

We measured CO 2 production and O 2 consumption using stop-flow respirometry with 375 

an integrated respirometry system (FMS-3; Sable Systems International Inc., Las Vegas, 376 

Nevada, USA). We zeroed (using pure N 2 gas) and spanned the CO 2 and O 2 sensors daily 377 

with a primary standard (1011.11 ppm) and outside air (assumed value: 20.95%), 378 

respectively. For measurements of syringe gases, we flowed nitrogen (N 2 ) from a gas 379 

cylinder through a column of DRIERITE–Ascarite II–DRIERITE to scrub residual water 380 

vapor and CO 2. Flow rate was regulated at 150 mL min -1 by the FMS-3’s built-in mass-flow 381 

controller before the dry, CO 2 -free air passed sequentially through the flow meter, CO 2382 

sensor (fractional concentration), and O 2 sensor (%). Air samples were injected into a three-383 

way valve located between the flow meter and the WVP sensor. To prevent dilution effects 384 

from residual moisture or CO 2 from each syringe injection (Lighton, 2018), we plumbed a 385 

30-mL column of magnesium perchlorate (Mg(ClO 4 ) 2 ) before the CO 2 sensors to remove 386 

water vapor, followed by a 30-mL column of Ascarite II–Mg(ClO 4 ) 2 between the CO 2 and O 2387 

sensors to scrub CO 2 .388 

We first injected an empty chamber sealed just prior to the first chamber holding an 389 

animal, followed by sequentially injecting all 10 chambers containing animals, followed by 390 

an empty chamber sealed immediately after the last animal chamber. For each injection, we 391 

slowly injected 10 mL of syringe air into the N 2 stream via the three-way valve. 392

Respirometry analyses 394 

We determined the syringe seal time as the fractional hours from sealing to injection (± 2 395 

seconds). We adjusted the initial and final volumes to account for the body volume of each 396 

queen (estimated from bee mass measured immediately before respirometry and an assumed 397 

tissue density of 1 g cm -3 ) and defined injection periods as the time from the start of the 398 

injection to the opening of the three-way valve for the next injection, with the final peak 399 

ending 2.5 minutes after injection (the time-constant for washout given the volume of the 400 

respirometry setup and flow rate was approximately 2 minutes). We corrected the baseline 401 

drift of both O 2 and CO 2 sensors by adjusting the trace based on the slope between the gas 402 

concentrations before and after each peak. We converted the drift-corrected values to 403 

fractional proportions, multiplied by flow rates (mL min -1 ), divided by 60 to convert to mL 404 

sec -1 for second-by-second readings, and summed to estimate total gas volumes in the 405 

injected sample. We calculated the Respiratory Quotient (RQ) as the ratio of mass-specific 406 

CO 2 production to O 2 consumption (spV CO 2 and spV O2, respectively). 407 

Using each queen’s thermal response curves for mass-specific gas exchange, we 408 

calculated activation energy ( Ea), which describes how sensitive metabolic rate is to 409 

temperature changes, and the intercept (log b0 ), which represents the baseline metabolic rate 410 

at a standard reference temperature, to compare across overwintering thermal regimes, and 411 

across taxa (38, 39). 412

simulated lipid depletion in bumble bee queens using empirically derived metabolic 416 

parameters under alternative overwintering scenarios. After six weeks of exposure to the 417

We modeled temperature-dependent CO 2 production for each queen using Arrhenius 420

of body mass, a threshold associated with diapause termination and forced emergence (20, 433

Body mass and proportional mass loss 447 

To quantify changes in body mass during the six-week overwintering exposure, we 448 

summarized each queen’s mass as: (1) pre-diapause mass, and (2) post-treatment mass, 449 

defined as the mass measured immediately before the first respirometry trial. For each queen, 450 

we calculated absolute mass loss as the difference between pre-diapause mass and post-451 

treatment mass ( Δmass = pre − post), and proportional mass loss as absolute mass loss 452 

standardized by pre-diapause mass ( Δmass / pre). 453 

We tested whether pre-diapause mass (recorded before dormancy induction) and post-454 

diapause (measured after 6 weeks of dormancy, immediately before the first respirometry 455 

trial) differed among overwintering treatments using linear models, followed by Tukey-456 

adjusted pairwise comparisons using estimated marginal means, where appropriate. We tested 457 

whether pre-diapause mass predicted proportional mass loss (pre - post / pre) and whether 458 

this relationship differed among treatments using a generalized linear model (GLM) with a 459 

quasi-binomial error distribution, and compared slopes among treatments using emtrends 460 

(emmeans) on the fitted interaction model. For post-hoc contrasts of quasi-likelihood models, 461 

we report unadjusted pairwise comparisons because quasi families lack a true likelihood and 462 

Tukey-style multiplicity adjustments are derived under full likelihood assumptions (47, 48). 463
