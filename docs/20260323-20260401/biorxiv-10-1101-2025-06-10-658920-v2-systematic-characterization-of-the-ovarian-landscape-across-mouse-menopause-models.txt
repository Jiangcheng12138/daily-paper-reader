Abstract 17 

Menopause affects not only fertility but also systemic health. Yet mechanisms underlying this 18 

coupling are still poorly understood, partly due to the absence of robust, age-relevant preclinical models 19 

with comprehensive molecular and phenotypic characterization. To address this, we systematically 20 

compared three candidate mouse models of menopause: (1) intact aging, (2) chemical ovarian follicle 21 

depletion using 4-vinylcyclohexene diepoxide (VCD) administered at multiple ages, and (3) Foxl2 22 

haploinsufficiency, a genetic model based on a gene linked to human premature ovarian failure. Through 23 

histology, serum hormone profiling, single-cell transcriptomics and machine-learning approaches, we 24 

identify both shared and model-specific features of follicle loss, endocrine disruption, and transcriptional 25 

remodeling. Our comparative framework enables informed selection of context-appropriate preclinical 26 

rodent models to study menopause and the broader physiological consequences of ovarian aging. 27

Introduction 33 

Women are born with a finite oocyte reserve, and its progressive depletion with age leads to the 34 

irreversible loss of ovarian function, known as menopause ( 1-3 ). Epidemiological studies link later age-at-35 

menopause with increased lifespan and reduced risk of age-related diseases ( e.g . cardiovascular disease, 36 

osteoporosis, and neurodegeneration) ( 4-6 ). Conversely, menopause is accompanied by a sharp rise in 37 

age-associated pathologies, underscoring widespread consequences of ovarian aging ( 7). Despite its 38 

public health relevance, how ovarian decline contributes to systemic aging is still poorly understood, in 39 

part due to the dearth of well-characterized, aging-relevant preclinical models. 40 

Menopause is characterized by endocrine reprogramming that extends beyond the reproductive 41 

system ( 8). As follicular reserves decline, reduced steroidogenic activity disrupts feedback regulation of 42 

the hypothalamic-pituitary-gonadal (HPG) axis, leading to sustained hormonal shifts ( 8). Circulating 43 

estrogen and anti-Müllerian hormone (AMH) fall to near-undetectable levels, while follicle-stimulating 44 

hormone (FSH) and inhibin A (INHBA) rise ( 9-11 ). These changes influence multiple somatic systems, 45 

including metabolic, skeletal, and central nervous systems ( 12, 13 ). However, dissecting the mechanisms 46 

linking ovarian dysfunction to systemic physiology requires robust preclinical models recapitulating key 47 

biological features of menopause. 48 

The most frequently used rodent models capture menopausal physiology only imperfectly ( 14 ). 49 

Although intact aging mice are widely used, they do not undergo true menopause; instead, they enter a 50 

state of “estropause,” retaining low but detectable estrogen levels and lacking the postmenopausal 51 

endocrine milieu observed in postmenopausal women ( 15, 16 ). Although ovariectomy (OVX) achieves 52 

rapid estrogen depletion, it lack a gradual endocrine transition, and it depletes residual post-reproductive 53 

ovarian tissue, which is known to exert androgenic and paracrine effects ( 17 ). Thus, while OVX models 54 

acute hormone loss, it incompletely models the progressive ovarian decline characteristic of menopause. 55 

Repeated injections with 4-vinylcyclohexene diepoxide (VCD) leads to dramatic follicle depletion through 56 

selective targeting of small, pre-antral follicles, inducing progressive ovarian failure while preserving a 57 

post-reproductive ovarian tissue ( 15 ). This approach produces estrous acyclicity, estrogen deficiency, and 58 

compensatory FSH elevation, resembling several endocrine features of human menopause ( 18, 19 ). 59

However, prior studies have primarily applied VCD to young adult mice (~2-3 months), limiting insight into 60 

how the timing of ovarian failure intersects with organismal aging ( 20, 21 ). Because aging alters 61 

metabolic, immune, and endocrine states in profound ways ( 22-25 ), understanding menopausal biology 62 

requires evaluating ovarian decline in the context of middle-aged biology. 63 

Genetic models informed by human physiology can also provide useful tools to understand 64 

ovarian decline and its systemic impacts. Haploinsufficiency of Foxl2 , a gene encoding a transcription 65 

factor essential for granulosa cell identity and ovarian maintenance (26-29 ), is causally linked to 66 

premature ovarian insufficiency in humans ( 30-32 ). In adult mice, Foxl2 expression is primarily restricted 67 

to ovarian granulosa cells and pituitary gonadotroph-lineage cells ( 33 ). While complete Foxl2 knockout 68 

leads to severe developmental defects and perinatal lethality, heterozygous carriers have been 69 

incidentally reported to be subfertile, suggestive of milder ovarian dysfunction that may better recapitulate 70 

menopausal transition ( 28, 29 ). Unlike surgical or chemical models, Foxl2 haploinsufficiency represents a 71 

physiologically relevant genetic perturbation that enables investigation of early and potentially subclinical 72 

features of ovarian decline without exogenous insult. Rather than modeling overt menopausal hormone 73 

depletion alone, this model may provide insight into early trajectories of ovarian dysfunction that precede 74 

systemic endocrine collapse. 75 

Collectively, these models could help recapitulate distinct mechanisms of ovarian decline in a 76 

preclinical setting. However, differential impact of VCD with age-at-exposure and Foxl2 haploinsufficiency 77 

have not been systematically characterized and benchmarked within a unified framework. Such 78 

comparative characterization is essential for interpreting prior literature and for selecting appropriate 79 

models tailored to understanding systemic impacts of menopause-like ovarian failure. 80 

Here, we present a comprehensive resource evaluating three complementary mouse models of 81 

ovarian aging: (1) intact aging, (2) VCD-induced follicle depletion initiated at multiple adult ages, and (3) 82 

Foxl2 haploinsufficiency. Our objective is to systematically benchmark shared and divergent features 83 

across multiple biological scales. We integrate histological profiling, endocrine measurements, and single-84 

cell transcriptomics to characterize ovarian structural, functional, and molecular trajectories. Additionally, 85 

we develop hormone-based and transcriptome-based ovarian aging clocks to provide quantitative 86 

measures of reproductive aging across models. This comparative framework establishes a foundational 87

A systematic framework for modeling menopause in mice 92 

To systematically assess and compare physiologically relevant menopause models, we 93 

characterized three rodent models that represent distinct mechanisms of ovarian dysfunction: (1) the 94 

intact aging model (“Aging model”), (2) a chemically-induced model using repeated intraperitoneal 95 

injections of 4-vinylcyclohexene diepoxide (VCD, “VCD model”) at various age-of-exposure ( 15, 34 ), and 96 

(3) a genetic model of Foxl2 haploinsufficiency (“ Foxl2 +/- model”) ( 26 ). Our aim was to establish a 97 

comparative framework to evaluate these models in terms of histological, hormonal, and integrated 98 

ovarian health outcomes to evaluate their suitability for modeling key aspects of ovarian aging (Fig. 1A). 99 

The Aging model involved comparison of young adult (4 months) and old (20 months) female 100 

C57BL/6JNia mice. We selected these timepoints to capture the transition from reproductive to firmly 101 

post-estropausal states, as C57BL/6 females typically enter estropause ~12 months of age and show 102 

pronounced ovarian aging phenotypes by 20 months ( 35, 36 ). The VCD model included C57BL/6J female 103 

mice injected with vehicle control (safflower oil, hereafter referred to as “CTL”) or VCD starting at 3, 6, 8, 104 

or 10 months of age. This scheme allowed us to examine how the timing of ovarian insult (potentially 105 

modeling varying “age-at-menopause”) could impact ovarian physiology, particularly when VCD exposure 106 

occurs closer to natural estropause ( 35 ). Importantly, incorporating multiple age-at-injection paradigms 107 

should help disentangle ovarian-intrinsic failure mechanisms and how it interacts with the aging milieu. 108 

The Foxl2 haploinsufficiency model compared Foxl2 +/+ and Foxl2 +/- female littermate mice from our 109 

transgenic colony at young (3-4 months), early middle-age (8-10 months) and later middle-age (12-13 110 

months) to test whether partial loss of Foxl2 leads to anticipated occurrence of age-associated ovarian 111 

phenotypes compared to wild-type control mice. We selected the heterozygous model because complete 112 

Foxl2 loss is associated with severe developmental defects and high perinatal lethality (28) , and 113 

heterozygous mutations of FOXL2 are sufficient to drive both syndromic and isolate premature ovarian 114 

failure in humans patients. 115

To evaluate the impact of Foxl2 haploinsufficiency on ovarian aging phenotypes, we generated a 116 

knock-out Foxl2 allele using mice carrying a regenerated floxed Foxl2 locus ( 26 ), which allowed us to 117 

generate mice carrying a constitutive deletion of Foxl2 ’s single exon (fig. S1A; see Materials and 118 

Methods). Importantly, RT-qPCR analysis confirmed that Foxl2 expression was significantly reduced in 119 

Foxl2 +/- ovaries compared to Foxl2 +/+ controls (genotype effect, p-value ~0.0206; fig. S1B). Additionally, 120 

fertility outcomes for Foxl2 +/+ and Foxl2 +/- females from our breeding colony confirmed previous reports of 121 

decreased fertility of Foxl2 haploinsufficient mice (figs. S1C,D and table S1; see Materials and Methods). 122 

Specifically, adult Foxl2 +/- females showed significantly smaller first litters (p-value ~0.0161; fig. S1C), and 123 

increased latency to first time to delivery after male pairing compared to matched Foxl2 +/+ female controls 124 

(p-value ~0.0260; fig. S1D), suggesting impaired reproductive potential consistent with ovarian 125 

dysfunction or premature ovarian aging. 126

Histological and tissue-level remodeling across mouse menopause models 128 

A decline in ovarian follicle numbers is a well-established hallmark of ovarian aging and 129 

menopause (37 ). Thus, we performed histological analysis of ovarian follicle populations using 130 

hematoxylin and eosin (H&E) staining (Figs. 1B-G and fig. S2A). As expected, the Aging model showed a 131 

significant reduction in follicles at all developmental stages in old females, including primordial, primary, 132 

secondary, and antral follicles, as well as corpora lutea (Figs. 1B,E and fig. S2B), consistent with the 133 

established progressive follicular depletion that accompanies natural ovarian aging ( 38, 39 ). At 5 months 134 

post-injections, VCD-treated animals also showed significant depletion of follicle numbers, regardless of 135 

age-at-injection (Figs. 1C,F and fig. S2C). To note, due to the terminal nature of histological analysis, 136 

ovaries were collected for H&E staining at a single 5 months post-injection time point. This endpoint was 137 

selected to align with the conclusion of longitudinal serum hormone profiling (see below). In contrast, we 138 

did not observe any significant consistent differences in combined follicle counts between Foxl2 +/+ and 139 

Foxl2 +/- ovaries (Figs. 1D,G and fig. S2D). 140 

Next, we leveraged Picrosirius Red and Sudan Black B staining of ovarian tissue to quantify 141 

fibrosis and lipofuscin accumulation, respectively (Fig. 2A). Previous studies have demonstrated 142 

increased fibrosis and lipofuscin deposition in aged ovaries, reflecting progressive remodeling with 143

fibrosis and lipofuscin accumulation in old females compared to young controls (p ~0.0190 and p 145

findings indicate that structural remodeling features can emerge independently of substantial reductions 153 

in follicle number, highlighting both shared and model-specific aspects of ovarian aging phenotypes. 154

Comparative endocrine alterations across mouse menopause models 156 

To evaluate how each model perturbs endocrine regulation, we measured serum levels of AMH, 157 

FSH and INHBA, key markers of ovarian reserve and HPG axis regulation ( 43, 44 ) (Fig. 3A and table S2). 158 

Estradiol was not quantified due to known technical limitations affecting assay reliability in rodent samples 159 

(see Discussion). To note, due to a change of FSH quantitation assays offered at the UVA ligand core 160 

over the course of our study, FSH measurements were harmonized across methods using a polynomial 161 

correction model (fig. S3A; see Materials and Methods). 162 

In the Aging model, old females showed expected decreases in circulating AMH and INHBA and 163 

increased circulating FSH compared to young females, consistent with diminished ovarian reserve ( 44, 45 )164 

(Figs. 3B,E,H). A similar pattern was observed in the VCD model, whereby AMH and INHBA were 165 

decreased and FSH was increased compared to the vehicle control groups at 5 months post-injection 166 

timepoint, regardless of age-at-injection (Figs. 3C,F,I). To enable a comprehensive analysis of endocrine 167 

responses across reproductive life stages, we also performed longitudinal hormone profiling over a five-168 

month period following VCD exposure at various ages (3, 6, 8 and 10 months; figs. S3B-D). Interestingly, 169 

VCD injections at older ages resulted in attenuated hormonal shifts compared to younger counterparts, 170 

suggesting that ovarian or systemic factors at midlife modulate sensitivity to VCD-induced insult to 171

increased (consistent with younger follicle pools), whereas FSH levels were elevated and INHBA levels 173

Next, we applied our previously described composite ovarian health index, which integrates 178 

information from follicle counts and serum hormone levels to yield a holistic measure of ovarian health ( 36 )179 

(Figs. 3K-M). As expected, the index was significantly lower in old vs . young animals and VCD-treated 180 

animals vs. CTL (regardless of age-at-injection), reflecting impaired ovarian health (Figs. 3K,L). In 181 

contrast, Foxl2 +/- mice did not differ significantly from wild-type animals (Fig. 3M), likely due to elevated 182 

AMH levels. 183 

Together, these results provide a comprehensive characterization of histological and endocrine 184 

features of three mechanistically distinct mouse menopause models. Both the Aging and VCD models 185 

exhibit robust phenotypic hallmarks of ovarian decline, including depletion of follicular reserves, tissue 186 

remodeling, and disrupted endocrine profiles. In contrast, the Foxl2 haploinsufficiency model displays a 187 

distinct phenotype characterized by preserved follicle counts and elevated AMH levels, but with 188 

anticipated aged-like stromal remodeling and reproductive impairment. These findings indicate that the 189 

three models capture overlapping yet non-identical dimensions of ovarian aging biology, underscoring the 190 

importance of selecting models based on specific structural, endocrine, or regulatory processes under 191 

investigation. 192

A hormone-based clock, “OvAge”, for non-terminal prediction of ovarian aging 194 

Although the ovarian health index is a useful metric of ovarian health, its partial reliance on post-195 

mortem histological measurements limits its applicability in longitudinal studies. Thus, we aimed at 196 

developing a complementary, non-terminal metric of ovarian aging, using a hormone-based predictive 197 

machine-learning model, which we termed “OvAge” (Fig. 4A). By integrating information on AMH, FSH, 198 

and INHBA levels, we sought to develop a model that provides a more comprehensive assessment of 199

the clock, we trained a random forest regression model using serum hormone data (AMH, FSH, and 201 

INHBA) from animals with intact ovarian function and no experimental perturbation, including animals 202

haploinsufficiency model. In addition, to further increase statistical power, we also took advantage of 204 

similar data from Fshr +/+ animals derived from our previously published study of Fshr haploinsufficient 205

testing (n = 68) sets to enable robust evaluation of model performance. 207 

Model performance evaluation showed strong agreement between predicted OvAge and 208 

chronological age (Spearman’s Rho ~0.795; p-value ~5.5 × 10 -16 ), confirming successful model 209 

construction (Fig. 4B). Additionally, feature importance analysis revealed that AMH contributed most 210

hormonal measurements from VCD-injected and Foxl2 +/- animals (Figs. 4D,E). In the VCD model, 212 

predicted ovarian age was consistently higher than chronological age across all age-at-injection groups 213 

(Fig. 4D), suggestive of accelerated endocrine aging. In contrast, predicted ages of Foxl2 +/- animals did 214 

not substantially differ from their wild-type counterparts (Fig. 4E), likely due to the importance of AMH 215 

levels in OvAge calculation. 216 

To quantify the degree of divergence between predicted and chronological age, we computed 217 

hormonal age acceleration, defined as the difference between OvAge-predicted and chronological age 218 

(Figs. 4F-I). For the VCD model, we analyzed animals at 30-, 90-, and 150-days post-injection. In the 219 

VCD model, predicted ovarian age exceeded chronological age following VCD exposure (Figs. 4F-H). 220 

Analysis revealed significant treatment effects on age acceleration at 30, 90, and 150 days post-injection. 221 

Post hoc analyses confirmed significant increases in age acceleration at most timepoints; however, 222 

animals injected at 10 months of age did not exhibit a significant difference at 150 days post-injection 223 

(adjusted p-value ~0.142; table S3). This pattern may reflect convergence of ovarian aging trajectories 224 

between control and VCD-treated animals as they approach estropausal onset (~12-15 months in 225 

C57BL/6 mice ( 35 )). In contrast, Foxl2 haploinsufficient animals did not show a consistent increase in 226 

OvAge or age acceleration (genotype effect, p-value ~0.401; Fig. 4I). This lack of hormonal age 227

Together, our OvAge analysis captures divergent ovarian aging trajectories in response to distinct 231 

biological perturbations. The clock recapitulates expected aging patterns in naturally aged animals, 232 

detects accelerated ovarian aging following VCD exposure, and reveals a distinct endocrine trajectory in 233 

the Foxl2 haploinsufficiency model. These findings support the utility of OvAge as a non-terminal 234 

framework for assessing ovarian aging and highlight model-specific differences that may inform the study 235 

of reproductive senescence. To facilitate broader use of this framework, we developed an interactive 236 

online R Shiny application that enables researchers to estimate ovarian age using serum hormone 237 

measurements (https://minhooki.shinyapps.io/OvAge_Predictor/). 238 

Importantly, although the Foxl2 haploinsufficiency model did not exhibit overt ovarian dysfunction 239 

based on ovarian health index or OvAge (Figs. 3M, 4I), the absence of endocrine age acceleration does 240 

not exclude underlying molecular perturbations. Indeed, Foxl2 +/- animals displayed significant 241 

accumulation of ovarian fibrosis and lipofuscin (Figs. 2D,G,J,M), histological hallmarks associated with 242 

tissue aging and cell stress. Together with the established role of FOXL2 in granulosa cell identity and 243 

ovarian maintenance, its association with premature menopause in humans ( 30, 31, 48 ), and the 244 

reproductive impairment phenotypes observed in our cohort (Fig. S1C, D), our findings suggest that Foxl2 245 

haploinsufficiency likely disrupts ovarian tissue homeostasis prior to overt depletion of follicular pools, 246 

deserving further investigation of the accompanying transcriptional landscape across ovarian cell types. 247

RNAscope validation of ovarian cell type identities 288 

To validate cell type assignments, we performed RNAscope, a high-sensitivity in situ hybridization 289 

assay for both well-characterized and less frequently profiled ovarian cell populations (Fig. 5I and figs. 290 

S6A-C). Granulosa cells ( Fshr ), theca cells ( Cyp11a1 ), stromal cells ( Pdgfra ) and SMCs ( Acta2 ) were 291 

validated using established marker probes, confirming their presence in the dataset (fig. S6A). Epithelial 292 

cells ( Upk1b ), BECs ( Flt1 ) and LECs ( Prox1 ) were similarly detected, supporting the robustness of 293 

annotations (fig. S6B). Importantly, we validated the presence of various immune cell subsets, including 294 

NK ( Klrb1c ), NKT ( Klrb1c , Cd3e ), CD8 + T ( Cd3e , Cd8b1 ), CD4 + T cells ( Cd3e, Cd4 ), DNT ( Cd3e, with no 295 

Cd8b1/Cd4 signal) and B ( Cd19 ) cells (fig. S6C). We also confirmed presence of less commonly reported 296 

ovarian cell types, including CD8+ NKT ( Klrb1c, Cd3e, Cd8b1 ) and DPT ( Cd3e, Cd8b1, Cd4 ) cells (Fig. 297 

5I). These results further support the accuracy of the single-cell transcriptomic annotations and highlight 298 

the diverse somatic and immune microenvironments present across the aging models. 299 

Together, a total of 19 cell types, 8 non-immune and 11 immune types, were shared across all 300 

three models (fig. S6D). Some cell types were not shared across models, potentially due to technical 301

Understanding how ovarian decline contributes to systemic aging requires robust experimental 648 

models that accurately recapitulate the physiological features of menopause. In this study, we 649 

systematically evaluated three mechanistically distinct mouse models of menopause: an intact aging 650 

model, a chemical follicle depletion model using VCD exposure, and a genetic model of Foxl2 651 

haploinsufficiency. By integrating histological, hormonal, and transcriptomic profiling across these models, 652 

we provide a comparative framework for understanding shared and model-specific trajectories of ovarian 653 

aging. Findings from our study provide a critical comparative resource for identifying conserved and 654 

divergent features of ovarian aging and evaluating the suitability of each model, with the goal of 655 

advancing both mechanistic insights and translational strategies in menopause research. 656 

The intact Aging model showed expected hallmarks of physiological ovarian decline, including 657 

depletion of follicles across developmental stages, endocrine disruption, increased fibrosis and lipofuscin 658 

accumulation, and broad transcriptomic remodeling. At the cellular level, aging was associated with 659 

expansion of the immune compartment, reduction of most non-immune populations, and strong 660 

transcriptional perturbation in granulosa cells. These findings are consistent with prior scRNA-seq studies 661 

of mouse ovarian aging and reinforce the view that granulosa cells are among the most transcriptionally 662 

responsive somatic populations during ovarian decline (41, 49 ). Aging was also accompanied by 663 

increased transcriptional variability, altered TE expression, and coordinated pathway-level changes 664 

involving mitochondrial dysfunction and immune remodeling, indicating loss of tissue homeostasis across 665 

multiple biological scales. 666 

The VCD model led to the appearance of several key features of accelerated ovarian failure. 667 

VCD-treated animals exhibited marked follicle depletion, endocrine disruption, increased fibrosis and 668 

lipofuscin accumulation, and elevated ovarian age as measured by both OvAge and transcriptome-based 669 

clocks. Importantly, by varying the age at VCD exposure, we demonstrate that ovarian failure can be 670 

induced across different stages of the reproductive lifespan, including near the estropausal transition. 671 

This establishes the VCD paradigm as a flexible system for modeling age-at-menopause, enabling the 672 

generation of ovarian failure within an age-relevant systemic context. Additionally, we observed that the 673 

magnitude of endocrine and transcriptomic acceleration was greater when VCD exposure occurred at 674

Experimental Design 763 

This study was designed as a comparative resource to systematically evaluate mouse models of 764 

menopause and ovarian aging. Our objective was to determine how distinct mechanisms of ovarian 765 

dysfunction (physiological aging, chemically induced follicle depletion, and genetic disruption of granulosa 766 

cell identity), shape structural, endocrine, and molecular features of ovarian aging. To this end, we 767 

performed integrated histological, hormonal, and single-cell transcriptomic profiling across three models: 768 

intact aging, VCD-induced ovarian failure, and Foxl2 haploinsufficiency. 769 

Prespecified components included standardized histological assessment of follicle abundance 770 

and tissue remodeling, endocrine profiling of ovarian reserve markers, single-cell RNA sequencing to 771 

characterize cellular composition and transcriptional states, and integrative computational analyses to 772 

quantify molecular aging trajectories and regulatory programs. Harmonized preprocessing and analytical 773 

pipelines were applied across models to enable direct comparisons. 774

Ovarian health index calculation 906 

The ovarian health index was calculated as previously described ( 36 ). The scoring framework 907 

and reference thresholds were derived from the original cohort reported in that study. The index 908 

integrates two components: ovarian hormone levels (AMH, FSH, and INHBA) and follicle counts, which 909

lutea. Each parameter was assigned a score based on a three-tier system. Values exceeding the median 911 

of the young female group were assigned a score of 3, values positioned between the medians of the 912 

young and old groups were assigned a score of 2, and values below the median of the old group were 913 

assigned a score of 1. The hormone score was calculated as the mean of the individual scores for AMH, 914 

FSH, and INHBA. This hormone score was then combined with the follicle score in a 1:1 ratio to generate 915 

the overall ovarian health index, which was subsequently scaled to a 0-100 range for standardization. 916
