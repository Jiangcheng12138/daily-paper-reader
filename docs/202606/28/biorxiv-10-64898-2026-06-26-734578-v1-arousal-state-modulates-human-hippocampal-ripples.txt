Arousal modulation of ripples 

2

Abstract 1

2

Hippocampal ripples are transient, high-frequency oscillations linked to memory replay and 3

consolidation. Ripples are well-characterized in rodents to occur during periods of behavioral 4

inactivity (i.e., sleep, rest), viewed as “offline” states where replay can emerge with limited sensory 5

interference. However, human studies have increasingly observed ripples during active tasks, 6

raising the questions of whether ripple genesis and function have been misunderstood or whether 7

there are fundamental species differences. We propose that low arousal states—predominant 8

during offline sleep and transient during wake—may constitute a common mechanism of ripple 9

genesis that reconciles these observations. We recorded directly from human hippocampus during 10 

sleep and wake, measuring arousal via sleep staging, pupillometry, and heart rate. Ripple 11 

occurrence consistently tracked low arousal: rates were maximal in NREM sleep, small-pupil wake 12 

states, and slow heart rate periods across sleep and wake. This modulation was stronger in anterior 13 

than posterior hippocampus and was hippocampus specific: ripple-like activity outside the 14 

hippocampus showed an opposite modulation, increasing with high arousal. These results resolve 15 

apparent species differences and provide a unifying view of offline periods as arousal dips that can 16 

emerge across behavioral states, including transiently during active wake, suggesting hippocampal 17 

ripples, and memory consolidation, occur continuously intermixed with cognition. 18

Introduction 19 

> 20

Memory formation is a process that extends far beyond the initial moment of encoding. After 21 

experiences are first encoded in the hippocampus, their transformation from transient 22 

representations into enduring memories unfolds over timescales ranging from minutes to years. A 23 

key mechanism underlying this consolidation process is memory replay, the repeated reactivation 24 

of hippocampal memory representations, thought to drive coordinated cortical reinstatement and, 25 

over time, the formation of distributed cortical memory representations (Born & Wilhelm, 2012; 26 

McClelland et al., 1995). In rodents, replay has predominantly been observed during sleep and 27 

awake rest (Kudrimoti et al., 1999; A. K. Lee & Wilson, 2002; Skaggs & McNaughton, 1996; 28 

Wilson & McNaughton, 1994), as these “offline” periods of inactivity are thought to provide 29 

privileged moments for internal replay to transform memory representations while limiting 30 

external interference from new sensory information (Hasselmo, 2006; Klinzing et al., 2019; 31 

Mednick et al., 2011). 32 

Hippocampal memory replay can be studied through the detection of ripples—transient, 33 

high-frequency oscillations in the local field potential (LFP) that correspond to the local circuit 34 

firing during memory replay spike sequences (Buzsáki, 2015; Girardeau & Zugaro, 2011). In 35 

rodents, disruptions to ripples and ripple-associated activity during periods of sleep or inactivity 36 

impair memory (Aleman-Zapata et al., 2022; Ego-Stengel & Wilson, 2010; Girardeau et al., 2009; 37 

Y. Zhang et al., 2021). Consistent with rodent findings, human studies have shown that 38 

hippocampal ripples occur prominently during sleep, coordinated with other memory-related sleep 39 

oscillations (Ngo et al., 2020; Schreiner et al., 2024; Skelin et al., 2021; Staresina et al., 2015, 40 

2023). These observations support the use of ripples as a cross-species marker of replay and 41 

underscore the importance of offline states for memory consolidation. However, a recent body of 42 

work has suggested human hippocampal ripples occur during active wakefulness, as ripples have 43 

been observed during cognitive tasks ranging from perception (Y. Y. Chen et al., 2021), to memory 44 

(both encoding and retrieval; Henin et al., 2021; Norman et al., 2019; Sakon et al., 2024; Sakon & 45 

Kahana, 2022; Vaz et al., 2019) to planning and decision making (He et al., 2026; Zhou et al., 46 

2026), with rates in some cases exceeding those typically reported during sleep. The observance 47 

of ripples during active tasks in humans stands in tension with the rodent literature, where they are 48 

predominantly associated with rest and inactivity, complicating the tight coupling of ripples—and 49 

by proxy replay and consolidation—with offline states. Further challenging the cross-species 50 

comparison, while rodent studies have focused largely on hippocampal ripples specifically, in 51 

humans ripples have also been studied in the amygdala and broader medial temporal lobe (MTL) 52 

structures (Clemens et al., 2007; Cox et al., 2020; Sakon et al., 2024; Sakon & Kahana, 2022; 53 

Staresina et al., 2023; Vaz et al., 2019; H. Zhang et al., 2024), as well as in neocortical regions 54 

(Dickey et al., 2022; Verzhbinsky et al., 2024). Thus, the human ripple literature appears to diverge 55 

from the rodent in both the behavioral context (offline sleep/rest vs. online wake) and anatomical 56 

location (hippocampus vs. broader MTL) in which ripples are reported. These observations raise 57 

a fundamental question: what determines when hippocampal ripples occur? Is there a unifying 58 

mechanism that could bring together the rodent and human literature—one that accounts for their 59 

high rates during offline states like sleep while also explaining their occurrence during active 60 

waking tasks? 61 

Given the tight coupling of hippocampal ripple events with offline sleep states in rodents, 62 

one candidate mechanism governing their occurrence is arousal. Consistent with this view, ripples 63 

do not occur uniformly across sleep, but instead co-vary with arousal level across sleep stages, 64

with rates greatly elevated in NREM and attenuated in REM (Buzsáki, 2015; Joo & Frank, 2018). 65 

Furthermore, within NREM sleep, ripple occurrence has been tightly linked to reductions in 66 

arousal in rodents, as indexed by momentary decreases in acetylcholine (Y. Zhang et al., 2021, 67 

2024) and pupil diameter (H. Chang et al., 2025). This relationship between hippocampal ripple 68 

events and pupil-linked arousal was also observed previously during waking in rodents (Farrell et 69 

al., 2024; McGinley, David, et al., 2015; Nguyen et al., 2024). Together, these findings from 70 

rodents suggest that low arousal promotes ripple genesis. Yet, whether this arousal-ripple 71 

relationship holds in humans remains an open question. Critically, while arousal drops 72 

substantially during sleep, it also fluctuates dynamically during wakefulness and while performing 73 

cognitive tasks. This raises the possibility that, if arousal similarly governs ripple occurrence in 74 

humans, it may serve as a unifying variable across species and behavioral states: sustained low 75 

arousal during sleep could account for the predominance of ripples during sleep in rodents, while 76 

transient arousal dips during active tasks could drive their occurrence during wakefulness in 77 

humans. Under this framework, offline states would be reframed not as overt behavioral inactivity, 78 

but instead as periods of low arousal that can occur across both sleep and wake, with ripple rates 79 

increasing with the degree of arousal reduction more generally. 80 

Here, we tested the relationship between arousal and hippocampal ripple genesis in 81 

humans. Using invasive intracranial recordings in humans, we measured hippocampal ripples 82 

during both overnight sleep and separate periods of wake, indexing arousal via sleep staging and 83 

pupillometry, respectively. Across sleep and wake recordings, a consistent result emerged: 84 

hippocampal ripples occurred preferentially during states of low arousal, with elevated ripple rates 85 

during NREM in sleep and small pupil states in wake. To bridge across recordings, we leveraged 86 

heart rate—a measure of arousal obtainable during both sleep and wake—and replicated the 87 

finding, demonstrating that ripples occur preferentially during periods of low heart rate regardless 88 

of behavioral state. Finally, we showed that this relationship is anatomically specific to the 89 

hippocampus and strongest anteriorly: Ripple events in the anterior hippocampus consistently 90 

demonstrated tighter coupling to arousal than the posterior hippocampus, while ripple-like events 91 

in the amygdala showed the opposite pattern, with rates increasing during periods of high arousal. 92 

Together, these results offer a parsimonious account of when and why hippocampal ripples occur 93 

in humans, one that reconciles the human and rodent literatures, reframes offline states as dynamic 94 

periods of low arousal, and connects ripple occurrence to underlying mechanisms of arousal 95 

physiology. 96

Results 98 

99 

We hypothesized that hippocampal ripple rates would increase during low arousal states, tracking 100 

both large arousal drops across sleep stages and subtler dips within wakefulness (Fig. 1a). To test 101 

this, we performed human intracranial recordings in 20 neurosurgical patients during both 102 

overnight sleep and a wakeful fixation task with pupillometry (Fig. 1; see Methods). Recordings 103 

targeted the hippocampus (119 electrodes) and amygdala (77 amygdala electrodes; Fig. 1b; Table 104 

S1). The hippocampus was the main site of investigation, as it is the primary locus of ripple 105 

genesis, and was subdivided into anterior (69 electrodes) and posterior (50 electrodes) regions, as 106 

these areas are known to differ functionally (Moser & Moser, 1998; Poppenk et al., 2013; Strange 107 

et al., 2014; Fig. 1b). Amygdala recordings were analyzed given recent human studies reporting 108 

amygdala ripple activity (Cox et al., 2020; Sakon et al., 2024; Staresina et al., 2023; H. Zhang et 109 

al., 2024), and to provide a proximal MTL comparison region for hippocampal effects. Ripple 110 

events were detected across anatomical regions using an established detection and spectral 111 

verification method (Y. Y. Chen et al., 2021), following consensus guidelines (A. A. Liu et al., 112 

2022; Fig. 1c). This approach detects putative ripple events and then confirms spectral evidence 113 

of ripple band oscillations, excluding other broadband events which could also drive increases in 114 

ripple band power (e.g., broadband gamma, interictal spikes). Ripple events were examined across 115 

levels of arousal during sleep via staging (e.g., NREM vs. wake) and during wake via pupillometry 116 

(e.g., diameter small vs. large; Fig. 1d). 117

Hippocampal ripple rate increased during NREM states in sleep 133 

We first assessed the relationship between hippocampal ripple events and arousal fluctuations 134 

during overnight sleep. Arousal was measured by sleep staging, with NREM and wake reflecting 135 

low and high arousal states, respectively. Two nights of sleep were recorded and scored from each 136 

participant, except for one participant for whom only one night was included (41 total nights; see 137 

Methods; Table S1). Participants slept on average 7.5 h (SD = 1.7 h), with each night containing 138 

at least 1 hr of NREM and 1 hr of wake (Fig. S1; Table S2, S3). To test the influence of sleep stage 139 

on hippocampal ripple rate, we employed a linear mixed-effects model with fixed effects of sleep 140 

stage (NREM, REM, wake) and region (anterior, posterior) and random intercepts for electrodes 141 

nested within participants. Across the hippocampus, sleep stage predicted ripple rate (main effect 142 

of sleep stage: χ²(2) = 405.78, p < 0.001; Fig. 2d), with ripple rates highest during NREM (model 143 

mean ± SEM = 0.050 ± 0.002 Hz), reduced during REM (0.035 ± 0.002 Hz), and lowest during 144 

wake (0.032 ± 0.002 Hz; NREM – REM: t(577) = 11.66, p < 0.001; NREM – wake: t(575) = 14.76, 145 

p < 0.001; REM – wake: t(577) = 2.89, p = 0.011; all Tukey-corrected; Fig. 2d), consistent with 146 

prior reports in rodents and humans (Buzsáki, 2015; Y. Y. Chen et al., 2021; Jiang et al., 2020; 147 

Joo & Frank, 2018). 148 

While increased hippocampal ripple rates during NREM has previously been observed 149 

across species, recent evidence suggests there may be important differences in hippocampal ripple 150 

activity across the long axis in humans (De Filippo & Schmitz, 2023; Jiang et al., 2020; Staresina 151 

et al., 2023; To et al., 2025; J. Zhang et al., 2025). We thus sought to examine if there were any 152 

differences in arousal-ripple modulation between anterior and posterior hippocampus. To test this, 153 

we evaluated the effect of sleep stage on ripple rate separately for each hippocampal region 154 

(anterior, posterior), as well as the interaction between region and sleep stage, in the mixed-effects 155 

model described above. Ripple rate tracked sleep stage in both anterior and posterior hippocampus 156 

(joint F-tests: anterior: F(2, 575.37) = 201.47, p < 0.001; posterior: F(2, 575.37) = 6.27, p = 0.002), 157 

with rates higher in NREM than wake in both regions (Fig. 2b, d; anterior hippocampus: NREM– 158 

wake: t(572) = 18.79, p < 0.001; NREM–REM: t(574) = 15.62, p < 0.001; REM–wake: t(574) = 159 

2.99, p = 0.008; posterior hippocampus: NREM–wake: t(572) = 3.51, p = 0.001; NREM–REM: 160 

t(575) = 2.18, p = 0.075; REM–wake: t(575) = 1.27, p = 0.412; all Tukey-corrected). However, 161 

the magnitude of this modulation was significantly greater—3x larger—in anterior than posterior 162 

hippocampus (sleep stage × region: χ²(2) = 107.22, p < 0.001), driven by substantially elevated 163 

ripple rates in anterior than posterior hippocampus during NREM ( t(205) = 8.88, p < 0.001; Tukey-164 

corrected; Fig. 2b, d). Rates in the two regions were matched during REM ( t(213) = 1.12, p =165 

0.266) and wake ( t(205) = 0.25, p = 0.801), suggesting the anterior-posterior NREM difference 166 

reflected a functional or mechanistic distinction rather than a measurement difference. 167 

Additionally, ripple events in anterior hippocampus showed a pronounced post-ripple wave (PRW, 168 

Hussin et al., 2020) in the LFP that appeared largest in NREM (Fig. 2c). To quantify this effect, 169 

we computed the mean LFP amplitude in a 50 to 150ms post-ripple window baselined to a -150 to 170 

-50ms pre-ripple window and evaluated how it was impacted by sleep stage (NREM, REM, wake) 171 

and region (anterior, posterior) with a mixed-effects model. Anterior hippocampal PRW-amplitude 172 

varied across sleep stages and was greatest in magnitude in NREM (F(2, 237.09) = 20.91, p <173 

0.001; NREM – REM: t(233) = –6.46, p < 0.001; NREM – wake: t(233) = –4.00, p < 0.001; REM 174 

– wake: t(233) = 2.50, p = 0.035; all Tukey-corrected; Fig. 2c). This effect in anterior hippocampus 175 

was stronger than that in posterior hippocampus (region: χ²(1) = 18.26, p < 0.001; region × sleep 176 

stage: χ²(2) = 15.30, p < 0.001), where the PRW did not significantly vary across sleep stages (F(2, 177 

237.09) = 0.31, p = 0.733; Fig. 2c). 178

Motivated by the striking increase in hippocampal ripple rate during NREM sleep, which 206 

was more pronounced in anterior than posterior hippocampus, we next investigated how this 207 

NREM-ripple modulation varied continuously along the hippocampal long axis. For each 208 

electrode, we quantified the degree to which ripple rates increased during NREM compared to 209 

wake by computing a NREM selectivity index (SI = (NREM rate – wake rate) / (NREM rate + 210 

wake rate), where positive and negative values reflected greater ripple rates during NREM and 211 

wake, respectively. Electrodes were colored by their SI value and plotted in a normalized 212 

hippocampal space across participants, along the anterior-posterior (AP) and inferior-superior (IS) 213 

anatomical axes (Fig. 2f; see Methods). NREM selectivity was strongest in the most anterior 214 

hippocampal electrodes with SI values decreasing toward posterior hippocampus (Fig 2f; mixed 215 

effects model predicting SI as a function of AP position: slope (anterior to posterior) = –0.641 ± 216 

0.085, t(109.84) = −7.57, p < 0.001). Replicating this effect, in a mixed-effects model predicting 217 

ripple rate as a function of sleep stage and continuous AP position, a significant sleep stage × AP 218 

position interaction emerged (χ²(2) = 87.14, p < 0.001), with NREM ripple rates highest in anterior 219 

hippocampus and decreasing posteriorly (slope (anterior to posterior) = –0.067 ± 0.008 Hz, t(209) 220 

= −8.46, p < 0.001). Together, these results demonstrate that hippocampal ripple genesis is favored 221 

during the low-arousal state of NREM, an effect specific to the hippocampus and strongest 222 

anteriorly. 223

Hippocampal ripple rate increased during small pupil states in wake 240 

Having shown that ripple rates increase to their highest levels during low arousal states in sleep 241 

(i.e., NREM), we asked whether a similar relationship holds for low arousal states during 242 

wakefulness. In contrast to the large arousal changes that occur across sleep stages, arousal 243 

fluctuations within wake are more subtle and transient in nature (McCormick et al., 2020; 244 

McGinley, Vinck, et al., 2015). To capture these dynamics, we performed joint sEEG-pupillometry 245 

recordings during periods of wake, where small and large pupil diameter indexed low and high 246 

arousal, respectively (McGinley, David, et al., 2015; Murphy et al., 2014; Reimer et al., 2014, 247 

2016). However, for simultaneous sEEG-pupillometry recordings, standard desktop methods pose 248 

considerable practical challenges, as neurosurgical patients are often reclining in bed and wearing 249 

large head wraps. We addressed these constraints with a custom head-mounted system using 3D-250 

printed flexible frames to secure cameras monitoring both eyes, enabling high-fidelity continuous 251 

pupil diameter measurement synchronized to neural signals that is tolerant to head position and 252 

motion (Fig. 1d, 3a; see Methods). Joint recordings were performed during a controlled wake 253 

fixation task where participants alternated between eyes-open fixation periods (~15-17s) and brief 254 

eyes-closed (~3s) recovery periods (median task blocks per participant: 4, IQR: 4, 6; mean total 255 

eyes-open fixation time per participant ± SD: 15.68 min ± 7.36 min; Fig. 1d; Table S1; see 256 

Methods). A simple fixation task was chosen to minimize visual transients and thereby isolate 257 

endogenous, pupil-linked arousal fluctuations separate from stimulus-driven changes or eye-258 

motion-related artifacts. Finally, experiments were performed in a controlled low-light setting (see 259 

Methods). 260 

We examined how arousal fluctuations during wake, as captured by pupil diameter during 261 

the eyes-open fixation periods, influenced ripple rates. Overall, in the wake fixation task, mean 262 

ripple rate was significantly lower than that observed in NREM sleep (wake fixation task mean 263 

rate: 0.035 ± 0.0025 Hz; NREM – wake fixation task: t(412) = 5.18, p < 0.001), consistent with a 264 

wide literature (Buzsáki, 2015; Y. Y. Chen et al., 2021; Jiang et al., 2020; Joo & Frank, 2018). 265 

While mean ripple rate was slightly elevated for eyes-closed periods (Y. Y. Chen et al., 2021), 266 

there was no significant difference compared to eyes-open periods (χ²(1) = 3.39, p = 0.065; Fig. 267 

3b). In the eyes-open periods, ripple rates were calculated per pupil sextile where sextile 1 268 

corresponded to the smallest pupil diameter (lowest arousal) and sextile 6 to the largest (highest 269 

arousal; Fig. 3c), following recent work that linked replay rates to pupil sextile during sleep in the 270 

rodent (H. Chang et al., 2025). To evaluate the relationship between pupil sextile and hippocampal 271 

ripple events, mixed effects models were applied with predictors of pupil sextile (1-6) and 272 

hippocampal region (anterior, posterior). Mirroring our sleep results, during the wake fixation task 273

Low arousal states promoted hippocampal ripple genesis across measures 491 

Our goal was to examine the impacts of arousal on hippocampal ripple genesis, as we hypothesized 492 

that arousal may provide a unifying account for ripple event occurrence across both sleep and wake 493 

states. As predicted, low arousal states consistently promoted higher hippocampal ripple rates 494 

across sleep and wake, during both large and small fluctuations in arousal, and across multiple 495 

measures of arousal physiology (Fig. 5a). At the broadest level of arousal variation, hippocampal 496 

ripple rates were maximal during NREM sleep and minimal during wake. This relationship 497 

extended beyond discrete stage boundaries, with ripple rate increasing during low heart rate 498 

moments of sleep. This arousal-ripple coupling persisted with smaller arousal variations: ripple 499 

rates increased during low heart rate moments within NREM and wake stages individually, but 500 

with reduced magnitude, consistent with the narrower arousal ranges sampled within any single 501 

stage. Similarly, during the wake fixation task, despite generally lower ripple rates and a smaller 502 

arousal range than that spanned across sleep, pupil size predicted ripple rate, demonstrating that 503 

the pronounced arousal-ripple relationship during sleep extends to wake. Strikingly, the magnitude 504 

of the wake pupil effect approached that of the sleep heart rate effect, despite the smaller arousal 505 

range sampled during wake—suggesting that ripple generation may be particularly coupled to the 506 

arousal systems reflected by pupil diameter. Across all arousal measures, the effect was 507 

consistently greater in anterior compared to posterior hippocampus. In contrast, amygdala ripple-508 

like events demonstrated the opposite relationship to arousal state, with rates highest during the 509 

wake periods in the sleep recordings and strikingly increased during moments of high heart rate 510 

within these wake periods—reinforcing the regional specificity of arousal-ripple modulation. 511 

Beyond rate, ripple events vary in morphological features—amplitude, duration, peak 512 

frequency—and recent evidence has suggested that these attributes may inform the strength or 513 

efficacy of the event (Y. Y. Chen et al., 2021; Fernández-Ruiz et al., 2019; Ngo et al., 2020; 514 

Ramirez-Villegas et al., 2015; Robinson et al., 2026; Sebastian et al., 2023; Tong et al., 2021). We 515 

characterized these ripple attributes to examine if they were likewise shaped by arousal state (H. 516 

Chang et al., 2025). Overall, hippocampal ripple amplitude and duration demonstrated similar 517 

arousal modulations as rate in both sleep and wake recordings, with effects strongest in anterior 518 

hippocampus: consistent with prior work, anterior hippocampus ripple event amplitude and 519 

duration increased during large arousal drops across sleep stages (i.e., NREM; effect of sleep stage 520 

on duration: F(2, Inf) = 184.26, p < 0.001; on amplitude: F(2, Inf) = 7224.54, p < 0.001; Buzsáki, 521 

2015; Y. Y. Chen et al., 2021; Fig. 5b); extending this, for anterior hippocampus both measures 522 

also tracked subtle arousal dips during wake indexed by pupillometry (effect of pupil sextile on 523 

duration: z = −2.00, p = 0.045; on amplitude: z = −2.57, p = 0.010; H. Chang et al., 2025; Fig. 5b). 524 

Similar but attenuated trends were observed for posterior hippocampus ripple amplitude and 525 

duration whereas events detected in the amygdala showed a different pattern of modulation (e.g., 526 

largest ripple amplitude and duration during wake periods of sleep recordings; Supplemental 527 

results). On average, in mixed-effects models where region represented all three anatomical 528 

divisions (anterior hippocampus, posterior hippocampus, or amygdala), ripple event amplitude and 529 

duration were overall greatest in anterior hippocampus, attenuated in posterior hippocampus, and 530 

substantially smaller in the amygdala in both sleep (main effect of region on duration: χ²(2) = 531

MTL structures and within the hippocampus itself (Fig. 5b). In addition to these more commonly 535

Discussion 555 

> 556

Observations of hippocampal ripples during cognitive tasks in humans challenge the established 557 

coupling between ripples, replay, and offline states of inactivity from rodents. Here, we propose 558 

that fluctuations in arousal could offer a parsimonious account of ripple genesis across both sleep 559 

and active wake states, one that may aid in reconciling the human and rodent ripple literatures. 560 

Using invasive intracranial recordings in humans during both sleep and wake, combined with sleep 561 

staging, pupillometry, and heart rate measurements of arousal, we show that hippocampal ripples 562 

occur predominantly during low arousal states regardless of whether an individual is asleep or 563 

awake: ripples preferentially occurred during NREM stages in sleep, small pupil states in wake, 564 

and low heart rate states throughout sleep and wake. This robust arousal-ripple relationship was 565 

anatomically specific, with the strongest coupling observed in anterior hippocampus, while 566 

amygdala ripple-like events showed the opposite pattern, increasing during high arousal states. 567 

Together, these findings favor an important role for arousal in shaping when hippocampal ripples 568 

occur. 569 

Across three measures of arousal physiology—sleep stages, pupil diameter, and heart 570 

rate—hippocampal ripples occurred predominantly during moments of low arousal in both sleep 571 

and wake. These results replicate and extend recent rodent findings that reductions in pupil-linked 572 

arousal (i.e., pupil constriction) are associated with ripple and replay occurrence during sleep (H. 573 

Chang et al., 2025) and ripple occurrence during wake (Farrell et al., 2024; McGinley, David, et 574 

al., 2015; Nguyen et al., 2024). Here, we demonstrate the same pupil-ripple relationship in humans 575 

during wake and additionally reveal the arousal-ripple relationship using heart rate measures 576 

across both sleep and wake. Our findings also align with recent work identifying causal 577 

mechanisms that link arousal physiology to hippocampal ripple genesis: both endogenous 578 

increases in acetylcholine (ACh) and optogenetic stimulation of medial septal cholinergic neurons 579 

were found to suppress hippocampal ripple genesis in rodents, while drops in ACh coincided with 580 

ripple occurrence (Vandecasteele et al., 2014; Y. Zhang et al., 2021, 2024). Importantly, ACh has 581 

been shown to co-vary with both sleep stages (i.e., low ACh during NREM, high during REM and 582 

wake; Marrosu et al., 1995) and pupil diameter (i.e., low ACh during small pupil; Reimer et al., 583 

2016), suggesting that fluctuations in cholinergic tone may be a key mechanism underlying the 584 

arousal-ripple relationship we observe. However, ACh is not the only neuromodulator implicated 585 

in both pupil and sleep dynamics. Noradrenaline (NA) has also been associated with changes in 586 

arousal and pupil diameter (Reimer et al., 2016) as well as with sleep spindle occurrence (Aston-587 

Jones & Bloom, 1981; Kjaerby et al., 2022; Osorio-Forero et al., 2021), which is notable given 588 

that ripple-spindle coordination is thought to reflect the hippocampal-cortical communication 589 

necessary for memory consolidation (Buzsáki, 2015; Sirota et al., 2003; Staresina et al., 2015). 590 

However, the evidence for a direct role of noradrenaline in ripple genesis is mixed (Ul Haq et al., 591 

2012), although there is growing evidence that activity in the locus coeruleus, the brain’s primary 592 

noradrenergic nucleus, influences ripple-spindle coupling during sleep (Novitskaya et al., 2016; 593 

Swift et al., 2018; Yang & Eschenko, 2025). Together, the convergence of our results in humans 594 

with the causal rodent literature suggests that cholinergic arousal in particular, but perhaps in 595 

coordination with noradrenergic arousal, is a key determinant of the low arousal states that promote 596 

hippocampal ripple genesis. 597

uniform within the hippocampus or across medial temporal lobe structures (i.e., hippocampus and 599 

amygdala). Within the hippocampus, arousal-ripple coupling was consistently stronger in anterior 600

2026), however, the PRW appears to be reliably observed across human and NHP recordings. 633

et al., 2020). Together with this account, our finding that the PRW is sensitive to both arousal and 639 

anatomy underscores that it may reflect a physiologically meaningful aspect of ripple genesis or 640

Across species, ripple rates are typically maximal during NREM sleep and minimal during active 649 

behaviors (Buzsáki, 2015; Joo & Frank, 2018; A. A. Liu et al., 2022). In addition, changes in ripple 650 

rates are typically sensitive to slower shifts in behavioral state, rather than rapidly to sensory 651 

stimulation (Buzsáki, 2015; Y. Y. Chen et al., 2021; Leonard et al., 2015; Leonard & Hoffman, 652 

2017). However, several human studies report ripple rates during tasks to be equal to or greater 653 

than that observed during NREM sleep in humans (i.e =/> 0.2Hz; Jiang et al., 2019, 2020; other 654 

studies observe even lower NREM rates; Y. Y. Chen et al., 2021; Dickey et al., 2022; Ngo et al., 655 

2020; Skelin et al., 2021; Staresina et al., 2015, 2023). Furthermore, recent human studies have 656 

reported rapid event-related increases in ripple rate in response to stimulus presentation (e.g., J. 657 

Liu et al., 2026; L. Wang et al., 2026). 658 

Together, these findings appear difficult to align with prior animal work on when ripples 659 

occur. We suggest two key factors may account for this apparent inter-species difference: i) ripple 660 

detection methodologies; ii) arousal state modulations during task performance. First, ripple 661 

detection methodologies vary across species and research groups. Many human studies identify 662 

ripples from transient increases in power within a predefined ripple-frequency band. Importantly, 663 

task engagement and stimulus processing can produce broadband increases in high-frequency 664 

activity in the hippocampus (Axmacher et al., 2006; Colgin & Moser, 2010; Henin et al., 2019; 665 

Lachaux et al., 2012; Sederberg et al., 2007; Watrous et al., 2015), which can elevate ripple-band 666 

power without reflecting a discrete ripple oscillation. If not explicitly rejected, such broadband 667 

events could inflate waking ripple rates above that of sleep and create apparent stimulus-related 668 

increases. For this reason, prior consensus recommendations have emphasized the importance of 669 

spectral confirmation methods that verify ripples as discrete oscillatory events (A. A. Liu et al., 670 

2022; van Schalkwijk & Helfrich, 2026). Indeed, as shown in the present study, the use of ripple 671 

spectral confirmation results in a distribution of ripple rates that is highest during NREM sleep and 672 

attenuated during wake (Y. Y. Chen et al., 2021), as observed in rodents (Buzsáki, 2015; Joo & 673 

Frank, 2018). Second, arousal state may help reconcile ripple occurrence across animal and human 674 

studies. The state-dependent framework we propose suggests arousal dips may link ripple 675 

occurrence across sleep, quiet wakefulness, and active task performance. In humans, ripples 676 

observed during cognitive tasks may arise during transient low-arousal moments embedded within 677 

otherwise active behavior. Indeed, our findings show that even subtle reductions in arousal during 678 

resting fixation are associated with increased ripple occurrence. This provides a more harmonious 679 

account of prior observations of ripples across diverse cognitive tasks: such studies may have 680 

captured genuine ripple events, but these events may have reflected endogenous arousal 681 

fluctuations rather than the cognitive or sensory events used to structure the analysis. Under this 682 

framework, task variables influence ripple occurrence only insofar as they modulate arousal, 683 

whereas endogenous arousal fluctuations provide the more proximal predictor of when ripples 684 

occur during wakefulness. While this may suggest a re-interpretation of ripple occurrence in 685 

previous literature, it would not necessitate revising the functional understanding of ripples in 686 

studies where cognitive events converge with arousal variations; instead, this framework may offer 687 

a mechanistic account of what promotes ripple occurrence in these studies. Finally, anatomical 688 

specificity may also help explain discrepancies across the human ripple literature. In addition to 689 

hippocampal ripples, some human studies have included ripple events detected across broader 690 

MTL or cortical regions. As our amygdala and cortical findings illustrate, non-hippocampal high-691 

frequency events can show arousal relationships that differ from, or even oppose, those observed 692

While our findings establish a robust arousal-ripple relationship in humans, several 698 

limitations exist. First, as our wake recordings were performed during a simple fixation task— 699 

chosen to optimize for clean pupil measurements without visual transients or eye movements—no 700 

behavioral measure of memory was obtained. This leaves open the question of how the ripple-701 

arousal dynamic we observed relates to mnemonic behavior. However, separate literatures have 702 

linked ripples to memory replay and behavior (Buzsáki, 2015; Joo & Frank, 2018), and arousal 703 

states to memory performance (Aston-Jones & Cohen, 2005; Clewett & Murty, 2019; Hasselmo, 704 

2006; Sara, 2009), with recent rodent work directly connecting pupil-linked arousal to the 705 

expression of memory replay and behavior (H. Chang et al., 2025). Thus, while the arousal 706 

framework predicts that endogenous arousal fluctuations should remain the primary determinant 707 

of ripple occurrence regardless of task, the finding that low arousal states promote ripple genesis 708 

suggests that low arousal moments during mnemonic tasks may impact memory replay and 709 

subsequent behavior (H. Chang et al., 2025). Second, the clinical depth electrodes used in our 710 

human intracranial recordings do not permit sampling from the large numbers of single neurons 711 

necessary to robustly characterize spiking sequences and directly measure memory replay. Recent 712 

work has demonstrated ripple-locked activity of stimulus-specific neurons in human hippocampus 713 

(Kunz et al., 2024) and ripple-locked memory reactivation in macaque hippocampus (Abbaspoor 714 

et al., 2025). However, future work leveraging advances in high-density electrode technology, or 715 

recordings in non-human primates where denser neural sampling is currently feasible, in 716 

combination with arousal measurements will be critical for establishing whether primate ripple 717 

events observed during transient moments of low arousal in wakefulness carry the replay of 718 

memory content central to consolidation theories (Reithler et al., 2025). Finally, given our proposal 719 

that low arousal states, and in particular moments of low cholinergic arousal, may promote ripple 720 

genesis, future work could examine human hippocampal ripples during direct perturbations of 721 

cholinergic arousal systems (i.e., pharmacological or neuromodulatory approaches) to provide 722 

causal evidence for this relationship in humans. 723 

In conclusion, we report a robust relationship between low arousal states and human 724 

hippocampal ripple genesis, suggesting that arousal may be a key driver of ripple occurrence across 725 

both sleep and wake. These findings align with a growing focus on arousal as a fundamental factor 726 

shaping neural dynamics and behavior (Aston-Jones & Cohen, 2005; McCormick et al., 2020; 727 

McGinley, David, et al., 2015; Raut et al., 2025; Sara, 2009). Moreover, a substantial literature has 728 

demonstrated that arousal specifically influences mnemonic behavior (Aston-Jones & Cohen, 729 

2005; Clewett & Murty, 2019; Sara, 2009), with fluctuations in neuromodulators, like ACh, 730 

thought to drive different states of cognitive focus (i.e., external versus internal; Tarder-Stoll et al., 731 

2020) and memory processing (i.e., encoding versus consolidation; Hasselmo, 2006; Hasselmo & 732 

McGaughy, 2004) across wakefulness. Our suggestion that fluctuations in arousal may drive ripple 733 

occurrence in both wake and sleep, together with this literature, supports a broader view in which 734 

ripples and memory consolidation are not behaviorally segregated from other cognitive processes, 735 

restricted to sleep alone. Instead, transient dips in arousal could promote the emergence of ripples 736 

and consolidation intermixed with ongoing cognition. This dynamic view aligns with growing 737 

evidence suggesting that the brain may continuously fluctuate between external (i.e., online; 738

Human participants 748 

Intracranial recordings were obtained from 20 human participants (10 F/10 M; mean age: 39.75 749 

years, age range: 21–64 years; see Table S4) undergoing invasive monitoring as part of their 750 

treatment for refractory epilepsy at the Hospital of the University of Pennsylvania (Philadelphia, 751 

Pennsylvania, USA). One participant (P4) underwent two surgeries with distinct electrode 752 

implants, yielding 21 implants across these 20 participants. Recordings were performed using 753 

stereo-electroencephalography (sEEG) depth electrodes (Ad-Tech Medical Instrument Corp., WI, 754 

USA). All experimental procedures were approved by the Institutional Review Board at the 755 

University of Pennsylvania Perelman School of Medicine (IRB protocol number: 821778), with 756 

participants providing verbal and written consent. 757 

> 758

Experimental Design 759 

We tested the hypothesis that low arousal states are associated with hippocampal ripple genesis 760 

across sleep and wake. To do so, each participant underwent intracranial recordings during 761 

overnight sleep and a wakeful fixation task with pupillometry, enabling within-participant analyses 762 

of hippocampal ripples across the large arousal fluctuations that accompany sleep stage transitions 763 

(measured by sleep staging and electrocardiogram, EKG) and the finer fluctuations present during 764 

wakefulness (measured by pupillometry and EKG; Fig. 1). 765 

> 766

Fixation Task 767 

To examine how arousal fluctuations during wakefulness influence ripple genesis, we employed a 768 

simple resting fixation task with simultaneous pupillometry, EKG, and intracranial recordings. A 769 

fixation task was chosen to optimize pupil recordings by limiting confounds from visual transients 770 

and eye movements, allowing measurement of endogenous pupil-linked arousal fluctuations 771 

during resting wakefulness (T. Liu et al., 2026). 772 

The task was presented on an adjustable monitor (27”, 3840 × 2160; connected to an 773 

Alienware laptop m18 R2 running Windows 11) at a viewing distance of 57 cm using MATLAB 774 

(R2023a, MathWorks, MA, USA) and Psychtoolbox (3.0.19; Brainard, 1997). To optimize pupil 775 

measurements, low level lighting conditions were kept constant across recordings by closing all 776 

blinds, turning off all lights and monitors, and then turning on the same dim lighting configuration 777 

in all patient rooms (Mathôt & Vilotijević, 2023). To confirm controlled and dim lighting 778 

conditions, illuminance, measured next to the participant’s head at eye level with the task monitor 779 

on, was recorded at the start of each data collection period (Dr.meter LX1330B Digital Illuminance 780 

Light Meter; mean ± SD: 13.43 ± 5.73 lux). 781 

During the fixation task, participants completed a series of trials, each consisting of an 782 

eyes-open phase followed by an eyes-closed phase (Fig. 1d). During the eyes-open phase, 783 

participants fixated on a central cross for 17 s before being visually prompted to ‘close eyes’. 784 

Participants were instructed to minimize blinking during these eyes-open phases. During the eyes-785 

closed phase (3 s), participants would blink or keep their eyes shut until a brief auditory tone was 786 

played to signal the return to eyes-open fixation. To motivate participants’ engagement, on 30% 787 

of eyes-open phases the fixation cross slowly rotated at a random time and participants were 788 

instructed to indicate the direction of this rotation by pressing the left or right arrow key on a 789 

handheld keypad. Experiments were broken into task blocks containing either 10 or 15 eyes-open 790 

fixation periods, depending on the participant’s comfort to perform extended fixations, with 791

Pupil recordings and preprocessing 795 

Simultaneous pupillometry and intracranial recordings in the epilepsy monitoring unit present 796 

practical challenges, as patients are typically reclining in bed and wearing large head wraps. To 797 

accommodate this setup, we employed a head-mounted pupillometry system and designed custom 798 

3D-printed flexible frames to secure Pupil Labs world and dual eye cameras (Pupil Labs Core de-799 

cased, 120 Hz world camera, 200 Hz binocular eye cameras; Fig. 1d). This setup allowed us to 800 

record eye camera video synchronized to neural signals via ZMQ (ZeroMQ 4.0, matlab-zmq: 801 

https://github.com/fagg/matlab-zmq), with continuous measurements of pupil diameter extracted 802 

from the eye camera videos. The pupillometry system was calibrated at the onset of each data-803 

collection session using single-point calibration, with additional calibration performed as needed 804 

between blocks. Pupil diameter (mm) from both eyes was exported using Pupil Labs Pupil Player 805 

(v3.5.7) before being post-processed and analyzed using custom scripts with MATLAB 2022b. 806 

Artifacts and blinks were detected and removed using the Pupil Labs blink detector (Pupil 807 

Player v3.5.6), which identifies blinks based on abrupt changes in the confidence of the pupil-808 

ellipse fit. An additional 50 ms was removed before and after each detected event to account for a 809 

blink-associated pupillary response (Diamond, 2001; Yoo et al., 2021). The pupil data were then 810 

manually inspected to ensure complete blink removal; if any blink-associated response remained, 811 

additional samples were removed until the data were fully blink-free. Outlier pupil values that 812 

exceeded 2.5 SD from the mean were additionally removed as likely signal artifacts. Trials were 813 

excluded from a block if more than 70% of the trial was removed or if the blink rate exceeded 0.5 814 

blinks/second and more than 30% of the trial was removed. If more than 50% of trials in a block 815 

were excluded, that block was excluded from analyses. 816 

After blink and artifact removal, data were interpolated using linear interpolation and low-817 

pass filtered below 4 Hz before being z-scored within blocks for final analyses (Krishnamurthy et 818 

al., 2017). Data from a single eye were used for analyses—with the right eye used by default, or 819 

the left eye if the camera angle yielded a suboptimal recording from the right. Only eyes-open 820 

periods of the wake fixation task were included in pupil analyses. 821

Electrophysiological recording 823 

Intracranial sEEG data during sleep and the wake fixation task were acquired at a sampling rate of 824 

2 kHz with a bandpass of 0.3–500 Hz (4 th -order Butterworth filter) using a BlackRock Cerebus 825 

system (BlackRock Microsystems, UT, USA). Initial recordings were referenced to a depth 826 

electrode contact within the skull or white matter, distant from pathological zones. During the 827 

wake fixation task, trial events were tracked using a photodiode sensor (attached to the stimulus 828 

monitor) synchronously recorded at 30 kHz. All additional data processing was performed offline. 829

Electrode localization and selection 864 

To identify sEEG electrode contacts within the hippocampus, amygdala, and cortex, a post-865 

operative CT scan was co-registered to a pre-operative T1 anatomical MRI scan for each 866 

participant using the YAEL (Your Advanced Electrode Localizer; Z. Wang et al., 2023) module 867 

of RAVE (Reproducible Analysis and Visualization of iEEG software; Magnotti et al., 2020), with 868 

3D coordinates for each electrode computed with RAVE. For each participant, electrodes located 869 

in the hippocampus and amygdala were manually identified in native space, with visualization 870 

performed in RAVE and ITK-SNAP (v3.6.0; Yushkevich et al., 2006). Additionally, for each 871 

depth electrode targeting these structures, a white matter contact and the most lateral grey matter 872 

contact were identified to serve as the re-reference (described below) and comparison recording 873 

site (see Supplement) for hippocampal and amygdala recordings. 874 

Given known functional differences across the long axis of the hippocampus (Moser & 875 

Moser, 1998; Poppenk et al., 2013; Strange et al., 2014), we sought to classify hippocampal 876 

electrodes as anterior or posterior. To do so, an expert (EMS) manually identified the uncal apex 877 

for each participant’s left and right hippocampi, with electrodes anterior to this landmark assigned 878

sEEG preprocessing and spectral decomposition 932 

All sEEG signal processing was performed using custom MATLAB scripts (R2022b & R2024b, 933 

MathWorks, MA, USA). All identified hippocampal and amygdala electrodes (see Electrode 934 

Localization and Selection ) were notch filtered (60 Hz and harmonics) and re-referenced to a 935 

proximal electrode contact within the white matter on the same probe (Y. Y. Chen et al., 2021). 936 

Re-referenced signals from each electrode were downsampled to 1 kHz and spectrally decomposed 937 

using Morlet wavelets (7 cycles) with center frequencies spaced linearly from 1 to 200 Hz in 1 Hz 938 

steps. 939 

> 940

Ripple detection 941 

After preprocessing, ripple events were identified across regions (hippocampus, amygdala, cortex) 942 

in three stages: i) time-domain detection for identifying putative ripple events; ii) frequency-943 

domain assessment for accepting or rejecting ripple events; iii) electrode-wise ripple rejection 944 

thresholding for inclusion or exclusion (Y. Y. Chen et al., 2021); following recent consensus 945 

methodologies (A. A. Liu et al., 2022). 946 

Time-domain detection: Notch filtered and re-referenced signals were bandpass filtered 947 

from 80 to 120 Hz (ripple band) using a 4 th -order FIR filter. The root mean square (RMS) of the 948 

bandpass-filtered signal was calculated and smoothed using a 20 ms window. Ripple events were 949 

identified as periods with ripple band RMS amplitude between 2.5 and 9 standard deviations above 950 

the mean. Detected ripple events with a duration shorter than 38 ms (3 cycles at 80 Hz) were 951 

rejected. 952 

Frequency-domain assessment: The spectral amplitude distribution (1–200 Hz) for each 953 

detected ripple event was computed by averaging the normalized instantaneous amplitude between 954 

the onset and offset of the ripple event (using spectrally decomposed data). Spectral amplitude was 955 

normalized to percent change from baseline by applying a correction at each frequency based on 956 

the mean amplitude across the entire recording for a given electrode and frequency. For the spectral 957 

distribution of each detected ripple event, spectral peaks were identified using the findpeaks 958 

function in MATLAB, and events were rejected if: (1) there was more than one peak in the ripple 959 

band; (2) the most prominent and highest peak fell outside the ripple band; (3) the ripple peak 960 

width exceeded 3 standard deviations from the mean peak width for that electrode and recording 961 

session; or (4) high-frequency activity peaks exceeded 80% of the ripple peak height (following 962 

steps from Y. Y. Chen et al., 2021). Any remaining ripples with amplitude exceeding 300 μV were 963 

considered artifactual and rejected. This spectral assessment was designed to ensure detected 964 

events had a frequency profile consistent with true oscillatory ripples, thereby excluding other 965 

high-frequency activity (i.e., broadband gamma and interictal spikes) that may overlap temporally 966 

but differ spectrally (Y. Y. Chen et al., 2021; A. A. Liu et al., 2022; Maslarova et al., 2025; van 967 

Schalkwijk & Helfrich, 2026). 968 

Electrode-wise ripple rejection thresholding: the ripple rejection rate was calculated for 969 

each electrode within each recording block. Electrodes with a rejection rate exceeding 60% were 970 

excluded from that recording block, as these likely reflected noisy recordings. Electrodes excluded 971 

from 75% or more of recording blocks were removed from all analyses. 972

Statistical analysis 976 

To test our hypothesis that low arousal is coupled with increases in hippocampal ripples across 977 

states, we quantified the relationship between arousal (sleep stage; pupil size; RR interval) and 978 

ripple attributes (rate; amplitude; duration; peak frequency; post-ripple wave). To enable rate-979

corresponded to large pupil and high arousal states. Large RR interval sextiles (i.e., sextile 6) 987 

corresponded to slow heart rate and low arousal states, while small RR interval sextiles (i.e., sextile 988 

1) corresponded to fast heart rate and high arousal states. For Fig. 5, pupil sextiles 1 and 2 were 989

Mixed-effects analyses of arousal-ripple relationships 994 

Across analyses, the relationships between arousal (pupil size; sleep stage; RR interval) and ripple 995 

attributes (rate; amplitude; duration; peak frequency; post-ripple wave) were assessed using linear 996 

mixed-effects models ( lme4 package; Bates et al., 2015) in R (R Core Team, 2021). In all models, 997 

ripple attributes were predicted by arousal as a fixed effect, and participant and electrode were 998 

included as random effects, to accommodate nested data (electrodes) within participants. For 999 

analyses of ripple modulation differences between the hippocampus and amygdala, and across the 1000 

hippocampal long axis, electrode location (hippocampus/amygdala; anterior/posterior; anterior-to-1001 

posterior proportional position) was included as a categorical fixed effect with an interaction with 1002 

arousal. 1003 

Mixed-effects models were assessed using a combination of approaches depending on 1004 

predictor type. For categorical predictors (i.e., sleep stage), the Anova function from the car 1005 

package (Fox & Weisberg, 2019; Type II Wald chi-square tests) was used to assess overall 1006 

significance of each fixed effect, with p-values for individual factor levels obtained via lmerTest 1007 

(Kuznetsova et al., 2017) using Satterthwaite’s degrees of freedom method (which provides a 1008 

conservative adjustment accounting for the complexity of the mixed-effects structure). Post hoc 1009 

comparisons were performed using the emmeans package (Lenth, 2022), with Tukey’s adjusted p-1010 

values and Kenward-Roger degrees of freedom, to control for multiple comparisons while 1011 

maintaining statistical power through improved degrees of freedom estimation. For continuous 1012 

predictors (i.e., pupil sextile, RR interval sextile), significance was assessed from model 1013 

coefficients via lmerTest , providing direct inference on linear effects; where continuous predictors 1014 

interacted with categorical factors, estimated slopes were obtained using emtrends with Kenward-1015 

Roger degrees of freedom, enabling interpretation of how continuous effects (e.g., impact of pupil 1016 

sextile on ripple rate) vary across categorical levels (e.g., anatomical region). For analyses of ripple 1017 

amplitude and duration, given the large sample size (each ripple an independent sample, versus 1018 

rate calculations which collapse counts within time bins), degrees of freedom were estimated using 1019 

the asymptotic (z) approximation, as Kenward-Roger and Satterthwaite corrections converge on 1020 

this solution at large sample sizes and impose computational costs without meaningful differences 1021
