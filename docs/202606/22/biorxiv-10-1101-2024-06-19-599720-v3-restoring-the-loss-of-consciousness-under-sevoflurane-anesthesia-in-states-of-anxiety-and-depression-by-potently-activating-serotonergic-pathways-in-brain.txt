# Abstract 38 

Preoperative anxiety and depression are significant risk factors for prolonged general 39 

anesthesia emergence, raising postoperative complications and mortality. However, the neural 40 

mechanisms underlying this association remain unclear, limiting effective interventions and 41 

development of effective preventive and therapeutic strategies. Previous studies have shown 42 

that sleep-wakefulness neural circuit is closely related to neural circuitry of anesthesia recovery, 43 

with 5-Hydroxytryptamine (5-HT) playing a pivotal role of anesthesia arousal. Herein, we 44 

utilized pharmacology, optogenetics, chemogenetics, fiber photometry and retrograde tracing 45 

to demonstrate that activation of the 5-HTergic neural circuit between the central amygdala 46 

(CeA) and medial prefrontal cortex (mPFC) accelerates recovery from sevoflurane anesthesia. 47 

Particularly for individuals with anxiety- and depression-like symptoms, who exhibit more 48 

severe delayed emergence, it is necessary to enhance 5-HT neuronal activity by further 49 

optimizing parameters. To further validate the reliability of our findings and the specificity of 50 

viral tools, we successfully bred TPH2-Cre transgenic mice and repeated key experiments, 51 

which robustly confirmed all original conclusions. Pharmacological experiments further 52 

demonstrated that both 5-HT₁A and 5-HT₂A receptors mediate the pro-arousal function of this 53 

circuit. Collectively, our study identifies a key CeA-mPFC serotonergic pathway linking 54 

emotional disorders to abnormal anesthetic emergence, providing potential targets for 55 

optimizing anesthesia management in vulnerable patients. 56 

Keywords: General anesthesia, Delayed emergence, Sevoflurane, 5-hydroxytryptamine, 57 

Central amygdala nucleus, Medial prefrontal cortex, Consciousness, Anxiety, Depression 58 59 

# 1 Introduction 60 

Anxiety and depression represent significant global public health burdens, with prevalence 61 

rising by 26% and 28%, respectively, following the COVID-19 pandemic 1. These conditions are 62 

highly prevalent among surgical patients and are clinically significant predictors of adverse 63 

perioperative outcomes 2. Preoperative anxiety and depression are correlated with more severe 64 

delayed emergence from anesthesia, a phenomenon linked to alterations in the neural circuits 65 

regulating the sleep-wake cycle regulation 3,4 . Given these risks, understanding the mechanisms 66

outcomes. Only through a meticulous exploration of these factors and the implementation of 68

General anesthesia represents a meticulously adjusted, drug-induced, controllable state 72 

that guarantees patient comfort and safety during surgical and invasive clinical procedures 7.73 

Among the myriad anesthetics available, sevoflurane, a potent inhalational anesthetic , has 74 

emerged as a cornerstone in modern surgery due to its rapid induction and smooth emergence 8.75 

However, despite its widespread use, the exact mechanisms by which sevoflurane affects the 76 

brain, particularly its intricate role in facilitating arousal from anesthesia, remain shrouded in a 77 

degree of mystery 9,10 . Notably, delayed emergence has also become a common complication of 78 

general anesthesia 11 . Yet, failure to regain consciousness promptly after anesthesia can lead to 79 

severe consequences, such as increased risks of hypoxemia, respiratory suppression, metabolic 80 

encephalopathy, and central nervous system damage, with potentially life-threatening outcomes 12–

. The causes of delayed emergence from general anesthesia are complex. Studies have shown that 82 

preoperative psychological status can be a potential predictor of the need for anesthesia. 83 

Preoperative anxiety and depression, two predominant psychological comorbidities in surgical 84 

patients, are closely correlated with prolonged emergence duration and elevated risks of 85 

postoperative adverse complications 16,17 .86 

5-Hydroxytryptamine (5-HT), commonly referred to as serotonin, is a crucial monoamine 87 

neurotransmitter ubiquitously present in mammalian tissues 18,19 . Its role spans numerous aspects 88 

of central nervous system function, including cognitive processing, emotional regulation, 89 

sexual activity, thermoregulation , and even the regulation of food consumption. Studies have 90 

demonstrated that central 5-HT levels fluctuate dynamically during anesthesia, hinting at its pivotal 91 

role in modulating the effects of general anesthesia 20,21 . In particular, patients with anxiety-92 

depressive disorders usually have dysfunctional 5-HTergic neurons, resulting in a reduced 93 

regulation of their neural networks. Such neuronal impairment impairs neural network 94 

modulation, blunts responsiveness to arousal cues, and ultimately alters anesthetic emergence 95 

profiles. In addition, 5-HT imbalance is closely associated with dysfunction of the autonomic 96

those related to fear, anxiety, and depression 30,31 . It receives substantial inputs from the cerebral 106 

cortex and subcortical areas, and in turn, projects to key brainstem and diencephalic structures, 107 

as well as to regions engaged in hormonal, autonomic, and stress-related responses, thereby 108 

influencing the sleep-wake cycle 31 . Additionally, CeA also sends numerous projections to areas 109 

involved in arousal and rapid eye movement sleep, such as the pons-geniculate-occipital waves, 110 

further emphasizing its indispensable role in regulating sleep and wakefulness 32,33 .111 

The medial prefrontal cortex (mPFC) is intricately connected with arousal-related nuclei, 112 

including the locus coeruleus (LC) and DR 11,34 . This structural and functional entanglement 113 

indicates that the mPFC is pivotal in modulating consciousness levels , not only during anesthesia 114 

but also in the transition from anesthesia to wakefulness 35–37 . Recent research has demonstrated 115 

that inhibiting mPFC neurons prolongs the emergence of consciousness from general anesthesia, 116 

further solidifying its role as a key regulator of arousal and consciousness 38 . Additionally, studies 117 

have observed a decrease in serotonin levels in the frontal cortex during anesthesia, which may 118 

contribute to alterations in consciousness associated with anesthetic states. Researches have 119 

revealed widespread neural projections between the CeA and the mPFC , yet the role of the 5-HT 120 

neural pathway within the CeA-mPFC circuit during the process of emergence from general 121 

anesthesia remains unclear 39,40 . Therefore, this study aims to explore the involvement of 5-HT 122 

neurons in the CeA and mPFC, as well as their functional connectivity, in the restoration of 123 

consciousness during sevoflurane anesthesia. Particularly, the functional activity of this neural 124 

circuit under different degrees of delayed emergence is both intriguing and significant. 125

sevoflurane anesthesia, we established two classic mouse pathological models: chronic restraint 151 

stress (CRS)-induced and lipopolysaccharide (LPS)-induced anxiety/depression models. A full 152 

battery of behavioral paradigms including open field test (OFT), elevated plus maze (EPM), 153 

forced swim test (FST) and tail suspension test (TST validated robust anxiety- and depressive-154 

like behaviors in both CRS and LPS mice (Extended Data Fig. 1A-H). Compared with naive 155

2.2 Exogenous 5-HTP supplementation accelerates sevoflurane emergence in a dose-164 

dependent manner 165 

5-HTP, the direct blood–brain barrier-permeable precursor of 5-HT, elevates central 166 

serotonergic tone. We adopted multi-modal delivery strategies to characterize its pro-arousal 167 

effects across naive, CRS and LPS mice (Fig. 1C). Repeated intraperitoneal (i.p.) 24 h interval 168 

5-HTP injection produced no significant changes in anesthesia induction time at all tested doses 169 

(P > 0.05; Fig. 1D-F). In naive mice, 50 and 75 mg/kg 5-HTP significantly shortened emergence 170 

latency (P < 0.05, P < 0.01). Pathological mice displayed blunted sensitivity and required higher 171 

effective doses: CRS mice responded to 100 mg/kg, while LPS mice required 125 mg/kg 5-172 

HTP to achieve comparable arousal facilitation (P < 0.05). To isolate central-specific effects, 173 

we performed intracerebroventricular (ICV) cannulation and drug delivery (Fig. 1G). ICV 174 

infusion of 22.7 mmol/L 5-HTP robustly reduced emergence time with no impact on induction 175 

(P < 0.001), whereas the low 11.35 mmol concentration exerted no behavioral effect. (Fig. 1H) 176 

We next localized the core arousal-relevant region to the central amygdala (CeA). Basal c-Fos 177 

neuronal activity in the CeA was markedly suppressed post-sevoflurane exposure compared 178 

with pre-anesthetic baseline (Fig. 1I-J). Local CeA microinjection of 2.5 or 5 mg/mL 5-HTP 179 

selectively shortened emergence time without altering induction duration (P < 0.01, P < 0.001; 180 

Fig. 1K-L). Collectively, systemic, central ventricular and region-specific CeA delivery of 5-181 

HTP exert graded pro-arousal effects, with emotional disorder states desensitizing animals to 182 

peripheral serotonergic supplementation. 183

2.4 Optogenetic and chemogenetic activation of CeA serotonergic neurons promotes 201

2.9 Graded intervention intensity is required to rescue blunted serotonergic circuit 307 

function under emotional pathology 308 

Correlation regression analyses quantified the relationship between intervention strength 309 

and emergence latency across naive, CRS and LPS mice (Fig. 8). In wild-type animals, 310 

emergence time exhibited strong negative linear correlations with increasing 5-HTP dosage, 311 

optogenetic light power and CNO chemogenetic dose: stronger stimulation yielded faster 312 

recovery of consciousness (Fig. 8A, E, I). These inverse linear correlations were entirely lost 313 

in CRS and LPS mice (Fig. 8B–C, J–K). Low-to-moderate interventions failed to produce 314 

arousal benefits and even exerted mild inhibitory effects on emergence in pathological animals: 315 

75 mg/kg 5-HTP, 10 mW light and intermediate CNO doses all prolonged recovery time in 316 

CRS/LPS groups (Fig. 8D, H, L). These data indicate that anxiety and depression remodel 317 

serotonergic receptor sensitivity and intracellular downstream signaling cascades within the 318 

CeA–mPFC circuit, driving neuronal desensitization that necessitates escalated, high-intensity 319 

targeted interventions to reverse delayed anesthetic emergence. 320 

2.10 Summary of circuit functional remodeling under anxiety and depression 321 

Multi-modal pharmacological, fiber photometry, optogenetic, chemogenetic and viral 322 

tracing experiments, plus independent validation in Sert-Cre transgenic mice, collectively 323 

define a core bidirectional CeA-mPFC serotonergic circuit governing sevoflurane arousal. 324 

Under physiological conditions, activation of this circuit enhances neuronal excitability and 325 

accelerates consciousness restoration. Chronic anxiety and depression drive persistent 326 

hypoactivity of CeA/mPFC 5-HT neurons and blunt 5-HT1A/5-HT2A receptor-mediated 327 

synaptic transmission, which mechanistically explains the clinical phenotype of prolonged 328 

anesthetic emergence in emotionally vulnerable subjects. Escalated circuit-specific stimulation 329 

targeting these two receptor subtypes effectively rescues pathological arousal deficits, 330 

establishing this reciprocal serotonergic pathway as a critical therapeutic target for 331 

perioperative anesthetic management in patients with anxiety and depressive comorbidities(Fig. 332 

8M). 333

# 3 Discussion 334 

Since consciousness is modulated by multiple neural factors, prolonged emergence from 335 

general anesthesia poses substantial clinical risks. Therefore, elucidating the regulatory 336 

mechanisms governing anesthetic recovery remains an urgent and important research topic. 337 

Comorbid anxiety and depression have become a global public health crisis¹, and accumulating 338 

clinical evidence demonstrates that preoperative emotional disorders are tightly associated with 339 

delayed anesthetic emergence and severe postoperative complications. These adverse outcomes 340 

are largely attributed to disrupted neural circuits that regulate the sleep–wake cycle³,⁴. As one 341 

of the most widely used inhaled anesthetics, sevoflurane occupies a core position in clinical 342 

surgery and diagnostic procedures. A thorough dissection of the neural mechanisms underlying 343 

consciousness transitions during sevoflurane anesthesia not only provides direct guidance for 344 

optimizing perioperative anesthetic management, but also establishes a fundamental theoretical 345 

framework for understanding the general neurobiological rules of consciousness regulation. 346 

As an essential monoamine neurotransmitter in the central nervous system, 5-HT 347 

participates extensively in consciousness-related physiological functions, and numerous studies 348 

have confirmed its indispensable role in sleep–wake modulation⁴². Our previous work revealed 349 

that activating 5-HTergic neurons in the DR and their projections to the BLA effectively 350 

shortens sevoflurane emergence time. Both presynaptic 5-HT₁A autoreceptors in the DR and 351 

postsynaptic 5-HT₁A receptors in the BLA mediate this pro-arousal effect²⁰,⁴¹. Building on these 352 

findings, the present study further explores the neural circuit mechanisms by which the 5-353 

HTergic system regulates consciousness recovery during sevoflurane anesthesia. 354 

We first established mouse models with normal status or anxiety/depression-like 355

and depression-like behaviors exhibited significantly prolonged emergence. This pathological 358 

phenotype indicates that clarifying the aberrant neural circuit mechanisms underlying delayed 359 

emergence under emotional disturbance can, in turn, help reveal the physiological regulatory 360
