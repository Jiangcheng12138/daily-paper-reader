Abstract 17 

The mechanisms driving interstitial fluid flow through brain parenchyma remain unresolved, lim-18 

iting our understanding of how fluid transport contributes to glymphatic waste clearance and broader 19 

aspects of brain metabolism and neuronal activity. Existing theories based on diffusion or pressure 20 

gradients fail to explain sustained, directional flow through the tortuous extracellular space (ECS), 21 

particularly under normal physiologic conditions. Here we propose that electroosmosis, fluid motion 22 

driven by endogenous electric fields acting on charged brain tissue, provides a biophysically consistent 23 

mechanism for intraparenchymal interstitial fluid transport while generating pressure distributions 24 

favorable for periarterial influx and perivenous efflux. Using computational modeling informed by 25 

anatomical reconstructions of ECS microstructure and local field potential (LFP) recordings, we 26 

show that electroosmotic flow generates physiologically realistic velocities and reproduces brain state 27 

dependent differences in glymphatic transport, including the enhanced glymphatic flow observed 28 

during sleep compared to wakefulness. A physics-informed reduced-order model (ROM) further 29 

demonstrates that these microstructure-resolved results upscale consistently to tissue-level trans-30 

port. Moreover, electroosmotic flow can induce directional pressure gradients across perivascular 31 

interfaces, facilitating both influx and efflux. This mechanism provides a unifying framework linking 32 

neuronal activity, parenchymal flow, and compartmental pressure regulation. In contrast, pressure 33 

gradients substantially larger than physiological estimates generated much smaller velocities and 34 

failed to account for the observed transport rates. These findings address a major gap in glym-35 

phatic physiology and suggest that modulation of electric field properties, via endogenous activity 36 

or external neuromodulation, could serve as a therapeutic strategy to enhance solute clearance in 37 

neurological disorders. 38 

Keywords: electroosmotic flow, cerebrospinal fluid transport, glymphatic system, parenchymal flow 39

A major challenge in brain physiology is the lack of a unifying physical model that explains how fluid 41 

moves through the narrow and tortuous extracellular space; a process essential for nutrient distribution 42 

and waste clearance. Existing frameworks cannot account for sustained, directional transport under 43 

normal physiological conditions. We show that electroosmosis (fluid motion generated when endogenous 44 

neuronal electric fields act on charged cellular surfaces) provides a biophysically consistent mechanism for 45 

this transport. Using realistic extracellular microstructures and a validated tissue scale reduced order 46 

model, our model reproduces experimentally observed brain state dependent transport. Establishing 47 

electroosmosis as a possible contributor to interstitial fluid movement offers a new conceptual basis for 48 

linking neuronal activity to fluid circulation and for guiding strategies to enhance brain clearance. 49 

# Introduction 50 

The glymphatic system is a brain-wide clearance pathway that transports cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) 51 

through the central nervous system, facilitating solute exchange and removal of neurotoxic waste prod-52 

ucts 1–3 (Fig. 1a). Dysfunction of this system has been implicated in neurological disorders ranging 53 

from Alzheimer’s disease to traumatic brain injury, where impaired clearance accelerates neurodegen-54 

eration 4–7 . Understanding the mechanisms that regulate interstitial fluid (ISF) transport, particularly 55 

within the brain parenchyma, is therefore essential both for advancing basic physiology and for identify-56 

ing therapeutic strategies that enhance waste removal. The glymphatic system is commonly divided into 57 

three segments: influx of CSF along periarterial spaces, transport through the parenchymal extracellu-58 

lar space (ECS), and efflux along perivenous pathways 11;12 . While arterial pulsations and perivascular 59 

pressure gradients have been proposed as drivers of periarterial influx 13 , the mechanism sustaining 60 

parenchymal flow remains unresolved. An important distinction in glymphatic physiology is that trans-61 

port within the brain parenchyma should not necessarily be viewed as a direct continuation of fluid 62 

circulation within perivascular spaces (Fig. 1e). Periarterial and perivenous pathways form relatively 63 

open conduits surrounding blood vessels, where arterial pulsations, vasomotion, and other vascular os-64 

cillations can effectively drive fluid motion. In contrast, the parenchymal extracellular space consists 65 

of a highly confined and tortuous network of nanoscale channels whose hydraulic resistance is substan-66 

tially greater than that of perivascular pathways. Consequently, only a fraction of the fluid circulating 67 

within perivascular spaces is expected to exchange with the parenchyma at any given location. The 68 

physical mechanisms governing transport within these two compartments may therefore be fundamen-69 

tally different. While vascular pulsations may contribute to circulation within perivascular pathways 70 

and facilitate fluid exchange at tissue boundaries, the mechanism responsible for sustaining directional 71 

transport through the highly resistive extracellular space remains unresolved. The ECS (Fig. 1b), is a 72 

narrow, tortuous microenvironment, with widths on the order of tens to hundreds of nanometers, posing 73 

significant resistance to fluid motion 14;15 .74 

Classical studies established that diffusion in the ECS is strongly influenced by geometric factors 75 

such as tortuosity and volume fraction, which hinder the movement of macromolecules relative to free 76 

solution 16;17 . More recent high-resolution imaging has revealed that the ECS forms a heterogeneous 77 

nanoscale network whose structure varies dynamically across physiological states 18;19 . Ultrastructural 78 

analyses have also demonstrated that traditional chemical fixation methods can significantly underes-79 

timate ECS volume, whereas cryo-fixation preserves a larger and more physiologically representative 80 

extracellular space 8. Together, these studies highlight the importance of realistic ECS geometry when 81 

modeling interstitial transport in brain tissue. 82 

Pressure-driven bulk flow has been proposed as a mechanism for intraparenchymal transport, but 83 

there is little direct evidence supporting the presence of persistent pressure gradients capable of generat-84 

ing physiologically relevant flow through parenchyma. Moreover, recent computational reconstructions 85 

argue against pressure-driven intraparenchymal flow. Holter et al. 20 simulated interstitial flow in 3D 86

In general, waste accumulation within the parenchyma increases local osmotic pressure relative to 102 

incoming cerebrospinal fluid, creating a tendency for water to move into, and remain within, the tissue. 103 

While such osmotic forces may facilitate fluid entry from periarterial spaces, they do not inherently 104 

promote clearance toward downstream efflux pathways and may, in some circumstances, oppose it. 105 

Consequently, efficient glymphatic transport requires not only a mechanism capable of driving directional 106 

interstitial flow but also one capable of overcoming or balancing the osmotic and hydraulic constraints 107 

associated with continuous solute clearance. These considerations further suggest that passive pressure-108 

and diffusion-based mechanisms alone may be insufficient to fully explain the observed efficiency and 109 

directionality of glymphatic transport. 110 

Recent experimental observations suggest a link between neuronal dynamics and ISF movement. 111 

Imaging studies show that glymphatic transport is enhanced during sleep compared to wakefulness 21;23 ,112 

coinciding with shifts in neuronal activity toward high-amplitude, low-frequency oscillations. In par-113 

ticular, Jiang-Xie et al. 9 demonstrated that state-dependent changes in local field potentials (LFPs) 114 

directly correlate with CSF penetration, implicating electrical activity as a modulator of glymphatic 115 

function. Recorded LFPs during non-rapid eye movement (NREM) sleep have higher amplitudes and 116 

dominant low frequency compared to wake, as shown in Fig. 1c. Correspondingly, CSF penetration into 117 

the parenchyma is markedly increased during NREM (Fig. 1d). Moreover, local neuronal silencing re-118 

duced CSF influx in the same brain state, isolating neuronal activity as a causal driver of fluid movement. 119 

Recent human studies further support a link between neuronal activity and glymphatic function, showing 120 

that sleep-associated increases in EEG delta power and reductions in beta power are accompanied by 121 

enhanced glymphatic transport and reduced parenchymal resistance 24 . Together, these findings support 122 

the idea that endogenous brain electric fields play a causal role in parenchymal fluid dynamics. 123 

Here we propose that electroosmosis (EO), the motion of fluid driven by electric fields acting on 124 

charged surfaces, provides a biophysically consistent mechanism for ISF transport through the ECS. 125 

EO is not an unfamiliar phenomenon in physiology; it has been observed in other biological tissues 126 

where charged porous matrices interact with endogenous electric fields, establishing its plausibility as a 127 

transport mechanism 25–27 . The negatively charged extracellular matrix naturally satisfies the conditions 128 

for EO 28 , while ongoing neuronal activity generates time-varying electric fields capable of driving fluid 129 

motion 29–31 . Using anatomically reconstructed ECS geometries 32 and computational multiphysics simu-130 

lations informed by experimental LFP recordings, we show that electroosmotic flow produces physiologi-131 

cally realistic velocities, reproduces brain state dependent differences in intraparenchymal ISF transport, 132 

and intrinsically generates pressure gradients that reinforce influx and efflux. A physics informed reduced 133 

order model further demonstrates that these results extend consistently to tissue-scale transport. To our 134 

knowledge, this is the first quantitative study investigating electroosmosis as a contributor to glymphatic 135 

transport through brain parenchyma. Beyond its physiological relevance, an EO-based mechanism for 136 

parenchymal transport has broad implications for translational neuroscience: by linking neuronal ac-137 

tivity to fluid dynamics, it suggests strategies to modulate glymphatic function for therapeutic benefit, 138 

with potential applications in enhancing drug delivery and promoting clearance of pathological solutes 139 

in neurodegenerative diseases 13;33–35 .140

confined channels. The interstitial space (ISS) of brain parenchyma naturally satisfies the prerequisites 143 

for this phenomenon: the extracellular matrix carries a net negative surface charge 28 , and neuronal 144 

activity generates time-varying local electric fields 29;30 .145

The electric fields in the brain are inherently dynamic, with magnitudes and frequencies governed 161 

by neuronal activity 29 . To capture their effect on EO, we modeled the effective velocity spectrum as 162 

ueo,eff (f ) ∝ | ˆE(f )| · H(f ), where | ˆE(f )| is the spectral magnitude of the field and H(f ) is a low-pass 163 

transfer function representing frequency-dependent attenuation due to electric double layer relaxation and 164 

tissue-level low-pass filtering in the ECS 36 . This framework predicts that low-frequency oscillations more 165 

efficiently drive EO, while higher-frequency components are suppressed. As shown in Fig. 1g, spatially 166 

varying electric fields generated by action potentials and oscillatory activity interact with nearby charged 167 

surfaces, aligning flow along the orientation of axonal tracts. This coupling results in local directionality 168 

of flow, ensuring that EO does not simply produce random pore scale motion but instead channels fluid 169 

preferentially along neuronal structures. 170 

At a larger scale, the structural anisotropy of white matter bundles and aligned fiber tracts further 171 

organizes these flows (Fig. 1h) 37 . By guiding field propagation across extended tissue domains, this 172 

anisotropy channels EO-driven transport from periarterial influx zones into deeper parenchyma and 173 

toward perivenous efflux routes. Together, these features integrate molecular physics, neuronal activity, 174 

and tissue architecture into a coherent framework for directional fluid movement. 175 

Reported experimental results are consistent with this model: during sleep, LFPs show increased 176 

amplitude and dominant power at lower frequencies compared to wake (Fig. 1c). These state-dependent 177 

changes coincide with deeper CSF penetration into parenchyma (Fig. 1d), supporting the hypothesis 178 

that neuronal activity modulates glymphatic transport through EO. 179 

To assess the feasibility of electroosmosis as a mechanism for parenchymal flow, we constructed a 180 

three-dimensional computational model of a 4 μm3 brain tissue block. The geometry was derived from 181 

electron microscopy reconstructions of hippocampal neuropil reported by Kinney et al. 32 . As shown in 182 

Fig. 2a, a representative subvolume was cropped, surface-smoothed, and remeshed to improve quality for 183 

computational simulations. Since our focus was on fluid transport, the solid tissue domain was inverted 184 

to extract the fluid-accessible interstitial space, which was subsequently meshed (mesh statistics and 185 

independence analysis are provided in the Methods). The numerical implementation was independently 186 

validated in a microchannel system with experimental data reported by Peng et al. 38 , validation results 187 

provided in Supplementary Fig. S1. 188

A critical prediction of the EO hypothesis is that glymphatic parenchymal flow should depend on 230 

neuronal brain state. In vivo imaging shows that intraparenchymal ISF flow is enhanced during sleep 231 

compared to wakefulness 9;21 , coinciding with a shift in LFPs toward higher amplitude, lower frequency 232 

oscillations during sleep. To test whether EO can explain this correlation, we extracted LFP time-series 233 

from Jiang-Xie et al. and applied them as driving fields in our electroosmotic simulations of anatomically 234 

realistic ECS geometries 32 .235 

The resulting velocity fields (Fig. 3b) show noticeably higher velocity magnitudes during sleep com-236 

pared to wake. Physiological neural activity exhibits asymmetric, burst-like dynamics across multiple 237 

time scales, leading to nonzero effective transport over time. In addition, the highly heterogeneous and 238 

tortuous geometry of the extracellular space breaks spatial symmetry, allowing oscillatory electroos-239 

motic forcing to generate net transport through rectification effects 40 . As a result, time-dependent 240 

electroosmotic forcing can produce physiologically meaningful transport within realistic ECS geometries. 241

To assess the relative contribution of pressure-driven flow, we performed additional simulations in-262 

corporating a pressure gradient representative of arterial pulsation. We considered a gradient of 0.01 263 

Pa/ μm , which is approximately two orders of magnitude larger than experimentally reported pressure 264 

gradients in brain parenchyma ( ∼0.1–0.3 Pa/mm) 42 . Even under this elevated condition, the resulting 265 

interstitial velocities were on the order of 0.1 nm/s and remained approximately two orders of magnitude 266 

smaller than those generated by electroosmotic forcing (Supplementary Table S1). These results indicate 267 

that while pulsation may influence influx and modulate transport at the system level, pressure-driven 268 

flow alone is insufficient to drive significant bulk transport within the parenchymal ECS. 269 

Beyond driving local parenchymal transport, EO intrinsically generates directional pressure gradi-270 

ents, which may critically contribute to coordinating other components of the glymphatic system. As 271 

illustrated in Fig. 4, CSF enters the parenchyma via periarterial spaces, traversing porous arterial bound-272 

aries before exchanging with interstitial fluid. Fluid must then exit through perivenous boundaries into 273 

perivenous spaces, ultimately joining venous efflux routes. Conservation of mass requires that these cou-274 

pled compartments operate in balance, avoiding fluid buildup in any segment. Classical pressure-driven 275 

or diffusion-based models cannot readily explain how influx and efflux remain simultaneously regulated. 276 

Our results suggest that electroosmosis may provide a physical coupling between these processes, si-277 

multaneously supporting periarterial influx, parenchymal transport, and perivenous efflux through an 278 

activity dependent mechanism (Fig. 4). 279 

Electroosmotic flow driven by locally generated electric fields induces spatial pressure variations 280 

within the tissue. These distributed pressure differences give rise to a relative pressure minimum near in-281 

flow boundaries (e.g., periarterial regions) and a corresponding pressure elevation near outflow boundaries 282 

(e.g., perivenous regions). The pressure gradients emerge as a collective effect of spatially distributed 283 

electroosmotic forcing within the heterogeneous extracellular network. Neuronal activity propagates 284 

along axonal pathways and can generate locally directional extracellular currents; however, vascular and 285 

axonal orientations are not globally aligned, with vessels frequently traversing perpendicular to dominant 286 

fiber directions or varying along the same pathway 43 . The local pressure minimum facilitates CSF entry 287 

through periarterial boundaries, while the elevated efflux pressure supports clearance through perivenous 288 

boundaries. 289 

Thus, stronger neuronal activity, reflected in higher LFP wave power, not only enhances parenchymal 290 

flow but also amplifies periarterial influx and perivenous efflux through modulation of local boundary 291 

pressures. This provides a plausible mechanism for glymphatic efflux, a process that remains compara-292 

tively understudied and mechanistically unresolved in glymphatic physiology. Experimental studies have 293 

shown that chemogenetic inhibition of neuronal activity impairs glymphatic efflux and tracer clearance 9.294 

To our knowledge, the present electroosmotic model is the first quantitative transport framework capable 295 

of explaining how neuronal activity can enhance parenchymal transport, and facilitate activity-dependent 296 

glymphatic efflux. 297

for water to remain within the tissue and can therefore oppose fluid removal across efflux boundaries. 303 

The elevated pressures observed near the efflux regions in our simulations provide a potential means of 304 

overcoming this osmotic resistance while simultaneously facilitating exchange into downstream clearance 305 

pathways. In contrast, the reduced pressures observed near influx boundaries promote fluid entry from 306 

periarterial spaces. Together, these results suggest that the induced pressure field may serve a dual 307 

role: supporting hydraulic communication across compartment boundaries while maintaining effective 308 

clearance in the presence of solute concentration gradients that naturally arise during transport. 309 

As shown in Fig. 4, pressure differences scaled with the power of applied LFP-derived fields, sug-310 

gesting that neuronal oscillation intensity modulates not only parenchymal flow but also compartmental 311 

exchange across perivascular interfaces. This positions EO flow as an intermediate ’pump’ that couples 312 

periarterial influx with perivenous efflux, amplifying glymphatic clearance during sleep when oscillatory 313 

power is greatest. We further considered the role of dynamic ECS volume, which expands and contracts 314 

across brain states 41 . Because EO velocity depends primarily on surface electrokinetic forcing rather 315 

than absolute channel dimensions, our results remain valid, nonetheless, enhanced flow could itself pro-316 

mote ECS expansion, providing a feedback link between neural activity, electrokinetic transport, and 317 

microstructural remodeling. 318

terstitial fluid transport. By linking electric fields generated by neuronal activity to directional fluid 320

The brain is one of the most energy consuming organs of the human body, continuously generating 328 

metabolic waste. These include gases, metabolites such as lactic acid, misfolded or aggregated proteins, 329 

damaged organelles (e.g., mitochondria), and other macromolecules. These waste products are released 330 

into the interstitial space, where the glymphatic system facilitates their transport toward the venous 331 

circulation and ultimately into lymphatic pathways for clearance. The current understanding of glym-332 

phatic flow suggests that state dependent changes, including modulation of aquaporin-4 activity and 333 

ECS volume, contribute to enhanced fluid and waste transport, particularly during sleep. However, it 334 

remains unclear how waste is directionally transported through the highly tortuous parenchymal ECS. 335 

The mechanism proposed here provides this link: neural activity generates local extracellular electric 336 

fields which, in the presence of charged ECS interfaces, drive electroosmotic flow. This establishes a 337 

direct coupling between brain metabolism, through its regulation of neural activity, and fluid-mediated 338 

waste transport. Such a coupling may help explain how state dependent neural dynamics, including 339 

increased synchrony during sleep, contribute to enhanced clearance. It may also be particularly relevant 340 

in pathological conditions such as traumatic brain injury and neurodegenerative disease, where both 341 

electrical activity and fluid transport are disrupted. Furthermore, recent reports demonstrating that 342 

focused ultrasound can enhance glymphatic flow may be consistent with modulation of electrokinetic 343 

mechanisms, providing additional context for the relevance of the proposed framework. 344 

In parallel, accumulating experimental evidence indicates that brain fluid transport can be actively 345 

modulated by physiological and external factors. Mechanical stimulation such as ultrasound has been 346 

shown to enhance glymphatic transport and interstitial tracer movement 45 , while modulation of vas-347 

cular pulsation can accelerate interstitial fluid drainage 46 . More recently, electrical neuromodulation 348 

approaches such as transcranial direct current stimulation have been reported to alter CSF–ISF ex-349 

change in vivo 47 . These findings collectively suggest that brain fluid transport may be influenced by 350 

multiple dynamic driving mechanisms beyond passive diffusion alone. The electroosmotic mechanism 351 

proposed here represents a complementary biophysical pathway that could operate within the charged 352 

extracellular microenvironment to couple neuronal electrical activity with interstitial fluid transport. At 353 

a broader level, mechanisms that enhance clearance efficiency, such as electroosmosis, may have influ-354 

enced the evolutionary optimization of brain morphology and glymphatic circulation 48 . Beyond resolving 355 

a longstanding gap in glymphatic physiology, this insight opens translational opportunities. For example, 356 

weak transcranial alternating current stimulation at slow-wave frequencies could enhance electroosmotic 357 

transport, accelerating clearance during sleep or aiding drug delivery 35 . Such approaches would leverage 358 

the brain’s endogenous mechanism rather than imposing external pressure or diffusion, offering a novel 359 

therapeutic pathway for neurodegenerative disease. Future work could investigate whether propagating 360 

neuronal activity patterns, such as those observed in epilepsy, give rise to directional interstitial fluid 361 

transport. The proposed electroosmotic mechanism suggests this as a testable hypothesis that can be 362 

examined using combined measurements of neural activity and fluid flow. The ROM framework devel-363 

oped here provides a practical path toward this goal, enabling tissue- and organ-scale simulations that 364 

would otherwise be computationally intractable. 365 

Our findings support a conceptual separation between perivascular circulation and intraparenchy-366

by nanoscale confinement, high tortuosity, and substantially greater hydraulic resistance. In this con-370 

text, intraparenchymal transport is unlikely to be a simple continuation of flow established within the 371

# Methods 379 

## Geometry Generation 380 

To reconstruct a realistic 3D geometry of the extracellular space, we used publicly available anatom-381 

ical data reported by Kinney et al. 32 , who reconstructed hippocampal neuropil from serial electron 382 

microscopy images. We downloaded the co-registered EM image stack, segmentation contours, and 3D 383 

surface models provided. The original EM images were traced using RECONSTRUCT, and 3D surfaces 384 

were generated with VolumeRover, as detailed in the original publication. 385 

We began with the STL surface mesh of the ECS-accessible space extracted from the provided data. 386 

Due to the highly complex, high-resolution topology of the mesh, it was not immediately suitable for 387 

computational fluid dynamics simulations. To prepare the geometry, we first imported the mesh into 388 

ParaView to crop a representative 4 μm3 volume that preserved key structural features while reducing 389 

computational burden. 390 

The cropped geometry was then imported into Blender for extensive cleanup. Each cellular component 391 

was isolated and inspected to correct inverted normals, fill holes, and eliminate surface artifacts. We 392 

performed localized remeshing and smoothing to improve watertightness and reduce mesh irregularities. 393 

Because our simulations targeted fluid transport, we applied a Boolean inversion to extract the fluid-394 

accessible domain (i.e., the void space between cells). 395 

This inverted, cleaned fluid domain was imported into SolidWorks, where we repaired non-manifold 396 

edges and ensured geometric validity for mesh generation and converted to a solid part. The final solid 397 

model was imported into COMSOL Multiphysics, where tetrahedral meshing with local refinement was 398 

used to resolve narrow ECS channels and enable stable electroosmotic simulations. 399 

This reconstruction pipeline—from EM image-based anatomical data to simulation-ready geome-400 

try—enabled us to model flow through a physiologically grounded ECS microenvironment. Code for 401 

geometry handling and STL processing is available upon request. 402

## Mesh Convergence Analysis 439 

To ensure the accuracy and stability of the numerical simulations, a mesh refinement study was conducted 440 

on the extracellular space geometry. Multiple unstructured tetrahedral meshes were generated with 441 

progressively smaller average element sizes. Key output metrics were evaluated at each refinement 442 

level. Convergence was assessed by comparing these metrics across mesh resolutions. The simulation 443 

results were shown mesh-independent when successive refinements produced changes below a predefined 444 

threshold. Final simulations were performed using the coarsest mesh that satisfied this convergence 445 

criterion to reduce computational cost while maintaining accuracy. 446

## Electric Field Power and Waveform Generation 457 

To characterize how electrophysiologically driven electric fields influence electroosmotic flow, we gener-458 

ated synthetic, time-varying electric field signals based on physiological LFP recordings. Experimental 459 

LFP data from rodent cortex during sleep and wake states were analyzed in the frequency domain using 460 

Fourier transforms to determine the typical spectral content and power distribution in each state. 461 

Rather than applying a single frequency or fixed waveform, we constructed a family of time-varying 462 

electric fields with varying combinations of amplitude and frequency. All generated signals were sinusoidal 463 

in shape but differed in these parameters to span a continuous range of field powers. , encapsulating 464 

contributions from both field amplitude and frequency when considered across a waveform family. 465 

These synthetic fields were applied as time-dependent inputs via the electroosmotic slip boundary 466 

condition. The resulting EO response, quantified by pressure gradients and flow rates, was then evaluated 467 

as a function of the driving field power. 468 

## ROM Development 469 

Microstructure resolved simulations, while mechanistically accurate, are computationally limited at tissue 470 

scales due to the complexity and resolution of the ECS geometry. A reduced order model addresses this 471 

by replacing the fully resolved geometry with a computationally efficient surrogate that captures the 472 

essential physics. Instead of resolving individual pore-scale features, a ROM operates on a homogenized 473 

continuum representation of the domain, parameterized by effective properties extracted from the full 474 

model. This makes ROM simulations orders of magnitude faster than their microstructure-resolved 475 

counterparts, enabling tissue-scale predictions that would otherwise be the. The key requirement for a 476 

valid ROM is that it reproduces the outputs of the high-fidelity model across the full range of physically 477 

relevant conditions, not just at a single operating point, ensuring the reduced representation captures 478 

the underlying physics. 479

transport coefficient computed from microstructure-resolved simulation data. 483
