Abstract 28 Idiopathic hypersomnia (IH) is a highly heritable sleep disorder characterized by 29 excessive daytime sleepiness, yet few genetic pathways contributing to 30 hypersomnolence have been identified. To expand genetic discovery beyond the limited 31 number of genome-wide significant loci associated with sleepiness-related traits, we 32 applied multi-trait clustering to sleep-associated genetic variation that did not reach 33 conventional significance thresholds. Integration with our cell-type-specific variant-to-34 gene mapping prioritized candidate effector genes for cross-species functional 35 screening. Among the strongest candidates was EGR2, which emerged as a distal 36 effector gene at the ADO–EGR2 locus in neurons. Neuronal knockdown of the 37 

Drosophila EGR2 ortholog stripe increased sleep duration and sleep consolidation, 38 while mutation of zebrafish egr2 orthologs similarly increased sleep. Together, these 39 findings demonstrate that sleep-regulatory pathways can be identified from genetic 40 signals below conventional significance thresholds and establish EGR2 as a conserved 41 regulator of sleep across species. 42

INTRODUCTION 43 Idiopathic hypersomnia (IH) is a chronic neurological disorder characterized by 44 excessive daytime sleepiness (EDS), prolonged sleep duration, and impaired arousal 45 despite apparently sufficient sleep opportunity ( 1). In contrast to disorders of sleep 46 fragmentation, such as insomnia or obstructive sleep apnea, IH is thought to reflect 47 dysregulation of sleep drive itself. Patients frequently report profound sleep inertia, 48 unrefreshing sleep, and cognitive impairment, highlighting a disruption in the processes 49 that govern the initiation, depth, and termination of sleep. The biological mechanisms 50 underlying IH remain poorly understood, and current treatments remain largely 51 symptomatic rather than based on mechanism ( 2, 3). 52 Genetic studies indicate that hypersomnolence is substantially heritable. Twin 53 studies estimate the heritability of daytime sleepiness traits at approximately 40% ( 4), 54 and a significant proportion of individuals with IH report affected first-degree relatives 55 (5), However, efforts to identify genetic contributors to IH have yielded relatively few 56 reproducible loci. The only genome-wide association study (GWAS) of IH published to 57 date ( 6) included just 414 IH cases and did not identify any variant reaching genome-58 wide significance. This apparent gap between heritability and discovery reflects not only 59 the paucity of large IH-focused cohorts but also phenotypic heterogeneity and the 60 polygenic architecture of sleep traits. In contrast, large-scale GWAS of hypersomnia-61 related phenotypes, including daytime sleepiness ( 7), sleep duration ( 8), and napping 62 (9), have revealed numerous associated loci. These observations suggest that genetic 63 influences relevant to hypersomnolence are distributed across partially overlapping 64 sleep-related traits rather than confined to a small set of disease-specific loci. 4hypersomnia-related phenotypes are difficult to capture using population-scale 66 approaches such as single-question survey responses or actigraphy, GWAS of these 67 traits are likely to remain underpowered. As a result, disease-relevant genetic signals 68 may often fall below conventional genome-wide significance thresholds. 69 A central challenge in IH research is not simply the identification of additional 70 loci, but the extraction of biologically meaningful signals relevant to pathological 71 sleepiness from a broad and pleiotropic genetic landscape. Variants associated with 72 sleep traits often influence multiple dimensions of sleep and circadian biology, 73 complicating efforts to isolate pathways specifically related to hypersomnia. One 74 approach to this problem is to identify groups of variants with shared patterns of 75 association across related sleep traits, allowing biologically meaningful signatures — 76 such as sleep fragmentation, circadian timing, or sleep propensity — to emerge from 77 complex trait datasets ( 7, 9). 78 Once such signals are identified, a second major challenge is linking the non-79 coding genetic variation emerging from GWAS studies to the candidate effector genes 80 they regulate. Nearest-gene annotation is frequently misleading, as regulatory variants 81 can act over long genomic distances through cell-type-specific chromatin interactions. 82 Integrative variant-to-gene (V2G) mapping frameworks that incorporate chromatin 83 accessibility and three-dimensional genome architecture provide an approach to 84 connect genetic signals to their target effector genes in a biologically relevant cellular 85 context. However, establishing the functional relevance of candidate genes ultimately 86 requires experimental validation in systems where sleep can be quantitatively measured 87 and genetically manipulated. Multi-trait clustering identifies a sleep propensity-associated variant cluster 113 To implicate genetic signals relevant to hypersomnolence beyond genome-wide 114 significant loci, we compiled sub-threshold variants ( P ≤ 5 × 10 ⁻) from large-scale 115 GWAS of daytime napping ( 9), daytime sleepiness ( 7), and sleep duration ( 8). Following 116 LD pruning, 4,254 independent variants were retained. For each variant, we extracted 117 association z-scores across 18 sleep phenotypes spanning self-reported traits, 118 accelerometer-derived measures, and obstructive sleep apnea (Table 1, 119 

Supplementary Table 1 ). We then applied Bayesian non-negative matrix factorization 120 (bNMF) to identify groups of variants with shared patterns of association across sleep 121 phenotypes. Across K=3-7, K=5 minimized the negative log-evidence (a Bayesian fit 122 criterion that penalizes added clusters ( 11 )) identifying it as the statistically optimal 123 solution. (Figure 1A,B; Supplementary Figure 1A ). DISCUSSION 289 Human genetic studies have demonstrated that sleepiness-related traits, including 290 daytime sleepiness, sleep duration, and daytime napping, are genetically correlated ( 7–291 

9). At the same time, emerging experimental efforts have shown that human sleep-292 associated loci can be translated into conserved sleep biology through variant-to-gene 293 mapping and cross-species functional validation ( 10 , 42 ). In the present study, we 294 integrate these complementary approaches by combining multi-trait clustering of sub-295 threshold sleep GWAS signals (to overcome the relative paucity of genome wide 296 significant signals) with cell type-specific regulatory mapping and experimental 297 validation across species. Using these epigenetically-informed methodologies, we 298 identify EGR2/stripe as a conserved regulator of sleep and demonstrate that sleep-299 regulatory pathways can be recovered from distributed genetic signals that fall below 300 conventional genome-wide significance thresholds. 301 Sleep traits are highly polygenic, suggesting that relevant pathways may be 302 distributed across many variants of modest effect rather than a small number of loci with 303 large effects. Individual associations below genome-wide significance are difficult to 304 interpret, as any single variant may represent a true but underpowered association or 305 statistical noise. By clustering sub-threshold variants according to their shared 306 relationships with multiple sleep phenotypes, we identified genetic signals more likely to 307

GWAS data sources and variant selection 391 Sub-threshold variants (P ≤ 5 × 10 ⁻) were extracted from three large-scale sleep 392 GWAS: daytime napping( 9) (UK Biobank, N = 452,633), daytime sleepiness( 7) (UK 393 Biobank, N = 452,071), and sleep duration( 8) (UK Biobank, N = 446,118). Five primary 394 traits were used as index phenotypes for variant extraction. All variants were mapped to 395 hg38/GRCh38 coordinates in the format CHR_POS_REF_ALT. Indels were excluded; 396 only biallelic SNPs were retained. ( Supplementary Table 1 )397 

Variant filtering and LD pruning 398 Variant filtering was performed using custom R scripts (choose_variants.R). Variants 399 were first clumped by genomic proximity using a 500 kb window (snp_clump() function). 400 LD pruning was then performed using LDlinkR SNPclip with the following parameters: r² 401 threshold = 0.05, MAF threshold = 0.001, reference population = EUR (1000 Genomes 402 Phase 3). After pruning, trait availability was assessed across the 18 UK Biobank sleep 403 phenotypes using count_traits_per_variant(). Variants with >60% missing traits were 404 flagged for proxy replacement. LD proxies were identified using TopLD API (primary) 405 with LDlinkR fallback (choose_proxies() function), selecting the proxy with maximum 406 trait coverage. Variant IDs were mapped to rsIDs using dbSNP build 155 VCF 407 (generate_varid_to_rsid_map_file.R). The final input matrix contained 4,254 408 independent variants ( Supplementary Table 2 ). 409 410
