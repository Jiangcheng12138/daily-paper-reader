Abstract 1

Background Ectothermic vertebrates exhibit substantial physiological plasticity in response to 2

environmental temperature fluctuations. Among them, amphibians show particularly pronounced 3

metabolic adjustments under cold conditions; however, the molecular mechanisms by which the liver 4

adapts to low temperatures remain poorly understood. To address this gap, we investigated the 5

hepatic transcriptional response to cold exposure in the African clawed frog ( Xenopus laevis ) using 6

RNA sequencing (RNA-seq) and quantitative PCR. 7

Results Exposure to 5°C for five days in X. laevis resulted in pronounced hyperglycemia and 8

extensive transcriptional reprogramming in the liver. RNA-seq analysis indicated that, relative to 9

frogs maintained at 24°C, 2,392 genes were upregulated, whereas 2,031 genes were downregulated. 10 

Notably, genes associated with gluconeogenesis, such as foxo1 , g6pc1 , and pck1 , exhibited 11 

significant upregulation, whereas genes related to glycolysis and glucose utilization, including gck ,12 

pfkm , and ldhb , were downregulated. Concurrently, cold exposure induced the expression of genes 13 

involved in fatty acid synthesis, desaturation, and cholesterol biosynthesis, such as srebf1 , acaca ,14 

fasn , scd , fads2 , srebf2 , and hmgcr . Conversely, genes related to fatty acid β-oxidation, including 15 

ppara , cpt1a , cpt1b , slc25a20 , acadl , hadha , and hadhb , were significantly suppressed. In alignment 16 

with this pattern, multiple components of the mitochondrial electron transport chain and ATP 17 

synthesis machinery were also downregulated. Additionally, gene groups involved in antioxidant 18 

defenses, such as gpx4 , gpx1 , prdx2 , prdx5 , prdx6 , and ferritin-related genes, were upregulated. 19 

Representative transcriptomic changes were validated using quantitative PCR analysis. 20 

Conclusions Exposure to cold temperatures induced coordinated metabolic reprogramming in the 21 

liver of X. laevis . This reprogramming was characterized by the activation of gluconeogenesis, 22 

suppression of glycolysis, fatty acid β-oxidation, and oxidative phosphorylation, and induction of 23 

lipid and cholesterol biosynthetic pathways. These metabolic adjustments suggest that frogs 24

Background 1

Ambient temperature is a critical environmental factor that influences the physiological 2

processes of ectothermic organisms, particularly in terms of metabolic regulation and cellular 3

homeostasis. Exposure to low temperatures imposes energetic constraints that necessitate the 4

reorganization of metabolic pathways to maintain homeostasis. Amphibians are valuable models for 5

investigating temperature-dependent physiological responses, such as cold tolerance and metabolic 6

adaptation. Freeze-tolerant species, such as Rana sylvatica , rapidly mobilize glucose from hepatic 7

glycogen at the onset of freezing to accumulate cryoprotectants [1, 2]. These species can endure 8

prolonged freezing periods through extensive metabolic reorganization and suppression of ATP-9

consuming processes during freezing [3–5]. These adaptations involve coordinated changes in 10 

carbohydrate metabolism, antioxidant defenses, and cellular stress responses, as demonstrated by 11 

physiological and transcriptomic analyses [3, 6]. Glucose metabolic responses to low temperature 12 

exposure have also been documented in amphibians, such as X. laevis [7]. Although this response 13 

may aid in maintaining energy homeostasis and cellular protection in cold environments, its 14 

molecular mechanisms remain unclear. Specifically, the transcriptional regulation that occurs in the 15 

liver during cold exposure and the regulation of glucose metabolism via gluconeogenesis and 16 

glycolysis are not fully understood. Recent transcriptomic analyses of the liver in freeze-tolerant 17 

amphibians have revealed differential expression of numerous cold-responsive genes [8]; however, 18 

the transcriptional responses in the livers of non-freeze-tolerant amphibians have not yet been fully 19 

elucidated. The liver, as a central metabolic organ, maintains systemic glucose homeostasis by 20 

regulating gluconeogenesis and glycolysis. Elucidating its transcriptional response is expected to 21 

provide key insights into the mechanisms underlying cold adaptation in amphibians. In addition to 22 

carbohydrate metabolism, lipid metabolism undergoes substantial changes in cold environments. 23 

Specifically, the synthesis of unsaturated fatty acids and alterations in fatty acid composition are 24

Adaptation to cold environments involves the regulation of membrane lipid composition and the 3

implementation of energy-saving strategies through metabolic suppression. In hibernating mammals, 4

including the Syrian hamster and 13-lined ground squirrel, marked reductions in metabolic rate and 5

body temperature during torpor are accompanied by the suppression of energy-intensive processes, 6

including transcription, translation, and protein synthesis [11, 12]. This enables survival during 7

prolonged cold exposure and extended periods of fasting [13, 14]. This metabolic reorganization 8

involves alterations in energy substrate utilization, modulation of mitochondrial function, and 9

coordinated changes in protein degradation and proteostasis regulation, all of which contribute to 10 

efficient energy conservation. Enhanced defense mechanisms against oxidative stress have been 11 

reported in hibernators and cold-tolerant animals. During hibernation and arousal, antioxidant 12 

defenses are upregulated, mitigating oxidative damage induced by reoxygenation and rewarming 13 

[15, 16]. Recent studies suggest that lipid peroxidation suppression mechanisms involving Gpx4 and 14 

ferritin contribute to cellular protection against cold stress and ferroptosis [3, 17]. Thus, cold 15 

adaptation is understood as an integrated strategy that includes not only the maintenance of 16 

membrane function through lipid remodeling but also the suppression of energy metabolism and the 17 

activation of cellular protection mechanisms. However, in non-hibernating amphibians, the changes 18 

in metabolic regulatory mechanisms and cellular protection mechanisms-related transcriptional 19 

programs in response to cold exposure remain largely unknown. Therefore, this study analyzed the 20 

hepatic transcriptome of X. laevis after exposure to 5 °C for 5 days using RNA -seq. The aim of this 21 

study was to elucidate the molecular basis of metabolic reprogramming in response to extreme 22 

thermal environments and advance the understanding of cold adaptation mechanisms in amphibians. 23

Methods 1

Animals and experimental design 2

In this study, adult African clawed frogs ( X. laevis ) were used as experimental subjects. Non-3

inbred wild-type frogs were used for the temporal analysis of serum parameters, whereas inbred 4

wild-type frogs (J-strain) [18] were selected for RNA-seq and quantitative PCR analysis. All 5

specimens were procured from the Amphibian Research Center at Hiroshima University (RRID: 6

SCR_019015) through the National BioResource Project (Japan). The frogs were maintained at 24°C 7

under a 12-hour light-dark cycle under standard housing conditions. For the cold exposure 8

experiment, frogs were individually housed in separate 1-liter water containers and transferred, 9

together with their housing water, to a 5°C incubator. In the time-course experiment, frogs were 10 

subjected to 5°C for up to 7 days. In the recovery experiment, frogs previously exposed to 5°C for 7 11 

days were returned to 24°C for 2 days prior to sampling. For RNA-seq and qPCR analyses, frogs 12 

were exposed to 5°C for 5 days. At designated time points, the animals were euthanized. Blood 13 

samples were collected by cardiac puncture using a syringe and stored at −80°C. The liver tissue was 14 

promptly excised, flash-frozen in liquid nitrogen, and stored at −80°C until further analysis. 15 

16

Corporation, Osaka, Japan) was used to measure triglyceride levels. To extract triglycerides from the 22 

liver, a previously reported protocol [19] was followed. The livers were homogenized in PBS, and a 23 

chloroform–methanol solution (2:1) was added to the homogenates. The samples were centrifuged at 24

RNA extraction and sequencing 13 

Total RNA was extracted from the liver tissue using TRIzol reagent (Thermo Fisher Scientific, 14 

USA) according to the manufacturer's protocol. The RNA samples were treated with DNase I to 15 

eliminate genomic DNA contamination. Prior to library preparation, RNA quality was assessed, with 16 

RIN values ranging from 7.9–8.9. 17 

RNA-seq libraries were constructed and sequenced by Novogene (Beijing, China) on an Illumina 18 

platform, producing 150 bp paired-end reads. A total of 278.3 million raw reads were obtained from 19 

the six libraries, with an average of 46.4 million reads per sample (range: 40.1–52.5 million). After 20 

filtering, 263.3 million clean reads remained, averaging 43.9 million reads per sample (range: 37.5– 21 

48.1 million). The Q20 and Q30 values exceeded 98% and 95%, respectively, indicating high-quality 22 

data. 23

RNA-seq data processing and differential expression analysis 1

Raw sequence reads were filtered to remove adapter sequences and low-quality reads. 2

Subsequently, high-quality reads were mapped to the X. laevis reference genome 3

(GCF_017654675.1, Xenopus_laevis_version 10.1) using HISAT2 (version 2.0.5). Transcript 4

assembly was performed using StringTie (version 1.3.3b), and gene expression levels were 5

quantified using featureCounts (version 1.5.0-p3). Differential expression analysis was performed 6

using DESeq2 (version 1.20.0). Genes with an adjusted P value (padj) < 0.05 and an absolute log₂ 7

fold change (|log₂FC|) ≥ 1 were defined as differentially expressed genes (DEGs) . Because X. laevis 8

is an allotetraploid species, differential expression analyses were conducted separately for annotated 9

L and S homoeologs. For readability, L/S suffixes were omitted in the main text unless distinction 10 

between homoeologs was relevant. Complete annotations are provided in Table S1. 11 

12 

Functional enrichment analysis 13 

Gene Ontology (GO) and Kyoto Encyclopedia of Genes and Genomes (KEGG) pathway 14 

enrichment analyses were executed utilizing clusterProfiler (version 3.8.1). Terms exhibiting padj < 15 

0.05 were considered significantly enriched. 16 

17 

Quantitative polymerase chain reaction (qPCR) 18 

Total RNA was reverse transcribed into complementary DNA (cDNA) using the PrimeScript™ 19 

RT reagent kit (with gDNA Eraser, Takara Bio, Japan). qPCR was performed using the 20 

THUNDERBIRD™ Next SYBR® qPCR Mix (TOYOBO, Osaka, Japan) on a CFX Connect Real-21 

Time PCR Detection System (Bio-Rad, Hercules, CA, USA). The PCR protocol included initial 22 

denaturation at 95°C for 60 s, followed by 39 cycles at 95°C for 10 s and 60°C for 30 s. Specific 23 

amplification was verified using melting curve analysis. Primer sequences are detailed in Table S2. 24

Statistical analysis 4

Data are presented as mean ± SEM. Statistical analyses were performed using one-way ANOVA 5

followed by Tukey’s multiple comparisons test for the time-course serum glucose data (Fig. 1). 6

Comparisons between the control (24°C) and cold-exposed (5°C for 5 days) groups in all other 7

experiments were performed using the exact two-sided Mann–Whitney U test. Differences were 8

considered statistically significant at P < 0.05. 9

Results 11 

Cold exposure induces reversible hyperglycemia in X. laevis 12 

To evaluate the physiological response to cold exposure, serum glucose concentrations were 13 

monitored over time in frogs maintained at 24°C and those exposed to 5°C. Serum glucose levels 14 

increased progressively during cold exposure and remained elevated throughout the exposure period, 15 

reaching significantly higher levels after 7 days compared to the control group (Fig. 1). Following 7 16 

days of exposure to 5°C, the frogs were returned to 24°C for 2 days, after which serum glucose 17 

concentrations decreased to near-baseline levels (Fig. 1). 18

Biochemical characterization of frogs following five days of cold exposure 15 

To clarify the physiological state linked to transcriptomic changes, serum and hepatic 16 

biochemical parameters were evaluated in frogs after five days of cold exposure (Table 1). Serum 17 

glucose concentrations were significantly higher in cold-exposed frogs compared to the control 18 

group. In contrast, lactate concentrations were notably reduced, whereas serum non-esterified fatty 19 

acid (NEFA) and triglyceride concentrations remained unchanged. The hepatic triglyceride content 20 

did not differ significantly between the groups. Furthermore, the TBARS levels in both serum and 21 

liver were comparable between the control and cold-exposed frogs (Table 1). 22

To elucidate the molecular mechanisms underlying cold-induced hyperglycemia, we analyzed the 2

genes implicated in FOXO1 signaling and hepatic glucose metabolism. FOXO1 is the principal 3

transcriptional regulator of gluconeogenic gene expression. RNA-seq demonstrated an upregulation 4

of foxo1 and its coactivator prmt1 in the livers of frogs exposed to cold conditions (Table 2 and Fig. 5

3), indicating the activation of FOXO1-associated transcriptional programs. Corresponding to the 6

enhanced gluconeogenic capacity, the expression levels of the gluconeogenic genes g6pc and pck1 7

were significantly elevated in cold-exposed frogs (Table 2 and Fig. 3). Conversely, the expression of 8

several genes involved in glucose utilization was reduced. The glycolytic genes gck and pfkm were 9

downregulated, suggesting suppression of glycolytic flux (Table 2 and Fig. 3). Additionally, the 10 

expression of ldhb , which preferentially catalyzes the conversion of lactate to pyruvate, decreased 11 

(Table 2 and Fig. 3). The expression of pc , which connects pyruvate metabolism to the tricarboxylic 12 

acid cycle and gluconeogenesis, was also downregulated (Table 2 and Fig. 3). The RNA-seq results 13 

were corroborated by qPCR analysis of selected genes, including foxo1 , g6pc1 , and pck1 , whereas 14 

prmt1 showed a similar trend that did not reach statistical significance ( P = 0.06) (Fig. 6a). 15 

Furthermore, the expression of gck , a critical enzyme involved in hepatic glucose utilization, was 16 

significantly reduced (Fig. 6a). 17

Discussion 13 

In this study, we examined the hepatic transcriptional response of X. laevis to cold exposure 14 

using RNA-seq and qPCR analyses. Cold exposure resulted in significant hyperglycemia and 15 

extensive transcriptional reprogramming in the liver. This reprogramming was characterized by the 16 

activation of the FOXO1-associated gluconeogenic pathway, induction of fatty acid and cholesterol 17 

biosynthetic pathways, suppression of fatty acid β-oxidation and oxidative phosphorylation, 18 

enhancement of antioxidant defense mechanisms, and downregulation of protein synthesis pathways. 19 

These findings indicate that the liver undergoes a coordinated metabolic reorganization to maintain 20 

energy homeostasis under cold stress. A prominent response to cold exposure was the activation of 21 

the FOXO1 signaling pathway and hepatic gluconeogenesis. The expression of foxo1 , g6pc1 , and 22 

pck1 was significantly upregulated, whereas that of gck was decreased. Although not statistically 23 

significant, prmt1 expression also tended to increase ( P = 0.06), further supporting activation of the 24

FOXO1-associated gluconeogenic pathway. These transcriptional changes were accompanied by a 1

marked increase in circulating blood glucose levels. The significant upregulation of the 2

gluconeogenic genes g6pc1 and pck1 , consistent with the upregulation of foxo1 expression, suggests 3

that the FOXO1-associated gluconeogenic program is activated in the liver under cold exposure. 4

Acute cold exposure induces profound metabolic remodeling in mammalian liver. FOXO1 is a key 5

transcriptional regulator of hepatic glucose production through the activation of gluconeogenic 6

genes, such as G6pc and Pck1 [20, 21]. Additionally, recent studies on freeze-tolerant wood frogs 7

have demonstrated the activation of FOXO3 signaling during freezing and thawing, suggesting a role 8

for FOXO-mediated regulation in coordinating antioxidant defenses and metabolic adaptation to 9

freezing stress [22]. Combined with the findings of the present study, this observation suggests that 10 

FOXO signaling activation may be a conserved adaptive response to cold stress in vertebrates. 11 

However, whether FOXO1 is directly involved in the transcriptional response observed in the livers 12 

of cold-exposed X. laevis remains to be elucidated. Further studies examining FOXO1 protein levels, 13 

phosphorylation status, and nuclear localization will be necessary to confirm its functional 14 

involvement. 15 

Following cold exposure, serum lactate levels decreased notably. Lactate, the primary end 16 

product of glycolysis, is a prevalent marker of glycolytic activity. Consequently, the reduction in 17 

circulating lactate, coupled with the observed downregulation of glycolysis-related genes such as 18 

gck , pfkm , and ldhb , indicates the suppression of glycolytic metabolism under cold conditions. A 19 

comparable decrease in lactate levels has been reported in hibernating ground squirrels during 20 

torpor, consistent with the profound metabolic suppression characteristic of hibernation[23]. 21 

Reduced glycolytic activity and limited lactate accumulation during torpor have been reported in 22 

hibernating ground squirrels, reflecting a shift away from glucose catabolism and contributing to 23 

metabolic suppression [24]. Conversely, in freeze-tolerant frogs, glycolytic activity and lactate 24

hadhb , and acaa2 indicates a reduction in mitochondrial fatty acid utilization under cold exposure. 23

Conclusions 15 

In summary, cold exposure induced significant transcriptional reprogramming in the liver of X. 16 

laevis , accompanied by pronounced hyperglycemia and coordinated alterations across various 17 

metabolic pathways. The responses include activation of the FOXO1-associated gluconeogenic 18 

program and suppression of glycolysis, fatty acid β-oxidation, oxidative phosphorylation, and 19 

protein synthesis. These findings support a model in which cold exposure initiates dual metabolic 20 

reprogramming in the liver. Activation of gluconeogenesis aids in maintaining circulating glucose 21 

levels, whereas suppression of energy-consuming processes establishes an energy-sparing program 22 

that reduces metabolic demand. Concurrently, the induction of lipid and cholesterol biosynthetic 23 

pathways likely facilitates lipid remodeling, aiding cellular adaptation to low temperatures. 24
