Abstract 26 

Sleep disruption is an early and prevalent feature of neurodegenerative disease, 27 

commonly attributed to neuronal circuit dysfunction or cell loss. However, sleep is tightly 28 

coupled to metabolic state, raising the possibility that systemic metabolic abnormalities 29 

contribute to disease-associated sleep phenotypes. Using Drosophila models of TDP-43 30 

proteinopathy, we investigated whether peripheral metabolic dysfunction plays a causal 31 

role in sleep disruption. We show that TDP-43 expression induces a chronic, starvation-32 

like metabolic state characterized by depletion of peripheral carbohydrate stores despite 33 

normal feeding. Restoration of sleep fails to correct these metabolic defects, whereas 34 

improving peripheral metabolic state robustly rescues sleep. A modifier screen of ~650 35 

RNAi lines identified Salt-inducible kinase 3 (SIK3) as a potent suppressor of both sleep 36 

loss and starvation sensitivity. Transcriptomic and spatial metabolomic analyses reveal 37 

that SIK3 selectively remodels a peripheral metabolic program centered on the pentose 38 

phosphate pathway and redox-associated metabolites without globally restoring energy 39 

stores. Together, these findings identify systemic metabolic dysfunction as a key driver 40 

of sleep disruption in TDP-43 proteinopathy and highlight peripheral metabolism as a 41 

potential therapeutic entry point for sleep dysfunction in neurodegenerative disease. 42

Introduction 47 

Sleep disruption is a prominent and often early feature of neurodegenerative disease, 48 

including amyotrophic lateral sclerosis (ALS) and frontotemporal dementia (FTD) 1–4 .49 

Alterations in sleep duration and continuity are frequently observed prior to advanced 50 

cognitive or motor decline, suggesting that sleep dysfunction may reflect early disease 51 

biology rather than be a consequence of later neuronal loss 1. At the same time, sleep 52 

plays a critical role in maintaining neuronal homeostasis, metabolic balance, and stress 53 

resilience 5–8 , raising the possibility that sleep disruption may not only mark disease 54 

onset but also contribute to disease progression. 55 

In parallel with sleep disturbances, metabolic dysfunction is increasingly 56 

recognized as a core feature of ALS and FTD 9–13 . Patients exhibit alterations in energy 57 

balance, including hypermetabolism, weight loss, altered glucose utilization, and 58 

dyslipidemia; metabolic state can predict disease trajectory and survival. Importantly, 59 

these metabolic abnormalities are not restricted to the central nervous system: 60 

peripheral tissues show altered carbohydrate and lipid metabolism, and systemic 61 

metabolic stress can precede or exacerbate neurological decline 11,12 . Despite this, most 62 

mechanistic studies of ALS/FTD have focused on neuronal vulnerability and circuit 63 

dysfunction, leaving unresolved whether systemic metabolic abnormalities actively 64 

influence behavioral phenotypes such as sleep. This gap is notable given that TDP-43, 65 

while prominently studied in neurons, is expressed across cell types and tissues and 66 

that pathological TDP-43 abnormalities have been documented in peripheral tissues in 67 

disease 14,15 . Consistent with this possibility, systemic metabolic state is tightly coupled 68 

to sleep regulation across species 16–21 .69

TDP-43 proteinopathy is a defining pathological feature of ALS, a large fraction of 70 

FTD, and other neurodegenerative diseases including Alzheimer’s 14,22–25 . TDP-43 is a 71 

multifunctional RNA-binding protein involved in transcription, splicing, RNA transport, 72 

and stress responses; in disease, TDP-43 becomes mislocalized, aggregated, and post-73 

translationally modified, with profound consequences for cellular function 14,24 .74 

Expression of human TDP-43 in Drosophila is toxic and recapitulates key ALS/FTD 75 

disease features, including neuronal degeneration and shortened lifespan 26–29 . In prior 76 

work, we showed that adult-onset expression of human TDP-43 in flies is associated 77 

with severe sleep loss and fragmentation, as well as brain metabolic dysregulation 30 .78 

Ataxin-2, a genetic suppressor of TDP-43 neurodegeneration, restored sleep and 79 

normalized brain glycogen levels 30 . These findings link the TDP-43-associated sleep 80 

disruption to an altered metabolic state in the nervous system. However, whether TDP-81 

43–induced sleep disturbances arise from brain-intrinsic metabolic dysfunction or 82 

whether systemic metabolic abnormalities play a causal role is not known. 83 

Here, we find that human TDP-43 expression in Drosophila induces a systemic, 84 

starvation-like metabolic state characterized by depletion of peripheral carbohydrate 85 

stores, despite normal feeding behavior. We further demonstrate that restoring sleep 86 

fails to correct these metabolic defects, whereas improving peripheral metabolic state 87 

through dietary or genetic means can robustly rescue sleep. Through an RNAi-based 88 

enhancer/suppressor screen of the sleep phenotype, we identify Salt-inducible kinase 3 89 

(SIK3) as a novel modifier of TDP-43. Behavioral analyses, transcriptomic profiling, and 90 

spatial metabolomics demonstrate that Sik3 knockdown suppresses both sleep loss and 91 

starvation sensitivity in TDP-43 flies without globally restoring energy stores. Instead, 92

Results 99 

TDP-43 expression induces peripheral metabolic dysfunction 100 

Sleep disruption is often accompanied by metabolic abnormalities, and metabolic stress 101 

can alter sleep behavior 31 . Our prior work focused on metabolic changes within the 102 

brain 30 , but TDP-43–expressing flies are also unusually sensitive to starvation 32 ,103 

indicating that systemic energy homeostasis may be compromised. We therefore 104 

determined whether TDP-43 proteinopathy induces peripheral metabolic defects. To 105 

assess global metabolic resilience, we examined survival under nutrient deprivation. 106 

Using the hormone-inducible GeneSwitch driver under control of the ubiquitous 107 

daughterless promoter ( Da GS) to achieve broad, adult-restricted expression of UAS-108 

TDP-43 52S , we confirmed that TDP-43 flies exhibited markedly reduced starvation 109 

resistance compared to controls (Fig. 1A). This effect raised the possibility of impaired 110 

energy storage or utilization. We next directly quantified key metabolites in the bodies of 111 

animals expressing TDP-43. In contrast to unchanged glucose and an increase in 112 

glycogen found in brains of these animals 30 , whole-body assays revealed dramatic 113 

reductions in glucose and glycogen in TDP-43 flies (Fig. 1B), indicating depletion of 114 

both carbohydrate stores. To determine whether reduced energy stores reflected altered 115

To assess whole-animal substrate utilization, we performed indirect calorimetry to 121

provides an index of fuel preference, with values closer to 1 indicating carbohydrate 124 

oxidation and lower values reflecting increased reliance on lipid or protein substrates 35 .125 

TDP-43 flies exhibited a consistently lower RQ than controls across the recording 126 

period, indicating reduced reliance on carbohydrate metabolism and increased use of 127 

alternative energy sources (Fig. 1C). This shift is consistent with the observed depletion 128 

of peripheral carbohydrate stores and supports the idea that TDP-43 flies exist in a 129 

chronic starvation-like metabolic state despite continued access to food. 130 

We next compared transcriptomic profiles of TDP-43 expression in brains and 131 

peripheral tissues (bodies) to assess whether molecular consequences differed 132

expressed in bodies and >3,000 in brains relative to controls, including a shared subset 135 

of 1821 transcripts altered in both contexts (Fig. 2B). Thus, TDP-43 elicits both common 136 

and tissue-specific transcriptional responses. To determine whether particular biological 137

Inducing sleep does not normalize peripheral metabolic defects in TDP-43 flies 151 

Sleep loss itself can disrupt metabolic homeostasis 31 , raising the possibility that 152 

peripheral metabolic abnormalities in TDP-43 flies are downstream of the sleep 153 

disruption. To address this directionality, we asked whether pharmacologically restoring 154 

sleep in TDP-43 flies was sufficient to normalize peripheral metabolism. We induced 155 

sleep in adult flies using gaboxadol, a GABAA receptor agonist that robustly increases 156 

sleep 36 . Gaboxadol treatment significantly increased total sleep time in TDP-43 flies and 157 

improved sleep consolidation (Fig. 3A; Fig S3). 158 

Despite effective sleep induction, peripheral metabolic defects persisted. Whole-159 

body glucose and glycogen levels remained significantly reduced in TDP-43 flies treated 160 

with gaboxadol (Fig. 3B). Moreover, gaboxadol-treated TDP-43 flies remained highly 161

Dietary glucose rescues sleep and peripheral metabolic defects in TDP-43 flies 168 

We next asked whether improving metabolic state in TDP-43 flies could restore sleep. 169 

To address this, we switched adult flies to a high-glucose diet and examined sleep and 170 

metabolic outcomes. Dietary glucose supplementation significantly improved sleep in 171 

TDP-43 flies, increasing total sleep time and restoring sleep architecture (Fig. 4A; Fig. 172 

S4). Although control flies also exhibited trends towards increased sleep duration on 173 

high glucose, the magnitude of rescue in TDP-43 flies was substantially greater, 174 

consistent with a preferential benefit to these animals which are under metabolic stress. 175 

Moreover, high-glucose feeding restored peripheral glucose levels in TDP-43 flies (Fig. 176 

4B) and rescued starvation sensitivity (Fig. 4C), demonstrating functional metabolic 177 

rescue. Thus, restoring peripheral glucose availability is sufficient to rescue sleep 178 

deficits in TDP-43 flies, linking metabolic state to sleep regulation. 179

TDP-43 sleep impairment is associated with peripheral PPP/redox dysfunction that is 212 

modified by Sik3 knockdown 213 

As noted, TDP-43 proteinopathy produces a systemic starvation-like state characterized 214 

by reduced peripheral energy stores and impaired metabolic physiology, and dietary 215 

glucose can improve both sleep and starvation resistance. To identify metabolic 216 

processes most strongly altered by TDP-43 and corrected by Sik3 knockdown, we 217 

performed transcriptomic profiling of fly bodies and brains across genotypes (control, 218 

TDP-43, Sik3 RNAi, and TDP-43 + Sik3 RNAi; Fig. 6A). Principal component analysis 219 

highlighted tissue-specific differences. In bodies, TDP-43 expression produced a 220 

pronounced shift along PC1 relative to controls, reflecting widespread transcriptional 221 

remodeling (Fig. 6B). Notably, TDP-43 + Sik3 RNAi samples remained separated from 222 

controls along this axis, and thus were not reverted to a normal state, while Sik3 RNAi-223 

only samples clustered near controls (Fig. 6B). In brains, separation was less 224 

pronounced and Sik3 knockdown did not substantially alter the brain transcriptome (Fig. 225 

6C). These patterns indicate that Sik3 does not globally normalize TDP-43–induced 226 

transcriptional changes in bodies, but instead induces a distinct peripheral 227 

transcriptional state with TDP-43. 228

of TDP-43–dependent transcriptional changes in bodies. Of 3,617 genes downregulated 230

Discussion 297 

Sleep disruption in neurodegenerative disease is widely attributed to dysfunction within 298 

the brain 1,44 , yet the contribution of broader, non-neural physiological processes to 299

disease-associated sleep phenotypes remains poorly defined. Here, we demonstrate 300 

that TDP-43 expression induces a chronic starvation-like state characterized by 301 

depletion of peripheral carbohydrate stores despite unchanged feeding, and that this 302 

metabolic state is sufficient to drive profound sleep loss. Restoring sleep alone fails to 303 

normalize peripheral metabolism, whereas improving metabolic state though dietary 304 

glucose availability rescues sleep, arguing that sleep disruption in this context reflects a 305 

downstream consequence of systemic metabolic imbalance. These findings support the 306 

idea that sleep phenotypes in neurodegenerative disease do not arise purely from 307 

dysfunction in the nervous system and that peripheral metabolic state plays a causal 308 

role. 309 

A growing body of work across species indicates that TDP-43 disrupts cellular 310 

metabolism 10,45,46 , although the emphasis has largely been on neuronal or CNS-intrinsic 311 

effects. In Drosophila and rodent models, TDP-43 has been linked to altered glucose 312 

utilization, mitochondrial dysfunction, oxidative stress, and impaired energy balance; in 313 

humans with ALS and FTD, systemic metabolic abnormalities—including 314 

hypermetabolism, weight loss, and altered lipid profiles—predict disease 315 

trajectory 9,10,45,46 . Emerging studies have implicated redox and pentose phosphate 316 

pathway–related changes in models of ALS and other neurodegenerative disorders, 317 

consistent with a role for NADPH-dependent buffering and biosynthetic pathways in 318 

disease vulnerability 10,47,48 . However, most prior work has not addressed whether such 319 

metabolic alterations are functionally linked to behavioral perturbations such as sleep, 320 

or whether peripheral metabolic dysfunction is sufficient to drive sleep disruption 321 

independent of overt neuronal injury. Our results demonstrate that peripheral metabolic 322

SIK3 has emerged as a conserved regulator of sleep across species, yet its 325 

functional role appears context-dependent 19,40 . In mammals, gain-of-function mutations 326 

in SIK3 increase sleep, while loss-of-function reduces sleep, consistent with a role in 327 

baseline sleep homeostasis 40 . In contrast, studies in Drosophila 49 and C. elegans 19 

328 

suggest a more nuanced function, with SIK signaling linked to metabolic state and 329 

stress-responsive sleep-like behaviors rather than constitutive control of sleep amount. 330 

In our study, Sik3 knockdown alone does not produce a strong baseline sleep 331 

phenotype, yet robustly suppresses sleep loss in the context of TDP-43 expression. The 332 

nature of this metabolic stress signal may be informative: prior work in Drosophila has 333 

shown that SIK3 regulates the NADPH/NADP ⁺ redox balance in the context of sugar 334 

tolerance 38 , and our metabolomic data show that TDP-43 expression elevates NADPH 335 

and 6-phosphogluconate while Sik3 knockdown normalizes these PPP-associated 336 

metabolites. This raises the possibility that SIK3 senses or responds to a redox or PPP-337 

linked metabolic signal specifically, rather than to bulk energy depletion, and that it is 338 

this axis — rather than glucose or glycogen availability — that is transduced into sleep 339 

regulatory output. 340 

The convergence of SIK3, a canonical sleep gene, with TDP-43, a defining 341 

proteinopathy in neurodegenerative disease, brings together sleep regulation and 342 

disease biology at a genetic level. Although prior work has largely focused on neuronal 343 

roles for SIK3 50 , its ability to suppress both sleep loss and starvation sensitivity in TDP-344 

43 flies highlights a role in coordinating systemic metabolic programs relevant to sleep. 345

and redox-related metabolism. The dissociation between persistent suppression of 349

suggests that metabolic re-routing, rather than restoration of energy stores per se ,351 

underlies sleep rescue. 352 

How is the peripheral metabolic state communicated to sleep-regulatory 353 

processes? One possibility is that chronic peripheral energy stress alters circulating 354 

metabolic signals or hormonal cues that directly influence sleep circuit neuronal 355 

excitability and arousal systems. Alternatively, peripheral redox imbalance may impose 356 

a systemic stress state that secondarily impairs brain metabolic resilience, which has 357 

already been implicated in sleep control. These models are not mutually exclusive, and 358 

it is possible that peripheral and central metabolic changes are not tightly coupled at the 359 

molecular level. Our findings support the idea that sleep disruption can arise from 360 

distinct metabolic perturbations — neuronal or peripheral — that converge on shared 361 

behavioral outputs. Such peripheral-driven sleep disruption may then feed forward to 362 

negatively impact brain health, consistent with evidence that chronic sleep loss 363 

exacerbates proteostatic stress, impairs metabolic homeostasis, and accelerates 364 

neurodegenerative processes 31,51,52 .365 

These findings raise additional questions to be addressed, including identifying 366 

signals that couple peripheral metabolic state to sleep-regulatory mechanisms and 367 

determining how peripheral metabolic interventions influence long-term 368
