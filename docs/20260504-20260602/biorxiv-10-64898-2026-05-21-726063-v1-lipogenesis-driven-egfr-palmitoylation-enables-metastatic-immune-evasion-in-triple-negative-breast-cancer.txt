Abstract. 36 

37 

Metastasis is the leading cause of death in triple-negative breast cancer (TNBC), yet how 38 

tumor cells evade immune recognition during dissemination remain unclear. Here, we 39 

show that de novo lipogenesis drives metastatic immune evasion through FASN-40 

mediated palmitoylation of EGFR. This modification establishes a lipid-dependent 41 

membrane signaling scaffold that sustains PI3K-AKT-mTOR signaling independently of 42 

MAPK compensation, thereby suppressing MHC-I antigen presentation through post-43 

translational regulation. As a result, metastatic-competent cells escape CD8+ T cell 44 

surveillance and colonize distant organs. Genetic or pharmacologic inhibition of fatty acid 45 

synthase (FASN), or expression of palmitoylation-deficient EGFR mutants restores MHC-46 

I surface expression, unleashes robust CD8 ⁺ T cell activation, and markedly impairs lung 47 

metastasis without affecting primary tumor growth. This lipid-dependent EGFR-PI3K-48 

mTOR axis operates independently of MAPK signaling and is not rescued by exogenous 49 

lipids, revealing a non-redundant metabolic dependency. Our results identify FASN-50 

driven EGFR palmitoylation as a tumor-intrinsic immunometabolic checkpoint that 51 

couples lipid metabolism to antigen-presentation and metastatic competence. Targeting 52 

this pathway with clinically advancing FASN inhibitors offers a promising strategy to 53 

suppress metastasis by restoring anti-tumor immunity in TNBC. 54

Introduction. 57 

58 

Triple-negative breast cancer (TNBC) is an aggressive disease marked by rapid metastatic 59 

spread, and poor responses to immunotherapy 1-3 . Although metastatic dissemination is the 60 

leading cause of death, the tumor-intrinsic mechanisms that allow cancer cells to evade immune 61 

recognition during metastatic progression remain poorly understood. Emerging evidence 62 

implicates de novo lipogenesis in tumor progression and immune regulation. Fatty acid synthase 63 

(FASN), the rate-limiting enzyme in de novo fatty acid synthesis, is frequently upregulated in 64 

aggressive cancers and correlates with poor patient outcomes 4. Beyond supporting membrane 65 

biogenesis, FASN produces palmitate that can modify signaling proteins via palmitoylation. 66 

These observations suggests that de novo lipogenesis sustains immune-evasive signaling states 67 

during metastatic progression. However, whether lipogenesis directly regulates antigen 68 

presentation during metastasis remains unknown. 69

Lipogenic signaling drives metastatic progression in TNBC. 77 

78 

TNBC cells rely heavily on de novo lipogenesis to support proliferation, migration, and survival 5.79 

However, the mechanisms linking this metabolic program to metastatic progression and immune 80 

evasion have remained unclear. 81 

82 

To directly address this, we used a SOX2/OCT4-GFP promoter reporter system 6 that 83 

prospectively identifies a highly metastatic subpopulation in TNBC. In both human MDA-MB-231 84 

cells and murine E0771-ML1 TNBC models 7-9 , these GFP + cells express high levels of stemness 85 

transcription factors (SOX2, OCT4 and NANOG), show increased chromosomal instability 86 

(CIN) 10 , and exhibit markedly superior metastatic capacity in vivo. 87 

88 

RNA-seq analysis of sorted OCT4/SOX2-GFP + metastatic cells from orthotopic primary tumors 89 

revealed selective enrichment of lipogenic and anabolic metabolic pathways, including fatty acid 90 

metabolism ( Fig. 1a and Extended Data Fig. 1a ). qPCR validation confirmed upregulation of key 91 

de novo lipogenesis genes, including ATP citrate lyase (ACLY), acetyl-CoA carboxylase 1 (ACC1 92 

encoded by ACACA), and as fatty acid synthase (FASN) ( Fig. 1b ). 93 

94 

To establish clinical relevance, we analyzed single-cell RNA-seq datasets from TNBC patients 11 .95 

Fatty acid metabolism signatures were significantly enriched in malignant epithelial cells with the 96 

highest expression of lipogenic genes observed in subpopulations expressing stemness markers 97 

(Extended Data Fig. 1c-d ). These data indicate that a distinct lipogenic program is associated 98 

with metastatic cell states in both experimental models and human TNBC. 99 

100 

To define the role of de novo lipid synthesis in metastatic progression, we focused on FASN, the 101 

rate limiting enzyme in palmitate synthesis. FASN is currently being evaluated as a therapeutic 102 

target in advanced cancers, including brain metastasis (NCT03179904). We used CRISPR to 103 

generate FASN knockout (FASN KO ) in both murine E0771 and human MDA-MB-231-LM2 TNBC 104

To understand how FASN deficiency reduces metastatic burden, we examined the primary 116 

tumors. As expected, FASN KO tumors showed clear FASN deficiency ( Extended data Fig. 3j )117

to controls ( Fig.1h and Extended data Fig. 3i ). These results indicate that de novo lipid synthesis 119 

supports the maintenance of metastatic cell states. To test whether this defect could be rescued 120 

by increasing the starting number of metastatic cells, we orthotopically injected mixtures of 121 

enriched GFP + cells (at a 2:1 GFP ⁺ to GFP ⁻ ratio). However, enriching for GFP ⁺ cells failed to 122 

rescue metastatic growth in FASN KO tumors ( Extended Data Fig. 3k-l ). This confirms that 123 

lipogenesis is required for maintenance and progression of metastatic-competent cells. 124 

125 

Pharmacologic inhibition of FASN produced similar results. We tested two inhibitors: TVB3166 (a 126 

preclinical analog), and TVB2640 (a clinically advanced compound currently in phase I clinical 127 

trials 4). Both the inhibitors reduced FASN protein levels in a dose-dependent manner ( Extended 128 

Data Fig. 4a ) without significantly affecting in vitro cell proliferation ( Extended Data Fig. 4b-c ). 129 

In vivo, we began FASN inhibitor treatment one week after tumor implantation. The treatment did 130 

not impair primary tumor growth but markedly suppressed lung metastatic burden and reduced 131 

the population of metastatic cells ( Fig. 1i and Extended Data Fig. 4d-e ) ( Fig. 1j and Extended 132 

Data Fig. 4f-h ), and TVB-3166 was well tolerated, with no significant changes in mouse body 133 

weight and no detectable organ toxicity ( Extended Data Fig. 4i-l ). 134

Lipogenic signaling sustains metastatic cell states within primary tumors. 141 

142 

To elucidate how de novo lipogenesis supports metastatic competence, we systematically profiled 143 

metabolic dependencies and the downstream signaling pathways across major stages of the 144 

metastatic cascade. 145 

146 

Lipidomic analysis revealed that FASN-competent (FASN WT ) tumors were highly enriched in free 147 

fatty acids, especially in palmitic, stearic, and oleic acids ( Fig. 2a and Extended Data Fig. 5a-148 

d). Pharmacologic inhibition FASN with TVB3166 produced similar a metabolic shift ( Fig. 2c ). 149 

Importantly, sorted OCT4/SOX2-GFP ⁺ metastatic cells from FASN WT tumors displayed markedly 150 

higher levels of free fatty acids than GFP - non-metastatic cells. This difference disappeared in 151 

FASN-deficient tumors, where GFP + and GFP - cells showed comparable fatty acid levels ( Fig. 152 

2b and Extended Data Fig. 5e-f ). In contrast, upstream TCA cycle intermediates remained 153 

unchanged ( Extended Data Fig. 5g ). These results establish de novo lipogenesis as a defining 154 

metabolic feature of metastatic-competent cells within orthotopic primary tumors. 155 

156 

We next performed transcriptomic profiling to uncover how suppression of lipogenic signaling 157 

reprograms pathways linked to metastatic progression. FASN-deficient tumors showed 158 

coordinated downregulation of gene sets involved in lipid elongation, mitochondrial 159 

bioenergetics, ATP synthesis, OXPHOS, respiratory chain assembly, and hypoxia response 160 

(Fig. 2d left panel ). At the same time, immune-related gene signatures were upregulated, 161 

including pathways for antigen presentation, T cell activation, and interferon responses ( Fig. 2d 162 

right panel ). Expression of canonical lipogenic regulators, including SREBP1, SREBP2, and the 163 

fatty acid transporter CD36 remained largely unchanged in both transcript and protein levels 164 

(Extended Data Fig. 5i-j ). 165 

166 

Gene set enrichment analysis (GSEA) showed elevated mTORC1 signaling in FASN WT tumors 167 

(Extended Data Fig. 5h ). This increase was particularly pronounced in OCT4/SOX2-GFP ⁺168 

metastatic cells and was selectively attenuated upon FASN deletion in this subpopulation, while 169 

remaining largely unaffected in GFP ⁻ cells ( Fig. 2e ). Notably, FASN deficiency did not increase 170 

MAPK signaling ( Extended Data Fig. 5k ). 171

Together, these findings indicate that de novo lipid synthesis supports a broad metabolic and 173 

signaling program in metastatic TNBC cells. This program includes enhanced mitochondrial 174 

fitness, PI3K-AKT-mTORC1 activity, and immune regulation. It does not rather solely on 175 

canonical lipogenic transcriptional programs. Importantly, this lipogenesis-dependent PI3K-176

Lipogenic signaling suppresses cell surface MHC-I expression through post-. 180 

translational regulation. 181 

182 

Given the upregulation of immune-related gene signatures upon FASN suppression, we next 183 

asked whether de novo lipogenesis reshapes the tumor immune microenvironment to promote 184 

immune evasion. Metastatic-competent cells, which depend most heavily on lipogenesis and 185 

mTORC1 signaling, are especially well positioned to use this pathway during dissemination. 186 

187 

Flow cytometric profiling of CD45 + immune cells from orthotopic tumors showed no significant 188 

differences in overall CD8 + or CD4 + T cell abundance between within FASN WT and FASN KO 189 

tumors ( Extended Data Fig. 6a-b ). However, FASN-deficiency enhanced CD8+ T cell effector 190 

phenotypes, as evidenced by increased frequencies of CD44 ⁺CD62- effector-like CD8+ T cells 191 

that expressed IFN g, TNF a, granzyme B, along with increased PD-1 expression ( Fig. 3a-d ). 192 

Concurrently, we observed a significant reduction in polymorphonuclear myeloid-derived 193 

suppressor cells (PMN-MDSCs) ( Fig. 3e) . Other populations, including CD4 ⁺ regulatory T cells, 194 

total macrophages, dendritic cells (cDC1 and cDC2), and most NK cells, remained largely 195 

unchanged ( Extended Data Fig. 6c-j ). Tumor-resident NK cells showed a reduction in the 196 

terminally differentiated CD27 ⁻CD11b ⁺ subset ( Extended Data Fig.6k-l ), consistent with a shift 197 

towards a more immunostimulatory tumor microenvironment. Pharmacologic FASN inhibition 198 

with TVB3166 induced similar immune changes, including more activated effector CD8 ⁺ T cells, 199 

and higher PD-1 expression ( Fig. 3f-h ). 200 

201 

These immune changes suggested that lipogenic signaling suppresses tumor cell antigen 202 

presentation during metastatic progression. Transcriptomic analysis revealed no significant 203 

changes in MHC-I antigen presentation gene signatures 12,13 upon FASN loss ( Fig. 3i ). In 204 

contrast, proteomic analysis revealed increased abundance of MHC-I components and the 205

Restoration of MHC-I expression following suppression of lipogenic signaling enhances 219 

CD8 ⁺ T cell-mediated anti-tumor immunity. 220 

221 

Since inhibition of lipogenic signaling restores MHC-I surface expression, we next tested whether 222 

this change improves tumor cell recognition by cytotoxic T cells. FASN-deficient E0771 cells 223 

showed increased presentation of the OVA peptide SIINFEKL)/H-2K ᵇ complex ( Extended Data 224 

Fig. 7c ) and triggered significantly stronger CD8 + T cell activation in co-culture assays ( Fig. 3o ). 225 

These results demonstrate that lipogenesis-dependent suppression of MHC-I directly limits tumor 226 

antigen presentation and restricts CD8 ⁺ T cell activation. 227 

228 

To establish causality in vivo, we generated a doxycycline-inducible conditional B2M knockdown 229 

system ( Extended Data Fig. 8a-b ). Knocking down B2M to reduce MHC-I levels largely rescued 230 

metastatic outgrowth and reversed CD8 ⁺ T cell activation caused by FASN deficiency ( Fig. 3p-q 231 

and Extended Data Fig. 8c-g ). In addition, FASN loss markedly reduced the number of 232 

circulating tumor cells (CTCs) derived from metastatic-competent populations, and this reduction 233 

was rescued by MHC-I depletion ( Fig. 3r ). 234 

235 

Together, these data establish lipogenic signaling as a tumor-intrinsic immunometabolic 236 

checkpoint. It suppresses MHC-I antigen presentation through post-translational mechanisms, 237 

restrains CD8 ⁺ T cell-mediated immune surveillance, and promotes metastatic dissemination in 238 

TNBC. 239

EGFR palmitoylation sustains a lipid-dependent membrane signaling state to PI3K-AKT-242 

mTOR activation and MHC-I suppression. 243 

244 

Previous studies have shown that MHC class I surface stability is often regulated by lysosomal 245 

degradation, ubiquitination, endosomal recycling, and altered membrane trafficking 13,14 .246 

However, RNA-seq analysis of sorted metastatic cells from FASN-deficient orthotopic tumors 247 

revealed no significant changes in gene signatures related to these pathways ( Fig. 3i and 248 

Extended Data Fig. 7d-e ). F-actin levels which can influence MHC-I membrane stability 15 , were 249 

also unchanged ( Extended Data Fig. 7f ). These findings suggested that lipogenic signaling 250 

regulates MHC-I through a distinct post-translational mechanism. 251 

252 

We previously observed that metastatic cells with high lipogenic activity display elevated PI3K-253 

AKT-mTOR signaling/mTORC1 signaling ( Extended Date Fig. 1a and Fig. 1b ). Given that 254 

EGFR is a key upstream regulator of this pathway, and can undergo S-palmitoylation, we 255 

hypothesized that FASN-derived palmitate promotes EGFR palmitoylation to sustain pro-256 

metastatic signaling in TNBC. Prior work in other cancers has shown that palmitate can drive 257 

EGFR palmitoylation 16 , which influences membrane organization and signaling durability 17 .258 

259 

To test this, we performed an acyl-biotin exchange (ABE) assay 18 to measure S-palmitoylation 260 

of EGFR. Palmitoylated EGFR was abundant in FASN WT cells but markedly reduced in FASN KO 

261 

cells ( Extended Data Fig. 9a ). This decrease in EGFR palmitoylation was accompanied by 262 

reduced downstream PI3K-AKT-mTOR signaling, whereas MAPK signaling remained 263 

unchanged ( Fig. 2f ). Prior work has shown that palmitoylated EGFR preferentially interacts with 264 

the PI3K regulatory subunit PIK3R1 (p85), enhancing PI3K recruitment and activation 19 . These 265 

results suggest that MAPK does not compensate for the loss of palmitoylated EGFR in TNBC. 266 

Together, these findings support a model in which lipogenesis-dependent EGFR palmitoylation 267 

organizes a membrane-associated signaling scaffold that stabilizes PI3K-mTOR activation 268 

during metastatic progression. 269 

270

mediated by the palmitoyltransferases ZDHHC20 and ZDHHC13 19,20 . To directly assess the 272 

functional importance these sites, we generated EGFR knockout cells and reconstituted them 273

To test whether exogenous lipids could bypass the need for de novo lipogenesis, we cultured 310 

FASN-deficient cells in lipid-rich conditions. Notably, FASN KO cells failed to restore intracellular 311

Discussion 320 

321 

Despite advances in immune checkpoint blockade, TNBC remains largely refractory to 322 

immunotherapy, in part due to tumor-intrinsic suppression of MHC-I antigen presentation 23 . Here, 323 

we identify a previously unrecognized lipogenesis-dependent immunometabolic program that 324 

drives metastatic immune escape in TNBC. Specifically, FASN-mediated de novo palmitate 325 

synthesis promotes EGFR palmitoylation, thereby establishing a membrane-associated scaffold 326 

that sustains PI3K-AKT-mTOR signaling and post-translationally suppresses MHC-I surface 327 

expression ( Fig. 4f ). 328 

329 

Notably, this lipid-dependent EGFR-PI3K-mTOR axis operates independently of MAPK signaling. 330 

In ovarian cancer, FASN inhibition suppresses PI3K-AKT-mTORC1 but triggers compensatory 331 

MAPK activation 24 . In striking contrast, MAPK signaling failed to compensate for the loss of 332 

palmitoylated EGFR in TNBC. This difference likely arises because palmitoylated EGFR 333 

preferentially recruits the PI3K regulatory subunit PIK3R1 (p85) over Grb2, thereby favoring PI3K 334 

signaling while limiting MAPK pathway engagement. This biased signaling is further amplified in 335 

the context of oncogenic KRAS, which is common in aggressive TNBC subsets. Consequently, 336 

disruption of EGFR palmitoylation selectively impairs the PI3K-mTOR arm without compensatory 337

340 

Our findings also refine current models of lipid metabolism in metastasis. While prior studies have 341 

emphasized adaptive lipid scavenging and organ-specific lipid availability 25 , we demonstrate that 342 

metastatic TNBC cells maintain a selective dependence on de novo palmitate synthesis to sustain 343 

EGFR palmitoylation and immune-suppressive signaling, even in lipid-rich lung 344 

microenvironment. Exogenous lipids failed to rescue palmitate levels, EGFR signaling, or 345 

metastatic fitness in FASN-deficient cells. 346 

347 

By showing that tumor-derived palmitate drives EGFR palmitoylation, our work establishes 348 

palmitoylation as a critical regulator of membrane signaling organization during metastatic 349 

immune escape. This creates a lipid-dependent signaling scaffold that maintains PI3K-mTOR 350 

activity and suppresses MHC-I through post-translational mechanisms. This pathway operates 351 

independently of canonical MHC-I trafficking or lysosomal degradation and is distinct from EGFR-352 

MAPK-mediated transcriptional repression of MHC-I via IRF1 and STAT1 26 .353 

354 

Our data also clarify a context-specific role for mTOR in immune regulation. Whereas mTORC1 355 

inhibition enhances MHC-I expression in some settings 27,28 , we show that in metastatic TNBC 356 

cells, mTOR activity downstream of palmitoylated mediates EGFR promotes post-translational 357 

suppression of MHC-I surface stability. 358 

359 

Therapeutically, these findings have important implications. Pharmacologic FASN inhibition using 360 

agents which are already in clinical trials, may selectively impair metastatic progression by 361 

restoring anti-tumor CD8 ⁺ T cell immunity. 362 

In summary, this study identifies lipogenesis-dependent EGFR palmitoylation as a tumor cell-363 

intrinsic immunometabolic checkpoint that couples fatty acid synthesis to metastatic competence 364 

and immune evasion. By revealing a druggable link between lipid metabolism, membrane 365 

signaling, and antigen presentation, our work identifies a promising therapeutic opportunity to 366 

disable metastasis and reactivate anti-tumor immunity in TNBC. 367
