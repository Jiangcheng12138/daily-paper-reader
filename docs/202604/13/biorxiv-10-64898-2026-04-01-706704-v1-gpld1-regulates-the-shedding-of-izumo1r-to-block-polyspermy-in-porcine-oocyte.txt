Abstract 10 

Mammalian fertilization commences with the essential interaction between sperm IZUMO1 11 

and its oocyte-surface receptor, JUNO. Following gamete fusion, JUNO is rapidly shed from 12 

the oocyte to establish a definitive membrane block to polyspermy, a pathological condition 13 

that remains a major hurdle in porcine in vitro fertilization (IVF). Despite its biological 14 

importance, the molecular networks driving JUNO cleavage has remained elusive. Here, by 15 

integrating proteomics, dual-color live-cell imaging, and functional perturbations, we identify 16 

the extended existence of JUNO and the GPI-specific phospholipase D1 (GPLD1) as the 17 

requisite enzyme mediating JUNO shedding in porcine oocytes. Targeted knockdown or 18 

pharmacological inhibition of GPLD1 stabilizes oocyte JUNO, prolongs the window of oocyte 19 

receptivity, and significantly exacerbates polyspermy, ultimately compromising embryonic 20 

developmental competence. Conversely, GPLD1 overexpression restricts redundant sperm 21 

adherence and enhances the efficiency of monospermic zygote formation and blastocyst 22 

development. Live-cell imaging reveals that fertilization triggers a transient, pulsed recruitment 23 

of GPLD1 in the oocyte, which precisely coincides with the biphasic kinetics of JUNO 24 

depletion. Our findings establish that the enzymatic cleavage of the GPI-anchor by GPLD1 is 25 

critical for JUNO release, defining a fundamental mechanism for the membrane-level block to 26 

polyspermy. This work provides a molecular framework for ensuring sperm-oocyte recognition 27 

and improving in vitro fertilization outcomes in mammals. 28 

Keywords: Fertilization; GPLD1; JUNO; Polyspermy; Embryonic development 29 

Introduction 30 

Fertilization is a highly orchestrated cascade of molecular and cellular events culminating in 31 

the recognition, adhesion, and fusion of a single spermatozoon with an oocyte to form a zygote. 32 

Prior to membrane fusion, sperm must complete capacitation and the acrosome reaction to 33 

penetrate the zona pellucida (ZP)-the specialized glycoprotein matrix surrounding the oocyte 34 

(Bleil and Wassarman., 1980; Prasad et al., 2000; Boja et al., 2003). The molecular and 35 

structural basis of sperm–ZP recognition and removement post fertilization have been 36

extensively characterized, with specific ZP glycoproteins and ovastacin identified as essential 37

Current insights into membrane-level gamete recognition derive primarily from murine and 40 

human models, leaving the regulatory mechanisms operating in other mammalian oocytes 41 

comparatively unexplored (Inoue et al., 2003; Lorenzetti et al., 2014; Barbaux et al., 2020; 42 

Lamas-Toranzo et al., 2020; Fujihara et al., 2020, 2021; Noda et al., 2020, 2022; Matsumura et 43 

al., 2022; Tang et al., 2022). 44 

The proteins located on membranes form the secondary species-specific molecular networks 45 

for recognition and fusion of sperm and oocyte. The identification of sperm IZUMO1 and its 46 

receptor in the oocyte membrane JUNO (also known as IZUMO1R) (Inoue et al., 2005; Bianchi 47 

et al., 2014), help us to elucidate the molecular mechanisms for this critical process. JUNO is a 48 

glycosylphosphatidylinositol (GPI)-anchored protein, and genetic ablation of Juno in mice 49 

abolishes sperm binding and fusion, resulting in complete female infertility (Bianchi et al., 50 

2014). The IZUMO1–JUNO interaction is evolutionarily conserved and mediates species-51 

specific gamete adhesion in mammals, including humans, mouse, porcine and bovine (Ohto et 52 

al., 2016; Kato et al., 2016; Aydin et al., 2016; Boult et al., 2025). Mutations in JUNO is related 53 

to ART failure due to polyspermy (Yu et al., 2018). Importantly, JUNO is rapidly shed from the 54 

oocyte into the perivitelline space shortly after fertilization, a process proposed to constitute a 55 

membrane-level block to polyspermy (Bianchi et al., 2014). However, despite its central 56 

importance, the molecular mechanism responsible for JUNO removal from the oocyte 57 

membrane remains unknown. 58 

JUNO is a glycosylphosphatidylinositol-anchored protein (GPI-AP) and a member of the folate 59 

receptor family; however, it is functionally distinct, involved in molecular adhesion rather than 60 

acting as a transporter for folate (Chen et al., 2013; Wibowo et al., 2013). GPI-anchored proteins 61 

are commonly regulated through enzymatic cleavage of their GPI anchors, a process mediated 62 

by GPI-specific phospholipases (Udenfriend et al., 1995). Among these, GPI-specific 63 

phospholipase D1 (GPLD1) is a well-characterized mammalian enzyme capable of cleaving 64 

GPI anchors between inositol and phosphate, thereby releasing GPI-anchored proteins from the 65 

plasma membrane (Brown and Rose, 1992; Ikezawa, 2002). GPLD1 has been implicated in 66 

diverse biological processes, including membrane protein remodeling and signal transduction, 67 

yet its role in mammalian fertilization has not been examined (Horowitz et al., 2020; Ren et al., 68 

2024). Notably, proteome studies indicate that GPLD1 is highly expressed in mammalian 69 

oocytes, particularly in oocytes derived from large follicles, and remains detectable throughout 70 

early embryogenesis (Wang et al., 2010; Li et al., 2022). These observations raise the intriguing 71 

possibility that GPLD1 may participate in the post-fertilization remodeling of the oocyte by 72 

regulating the shedding of GPI-anchored proteins such as JUNO. 73

Successful embryonic development requires establishing a robust block to polyspermy, as the 74 

entry of multiple sperm invariably causes chromosomal imbalance and early embryonic arrest 75 

(Nguyen et al., 2020). Although polyspermy is generally rare in most mammals, porcine 76 

fertilization presents a striking exception (Suzuki et al., 2003; Nagai and Moor, 1990). In pigs, 77 

polyspermy occurs in 30–40% of in vivo fertilizations even exceed 75% under in vitro 78 

conditions (Romar et al., 2016; Mahe et al., 2021), representing a major barrier to reproductive 79 

efficiency for sow. Despite its biological and agricultural significance, the molecular 80 

mechanisms underlying the exceptional susceptibility of porcine oocytes to polyspermy remain 81 

largely unknown. 82 

Here, we address this hypothesis by combining ultra-sensitive proteomic profiling with high-83 

resolution imaging and functional perturbation approaches to dissect membrane protein 84 

dynamics during porcine fertilization. We identify JUNO and GPLD1 as key components of a 85 

membrane regulatory axis that governs sperm–egg recognition, polyspermy prevention, and 86 

early embryonic development. By elucidating the spatiotemporal dynamics and functional 87 

interplay of JUNO and GPLD1, our study reveals a previously unrecognized mechanism of 88 

oocyte remodeling that contributes to fertilization fidelity and provides mechanistic insight into 89 

the unusually high incidence of polyspermy in pigs. 90

Comparative profiling of membrane-associated proteins identifies potential drivers of the 179

To identify the molecular effectors governing gamete fusion and the subsequent block to 181 

polyspermy, we systematically interrogated the expression and evolutionary context of key 182 

oocyte proteins. While JUNO and its membrane-organizing partner CD81 remained stable in 183 

total abundance across the fertilization transition (Fig. 4 G), their functional impact is known 184 

to be dictated by rapid post-fusion redistribution. In murine models, JUNO is cleared from the 185 

surface within 40 minutes to establish a definitive membrane block (Bianchi et al., 2014); 186 

however, our proteomic profiling suggests that porcine oocytes maintain a robust reservoir of 187 

JUNO and associated tetraspanins, such as CD9, which exhibited an obvious decline during the 188 

same interval (Fig. 4 G). This sustained presence of fusion-essential receptors may explain the 189 

high intrinsic susceptibility of porcine oocytes to polyspermy. 190 

A key feature of JUNO is its rapid release into the perivitelline space after fertilization, which 191 

prevents additional sperm from binding. Our proteomic data revealed that GPI-specific 192 

phospholipase D (GPLD1), which is an enzyme that cleaves the GPI anchor and releases GPI-193 

anchored proteins from the plasma membrane and is expressed in oocytes at MⅡ, IVF-4h, and 194 

IVF-24h stages (Fig. 4 G). This finding raises the hypothesis that GPLD1 may facilitate 195 

polyspermy block by cleaving JUNO or related GPI-anchored proteins from oocyte. 196 

Subsequent functional perturbations are required to determine whether GPLD1 directly 197 

regulates JUNO localization during fertilization. 198

To determine the functional significance of GPLD1 during fertilization, we first employed an 233 

siRNA-mediated knockdown strategy in porcine oocytes. GPLD1-specific siRNA was 234

DISCUSSION 329 

Sperm-egg recognition and fusion is essential for the success of animal fertilization, and the 330 

high rate of polyspermy in pig eggs results in more abnormal early embryonic development and 331 

lower litter size. JUNO mediate animal sperm-egg specific recognition, and is detached from 332 

the oocyte membrane after fertilization, preventing extra sperm from entering the egg. In the 333 

present study, we integrated proteomics with targeted functional analyses to characterize the 334 

molecular determinants of gamete recognition in the porcine oocyte. Our findings delineate a 335 

regulatory mechanism wherein the dynamic remodeling of membrane-associated proteins-336 

specifically the GPI-anchored receptor JUNO and its enzymatic regulator GPLD1, sperm–egg 337 

interactions and establishes a block to polyspermy. By determining the enzymatic activity of 338 

GPLD1 during fertilization, we demonstrate its critical contribution to restricting sperm 339 

adherence and safeguarding monospermic fertilization. 340 

The expression and function of JUNO in regulating porcine oocyte fertilization 341 JUNO serves as the essential oocyte cytoplasm membrane receptor that binds the sperm 342 protein IZUMO1 to ensure the success of fertilization. In mice, JUNO becomes 343 undetectable on the oocyte surface within approximately 40 minutes post-fertilization, 344 a timeframe that precisely aligns with the establishment of the membrane block to 345 prevent extra sperm recognition (Bianchi et al., 2014). The analyses in current study 346 reveal that the JUNO in porcine oocytes declined post fertilization, but remain at a 347 detectable level through early cleavage stages. The observation with TEM also showed 348 that JUNO is partially released to PVS post fertilization, and the knockdown of JUNO 349 or PIPLC treatment significantly disrupted porcine sperm-oocyte fusion. Porcine 350 oocyte is exceptional susceptibility to polyspermy during in vitro fertilization compared 351 to other mammalian models (Himaki et al., 2025). The essential role of GPI-360 APs in mammalian fertilization has been recognized since the landmark discovery of 361 JUNO (Bianchi E et al., 2014), yet the molecule governing their rapid post-fertilization 362 cleavage remains a fundamental question in reproductive biology. A distinctive 363 characteristic of GPI-APs is their susceptibility to cleavage by specific phospholipases, 364 which facilitates the release of the protein from the cell membrane. By proteomic 365 analyses and IF observation, we identified the existence of GPLD1 366 (glycosylphosphatidylinositol specific phospholipase D1) in porcine oocyte. GPLD1 is 367 a soluble enzyme that plays a key role in regulating cell-surface signaling and function 368 by specifically cleaving GPI anchors (Cao et al., 2023). Unlike other phospholipases 369 such as PIPLC and GPI-PLC, GPLD1 uniquely hydrolyzes GPI anchors that contain 370 acylated inositol residues (Raikwar et al., 2005). In mice, its catalytic activity depends 371 critically on conserved histidine residues at positions 29, 125, 133, and 158 within the 372 active site (Raikwar et al., 2005). By cleaving GPI anchors, GPLD1 releases 373 membrane-bound GPI-APs from the cell surface, thereby modulating their localization 374 and function (Fujihara and Ikawa, 2016). To test the function of GPLD1, its expression 375 in porcine oocytes was knocked down by microinjection of specific siRNA or treatment 376 with an inhibitor (1-10-phenanthroline). This treatment led to an elevated level of 377 JUNO in porcine oocytes and improved their in vitro fertilization outcomes. Meanwhile, 378 the overexpression of GPLD1 reduced the level of JUNO and impaired fertilization in 379 porcine oocytes. Notably, GPLD1 is significantly upregulated in oocytes derived from 380 large follicles and persists during early embryonic development (Gad et al., 2020; Zhi 381 et al., 2022), suggesting a potential role in oocyte maturation, fertilization and embryo 382 development. Based on these findings, it can be concluded that GPLD1 regulates JUNO 383 activity and fertilization efficiency in porcine oocytes. 384

Species-specific sperm-oocyte recognition zona pellucida proteins in porcine 403

Besides sperm-oocyte recognition mediated by membrane protein, sperm firstly 405 recognizes and penetrates the zona pellucida-a glycoprotein-rich extracellular matrix. 406 Our proteomic analysis revealed that the porcine ZP is composed of ZP2, ZP3, and ZP4, 407 notably lacking ZP1. Unlike mice, where ZP1 provides critical structural cross-linking 408 for ZP filaments, porcine oocytes appear to rely exclusively on ZP2, ZP3, and ZP4 to 409 establish a functional sperm-binding scaffold ( Nishio et al., 2024 ; Yonezawa et al., 2012). 410 Among these, ZP3 and ZP4 are recognized as the principal molecular interfaces 411 mediating primary sperm recognition and adherence (Yonezawa et al., 2012). The ZP 412 block acts as a macroscopic mechanical filter which relies on the exocytosis of cortical 413 granules (CGs) to trigger the enzymatic cleavage of ZP2 by ovastacin (ASTL), thereby 414 hardening the matrix and preventing further sperm penetration (Burkart et al., 2012). In 415 current study, the level of ASTL did not change significantly, thus further research is 416 required to elucidate its function in cleavage ZP proteins in porcine oocyte post 417 fertilization. These findings reinforce the notion that ZP composition has evolved 418 divergently across mammals, likely reflecting distinct sperm-oocyte recognition and 419 susceptibilities to polyspermy. 420 

Concluding remarks 421 

Fertilization is a highly coordinated, multistep process that relies on precise molecular 422 interactions between sperm and oocyte, beginning with ZP recognition and culminating 423 in sperm-egg membrane fusion and activation of polyspermy block mechanisms. While 424 the requirement for the GPI-anchored receptor JUNO in primary sperm-egg recognition 425 is well documented, the spatiotemporal mechanisms governing its rapid post-426 fertilization cleavage have remained an enduring question in reproductive cell biology. 427 In this study, we provide compelling evidence that GPLD1 is the essential enzymatic 428 factor responsible for JUNO shedding in porcine oocyte.
