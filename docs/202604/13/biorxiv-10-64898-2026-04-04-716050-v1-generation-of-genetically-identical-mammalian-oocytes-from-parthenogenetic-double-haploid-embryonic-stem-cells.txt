## Abstract 33 

Generating genetically identical mammalian oocytes is challenging due to 34 

stochastic meiotic recombination. Here, we established parthenogenetic 35 

double-haploid embryonic stem cells (PG-DhESCs) possessing complete 36 

homozygosity. By employing blastocyst complementation in Prdm14-deficient 37 

embryos, we generated chimeric females that produced oocytes derived 38 

exclusively from these donor cells. Fertilization of these oocytes yielded viable, 39 

fertile, maternally semi-cloned (MSC) mice of both sexes. Although DNA 40 

methylation was largely restored during gametogenesis, subtle epigenetic 41 

defects correlated with increased body weight in MSC offspring. This study 42 

establishes a robust platform combining PG-DhESCs with blastocyst 43 

complementation to generate isogenic mammalian oocytes, overcoming 44 

traditional limitations in mammalian cloning. 45

## Introduction 49 

The generation of genomically identical mammalian oocytes remains an unmet 50 

challenge. Although stem cell-derived oocytes or those from cloned animals undergo 51 

meiosis, they fail to yield genetically uniform gametes due to the stochastic nature of 52

technology to achieve complete genetic uniformity 3. DH plants, generated via in vitro 54 

culture and chromosome doubling, possess identical homologous chromosomes. 55 

Consequently, meiotic divisions in DH plants produce gametes that are theoretically 56 

perfect genomic clones. This fundamental disparity between plant and mammalian 57 

reproductive strategies presents a critical frontier for reproductive biology. 58 

Haploid embryonic stem cells (haESCs) represent a unique class of stem cells 59 

that retain a single set of chromosomes, categorized based on their gametic origin 60 

into parthenogenetic (PG-haESCs) and androgenetic (AG-haESCs) lineages 4-7 . While 61 

AG-haESCs have been successfully utilized as functional surrogates for sperm to 62 

generate paternally semi-cloned (PSC) mice, this technology is inherently restricted to 63 

producing female offspring due to the absence of the Y chromosome 5,7 . Conversely, 64 

maternally semi-cloned (MSC) mice, generated by replacing the oocyte spindle with 65 

PG-haESCs, can yield both male and female progeny but suffer from extremely low 66 

birth rates 8. These limitations highlight a critical gap in generating genomically 67 

consistent gametes for reproductive applications. 68 

Here, we describe a strategy to overcome these barriers by exploiting the unique 69 

properties of parthenogenetic double haploid ESCs (PG-DhESCs). We demonstrate 70 

that these cells are generated through the spontaneous diploidization of PG-haESCs, 71 

resulting in a stable diploid state characterized by complete homozygosity across the 72 

entire genome 4,9 . Unlike conventional ES cells or hybrid embryos, which harbor 73 

heterozygous alleles, PG-DhESCs possess two identical sets of chromosomes 74 

derived from a single oocyte, effectively fixing the maternal genome in a homozygous 75 

state 9,10 . This feature provides a distinct advantage by eliminating genetic variability, 76 

thereby serving as an ideal starting material for generating isogenic gametes. 77 

We employed a blastocyst complementation approach 11 , injecting PG-DhESCs 78 

into Prdm14-deficient 8-cell stage embryos to generate chimeric females with germ 79 

cells derived exclusively from the injected stem cells. Following chromosomal 80 

duplication, these germ cells undergo meiosis to produce oocytes with uniform 81

mammalian oocytes, bridging the gap between stem cell biology and reproductive 85 

cloning. 86 

## Results 87 

Derivation and Characterization of PG-DhESCs 88 

To establish parthenogenetic double haploid embryonic stem cells (PG-DhESCs), 89 

we collected MII oocytes from B6D2F1 female mice harboring a heterozygous 90 

CAG-tdTomato transgene at the ROSA26 locus. Chemical activation of 271 oocytes 91 

yielded 34 haploid morulae and blastocysts, which were subsequently cultured in 92 

mouse ESC medium with Leukemia Inhibitory Factor (LIF) and 2i (2 inhibitor: 93 

CHIR99021 and PD0325901) to establish 17 PG-DhESC lines (Fig. 1A, 1B and 94 

Supplementary Table 1). Pluripotency assessment revealed that PG-DhESCs 95 

exhibited pluripotency comparable to that of conventional ESCs (Fig. 1C and 96 

Supplementary Fig. 1A, 1B). However, global DNA methylation analysis 97 

demonstrated significant genome-wide methylation loss during in vitro culture, 98 

affecting even imprinted regions. Methylation levels progressively decreased with 99 

increasing cell passage (Fig. 1D), a phenomenon consistent with previous reports 100 

under 2i culture conditons 12 .101 

Generation of Germ Cell-Deficient Mice via CRISPR/Cas9 102 

To create a host background devoid of endogenous germ cells, we targeted 103 

Prdm14—a gene essential for primordial germ cell (PGC) development 13,14 —using 104 

CRISPR/Cas9. Two sgRNAs targeting the second exon of Prdm14 (Fig. 1E) were 105 

co-injected with Cas9 protein into (FVB♀ × B6D2F1♂) zygotes (Supplementary Fig. 106 

2A). Of 143 two-cell embryos transplanted into pseudopregnant recipients, 15 pups 107 

were born, with 14 (6 females, 8 males) surviving to adulthood (Fig. 1F). Sanger 108

Production of Genetically Uniform Oocytes via Blastocyst Complementation 115 

To generate maternally semi-cloned (MSC) mice possessing genetically identical 116 

oocytes, we injected PG-DhESCs into Prdm14-knockout embryos at the 8-cell stage 117 

(Fig. 2A, 2B). Of the 1,407 injected embryos transferred, 52 F0 pups were born, with 118 

29 healthy adults (17 males and 12 females) reaching sexual maturity (Fig. 2C and 119 

Supplementary Table 3). Notably, five females exhibited chimeric coat colors. Upon 120 

mating with ICR males, two of these five chimeric females (both derived from 121 

PG-DhESC-1) produced offspring. One female produced nine litters totaling 50 pups 122 

(19 females and 31 males), while the other produced 4 pups; notably, all offspring 123 

were without coat color segregation (Fig. 2D). 124 

Offspring coat color served as a primary screening metric. Mixed coat colors 125 

(white, grey, and agouti) revealed host-derived contributions resulting from failed 126 

Prdm14 knockout. In contrast, a uniform grey coat validated the PG-DhESC origin 127 

(B6D2F1 background), confirming successful host Prdm14 depletion and germline 128 

transmission to generate MSC mice. All 54 MSC mice obtained exhibited uniform grey 129 

coats (Fig. 2D). In contrast, control crosses (FVB♀ × B6D2F1♂ F1 females × ICR 130 

males) yielded F2 offspring with distinct coat color segregation (Supplementary Fig. 131 

3A ), confirming that the oocytes were exclusively derived from the donor 132 

PG-DhESC-1 line. 133 

Host mice were generated by crossing FVB females with B6D2F1 males, 134 

conferring an FVB mitochondrial background. PG-DhESCs were derived from 135 

C57BL/6J females (Rosa26-tdTomato) crossed with DBA/2 males, retaining the 136

Epigenetic and Phenotypic Characterization of MSC mice 144 

Given the methylation defects observed in PG-DhESCs (Fig. 1D, 2E, 2G), we 145 

assessed epigenetic restoration in MSC mice. Whole-genome bisulfite sequencing 146 

revealed significant recovery of DNA methylation levels—particularly at maternally 147 

imprinted genes—compared to parental PG-DhESCs (Fig. 2E–2G). MSC mice 148 

exhibited increased body weight compared to controls (Fig. 2H), potentially reflecting 149 

incomplete restoration of maternal imprinting, which is known to restrict growth in 150 

mice 15 . This epigenetic and phenotypic signature further confirms the derivation of 151 

MSC mice. 152 

## Discussion 153 

In our study, neither the chimeric female mice generated from PG-DhESC-1 nor 154 

the high-passage PG-DhESC-1 cells exhibited red fluorescence, despite its presence 155 

in early-stage cultures. To investigate this, we performed PCR analysis for the 156 

tdTomato DNA fragment in both early- and high-passage PG-DhESC-1 cells, but 157 

failed to amplify the target sequence. This suggests that PG-DhESC-1 cells originated 158 

from tdTomato heterozygous oocytes that segregated the transgene during the first 159 

meiotic division. Consequently, the red fluorescence observed in early embryos and 160 

cell lines likely represents residual maternal tdTomato mRNA and protein. Notably, 161 

several PG-DhESC lines exhibited loss of red fluorescence during culture, indicating 162 

that the absence of fluorescence in chimeric mice was likely attributable to the donor 163

cells themselves. The derivation of MSC mice from PG-DhESC-1 was further 164 

corroborated by a uniform grey coat, mitochondrial SNP analysis, and the incomplete 165 

recovery of DNA methylation and fat-related phenotypic signatures in F1 offspring. 166 

Although PG-DhESCs undergo genome-wide DNA methylation loss during in 167 

vitro culture in ESC medium with LIF and 2i 12 , genomic imprinting is known to be 168 

reprogrammed during gametogenesis 17 . We hypothesized that aberrant methylation 169 

would be corrected as PG-DhESCs pass through the female germline. Our results 170 

support this, though the increased body weight of MSC mice indicates that 171 

methylation restoration in certain regions may be incomplete. 172 

Semi-cloning technology holds significant promise but faces challenges. 173 

Traditional approaches rely heavily on the stability of haESCs 5,8 . Although the a2i 174 

culture system maintains methylation without knocking out imprinting control regions 16 ,175 

it still requires periodic sorting to sustain haploidy. Furthermore, paternal semi-cloning 176 

is restricted by its inability to clone maternal genomes or produce male offspring, 177 

while maternal semi-cloning is technically demanding and precludes mitochondrial 178 

cloning due to oocyte enucleation. 179 

In summary, our study presents a novel strategy for generating maternally 180 

semi-cloned mice using parthenogenetic double haploid embryonic stem cells. This 181 

approach overcomes key limitations of previous semi-cloning methods, including 182 

extremely low birth rates and the absence of male offspring. Furthermore, our work 183 

introduces a unique innovation: the successful cloning of the oocytes, enabling the 184 

production of mice with mitochondria and nuclear genomes of identical origin—a feat 185 

not readily achievable with previously reported semi-cloning techniques. 186
