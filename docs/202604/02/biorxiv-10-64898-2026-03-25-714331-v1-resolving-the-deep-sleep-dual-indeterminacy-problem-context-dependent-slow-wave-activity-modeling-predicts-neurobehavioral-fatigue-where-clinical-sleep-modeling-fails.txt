Abstract 9

Deep sleep is widely considered to be the most recuperative component of sleep restoration. 10 Accordingly, a positive relationship between naturally occurring deep sleep and function (e.g., 11 cognitive performance) is often assumed. However, this assumption warrants closer 12 examination—particularly given the rise of sleep tracking that emphasizes traditional sleep 13 metrics and their implied predictive value. We present evidence that while clinical deep sleep 14 scoring provides no predictive value, slow-wave activity (SWA) exhibits a paradoxical 15 association with both improved and worsened neurobehavioral fatigue following sleep 16 deprivation. Specifically, we found that SWA-based models account for approximately 50–60% 17 of the inter-individual variance in recovery from sleep deprivation. Remarkably, when regressed 18 against recovery from sleep deprivation, SWA during the baseline sleep night showed a negative 19 association (normalized β = (–)0.5, p = 0.001) while in the same model SWA during the 20 subsequent wakefulness period showed an opposite positive association (normalized β = 0.5, p = 21 0.001). Furthermore, although the group-averaged SWA while behaviorally awake increased 22 with impairment across the sleep deprivation period, individual-level data revealed an inverse 23 relationship: individuals more resilient to sleep deprivation exhibited greater SWA in-between 24 mental test sessions and less corresponding impairment during wakefulness suggestive of a 25 protective effect. These findings identify a Deep Sleep Dual Indeterminacy Problem — 26 simultaneous measurement and causal indeterminacy — that explains why clinical sleep staging 27 fails as a functional biomarker across a wide range of outcomes, and provide a principled 28 framework for next-generation sleep metrics grounded in continuous electrophysiology and 29 temporal modeling. 30

Introduction 32 

Deep sleep is a critical component of cognitive restoration¹⁻³. As a result, a positive relationship 33 between measured duration of deep sleep — specifically as codified by clinical scoring (AASM 34 N3) — and functional outcomes is often assumed. This assumption merits renewed scrutiny, 35 particularly in light of the recent proliferation of consumer sleep trackers that target clinical sleep 36 metrics. Their widespread adoption has conferred unwarranted social credibility on these 37 measures, further amplified by large-scale population studies that, by virtue of enormous sample 38 sizes, lend statistical weight to weak effects while obscuring fundamental interpretation issues⁴. 39 While an obvious concern among specialists has been the accuracy of devices and algorithms 40 against the clinical gold-standard polysomnography (PSG)⁵, these efforts have largely ignored a 41 more fundamental issue: the construct validity of the AASM scoring model itself. Consumer 42 sleep trackers have nonetheless popularized clinical sleep scores as accurate and interpretable 43 biomarkers of brain restoration or, more generally, health⁴⁻⁷. This uncritical acceptance has 44 already moved these metrics into actionable tools — directly, and more consequentially, as 45 unsupported composite ‘functional’ scores⁶ and AI health coaching pipelines built on weak 46 evidence and expert opinion⁷. 47 Indeed, when subjective sleep quality is predicted from the gold-standard PSG, findings are 48 inconsistent across studies and notably, N3 does not emerge as a reliable contributor in any of 49 them⁸⁻¹¹. Some evidence even suggests N2 — labeled by consumer trackers as ‘light sleep’ and 50 implicitly poor sleep — may in fact be a positive correlate of subjective sleep quality, while N1, 51 lumped together with it, shows the opposite relationship⁹. In one study, N3 was even associated 52 with the lightest subjective sleep experience¹². While these studies used subjective outcomes, this 53 lack of robust and consistent associations between sleep scoring and functional outcomes extends 54 to objective performance measures as well. For example, the neurobehavioral gold-standard for 55 fatigue is the Psychomotor Vigilance Test (PVT) — a test uniquely sensitive to acute and 56 chronic homeostatic effects and circadian modulation¹³ʹ¹⁴. No studies have demonstrated a clear 57 positive association between clinical sleep stages including N3 and subsequent PVT 58 performance. In a prior study from our group, Vattikuti et al. demonstrated this lack of 59 correlation with conventional sleep scoring but found that when focusing on sleep 60 microarchitecture, a strong functional signal emerged — specifically in REM neural activity¹⁵. 61 The findings supported the hypothesis that clinical sleep scoring is a highly lossy compression of 62 the underlying electrophysiology — the functionally relevant information resides in the raw 63 signal but is discarded by the staging process. Here we test whether a parallel signal exists in 64 deep sleep, and whether the resulting framework can explain why clinical staging fails as a 65 functional biomarker — with implications for the development of next-generation sleep metrics 66 grounded in continuous electrophysiology and temporal modeling. 67

We argue that operationalizing deep sleep as a predictor is more complex than a simple 68 measurement problem — it is simultaneously a measurement and a causal modeling problem. 69 We term this the Deep Sleep Dual Measurement and Causal Indeterminacy Problem — the 70 former reflecting the measurement indeterminacy introduced by discrete sleep staging, which 71 discards the continuous electrophysiological signal, and the latter capturing the reverse causation 72 inherent in a variable that conflates recovery with the homeostatic drive for recovery. This 73 duality stems directly from the foundational work of Borbély and colleagues. Borbély’s 74 pioneering two-process model established that deep sleep increases following sleep deprivation, 75 reflecting a homeostatic rebound — meaning elevated deep sleep may index the drive for 76 recovery as much as recovery itself. Thus, elevated deep sleep may represent better recovery, a 77 response to greater neurophysiological demand (sleep debt), or both. Borbély himself further 78 cautioned against over-reliance on sleep staging, arguing that NREM stages represent a crude 79 and arbitrary subdivision of a continuous process¹. 80 Addressing this gap, the present study uses data from a controlled sleep manipulation paradigm. 81 We leverage clinical-grade PSG with expert scoring from a professional sleep laboratory unit 82 along with the PVT to investigate the nuanced relationships between deep sleep physiology and 83 objective fatigue, investigating standard manual polysomnographic scoring but also slow-wave 84 activity (SWA). SWA is a key feature of deep sleep regarded as the primary restorative 85 component³. By comparing the predictive value of standard sleep staging vs. quantitative SWA 86 measures, our goal is to explicate the extent to which these measures relate to functional 87 outcomes – a comparison that has implications for the interpretation of sleep “scores”, and that 88 informs improved quantitative assessments. 89 We conducted secondary analyses on data originally collected for a non-invasive slow oscillation 90 (SO) (closely related to SWA) stimulation study. This dataset was well-suited for our purposes, 91 as it included ~45 hours of sleep deprivation recordings across several healthy young adult 92 sleepers, along with baseline and recovery physiology and performance measures. Although 93 neural stimulation was part of the original design, we treated its effects as noise for the current 94 analysis. There are three possible effects: 1) increased induced-SO, 2) reduced SO due to sleep 95 disruption, or 3) no effect. This can be treated as noise, a random variable, potentially reducing 96 the effect size of our observed variables (predictors). 97 In addition to clinical sleep scores, we examined the full continuum of SWA throughout 98 behavioral sleep (agnostic to sleep scores) and throughout behavioral wake periods. “Behavioral” 99 in this context refers to whether the subject is outwardly engaged or disengaged from its 100 environment. In contrast, neurophysiological sleep processes are now recognized to occur, at 101 least in part, across both behavioral sleep and wake and represent local processes¹⁶ʹ¹⁷. For 102 example, prior human studies have also shown an increase in SWA during prolonged 103 wakefulness, though occasionally at higher spectral frequencies¹⁸⁻²⁰. 104

First, we confirmed that the key clinical and research standard variables changed as expected 105 with the experimentally manipulated sleep conditions. Then, as a benchmark, we investigated the 106 relationship between clinically scored deep sleep (N3) and subsequent vigor (inverse of fatigue) 107 as measured using the PVT. This was evaluated under several sleep-wake conditions. Having 108 failed to find any relationship with sleep parameters based on clinical sleep scoring, we 109 performed more detailed investigations of SWA and vigor and consistently found both strong 110 positive and negative associations. 111 

Results 112 

Clinically-Scored Deep Sleep: A Null Result Across All Operationalizations 113 

We first examined whether clinically-scored N3 sleep predicted neurobehavioral vigor, 114 establishing a benchmark against the current standard. Regression models revealed no significant 115 association between N3 and PVT-based vigor across all conditions tested. Specifically, four sets 116 of regressions — comparing N3 fraction during the baseline sleep satiation night (SLEEPSAT3) 117 with performance during baseline, resilience, and recovery wakefulness periods, and N3 during 118 the first recovery sleep night (SLEEPR1) with next-day recovery performance — all yielded 119 non-significant results (p = 0.4–1.0). Repeating these analyses using total N3 duration yielded 120 equivalent null results, as did a normalized measure of recovery night N3 relative to satiation 121 night N3. To confirm the null results were not attributable to failed experimental manipulation, 122 we verified that N3, sleep onset latency, sleep efficiency, and PVT-based vigor all changed as 123 expected across sleep pressure conditions (Figures S1, S2). Across multiple operationalizations 124 of the deep sleep variable and multiple sleep pressure conditions, clinically-scored N3 carried no 125 detectable functional signal for neurobehavioral resilience to, or recovery from, extreme sleep 126 loss. 127

Wakefulness SWA During Sleep Deprivation Positively Predicts Vigor — Evidence 145 

for Active Recovery 146 

Having established that sleep-satiation SWA negatively predicts vigor, consistent with residual 147 debt, and that recovery sleep SWA carried little independent signal, we next asked whether SWA 148 measured during the sleep deprivation wakefulness period (WAKESD) carried additional 149 information. Unadjusted WAKESD SWA showed a significant positive association with 150 recovery (r = 0.40, p = 0.046) but fell short of significance for resilience (r = 0.30, p = 0.1). The 151 consistent positive direction across both outcomes was suggestive of a genuine recovery effect. 152 Recognizing that the normalization denominator may confound interpretation, we examined 153 whether normalizing for inter-individual differences in SWA amplitude clarified the signal. An 154 adjusted WAKESD SWA — normalized by dividing by SLEEPSAT3 SWA — substantially 155 strengthened both associations: explaining 38% of variance in resilience (r = 0.62, p = 0.001) and 156 61% in recovery (r = 0.78, p = 7×10⁻⁶) (Figures 2a, 2b). Critically, this association was positive 157 — the opposite sign from the sleep satiation result — consistent with the interpretation that 158 wakefulness SWA under sleep pressure reflects active neural recovery rather than residual debt. 159 In the following section, we provide strong evidence that this is an independent sleep-like 160 recovery effect. 161

Discussion 190 

In this study, we demonstrate that deep sleep measures reflect both ongoing recovery from 191 neurobehavioral fatigue and accumulated sleep debt — a duality that is precisely what the Deep 192 Sleep Dual Measurement and Causal Indeterminacy Problem predicts. Without proper 193 contextualization, endogenous changes in these physiological markers cannot reliably be used to 194 infer functional states or outcomes. While SWA measures showed strong associations with 195 fatigue, clinical scoring of N3 provided no predictive value — a finding anticipated by Borbély 196 himself, who characterized NREM staging as ‘a crude and rather arbitrary subdivision of a 197 continuous process’¹. 198 The discrepancy between SWA and clinically scored deep sleep may seem at odds with large 199 population studies like All of Us, which report significant correlations between consumer-200 derived sleep staging and chronic illness traits⁴. However, these associations warrant careful 201

Large sample sizes alone cannot disambiguate causal indeterminacy — they 202 simply render detectable whatever directional bias happens to dominate in that particular dataset. 203 This problem is compounded by consumer devices, which do not directly measure 204 neurophysiology — their algorithms infer sleep architecture from peripheral correlates that are 205 nonspecific to the underlying electrophysiology and unpredictably confounded by the very 206 conditions being predicted, and their fidelity degrades when applied to the clinical populations 207 whose outcomes are being predicted²⁶ -²⁸. These interpretation issues extend to direct SWA 208 measurement as well — della Monica et al.¹¹ found that SWA% reversed sign as a predictor of 209 subjective sleep quality across age groups, the same problem of sample composition determining 210 the direction of association. 211 Our results also shed light on resilience to sleep loss. Many practices — mindfulness, meditation, 212 rest breaks, stress reduction — are explored as ways to mitigate cognitive load during 213 wakefulness, yet their neurophysiological mechanisms remain poorly understood. Our findings 214 suggest that covert sleep-like neural activity during wakefulness, indexed by wake SWA, may 215 represent one such mechanism — and that individual differences in resilience may reflect the 216 degree to which the brain engages sleep-like processes while behaviorally awake. This reframes 217 a foundational assumption of the two-process model: Process S may not accumulate 218 monotonically as a function of time awake but may be partially discharged in real time through 219 covert neural activity. Put simply, the brain does not wait passively for behavioral sleep to repay 220 its debt; it steals rest where it can. This also resolves a puzzle in prior resilience modeling work. 221 Counterintuitively, Subramaniyan et al. (2024)²⁵ found that higher baseline sleep SWA — 222 ostensibly a marker of deeper, more restorative sleep — predicted greater vulnerability to sleep 223 deprivation rather than greater resilience. Yet extracting this signal required substantial 224 methodological scaffolding and still yielded only moderate performance (AUC = 0.68, 225 sensitivity = 0.50). Both the counterintuitive direction and the methodological difficulty are 226 explained by the same thing: baseline sleep SWA indexes homeostatic debt rather than recovery 227 capacity, and trying to build a classifier on one half of a constitutively context-dependent signal 228 is precisely as hard as the results suggest. The Dual Measurement and Causal Indeterminacy 229 Problem provides the first unified account of these contradictions. 230 These findings call for a fundamental reappraisal of how deep sleep is measured, modeled, and 231 interpreted. Clinical staging should be treated as descriptive architecture rather than a functional 232 biomarker. Consumer sleep scores and the AI coaching pipelines built on them lack the temporal 233 modeling and physiological grounding necessary for valid functional inference — and the 234 compounding layers of measurement, causal, and device-level indeterminacy make them 235 particularly unreliable. Statistical power alone is not a substitute for construct validity, and these 236 limitations should be confronted before clinical adoption and, more urgently, before millions of 237 users act on them. The Dual Indeterminacy framework provides a principled basis for next-238 generation clinical sleep metrics — physiologically grounded, temporally contextualized, and 239

Methods 242 

Data 243 

Participants were 23 healthy young adults screened for sleep disorders and health problems. To 244 help ensure that participants entered the laboratory with minimal sleep debt, participants were 245 asked to maintain an 8-hour sleep schedule while at-home for at least 7 days. This was followed 246 by three baseline nights in the sleep laboratory with 10-hour time-in-bed (TIB) each night. 247 Fatigue was assessed using objective and subjective scores. The former was assessed using the 248 neurobehavioral gold-standard, reaction time on visual computer-based 10-minute psychomotor 249 vigilance task (PVT)²¹. Testing was conducted at regular 75-minute intervals (interstimulus 250 interval [ISI]: 2-10 seconds, with a uniform distribution). 251 

Study Protocol 252 

In brief, the sleep protocol consisted of establishing a sleep satiated (rested) state; then, after a 253 full normal wake period, a short sleep opportunity with or without SO stimulation; followed by 254 sleep deprivation; and, finally, recovery sleep periods. We used five time periods defined by the 255 study protocol (see Figure M1) for predictor and outcome measures: 1) the sleep satiation night 256 prior to sleep deprivation (SLEEPSAT3), 2) the wake period immediately following sleep 257 satiation (WAKESAT3)(aka baseline), 3) the sleep deprivation wake period (SD), 4) the first 258 sleep recovery night (SLEEPR1), and 5) the wake period after the first recovery night 259 (WAKER1). Our SWA power measures were intentionally agnostic to sleep-wake behavior and 260 sleep-scoring. We visually confirmed that these putative SWA predictive signals were likely due 261 to DOWN-state events (the critical component of SWA)²²ʹ²³. 262 263

Our main outcomes were defined by two measures of vigor (inverse of fatigue) across 275 individuals - resilience to and recovery from extreme sleep loss. Vigor (fatigue) was defined 276 using objective PVT performance. For both sleep predictors and vigor (or fatigue) outcomes, we 277 used time-weighted average estimates for given time periods, a form of interpolation. This was 278 derived by taking the discrete integral across the period and computing the weighted average, 279 with weights determined by the duration between samples. This is a commonly-used method for 280 interpolating irregularly sampled data. It is intended to give equal weight to all samples. For 281 example, this was important for the wakefulness metrics derived from the MWTs because the 282 samples become more widely distributed as sleep debt accrues across individuals; less wake data 283 with sleep deprivation. Glossary 295 

Neurobehavioral Fatigue: Objective fatigue measured using the Psychomotor Vigilance Test 296 (PVT). 297 

PVT (Psychomotor Vigilance Test): A neurobehavioral measure used to assess objective 298 fatigue. 299 

Recovery (from extreme sleep loss): A measure of vigor, calculated as the ratio of the time-300 weighted average PVT-RT during WAKESAT3 over the PVT-RT during WAKER1. Higher 301 values indicate greater recovery. 302 

Resilience (to sleep loss): A measure of vigor, calculated as the ratio of the time-weighted 303 average PVT-RT during WAKESAT3 over its counterpart during SD. A larger value indicates a 304 more rested state. 305 

SWA (Slow-Wave Activity): Power in the slow-wave activity band (1-4Hz), regarded as the 306 primary restorative component of deep sleep. 307 

Vigor: The inverse of fatigue, defined using objective performance on the PVT through the 308 measures of Resilience and Recovery. 309 

SLEEPSAT3 (Sleep Satiation Night 3): The third night of 10-hour scheduled sleep, used for 310 baseline physiological measures. 311

S4. Rate of Fatigue Accumulation Varies Across Individuals and Is Predicted by 429 

Wakefulness SWA 430 

Previously reported results for sleep deprivation physiology consistently show a positive 431 association between SWA and fatigue at the group level — the opposite direction from our 432 cross-individual finding, where elevated wakefulness SWA was associated with greater vigor. 433 This apparent conflict is resolved by distinguishing group-averaged from cross-individual 434 effects. 435 To illustrate this, we first examined the association between SWA and vulnerability (the inverse 436 of our resilience measure) at the group level. As shown in Figure S3a, elevated SWA was 437 associated with increased vulnerability to sleep loss (r = 0.76, p < 0.001), consistent with prior 438 literature — on average across individuals, SWA and fatigue increase together as sleep debt 439 accumulates, with a circadian modulation also evident in both traces. 440 This group-vs-individual dissociation motivates a specific hypothesis: while all individuals are 441 affected by prolonged wakefulness, individuals differ substantially in their relative vulnerability, 442 and this variability may reflect individual differences in a protective covert wakefulness SWA. 443 Examination of this hypothesis confirmed the prediction (Figure S3b) — individuals in the 444 lowest vulnerability tertile showed higher wakefulness SWA than those in the highest 445 vulnerability tertile, and this difference became more pronounced with greater sleep loss. As 446 noted, wake EEG recordings were collected separately from performance testing; whether 447 elevated SWA also occurred during task execution remains unknown. The results suggest this 448 may not be the case, and continuous recordings during performance testing are warranted to 449 investigate this further. 450
