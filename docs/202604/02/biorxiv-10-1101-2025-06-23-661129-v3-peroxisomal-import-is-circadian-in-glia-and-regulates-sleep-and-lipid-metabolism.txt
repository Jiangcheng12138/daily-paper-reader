Abstract 19 

20 

Peroxisomes are critical organelles that detoxify cellular waste while also catabolizing and 21 

anabolizing lipids. How peroxisomes coordinate protein import and support metabolic 22 

functions across complex tissues and timescales remains poorly understood in vivo .23 

Using the Drosophila brain, we discover a striking enrichment of peroxisomes in the 24 

neuronal soma and the cortex glia that enwrap them. Unexpectedly, import of peroxisomal 25 

proteins into cortex glia, but not neurons, oscillated across time and peaked in the early 26 

morning. Rhythmic peroxisomal import in cortex glia autonomously required the circadian 27 

clock and Peroxin 5 (Pex5; peroxisomal biogenesis factor 5 homolog), with import 28 

persistently elevated in clock mutants. Notably, reducing Pex5 in cortex glia, but not 29 

neurons, caused hyperactivity and reduced total sleep. Moreover, brain lipid metabolism 30 

was dramatically altered upon Pex5 knockdown, with glia impacting sphingolipids and 31 

triacylglycerols, and neurons impacting phospholipids. The cell-type specificity of these 32 

Pex5 phenotypes highlights unique roles for peroxisomal import in both sleep and lipid 33 

metabolism in the brain. 34

Introduction 37 

38 

Peroxisomes are responsible for vital metabolic activities ranging from lipid metabolism 39 

by beta-oxidation and ether lipid biosynthesis[1] ,[2] ,[3] to reactive oxygen species 40 

detoxification[4] ,[5] ,[6]. Accordingly, mutations afflicting peroxisomes can cause 41

numerous diseases, including Zellweger Syndrome[7] ,[8], a severe peroxisomal 42 

biogenesis disorder stemming from mutations in one of the 12 different PEX genes in 43 

humans[9]. While these diseases often manifest in the brain[10] ,[11] ,[12], why these 44 

diseases occur and how peroxisomes are regulated across diverse brain cells and 45 

timescales remains incompletely understood. 46

Peroxisomes require a unique import machinery capable of translocating fully folded and 48 

oligomeric substrates from the cytosol into the peroxisomal matrix (lumen)[13]. The 49 

Peroxin-5 protein Pex5 is a vital cog in this translocation machinery, as it functions as a 50 

major receptor for targeting proteins to the peroxisomal matrix by the peroxisomal 51 

targeting signal 1, or PTS1 (e.g. Ser-Lys-Leu, SKL)[14] ,[15] ,[16]. Notably, loss of Pex5 52 

from Drosophila to mice and humans causes severe defects, including motor and 53 

cognitive decline simultaneously with demyelination, axonal loss, and gliosis[17]. While 54 

Pex5 mutants are lethal[18], Pex5 disruption selectively in mouse oligodendrocytes 55 

caused pronounced neurological and lipidomic defects[5]. In contrast, neuronal or 56 

astrocytic Pex5 disruption led to mild defects[17]. While peroxisomal import may thus 57 

have cell-type specific effects in the brain, this has not been systematically explored. 58 

59 

Drosophila is an outstanding genetic model to interrogate peroxisomes and peroxisomal 60 

import function in the brain, given the capability to genetically track and manipulate 61 

peroxisomal import in a cell-type specific manner. The fly brain harbors a diversity of glial 62 

subtypes and neurons thought to share homologous functions with mammalian 63 

counterparts including astrocytes and microglia[19–23]. For example, neuronal synaptic 64 

processes are tiled by astrocyte-like glia in mammals[24] and insects[25], and fly axons 65 

are isolated and insulated by ensheathing (CNS) and wrapping glia (PNS), analogous to 66 

oligodendrocytes and Schwann cells[23,26,27]. In mammals, satellite glia enwrap single 67 

neuronal soma[28], while in adult flies, cortex glia encompass dozens of neuronal soma 68 

in a honeycombed morphology, and accordingly regulate neuronal activity among other 69 

functions[22] ,[29] ,[30] ,[31] ,[32] ,[33] ,[19]. 70

Alongside functionally homologous glia, flies also display complex behavioral repertoires 72 

ranging from memory to sleep[34] ,[35] ,[36] ,[37] ,[38] . While all behavior is tightly 73 

coordinated by rhythmic changes experienced under the shared 24-hour light/dark 74 

cycle[39] ,[40] , in complex metazoan brains the clock is controlled by a network of 75 

circadian pacemaker neurons [41] ,[42] ,[43] ,[44] ,[45] ,[46] ,[47]. Within these cells, negative 76 

transcriptional feedback loops between Period (Period/PER), Clock (Clock/CLK), and 77 

Cycle (cyc/BMAL1) enforce a ~24hour molecular rhythm[48]. Glia also express core 78 

circadian clock machinery and can modulate sleep through myriad pathways, including 79 

remodeling neuronal synapses and regulating metabolism[41,49–51]. Moreover, 80 

autonomous disruption of glial clocks directly disrupts sleep and circadian plasticity in 81 

clock pacemaker neurons in flies[44]. Although circadian rhythms govern metabolic 82 

homeostasis across organisms[52] ,[53] ,[54] and in diseases[55], how subcellular 83 

organelle dynamics in glia and neurons change across time remains underexplored. 84 

Here, we tested how peroxisomal import is coordinated in the fly brain in a cell-type 85 

specific manner. We discovered that cortex glia are hubs of abundant matrix peroxisomal 86 

proteins and high peroxisomal import, in contrast to other brain glia. Moreover, 87 

peroxisomal import in cortex glia but not neurons varied dramatically over circadian time, 88 

with elevated import in the early morning. Through genetic analyses, we determined that 89 

Pex5 and the core circadian clock autonomously regulates oscillatory peroxisomal import 90 

in cortex glia. Notably, loss of Pex5 in cortex glia disrupted sleep behavior and caused 91 

ectopic lipid droplets, while loss of Pex5 in all glia or all neurons profoundly altered 92 

multiple lipid classes. Together, our work identifies circadian fluctuations in peroxisomal 93 

import to specialized glia, which require Pex5 for sleep behavior, and highlights cell-type 94 

specific roles of Pex5 in brain lipid metabolism. 95

Peroxisomal import oscillates in cortex glia across time and requires Pex5 117 

While further characterizing peroxisomal import, we made the serendipitous finding that 118 

import fluctuated across circadian time in cortex glia but not neurons (Figure 2A-B; Figure 119 

S2I-K; Zeitgeber 0 (ZT0) denotes lights-on). While neurons had consistently high levels 120 

of PTS1 that colocalized with PMP70, cortex glia had three-fold higher levels of PTS1 at 121 

ZT2 and ZT4 (Figure S2I-K) compared to a nadir at ZT8 (Figure 2C-F; Pearson decreases 122 

from 0.6 to 0.2, while PTS1 puncta number decreases from 28 to 8). A similar enrichment 123 

of morning import was observed with an anti-SKL antibody (Figure S2B-D). Import 124 

oscillations also occurred under constant darkness implicating endogenous clock control 125 

(Figure 2G-J; Figure S2L-N). While cortex glia PTS1 puncta remained low in the evening, 126 

the colocalization between PTS1 and PMP70 at ZT14 was higher than at ZT8 (Figure 127 

2C), and we often observed diffuse PTS1 membranous signals at ZT14 (Figure S2E). 128 

129 

Intrigued, we tested whether the central regulator of peroxisomal import, Pex5 (Figure 130 

1A), also oscillated in the brain. We used CRISPR to generate a knock-in FLAG tag into 131 

the endogenous Pex5 locus (Figure 2K; Figure S2G and see Methods). Importantly, 132

Pex5.FLAG flies were viable and had wild type levels of Pex5 transcript (Figure S2F-H’), 133 

unlike Pex5 loss-of-function alleles, which are lethal[18]. Using Pex5. FLAG, we observed 134 

that Pex5 protein robustly oscillated across time in the brain under light-dark and dark-135 

dark conditions, peaking at ZT2 during the height of PTS1 import in cortex glia (Figure 136 

2L-O; Figure S2A; note that the majority of Pex5 is expected to be cytosolic). Moreover, 137 

this oscillatory signature is present in single cell RNA sequencing data, where transcripts 138 

of Pex5 , Pex11 , and the peroxisome proliferator-activated receptor (PPAR) homolog 139 

Eip75B peak at similar times in cortex glia[43] (Figure S3 A-B). To test if Pex5 is obligatory 140 

for basal levels of peroxisomal import in glia, we knocked-down Pex5 using an RNAi that 141 

reduces Pex5 transcripts by over 50% [4]. Pex5 KD specifically in cortex glia strongly 142 

suppressed overall peroxisomal import, with reduced PTS1 puncta that no longer 143 

oscillated across time, but did not alter PMP70+ puncta (Figure 2P-T). This phenotype 144 

was replicated with a second Pex5 RNAi line (Figure S4A-D). Thus, in cortex glia, 145 

peroxisomal import and Pex5 levels are highest in the morning, and oscillations in 146 

peroxisomal import require Pex5. 147

The circadian clock autonomously controls peroxisomal import in cortex glia 149 

150 

To test if the circadian clock regulated peroxisomal import, we utilized period arrhythmic 151 

mutants (per 01 ), and measured PTS1 import in cortex glia at the apex (ZT2) and nadir 152 

(ZT8) of import. Strikingly, per mutants had persistently elevated PTS1 import in cortex 153 

glia (Figure 3A-E). As all glia and subsets of neurons express the core circadian clock 154 

machinery[61] ,[62] ,[63] ,[64] ,[65] ,[43], we next investigated whether cortex glia 155 

autonomously require a functioning circadian clock by leveraging a dominant-negative 156 

Clock transgene[66] ( UAS-Clk DN ) or a validated per RNAi line [67] ,[68,69]. In both 157 

manipulations, autonomously disrupting the cortex glia circadian clock triggered aberrant 158 

and persistently elevated levels of PMP70/SKL at ZT8 (Figure 3F-J; Figure S6A-E). 159 

Additionally, removing the PPAR homolog and downstream target of Per, Eip75B , in 160 

cortex glia phenocopied the effects of per and Clk DN and persistently elevated import 161 

(Figure S5A-C). Thus, the circadian clock autonomously controls rhythmic peroxisomal 162 

import into a specialized subset of brain cells, the cortex glia. 163

Loss of Pex5 selectively in cortex glia disrupts sleep 164 165 

Given the striking circadian oscillations in peroxisomal import in cortex glia, and that 166 

cortex glia regulate sleep behavior[70] ,[71], we next examined whether removing Pex5 in 167 

different cells altered sleep. Remarkably, disrupting Pex5 in cortex glia reduced both 168 

daytime and nighttime sleep, with shorter sleep bout duration (Figure 4A-D). This 169 

phenotype likely stems from hyperactivity, as total daytime activity was elevated in Pex5 KD 

170 

flies (Figure 4E-G). Similar deficits occurred under constant darkness (Figure 4H-N), and 171 

there were no clear defects in sleep latency or circadian rhythmicity (Figure S7A-B). In 172 

contrast, knocking-down Pex5 in ensheathing glia had no impact on daytime sleep and 173 

modest effects on nighttime sleep (Figure S7K-R). Similarly, removing Pex5 from neurons 174 

with nSyb-GAL4 did not consistently affect daytime or nighttime sleep (Figure S7C-J). 175 

Thus, Pex5 plays a critical, cell-type specific role in cortex glia that regulates activity and 176 

total sleep. 177

Pex5 is essential for lipid homeostasis in the adult brain 179 

180 

Intriguingly, wakefulness causes accumulation of lipid droplets in cortex glia and 181 

ensheathing glia[71] , and peroxisomes and peroxins[72] can directly interact with lipid 182 

droplets (LD), with Pex5 stimulating lipolysis[73]. We thus hypothesized that removing 183 

Pex5 would alter lipid metabolism in the brain. Indeed, knockdown of Pex5 in cortex glia 184 

dramatically increased lipid droplet number and size (Figure 5A-C) with lipid droplets 185 

appearing adjacent to PMP70 puncta. Knocking down Pex5 in neurons moderately 186 

increased lipid droplet number and size (Figure S8J-L). These data are consistent with 187 

Pex5 function impacting lipid droplet biology, either through peroxisomal import or direct 188 

effects on lipolysis through lipase recruitment [73]. 189

Discussion 229 

230 

Here, we uncover circadian regulation of glial peroxisomal dynamics and link clock-231 

controlled organelle biology to brain-wide lipid metabolism and sleep behavior. By 232 

systematic profiling of peroxisomal import in the brain, we elucidate that cortex glia have 233 

enriched and dynamic peroxisomal import early in the morning. The circadian clock and 234 

Pex5 autonomously control circadian import into cortex glia, and cortex glial Pex5 is 235 

essential for preventing lipid droplet accumulation and sleep loss. Moreover, glial Pex5 236 

is critical for TAG and sphingolipid homeostasis, whereas neuronal Pex5 was critical for 237 

specific glycerophospholipids, highlighting cell-type specific outcomes of peroxisomal 238 

import loss in the brain. 239 240 

An evolutionarily conserved role for Pex5 in glia 241 

Prior investigations discovered key roles for Pex5 in oligodendrocytes[5] and Schwann 242 

cells[6], with severe neurodegeneration and demyelination in Pex5 KO oligodendrocyte, 243 

and nonautonomous effects on ion channel distribution downstream of lysosomal 244 

dysfunction in Schwann cell Pex5 KO . In contrast, Pex5 KO neurons and astrocytes did not 245 

cause overt pathology[17]. By tracking peroxisomal import and conducting Pex5 246 

knockdown experiments across diverse brain cells, we found that cortex glia are 247 

selectively enriched for Pex5-dependent circadian peroxisomal import. Thus, in both flies 248 

and humans, glia play a central role in peroxisome-mediated processes, and circadian 249 

peroxisomal import should be investigated in mammalian glia which similarly enwrap 250 

neuronal soma. Homologues of the cortex glia based on structural morphology include 251 

satellite glia cells, protoplasmic astrocytes, and perineuronal 252 

oligodendrocytes[76] ,[77] ,[78] ,[79]. Notably, cortex glia extend fine membrane processes 253 

tiling dozens of neuronal cell soma, regulate neruonal activity[29] ,[31], and serve as 254 

reservoirs of energy and neutral lipids during development[80] ,[33] and adulthood[81]. 255 

Due to the close association of cortex glia with the neuronal cell soma, we speculate that 256

Circadian control of peroxisomal import in cortex glia 265 

266 

Circadian clocks regulate daily rhythms in organelle dynamics[52] ,[85], lipid 267 

metabolites[86] ,[87] ,[88], and transcriptomes in various 268 

tissues[89] ,[90] ,[91] ,[92] ,[93] ,[94] ,[95] . Our work raises the critical question of how 269 

pervasively peroxisomal proteins and functions vary across circadian time. While we 270 

demonstrate that Pex5 and peroxisomal import peak early in the morning at ZT2 and have 271 

a trough at ZT8, future studies should test if all or subsets of peroxisomal proteins are 272 

similarly circadian in cortex glia. Surprisingly, despite neurons containing high levels of 273 

peroxisomal matrix proteins and PTS1 puncta, we did not detect variations in import 274 

across time. Why do cortex glia undergo circadian peroxisomal import? The afternoon 275 

cessation of peroxisomal import at ZT8 could suggest a restorative break, as ZT8 aligns 276 

with an afternoon siesta thought to adjust for the hottest time of the day[96]. These 277 

restorative breaks may be essential for clearing damaged peroxisomes or for timing lipid 278 

metabolic loops. However, import does not correlate with activity, arguing against a direct 279 

correlation between sleep pressure and peroxisomal import. An alternative hypothesis 280 

would be a division of labor in the peroxisome, where at certain times of day specific 281 

functions like detoxification or lipid metabolism are favored by enhanced import via Pex5. 282 

While we link the circadian clock to glial peroxisomal import, period mutant larval fat 283 

bodies have ultradian variation in lipid metabolism and peroxisomal import and 284 

biogenesis[97], suggesting both clock and non-clock based mechanisms at play. 285 

Intriguingly, a putative deubiquitinase for Pex5, USP2, shows circadian fluctuations in 286 

localization to the peroxisome in liver that depend on the core clock[98], and a similar 287

Glial peroxisomes in sleep, neurodegeneration and lipid homeostasis 289 

Zellweger spectrum diseases[99] ,[100] are a group of heterogenous mutations in 290 

peroxisomal proteins that cause neurodevelopmental and neurodegenerative 291 

disorders.[101] As cortex glial Pex5 is essential for sleep and subject to circadian 292 

regulation, it may be fruitful to investigate for circadian modulation of Zellweger disorder 293 

phenotypes, including sleep. Our behavioral findings suggest a model wherein cortex glial 294 

communicate with closely associated neuronal soma to regulate behavioral processes 295 

such as sleep [102] ,[103]. Sleep deprivation induces a cytokine storm[104], and Pex5 296 

perturbation also induces pro-inflammatory cytokine upd3 in glial cells[4]. In flies, altered 297 

lipid droplet metabolism in cortex and ensheathing glia downstream of neuronal oxidative 298 

stress can cause sleep defects[71]. Moreover, a recent preprint reports that sleep itself 299 

can control peroxisomal abundance and downstream oxidative damage in neurons [105]. 300 

Given that Pex5 knockdown in cortex glia caused sleep loss and increased lipid droplets, 301 

we speculate that neurons in aged Pex5 KD flies could accumulate reactive oxygen species 302 

and upregulate cytokines through metabolic coupling between neurons and glia. 303 

Knockdown of Pex5 caused cell type-specific alterations to the young adult brain 304 

lipidomes, arguing that peroxisomes serve different metabolic functions in neurons 305 

(phospholipid homeostasis) versus glia (sphingolipid and TAG metabolism). The broad 306 

loss of TAG upon glial knockout may occur from increased lipolysis of lipid droplets[106] 307 

and/or decreased neutral lipid uptake and storage, which could be direct or secondary 308 

effects of Pex5 depletion. Enhanced lipolysis may reflect a shifting energy balance of glial 309 

cells with compromised peroxisomal import, and glial mitochondrial beta-oxidation is a 310 

key determinant of both fly viability and TAG and lipid droplet abundances[107] . Prior 311 

work on global pex2, pex3, and pex16 mutants also observed changes in sphingolipid 312 

homeostasis[108] ,[109]. The surprising phenotypic overlap of decreased d14, and 313 

increased d16, sphingolipids in glial Pex5 knockdown (Figure 5D-H) and glial knockdown 314 

of lace , the rate limiting enzyme in sphingolipid biosynthesis, suggests that Pex5 deficient 315 

glia are defective in de novo sphingolipid biosynthesis. Whether this effect is primary 316 

(defects in fatty acid substrates) or secondary (compromised glial health) remains to be 317

In conclusion, by studying Pex5 in the adult Drosophila brain and the dynamics of 323 

peroxisomal import, we identify extensive cell-type specific variability, with elevated 324 

import in neurons and cortex glia. In cortex glia, import and Pex5 levels are under strict 325 

circadian control. Our identification of sleep disruption upon Pex5 loss in cortex glia opens 326 

the avenue to a deeper understanding of peroxisome function in the brain, and the 327 

neurological consequences of peroxisomal disorders. 328 329

EXPERIMENTAL MODEL AND METHOD DETAILS Fly Husbandry and Stocks 

Drosophila melanogaster were maintained at 25°C, 60% relative humidity and 12-hour light/dark cycle unless specified otherwise. Adults were reared on an agar-based diet consisting of 0.8% cornmeal, 10% sugar, and 2.5% yeast. Fly stocks used in the present study are mentioned in the above resources table. Female flies (7 days old) were used in lipidomics and confocal experiments, while male flies were used in sleep studies from 6 to 10 days old. Flies and crosses were maintained in plastic vials and bottles in a thermoregulated chamber at 25 degree Celsius following a 12:12 h light-dark cycle with 60% humidity. All experiments were performed 2-3 times independently.

Adult Brain Immunohistochemistry

Tissues were then incubated with primary antibodies for 48 hours overnight at 4 oC, followed by three 15 minutes washes of 0.2%PBS-TritonX and then secondary antibody staining for another 48 hours at 4 oC. Samples were washed three times for 15 minutes each in 0.2% PBST and then mounted with microfrost slides in Prolong Diamond (Life technologies). Lipidomics extraction and LC-MS/MS 

20 female brains at day 7 ZT10-12 were dissected rapidly on ice-cold 1X phosphate-buffer saline and frozen on dry ice in chilled methanol (90% v/v) before storage at -80 oC until lipid extraction. A modified folch extraction was used and separate columns and standards were run on an Agilent 6430 Triple Quadrupole. Internal standards used included Equisplash™ LIPIDOMIX® Quantitative Mass Spec Internal Standard (Avanti #330731) and Oleic Acid-d9 (Avanti #861809), following a protocol similar to [112]. 

For free fatty acid analysis, an Agilent Zorbax RX-Sil column 3.0 x 100 mm, 1.8 μm particle size was used. The total LC run time was 19 min (with 2.5 min post-run) at a flow rate of 0.3 μL/min. Mobile phase A was composed of isopropanol/hexane/water (58:40:2, v/v) with 5mM ammonium acetate and 0.1% acetic acid. Mobile phase B consisted of isopropanol/hexane/water (50:40:10, v/v) with 5mM ammonium acetate and 0.1% acetic acid. Gradient elution consisted of 0 to 17 min of 0% mobile phase B and an increase from 17 to 18 min until 100% of mobile phase B and after 18 min the mobile phase decreased to 0% for 1 more min. The Agilent 6430 Triple-Quad LC/MS system (Santa Clara, CA) was operated in negative mode. Free fatty acids were identified as [M - H] - and it was run as a pseudoMRM.
