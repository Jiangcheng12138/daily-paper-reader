ABSTRACT 34 

The  corpus luteum (CL)  arises from  the  luteinization  of follicular granulosa cells (GCs) 35 

and theca cells,  marked  by  rapid  progesterone  elevation  and angiogenesis . Intriguingly ,36 

angiogenesis lags behind progesterone  elevation , creating an avascular phase during 37 

which luteal cells  must  fuel  intensive  steroidogenesis  without  perfusion. How  the 38 

avascular  CL meet s this energetic demand remains a mystery . Here , we  reveal  a novel 39 

cellular adaptive mechanism –GC  energy storage (GCES) –that  resolves this  enigma .40 

We demonstrate  that  upon  luteinization  initiation , GCs enter a  metabolically  quiescent 41 

state  yet  enhanc e glucose  uptake  via  SLC2A1 , convert ing  the glucose  into glycogen 42 

through  the  hCG  (LH) -MAPK -RUNX1 -Insulin signaling axis . Catabolism  of  this 43 

glycogen  reserve  supplies  the  energy  required  for  the  avascular  CL , ensuring  normal 44 

luteogenesis . GCES is evolutionarily conserved across  species . Genetic  or 45 

pharmacological  disruption  of GCES or glycogenolysis  induces  luteal insufficiency, 46 

whereas  time ly  glucose  administration  enhances  GCES , improv ing  luteal function  and 47 

optimize  reproductive  outcome  in  both  mouse  and  ovine  models . In human study, orally 48 

intake of glucose post -hCG significantly  augments  GCES and  enhances  pr ogest er one 49 

production  in women . These  results  advance  luteal  physiology  by uncovering a 50 

universal  re productive principle  with direct  clinic al  implication s. 51

INTRODUCTION 53 

The corpus luteum (CL) is a temporary ovarian endocrine structure critical for 54 

reproducti on . I t sustain s pregnancy  by  secreting  progesterone and  orchestrat es  the 55 

reproductive cycle  via  its cyclic formation and regression  [1, 2] . Luteal insufficiency, 56 

defined by  the  suboptimal progesterone production  and shortened luteal phase ,57 

represents a common etiology of female subfertility and is  closely  linked to 58 

reproductive disorders , including  menstrual disorders,  premenstrual spotting  and 59 

recurrent  pregnancy loss  [3 , 4]. It is estimated  that ~8% of reproductive -age women 60 

experience  luteal  insufficiency , with  the  incidences  escalating  in  parallel to maternal 61 

age  [3, 5-8]. Notably , luteal insufficiency  is  prevalent  in recurrent miscarriage cases  [9,62 

10], underscoring its  profound  impact on reproductive health. 63 

CL  arises from  the luteinization of granulosa cells (GCs) and  theca cells, a process 64 

triggered by the pre -ovulatory surge of luteinizing hormone (LH) or human chorionic 65 

gonadotropin (hCG , a n LH -equivalent hormone ) [11]. Luteinization  encompasses 66 

multiple coordinated cellular mechanisms,  such  as  proliferation cessation, 67 

steroidogen esis  reprogramming,  GC -layer  stiffening , cell  migration  and 68 

neovascularization  [2, 1 2-15]. LH (hCG )-activated downstream  regulators , including 69 

P21CIP1, P27KIP1, ERK1/2, PI3K/AKT, CEBPα/β , RUNX1/2,  NR4A2/NURR1, 70 

CREB,  cortisol/glucocorticoid receptors  and  H3K27me3/ H3K9me3  [1 6-25],71 

collectively  regulate  luteinization .72 

Although  derived  from GCs, luteal cells exhibit  a markedly  elevated  progesterone 73 

synthesis  capacity , exceeding that of their  GC  precursors by  se ver al  orders  of 74 

magnitude  [2 6]. This metabolic upscaling renders the CL one of the most  highly 75 

vascularized and  energetically demanding tissues.  Notably , the intrafollicular 76 

microenvironment remains  non -vascular ized , necessitat ing  de novo  angiogenesis for 77 

efficien t substance  delivery  to luteal cell s [27 -29] . Paradoxically, vascularization in the 78 

developing  CL lag s behind  the  increase  in progesterone production. This  temporal 79 

uncoupling  of  metab ol ic  demand  and  vascularization  creates a transient avascular  phase 80

This  investigation  re port s a novel cellular adaptive mechanism,  GC  energy storage 83 

(GCES), which  addres ses  this  enigma . By using  single -cell /spatial transcriptomics, 84 

targeted metabolic flux tracing, isotope tracing, and conditional gene silencing,  we 85

dampening overall metabolism to reduce energy expenditure coincides with the 87 

strategic redirection of absorbed glucose toward glycogenesis, establishing a glycogen 88 

storage pool.  During the avascular phase , the luteinizing GCs use  the  stored  glycogen 89 

to satisfy the  energy  demanding  of  elevated  progesterone synthesis,  thereby  ensuring 90 

normal luteogenesis . GCES  is evolutionarily conserved across  species . Disrupt ing 91 

GCES  or  glycogenolysis  precipitate  luteal insufficiency, while  enhancing  GCES 92 

improves  luteal  function  and  fertility outcomes  in  mammals .93

3.  Enhanced glucose uptake supports glycogen biosynthesis in GCs during early 208

Intriguingly,  despite transitioning to a metabolically quiescent state, GCs at H6 exhibited  a210 

significant  increase  in glucose uptake  compared to  H0  controls  (Figure 3A).  Transporter 211 

expression profiling identified SLC2A1 as the predominant  glucose transporter  of this enhanced 212 

uptake  (Figure 3B ; S3A). Intracellular glucose  can be directed toward ATP generation or  storage 213 

as lipids  or  glycogen (Figure 3C)  [32] . Metabolic flux analysis  ruled out  significant  catabolic 214 

utilization  (Figure  2A, B ),  and nor for the lipid storage since colorimetric assays revealed the 215 

reduced intracellular free fatty acids and triglycerides  (Figure  S3B, C ).  To directly assess 216 

glycogen conversion,  we performed ¹ ³C-glucose tracing coupled with  Liquid Chromatograph -217 

Tandem Mass Spectrometer ( LC -MS/MS ). hCG -treated GCs  exhibited  pronounced ¹ ³C218 

enrichment in glycogen ( 35 % incorporation v er sus , 8% in controls; Figure 3D , E), demonstrating 219 

preferential glycogen biosynthesis. Consistent  with this , Western  blotting  and qRT -PCR  analysis 220 

detected activation of the glycogenesis pathway following hCG  administration  (Figure 3F ; S3D). 221 

Complementary Periodic Acid -Schiff (PAS) staining and colorimetric quantification confirmed 222 

a significant elevation in glycogen content within H6 GCs  (Fig ure  3G , H). 223

functional correlates  throughout  luteinization  process . Integrated analysis defined 225

3I) and PAS staining  (Fig ure  3J).  Notably, during glycogenesis phase, ATP production 228

occurred within the developing  CLs  prior to the establishment of internal 233 

vascularization  (Figure 3M) .234

4.  Mobilization of glycogen storage to fuel luteal steroidogenesis during the 264 

avascular phase 265 

To  test  whether  the store d glycogen  in GCs is used  to fuel the  avascular  CL  for 266 

steroidogenesis , we  augmented  glycogen stor age  during glycogenesis phase via  2g/kg 267 

dose of  glucose  (glucose tolerance dose)  administration (Figure 4A -C). By 15 hours 268 

po st -hCG (H15), the g lucose -loaded  luteinizing  GCs exhibited  reduced NAD⁺/NADH 269 

ratio, increased ATP generation,  upregulated  luteal gene expression and increased 270 

progesterone  secretion  compared  to  the  controls  (Fig ure  4D , E; S4A). This functional 271 

enhancement persisted  until  H40  (Fig ure  S4B),  indicating the  glycogen  storage  as a 272 

critical determinant of luteal  function .273 

To further validate the functional  importance  of glycogen, RNA interference  was 274 

performed to suppress the  glycogen synthesis . In vitro  knockdown of  glycogenesis  genes 275 

Ugp2  and  Gys1  did not impair follicular development  and  ovulation but markedly reduced 276 

GC glycogen storage  in pre -ovulatory follicles (Fig ure  4F, G; S5A-C). This intervention 277 

precipitated a profound bioenergetic crisis at H15,  characterized by  increased 278 

NAD⁺/NADH ratio, diminished  MMP , reduced ATP  generation , significantly  suppressed 279 

progesterone synthesis and luteal gene expression  (Figure 4H -J;  S5D, E). In vivo  ovarian 280 

bursa delivery of  Ugp2  and  Gys1  interference plasmids (Fig ure  4K -M; S6A) recapitulated 281 

this  bioenergetic crisis with : increased  NAD⁺/NADH, decreased ATP, downregulated 282 

luteal gene expression  and reduced serum progesterone (Fig ure 4N -P;  S6B). 283 

Complementarily , inhibiti on of  glycogen catabolism during the glycogenolysis phase 284 

using the PYGB inhibitor –Ingliforib  (Figure 4Q , R)  also  induced luteal insufficiency, 285 

evidenced by  decreased ATP  production , downregulated luteal gene  expression  and 286 

attenuated progesterone s ynthesis  in the  glycogen -constrained luteinizing GCs (Figure 287 

4S , T;  S6C).288 

To definitively investigate the functional necessity of glycogen storage  in vivo , the 289 

GC -specific  Gys1  conditional knockout mice (cKO -Gys1 ) were  generated  using a 290 

Gyp19a1 -Cre driver system (Figure 4U ; S6D). This genetic ablation abolished glycogen 291 

accumulation within GCs  of the mice  (Figure 4V). Concomitantly, cKO mice exhibited 292 

significant bioenergetic perturbation in their avascular CLs, including  elevated 293 

NAD⁺/NADH ratio, impaired ATP production and significantly suppressed progesterone 294 

synthesis along with attenuated luteal gene expression at H15 (Figure 4W , X;  S6E). 295 

Moreover, cKO mice displayed a significant  decrease  in litter size compared to 296 

Gys1 flox p/flox p (Figure 4 Y).297

DISCUSSION 466 

The acquisition of progesterone synthesis capacity precedes  the  vascularization in the 467 

CL . How does the avascular CL meet the substantial bioenergetic demands  for  the high 468 

level of  progesterone production  is still unknown . Our study resolves this enigma by 469 

identifying GCES as the key mechanism.  We demonstrate that  during early 470 

luteinization, hCG  (LH)  orchestrates  a metabolic transition in GCs via the MAPK -471 

RUNX1 -Insulin signaling axis, redirecting glucose toward glycogenesis . This 472 

strategically stored glycogen is subsequently mobilized to fuel intensive progesterone 473 

production throughout the avascular phase, ensuring normal  lute ogenesis  (Figure  7H). 474 

The GCES  process  fundamentally refines our understanding of  luteinization . This 475 

process  striking ly  parallel to oocyte nutrient provisioning for preimplantation embryos 476 

[34]  highlights conserved  regulatory  strategies in reproduct ion . Moreover, GCs emerge 477 

as a novel insulin -sensitive glycogenic cell type, distinct from hepatocytes and 478 

myocytes  [35,36] , with broad physiological implications .479 

GCES not only  serves  as an energy source  for  avascular CL but also profoundly 480 

influencing mature luteal function  (Figure  S4B), indicating  significant  translational 481 

promise . GCES  enhancement  improved luteal function  in both polytocous (mice) and 482 

monotocous (sheep) species , leading to marked increases in pregnancy rates.  In  human 483 

trials,  the  time ly  oral glucose administration  induces the  GCES  enhancement ,484 

increasing  progesterone  content  by  ~73%.  Crucially , this  GCES -mediated  luteal 485 

support  was achieved  solely through glucose intake,  bypassing  pharmaceutical  agents ,486 

thereby  being  exceptional safety . This is particularly significant  since  no treatment for 487 

luteal insufficiency ha s been reported to improve pregnancy rates in natural menstrual 488 

cycle  [3] . Therefore, w e propose a novel preconception strategy : peri -ovulatory intake 489 

of glucose or high -glycemic -index foods  (such as  cakes, milk tea, candies)  as a potential 490 

means to  mitigate luteal insufficiency and improve  fertility outcomes . Given its safety, 491 

accessibility,  ease of  use  and cost -effectiveness,  this  approach  could be widely adopted 492 

in populations with a higher incidence of luteal insufficiency , potentially reducing 493 

reliance on  conventional  luteal  phase  support –especially  in  women who experience  the 494 

dual risk factors of early pregnancy bleeding and a history of previous miscarriages 495 

[37,38 ].496 

In livestock production, GCES -based luteal enhancement could integrate 497 

seamlessly with estrus synchronization  and embryo transfer  to boost pregnancy rates 498

a distinct metabolic divergence. Unlike GCs, theca cells  neither  enter  metabolic quiesce 508

that enhancing GCES via  time ly  glucose administration improves luteal function  in 515 

different species including humans . These  insights  advance  reproductive physiology 516 

and hold substantial promise for  developing  novel  therapeutic approaches to address 517 

luteal  insufficiency  in both livestock and human .518 

LIMITATIONS 519 

While we  have  identified  the LH  (hCG )-MAPK -RUNX1 -Insulin axis  regulating 520 

glycogen storage in  GC s, the signaling pathway(s) inducing  metabolic quiescence 521 

remains un defined . Beyond  elevated  energy demands,  robust  progesterone production 522 

also  requires  substantial  cholesterol precursor,  yet  how  the developing  CL  maintains 523 

cholesterol  supply  during  its  avascular phase  has  not  been  investigated.  Furthermore , the 524 

mandatory  progesterone supplementation following embryo transfer in ART protocols 525 

[39,40] preclude s direct  assessment of GCES -mediated  improvement  in  mature  CL 526 

function  and  pregnancy outcomes  of  humans.  Future studies should aim to: (i) delineate 527 

the mechanisms governing GC metabolic quiescence and cholesterol provision, and (ii) 528 

quantify the effects of GCES enhancement on  human  pregnancy rates in natural 529 

conception cohorts. 530

Ex perimental  design 532 

This study  commenced with the prediction of GC metabolic alterations during early 533 

luteinization using multi -omics profiling. These predictions were experimentally 534 

validated, defining a novel  cellular adaptive mechanism, GCES . The functional 535 

impact of GCES on  luteogenesis  was then investigated through targeted 536 

manipulation in vitro and in vivo. Meanwhile, the regulatory pathway controlling 537 

GCES was elucidated. Finally, the translational potential of GCES was evaluated 538 

across species and in a preliminary clinical setting. 539 

(1)  Multi -Omics Profiling of GC Dynamics during Early Luteinization : Integrated 540 

single -cell and spatial transcriptomics analysis was performed to delineate high -541

GCES. 555

conditional knockout strategies to block glycogen storage; and (iii) 560 

pharmacological inhibition of glycogen catabolism. The functional consequences 561
