ABSTRACT 23 Glutamate dehydrogenase (GDH) is a key mitochondrial enzyme that 24 catalyzes the reversible oxidative deamination of glutamate to α-ketoglutarate, 25 thereby linking amino acid and carbohydrate metabolism. GDH forms catalytically 26 active hexamers and is regulated by various allosteric modulators, including ADP 27 and GTP. Here, we demonstrate that GDH undergoes auto-palmitoylation in the 28 presence of palmitoyl-CoA, leading to a dose-dependent inhibition of enzymatic 29 activity. Using acyl-PEG exchange assays and mass spectrometry, we show that 30 GDH monomers are predominantly mono-palmitoylated, with modification detected 31 at multiple cysteine residues, including Cys55, Cys115, and Cys197, among the six 32 cysteines in the mature enzyme. Blue Native PAGE analysis revealed that 33 palmitoylation disrupts the native hexameric assembly of mammalian GDH, which is 34 organized as a dimer-of-trimers, promoting dissociation into dimers. Importantly, this 35 modification is reversible, as incubation with mitochondrial acyl-protein thioesterases 36 1 (APT1) and, to a lesser extent, α/β hydrolase domain 10 (ABHD10) restores both 37 the hexameric structure and enzymatic activity. The modified Cys55 residues are 38 positioned near the trimer interface, providing a mechanism by which palmitoylation 39 could prevent hexamer formation, whereas Cys115 and 197 may destabilize 40 individual trimers. These findings establish S-palmitoylation as a novel regulatory 41 mechanism for GDH, linking mitochondrial lipid metabolism to the reversible control 42 of a central metabolic enzyme. 43 

1 INTRODUCTION 44 Mitochondrial metabolism is orchestrated by a complex network of enzymes 45 that integrate signals from multiple metabolic pathways 1, 2 . At the intersection of 46 amino acid and carbohydrate metabolism lies glutamate dehydrogenase (GDH), an 47 enzyme that catalyzes the reversible oxidative deamination of L-glutamate to α-48 ketoglutarate, producing NADH and ammonia 3 . This reaction is essential for amino 49 acid catabolism, ammonia detoxification, and anaplerotic replenishment of 50 tricarboxylic acid (TCA) cycle intermediates 4, 5 . GDH is ubiquitously expressed and is 51 particularly important roles in tissues with high metabolic demands, including the 52 liver, kidney, and brain 5 .53

3The quaternary structure of GDH has been extensively characterized. The 54 enzyme functions as a homohexamer, with each subunit harboring a catalytic site 6 .55 The GDH hexamer consists of two trimers arranged back-to-back, with extensive 56 inter-trimer contacts stabilizing the assembly 6, 7 . GDH activity is subject to complex 57 allosteric regulation by metabolites including GTP, ATP and palmitoyl-CoA 58 (inhibitors), as well as ADP and leucine (activators), allowing the enzyme to respond 59 dynamically to the cellular energy state and metabolic flux 6, 8 . Additional layers of 60 regulation include hormonal control and tissue-specific expression of different GDH 61 isoforms 7, 9, 10 .62 

S-palmitoylation, the reversible attachment of palmitate to cysteine residues 63 via thioester linkages, is an increasingly recognized post-translational modification 64 that regulates diverse cellular processes 11 . Unlike other lipid modifications, such as 65 myristoylation and prenylation, S-palmitoylation is reversible and can be dynamically 66 regulated by cellular signals. Palmitoylation is catalyzed by a family of zinc-67 dependent DHHC-domain containing palmitoyltransferases (ZDHHCs) 12, 13 . At the 68 same time, depalmitoylation is mediated by acyl-protein thioesterases, including the 69 APT/LYPLA family and α/β-hydrolase domain-containing (ABHD) proteins 14, 15 .70 Recent proteomic studies have revealed that S-palmitoylation is far more widespread 71 than previously appreciated, with hundreds of palmitoylated proteins identified 72 across different cellular compartments, including mitochondria 16-18 .73 Interestingly, some proteins can undergo auto-palmitoylation in the presence 74 of palmitoyl-CoA without requiring ZDHHC enzymes, suggesting that the 75 nucleophilicity of certain cysteine residues and their local microenvironment can 76 facilitate direct acyl transfer 19-21 . This ZDHHC-independent mechanism may be 77 particularly relevant in the mitochondrial matrix, where palmitoyl-CoA is generated 78 during fatty acid β-oxidation, and ZDHHC enzymes are less abundant or absent 79 compared to other cellular compartments 22 . Additionally, the pH of the mitochondrial 80 matrix is typically 7.5 – 8.2 23, 24 , approaching the pKa of cysteine sulfhydryl groups, 81 which would favour auto-palmitoylation. 82 GDH is known to be inhibited by palmitoyl-CoA 6 , but whether this inhibition is 83 due to allosteric binding or to auto-palmitoylation remains unresolved 25, 26 . Given the 84 presence of multiple cysteine residues in GDH, the comparatively high pH, and the 85 presence of palmitoyl-CoA in mitochondria during fatty acid oxidation, we 86

2.1 Palmitoyl-CoA inhibits glutamate dehydrogenase activity through 95 

auto-palmitoylation 96 We first re-examined the effect of palmitoyl-CoA on enzyme activity. Bovine 97 liver GDH was pre-incubated with increasing concentrations of palmitoyl-CoA (4 and 98 10 μM) for 10 minutes, followed by measurement of enzymatic activity in the 99 glutamate-forming (reductive amination) direction using NADH oxidation as the 100 readout (Figure 1A). Palmitoyl-CoA treatment resulted in dose-dependent inhibition 101 of GDH activity, with ≈35% inhibition at 4 μM and ≈85% inhibition at 10 μM palmitoyl-102 CoA (Figure 1B). This inhibition was not due to direct competition with substrates or 103 cofactors, as the palmitoyl-CoA was present only during the pre-incubation period 104 and was diluted 100-fold upon addition to the reaction mixture. This finding is also 105 consistent with previous studies demonstrating that several long acyl-CoAs can 106 inhibit GDH, while CoA itself and short acyl-CoAs do not 26 .107 We suspected that in the presence of 10 μM palmitoyl-CoA at pH 8.0, 108 deprotonated cysteine residues could act as nucleophiles and attack the carbonyl 109 group in palmitoyl-CoA (Fig. 2A). Thus, auto-palmitoylation, rather than allosteric 110 regulation, could explain the inhibition of GDH. To confirm that the observed 111 inhibition resulted from covalent modification of GDH via S-acylation, we used an 112 acyl-polyethylene glycol exchange (acyl-PEG) assay 27 . This method involves 113 blocking free cysteines with a non-reducible alkylating agent, S-methyl 114 methanethiosulfonate (MMTS), followed by selective cleavage of thioester bonds 115 with hydroxylamine (HAM), and subsequent labeling of the newly exposed cysteines 116 with a 5 kDa polyethylene glycol (PEG) mass tag. 2.2 Mass spectrometry identifies multiple palmitoylation sites 130 To identify which cysteine residue(s) are palmitoylated, we used differential 131 labeling and an acyl-switch strategy coupled with mass spectrometry. In this 132 approach, palmitoylated GDH was reacted with MMTS (methylthiolation, +46 Da) to 133 modify free cysteines. Next, the thioester bonds were cleaved with HAM and the 134 newly exposed cysteines were alkylated with iodoacetamide. This results in 135 carbamidomethylation (+57 Da) specifically at sites that were palmitoylated initially. 136 LC-MS/MS analysis identified peptides containing all six cysteine residues in the 137 mature GDH sequence (Supplemental Table 1), demonstrating good coverage of 138 cysteine-containing regions. For reference, the fragment spectra for the relevant 139 peptides with either IAA- or MMTS-modified peptides are included in Figure 3. 140 Analysis of MMTS-modified and IAA-modified peptides revealed carbamido-141 methylation at Cys55, Cys115, and Cys197 in hydroxylamine-treated samples 142 (Figure 4A, B). Cys55 yielded the highest spectral counts among the modified 143 residues, though differential ionization efficiencies of distinct peptides preclude 144 precise quantitative comparisons between sites. However, Cys89, Cys270, and 145 Cys319 showed little to no detectable modifications under these conditions. The 146 identification of a limited number of palmitoylation sites is consistent with our acyl-147 PEG exchange data, which showed primarily mono-palmitoylation with trace 148 amounts of di-palmitoylation. 149

2.3 Structural analysis reveals palmitoylation sites are near the core at 150 

the trimer interface 151 To understand how palmitoylation at Cys55, Cys89 and Cys197 might affect 152 GDH structure and function, we examined the three-dimensional structure of the 153 bovine GDH1 hexamer using the previously published structures PBD:1NR7 28 and 154 PBD:3ETD bound with the inhibitor bithionol 29 . The mature human GDH hexamer 155 forms back-to-back trimers, with extensive inter-subunit contacts stabilizing the 156 hexameric assembly (Figure 5A). This arrangement generates three distinct types of 157 subunit interfaces: intra-trimer contacts between adjacent subunits within each 158 trimeric ring, intra-dimer contacts between vertically stacked subunit pairs across the 159 two trimers, and the broader trimer-trimer interface formed by the collective back-to-160 back interactions. 161 Mapping the positions of all six cysteine residues onto the structure revealed 162 striking differences in their spatial locations (Figure 5B). Cys55 is located within the 163 glutamate-binding domain (amino acids 50 – 150) at the interface where subunits 164 from opposing trimers contact one another. In contrast, Cys274 and Cys319 are 165 positioned on the exterior surface of the hexamer, fully exposed to solvent and 166 distant from any subunit contact regions. Cys89, Cys115, and Cys197 occupy 167 intermediate positions with varying degrees of surface accessibility but are not 168 situated at critical assembly interfaces. 169 The location of Cys55 at the subunit A-D interface suggests a clear 170 mechanism by which palmitoylation could disrupt hexamer stability. The trimer-trimer 171 interface is tightly packed, with inter-subunit distances that accommodate precise 172 complementary surfaces but leave little room for additional bulk. The attachment of a 173 16-carbon palmitoyl chain (~20 Å in length) at Cys55 would introduce substantial 174 steric bulk directly into this interface. Given the proximity of two Cys55 in the 175 corresponding subunits, palmitoylation of both residues would introduce substantial 176 bulk and hydrophobicity (Figure 5C). Cys89 and Cys197, the other palmitoylated 177 residues, reside in a pocket near the interface between the A and F subunits (Figure 178 5D, E). 179

7To directly assess the effect of palmitoylation on GDH quaternary structure, 181 we analyzed palmitoylated GDH using Blue Native PAGE (BN-PAGE), which 182 separates protein complexes under non-denaturing conditions while preserving 183 native oligomeric states 30, 31 . Untreated GDH migrated predominantly as a hexamer, 184 with an apparent molecular weight of approximately 360 kDa (Figure 6A, 0 μM lane), 185 consistent with the enzyme's known hexameric structure. Treatment with increasing 186 concentrations of palmitoyl-CoA (2.5, 5, and 10 μM) resulted in a dose-dependent 187 decrease in the hexamer population and a concomitant increase in species migrating 188 at ≈120 kDa, consistent with dimers (Figure 6A). Occasionally, we also observed a 189 band at 240 kDa, which may represent a dimer of dimers or a tetrameric 190 intermediate. 191 Quantification of band intensities from three independent experiments 192 confirmed that palmitoyl-CoA treatment caused a significant, dose-dependent 193 decrease in the hexamer population (Figure 6B). At 10 μM palmitoyl-CoA, hexamer 194 levels were reduced to approximately 20% of control, with a reciprocal increase in 195 dimer formation (Figure 6C). The correlation between hexamer dissociation and loss 196 of enzymatic activity (Figure 1B) strongly supports that the hexameric state is 197 required for optimal GDH catalytic function and that S-palmitoylation-induced 198 oligomer disruption is the primary mechanism of enzyme inhibition. 199 

2.5 Acyl-protein thioesterases reverse GDH S-palmitoylation and restore 200 

structure and activity 201 

S-palmitoylation is a reversible modification that can be removed by acyl-202 protein thioesterases (APTs) and α/β-hydrolase domain-containing (ABHD) 203 enzymes 14 . APT1 is a well-characterized depalmitoylating enzyme that localizes to 204 mitochondria and regulates mitochondrial protein depalmitoylation 32 . To test whether 205 GDH palmitoylation is reversible, we co-incubated palmitoylated GDH with 206 recombinant APT1 (also known as LYPLA1) or its catalytically inactive S119A 207 mutant 32 . BN-PAGE analysis revealed that wild-type APT1 treatment restored the 208 hexameric structure of palmitoylated GDH, while the catalytically inactive S119A 209 mutant had no effect on oligomeric state (Figure 7A). Albumin present in the sample 210 served as an internal loading control, confirming equal protein loading across 211 conditions. 3 DISCUSSION 228 In this study, we have identified S-palmitoylation as a post-translational 229 modification of glutamate dehydrogenase that regulates both its quaternary structure 230 and catalytic activity. Our findings reveal several key insights: (1) GDH undergoes 231 auto-palmitoylation in the presence of palmitoyl-CoA at pH 8.0, predominantly on 232 three Cys residues; (2) palmitoylation disrupts the native hexameric structure of the 233 enzyme, promoting dissociation into dimers; (3) the structural disruption correlates 234 with dose-dependent inhibition of enzymatic activity; and (4) the modification is 235 reversible by mitochondrial acyl-protein thioesterases APT1 and ABHD10. These 236 results establish a potential link between mitochondrial lipid metabolism and the 237 regulation of a central metabolic enzyme. In addition, our findings provide 238 mechanistic insight into the long-standing observation that long-chain acyl-CoAs 239 inhibit GDH in vitro .240 The mechanism by which S-palmitoylation of Cys55, disrupts hexamer 241 formation can be rationalized by previously determined structures. Cys55 lies within 242 the glutamate-binding domain (residues ~50-150), which forms the core of the 243 hexamer and mediates the critical trimer-trimer interface through extensive 244

During 245 normal catalysis, this core region undergoes a "breathing" motion in which stacked 246 dimer pairs move 2.5-4% closer together as the catalytic cleft closes, then separate 247 as it opens 29 . This dynamic process is essential for catalytic turnover—the stacked 248 dimers must be able to move relative to each other for efficient substrate binding and 249 product release. The attachment of a 16-carbon palmitoyl chain at Cys55 would 250 introduce ≈21 Å of hydrophobic bulk directly into this interface 34 . Unlike non-covalent 251 inhibitors such as bithionol and GW5074, which bind to the nearby α-helix (residues 252 138-155) and inhibit GDH by restricting the breathing motion without causing 253 disassembly 29 , the covalent nature of palmitoylation creates a permanent steric 254 barrier. The results would predict that the palmitoyl chain cannot be accommodated 255 by the normal dynamic range of the interface and thus forces complete separation of 256 the trimer-trimer contacts. This interface—formed by the extensive antiparallel β-257 sheet between vertically stacked subunits—remains essentially unchanged during 258 catalysis (~0.1% distance variation) and represents the tightest and most stable 259 association within the hexamer. Cys55 is positioned to disrupt the lateral contacts 260 between these stacked dimers, not the vertical contacts within them. Consequently, 261 palmitoylation at Cys55 would specifically weaken the trimer-trimer interface while 262 leaving the intra-dimer β-sheet contacts intact, causing the hexamer in trimers. 263 However, our BN-PAGE results detect primarily dimers, not trimers. Thus, it is 264 conceivable that palmitoylation of Cys89 or Cys197 disrupts the interactions within 265 the trimer. 266 Our finding that hexamer dissociation correlates with loss of enzymatic activity 267 is consistent with previous studies demonstrating that the hexameric state is required 268 for optimal GDH function 6, 35 . Importantly, the reversibility of this process by APT1 269 and ABHD10 demonstrates that palmitoylation-mediated regulation is dynamic and 270 potentially responsive to changes in cellular metabolic state. The physiological 271 relevance of GDH palmitoylation remains to be fully established, but several 272 metabolically coherent scenarios can be envisioned. Under high-glucose conditions, 273 mitochondrial acetyl-CoA is derived primarily from glycolysis, whereas during fasting 274 or low-glucose states, fatty acids become the dominant energy source and long-275 chain acyl-CoAs such as palmitoyl-CoA are transported into the mitochondrial matrix 276 for β-oxidation. These examples highlight that S-acylation should be 306 considered as a versatile regulatory mechanism capable of modulating protein 307 function through multiple distinct mechanisms: membrane targeting, catalytic 308 enhancement, and allosteric inhibition via structural remodeling. 4 CONCLUSION 313 We have demonstrated that glutamate dehydrogenase undergoes reversible S-314 palmitoylation at Cys55, Cys89, and Cys197, thereby disrupting hexamer formation 315 and inhibiting enzymatic activity. This palmitoylation is reversed by mitochondrial 316 acyl-protein thioesterases APT1 and ABHD10, suggesting a dynamic regulatory 317 mechanism linking mitochondrial lipid metabolism to amino acid catabolism. These 318 findings reveal a previously unrecognized layer of GDH regulation and suggest that 319 palmitoylation may serve as a broader mechanism for coordinating metabolic flux in 320 response to changes in cellular energy status and nutrient availability. 321

5.8 Molecular Docking 440 Molecular docking simulations were performed using AutoDock Vina (version 441 1.5.6) 42 to investigate the binding orientation of palmitic acid within the active 442 site of mature glutamate dehydrogenase (GDH). The crystal structure of 443 bovine GDH (PDB ID: 3ETD) was retrieved from the Protein Data Bank. Prior 444 to docking, all crystallographic water molecules and co-crystallized ligands 445 (GTP, NADPH, glutamate, and bithionol) were removed. Polar hydrogen 446 atoms were added, and Kollman charges were assigned using AutoDockTools 447 (ADT). Palmitic acid (PLM) was obtained from the Protein Data Bank entry 448 and prepared by defining rotatable bonds and saving the structure in PDBQT 449 format. Docking simulations were performed by centering the search space on 450 each of the six cysteine residues (Cys55, Cys89, Cys115, Cys197, Cys270, 451 and Cys319) of monomer A within the GDH hexamer. In addition, docking was 452 performed at Cys55 of monomer D to evaluate the orientation of palmitate at 453 the monomer A–D interface. A grid box with dimensions of 20 Å × 20 Å × 20 Å 454 was defined for each docking run, centered to encompass the target cysteine 455 residue. Docking calculations were executed with an exhaustiveness value of 456 8, generating 9 poses per run. The resulting docking poses were ranked 457 based on predicted binding affinity (kcal/mol), and the top-ranked 458 conformation (lowest binding energy) for each site was selected for further 459 interaction analysis. Observed binding energies of top-ranked poses ranged 460 from −5.8 to −3.3 kcal/mol. Protein–ligand complexes and hydrogen bonding 461 interactions were visualized using PyMOL (version 3.1.3.1) from Shrödinger. 462
