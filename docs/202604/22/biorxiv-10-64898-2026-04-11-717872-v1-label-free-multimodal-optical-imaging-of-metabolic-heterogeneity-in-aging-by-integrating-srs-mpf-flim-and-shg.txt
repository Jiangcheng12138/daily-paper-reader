ABSTRACT 28 

Cellular metabolism is governed by the coordinated organization of macromolecules, including 29 

lipids and proteins, together with redox -active cofactors such as NADH and FAD. However, 30 

resolving these biochemical features quantitatively and spatially at subcellular resolution remains 31 

challenging because no single imaging modality can capture molecular composition, redox state, 32 

and tissue architecture simultaneously without labeling. Here, we present MANIFEST (Multi -33 

mod Al Nonlinear Imaging with Fluorescence Excitation and Statistical Temporal -resolved 34 

spectroscopy), a label -free imaging platform that integrates stimulated Raman scattering (SRS), 35 

second harmonic generation (SHG), multiphoton fluorescence (MPF), and fluorescence lifetime 36 

imaging microscopy (FLIM). The MANIFEST combines chemical imaging of lipids with 37 

autofluorescence - and lifetime -based quantification of NADH and FAD metabolism, enabling 38 

spatially resolved analysis of metabolic heterogeneity at organelle and tissue -compartment levels. 39 

We apply this framework to four distinct aging or disease models : amyloid -beta -treated tri -cultured 40 

brain cells, high -fat diet mouse liver, human non -ischemic cardiomyopathy tissue, and aging 41 

mouse retina. Across these systems, MANIFEST reveals disease -associated lipid remodeling, 42 

redox imbalance, disrupted metabolic zonation, collagen reorganization, and layer -specific 43 

metabolic changes. By integrating complementary nonlinear optical modalities into a single label -44 

free platform, MANIFEST provides a generalizable approach for high -resolution metabolic 45

Metabolic heterogeneity is a defining feature of both normal physiology and disease progression. 55 

Across aging tissues and pathological states, cells differ in their lipid composition, protein 56 

organization, redox balance, and extracellular matrix remodeling . These differences often emerge 57 

at subcellular, cellular, and tissue -compartment scales. Such biochemical variation is closely 58 

linked to functional state and disease trajectory in diverse systems, including neurodegeneration, 59 

metabolic disease, cardiovascular dysfunction, and retinal aging. However, directly visualizing 60 

these metabolic features in situ remains challenging because no single imaging modality can 61 

simultaneously resolve molecular composition, cofactor dynamics, and tissue structure with high 62 

spatial resolution and without exogenous labeling (1).63 

Current imaging strategies each capture only part of this landscape. Conventional 64 

fluorescence microscopy offers high sensitivity and molecular specificity (2), but it often depends 65 

on exogenous labels, is constrained in multiplexing capacity (3), and may perturb the biological 66 

system under study. Super -resolution fluorescence imaging methods can achieve exceptional 67 

spatial precision (4-11 ), yet this gain in specificity often comes at the expense of throughput, 68 

molecular breadth, and compatibility with large -scale tissue analysis. By contrast, label -free 69 

optical approaches offer the possibility of probing endogenous biochemical information directly, 70 

but most single -modality methods remain limited in the range of molecular features they can 71 

report. As a result, there is a substantial unmet need for imaging platforms that can quantitatively 72 

integrate multiple endogenous contrasts and map them across intact biological samples in a 73 

unified framework. 74 

Nonlinear optical microscopy provides a powerful foundation for such integration because 75 

distinct light –matter interactions generate complementary biochemical readouts (12 -14 ).76 

Stimulated Raman scattering (SRS) microscopy enables label -free imaging of major biomolecular 77 

classes, particularly lipids and proteins . Hyperspectral SRS (HS -SRS ) can further distinguish 78 

molecular subtypes through vibrational spectral signatures (15 -18 ). Second harmonic generation 79 

(SHG) selectively visualizes non -centrosymmetric structures such as collagen (19 , 20 ), providing 80 

structural information on extracellular matrix organization. Multiphoton fluorescence (MPF) 81 

detects endogenous fluorophores, including the metabolic cofactors NADH and FAD, while 82 

fluorescence lifetime imaging microscopy (FLIM) resolves their microenvironment -dependent 83 

lifetime states, enabling more detailed interpretation of redox and metabolic activity (21 -23 ).84 

Together, these modalities are highly complementary: SRS reports chemical composition, SHG 85 

captures structural organization, and MPF/FLIM provide functional readouts of metabolic 86 

cofactors. Integrating them into a single analytical framework can therefore reveal relationships 87 

between macromolecular composition and metabolic state that are inaccessible to any one 88 

modality alone. 89

Recent advances in computational imaging have further expanded the utility of nonlinear 90 

optical methods. HS -SRS analysis has enabled quantitative mapping of lipid subtypes, while 91 

image processing approaches have improved the spatial resolution of SRS and MPF (18 , 24 -26 ).92 

Nevertheless, most of these developments have emphasized macromolecular composition, 93 

whereas small metabolic cofactors remain under -characterized in relation to surrounding lipids, 94 

proteins, and tissue architecture. This limitation is especially important because NADH and FAD 95 

are central reporters of cellular metabolism . In particular, NADH exists in multiple functional 96 

states, including free NADH, enzyme -bound NADH, and NADPH -associated pools, each 97 

reflecting distinct biochemical activities (27 -30 ). A multimodal strategy that quantitatively links 98 

these cofactor states to endogenous molecular distributions would therefore provide a more 99 

complete view of metabolic heterogeneity in cells and tissues. 100 

Despite the strong potential of multimodal imaging approaches and advanced 101 

computational imaging techniques for quantifying metabolic activities, their combined application 102 

to metabolism studies has yet to be established. In the context of medical diagnostics, multimodal 103 

imaging approaches have been employed to identify pathological regions within tissues based on 104 

compositional contrast, without the need for exogenous labeling (31 -33 ). With a properly designed 105 

measurement framework and suitable analytical methods, multimodal imaging can enable the 106 

simultaneous characterization of multiple metabolic activities. Here, we present MANIFEST (Multi -107 

mod Al Nonlinear Imaging with Fluorescence Excitation and Statistical Temporal -resolved 108 

spectroscopy), a label -free multimodal imaging platform that integrates SRS, SHG, MPF, and 109 

FLIM for spatially resolved metabolic profiling. MANIFEST combines SRS imaging of lipid 110 

distributions with autofluorescence - and lifetime -based analysis of NADH and FAD, enabling 111 

quantitative assessment of both macromolecular organization and cofactor -associated metabolic 112 

state (anabolic versus. catabolic states) within the same biological specimen. By incorporating 113 

phasor analysis together with fluorescence lifetime curve analysis, the platform resolves detailed 114 

information from endogenous fluorophores and relates these measurements to lipid -rich 115 

organelles and tissue compartments. We apply MANIFEST across four distinct models of disease 116 

or aging : amyloid -beta -treated tri -cultured brain cells, high -fat diet mouse liver, human non -117 

ischemic cardiomyopathy tissue, and aging mouse retina. Across these systems, MANIFEST 118 

reveals spatially localized lipid remodeling, redox imbalance, altered collagen organization, 119 

disrupted metabolic zonation, and tissue -specific structural reorganization. These results 120 

establish MANIFEST as a generalizable framework for high -resolution, label -free analysis of 121 

metabolic heterogeneity in complex biological systems. 122

Metabolic alterations in brain cells treated with Amyloid -beta 157 

As a proof -of -concept, MANIFEST was employed to investigate the metabolic and compositional 158 

changes in tri -cultured brain cells, including neurons, astrocytes, and microglia, following 159 

treatment with amyloid -beta (Aβ) (37 ). HS -SRS microscopy provided insights into the chemical 160 

composition of lipid structures, specifically , the ratios of unsaturated lipids, triacylglycerol (TAG), 161 

cholesterol ester, and lipid amount . MPF and FLIM of NADH and FAD were used to assess key 162 

metabolic indicators such as the redox state , the unbound NADH ratio, and the NADPH ratio, 163 

offering a comprehensive view of cellular metabolic activity (Fig. 2a) .164 

Detailed examination of individual lipid droplets (LDs) revealed significant heterogeneity and 165 

distinct alterations induced by Aβ treatment (Fig. 2b) . In the Aβ -treated cells, LDs demonstrated 166 

a notable shift in their composition, characterized by a n increase in TAG and cholesterol ester 167 

content. This compositional change was accompanied by a clear shift in the metabolic state. 168 

Specifically, these LDs exhibited a significantly higher redox ratio, a lower unbound NADH ratio, 169 

and an elevated NADPH ratio (Fig. 2c) . These metabolic shifts collectively indicate an oxidized 170 

and stressed cellular environment, highlighting the detrimental impact of Aβ on brain cell 171 

metabolism and lipid homeostasis. 172

DISCUSSION 223 

In this study, we developed a multimodal imaging and analysis platform of MANIFEST that 224 

integrating SRS, SHG , MPF and FLIM for studying metabolic dynamics in a variety of organisms .225 

In previous research , the capabilities of a multimodal imaging method incorporating SRS, SHG, 226 

and MPF were demonstrated (34 ).Lipid subtype distributions and spatial information of metabolic 227 

activities were imaged from single cells to human tissues (38 -44 ). By combining FLIM in this study ,228 

chemical activities of small molecules (NADH, FAD) were further investigated , which could not be 229 

achieved by previous methods. By demonstrating the combined measurement approach on 230 

various sample types, MANIFEST shows the potential of a broad application in metabolic imaging. 231 

As a showcase of MANIFEST, amyloid beta driven metabolic and lipid compositional alterations 232 

were first examined in brain cells . Recent study observed LD accumulation and reactive oxygen 233 

species ( ROS ) increase related to tauopathy neurons (43 ). Using the similar approach , we also 234 

studied NADH metabolism and lipid subtype in individual LDs. As shown in the redox ratio images, 235 

an increase in NADH was detected, and this shift indicates metabolic dysfunction that contributes 236 

to increased ROS production. In addition, the reduced free NADH represents metabolic shift from 237 

glycolysis to OXPHOS (45 ), and the increase in NADPH also represents the increased lipid 238 

anabolism (46 , 47 ). The increased lipid amount, TAG and cholesterol ester , indicates that LDs are 239 

synthesized and accumulated actively in amyloid beta treated cells. These findings suggest that 240 

Aβ -induced neuroinflammation is closely coupled with metabolic reprogramming and lipid 241 

remodeling across multiple brain cell types (48 , 49 ).242 

In the liver tissue imaging , the role of SHG imaging is clearly visible. Due to the fibrosis induced 243 

by high fat diet, collagen fiber distribution changes can be clearly measured. From the correlated 244 

information of NADPH increase and lipid subtype analysis, active LD accumulation and oxidative 245 

stress increase by lipid synthesis were detected in the same manner as the brain cell 246 

measurements. Difference between the se two measurements is the increased free NADH in 247 

hepatocytes. It represents the increased glycolysis by high fat diet (50 ).248 

FLIM imaging revealed that NADPH level was increased in NICM samples, even though lipid 249 

amount wa s decreased. While NADPH measurement can serve as an indicator of lipid synthesis 250 

rate, the relationship between NADPH levels and lipid synthesis is not straightforward. Although 251 

NADPH levels typically rise during active lipid synthesis, NADPH can also accumulate due to 252 

other reasons, such as the defense against oxidative stress or the activation of specific signaling 253 

pathways. Therefore, elevated NADPH level alone do es not necessarily indicate increased lipid 254 

anabolic activity. In contrast to brain cell and liver tissue , LDs in NICM samples exhibited higher 255

Retina tissue imaging is also an important example of MANIFEST application in aging study. 263 

Aging alters multiple metabolic activities in retina . Using MANIFEST , the information about 264 

different metabolic pathways and where the alteration happens was revealed . The direct 265 

correlation between increased lipid synthesis and NADPH increase was also revealed, 266 

demonstrating the capability of MANIFEST approach. 267 

MANIFEST demonstrates broad utility for measuring correlated metabolic signals and identifying 268 

disease -associated metabolic signatures that may serve as biomarkers for specific diseases (34 ).269 

and their subtypes. Rather than focusing solely on the presence of target molecules, our approach 270 

is designed to interrogate disease mechanisms by capturing how metabolic pathways are 271 

functionally altered. By integrating multiple complementary metabolic readouts, MANIFEST helps 272 

reduce the ambiguity that can arise from the convergence of distinct biochemical pathways on 273 

similar phenotypes. 274 

A central strength of MANIFEST is its ability to simultaneously quantify multiple metabolic 275 

activities. For fluorescence lifetime imaging microscopy (FLIM), we combined two complementary 276 

analytical approaches —phasor analysis and biexponential fitting —to resolve and quantify three 277 

distinct cofactor species: free NADH, enzyme -bound NADH, and NADPH. Quantitative 278 

information from these species enables assessment of the metabolic processes governing their 279 

relative balance. Although previous multimodal imaging approaches incorporating coherent anti -280 

Stokes Raman scattering (CARS) have shown promise for biomedical applications ( 31 -33 ), they 281 

have largely focused on general disease detection and diagnosis. In contrast, MANIFEST 282 

incorporates stimulated Raman scattering (SRS) imaging, which provides quantitative functional -283 

group -specific information and enables comparison with reference spectra from lipid subtypes. 284 

Because SRS maintains a linear relationship with spontaneous Raman scattering, it supports 285 

quantitative analysis of molecular structure and facilitates imaging of multiple lipid subtype 286 

distributions. This quantitative chemical specificity is a key advantage of MANIFEST for metabolic 287 

phenotyping. 288 

Importantly, MANIFEST also leverages the high spatial resolution of optical microscopy to detect 289 

compositional and metabolic alterations at the subcellular organelle level. This capability provides 290 

a more refined view of disease progression and aging by revealing fundamental differences 291 

between healthy and diseased cells. Ultimately, these insights may support the development of 292 

more precise diagnostics and more effective therapeutic strategies. 293

Imaging Setup for MANIFEST 374 

The system is based on a n SRS setup. A custom -built upright laser -scanning microscope 375 

(Olympus) equipped with a 25× water -immersion objective (XLPLN, WMP2, 1.05 NA, Olympus) 376 

was utilized for near -IR throughput. A synchronized pulsed pump beam (tunable 720 –990 nm 377 

wavelength, 5 –6 ps pulse width, 80 MHz repetition rate) and a Stokes beam (1032 nm 378 

wavelength, 6 ps pulse width, 80 MHz repetition rate) were supplied by a picoEmerald system 379 

(Applied Physics & Electronics) and coupled into the microscope. The pump and Stokes beams 380 

were collected in transmission by a high -NA oil condenser (1.4 NA). A high -O.D. shortpass filter 381 

(950 nm, Thorlabs) was used to completely block the Stokes beam and transmit only the pump 382 

beam onto a Si photodiode to detect the stimulated Raman loss signal. The output current from 383 

the photodiode was terminated, filtered, and demodulated by a lock -in amplifier at 20 MHz. The 384 

demodulated signal was fed into the FV3000 software module FV -OSR (Olympus) to reconstruct 385 

images during laser scanning. Cardiac tissue and cell images were acquired at a resolution of 386 

512 × 512 pixels, while liver and retinal tissues were imaged at 1024 × 1024 pixels. The pixel 387 

dwell time was 20 μs. MPF, SHG, and FLIM were integrated into the SRS microscope to image 388 

the same region of interest using different modalities. Signals were collected using a389 

photomultiplier tube (PMT), and FLIM measurements were performed by adding a TCSPC 390 

module to the computer for signal collection. 391

FLIM Analysis 393 

The FLIM data were processed using custom -built Python scripts for phaosr plot (23 ) and lifetime 394 

curve fitting. As described in Fig. S1, a two -step analysis approach was employed. First, an 395 

uncalibrated phasor diagram was generate d. From this phasor diagram, pixels representing 396 

different chemical environments were clustered using the Mean Shift algorithm (52 ). Following 397 

clustering, the photon -counting histogram of each pixel cluster was averaged and fitted with a 398 

biexponential decay model. By using these fitting results as initial values for the precise fitting of 399 

individual pixel lifetime curves, a detailed two -component analysis of free and enzyme -bound 400 

flavin and NADH was achieved. Finally, the lifetime of enzyme -bound NADH was used to 401 

calculate the NADPH/NADH ratio (53 ).402

Following denoising, the water signal from the hyperspectral SRS images was removed according 406

analyzed. LD analysis, individual droplets were quantified using the 3D Objects Counter 408
