Abstract 13 

Interactions among brain networks support cognition and behavior. Oscillatory 14 

activity may contribute to this process by coordinating distant neuronal populations. A 15 

crucial step toward elucidating brain function and its emergent properties is 16 

understanding the mechanisms that dynamically modulate functional connectivity in 17 

response to environmental challenges and physiological demands. Dopamine (DA) is 18 

well-positioned to modulate this process, given its role in altering neuronal excitability 19 

and regulating oscillatory cognitive networks, such as the hippocampus-prefrontal 20 

cortex (HPC-PFC) networks. However, it is mostly unknown how dopamine modulates 21

dopamine levels. Our results show that dopamine dose-dependently induces HPC-PFC 23

Introduction 34 

Brain activity underlying behavior is highly flexible and emerges from the 35 

coordinated dynamics of distributed neuronal networks. Neural oscillations are believed 36 

to promote the formation of neuronal ensembles and facilitate communication between 37 

ensembles from distinct brain regions (Fries, 2005; Buzsáki, 2010). Oscillations are 38 

possibly the most energy-efficient mechanism for the temporal coordination of neural 39 

activity (Buzsáki & Draguhn, 2004). Thus, investigating how oscillatory synchrony 40 

operates is particularly interesting for understanding the dynamic coordination of brain 41 

activity. 42 

Fries (2005; 2015) proposes that interareal coherence creates brief, 43 

milliseconds-scale windows in which inputs from one neuronal group are preferentially 44

transmitted and read out by another. Because coherence is flexible and state-45 

dependent, it may be tuned by neurophysiological processes such as synaptic plasticity 46 

(Uhlhaas et al., 2010) and neuromodulation, particularly dopaminergic (Beeler & Dreyer, 47 

2019), thereby enabling selective, dynamic routing of information across brain 48 

networks. 49 

Dopamine (DA), acting through D 1- and D 2-like receptors, powerfully influences 50 

neuronal excitability and synaptic efficacy (Goto & Grace, 2005; Lezcano & Bergson, 51 

2002; Robinson & Sohal, 2017), which could provide the basis for DA to contribute to 52 

interareal synchronization. Specifically, the participation of DA in oscillatory synchrony 53 

has been investigated in the hippocampus-prefrontal cortex (HPC-PFC) networks, which 54 

are central to cognition and emotion and are coordinated by hippocampal theta rhythms 55 

(Benchenane et al., 2011; Ruggiero et al., 2021; Sigurdsson & Duvarci, 2016; Spellman 56 

et al., 2015). In working memory tasks, HPC-PFC theta synchrony varies dynamically and 57 

is usually correlated to individual performance (Benchenane et al., 2011; Fujisawa & 58 

Buzsáki, 2011; Sigurdsson et al., 2010; O’Neill et al., 2013). Interestingly, it is known that 59 

DA release in the cortex increases during working memory tasks (Phillips et al., 2004). 60 

Notably, Benchenane et al. (2010) showed that HPC-PFC theta coherence could be 61 

reproduced by PFC local DA administration in anesthetized rats. Furthermore, both 62 

agonism and antagonism of D 1- and D 2-like DA receptors are shown to be associated 63 

with changes in HPC-PFC theta synchrony (Xu et al., 2016; Perreault et al., 2017; Gener 64 

et al., 2019), reinforcing the DA proposal as a neural synchrony modulator (Beeler & 65 

Dreyer, 2019). 66

Together, the literature suggests that dopaminergic neurotransmission plays a 67 

role in regulating HPC-PFC oscillatory activity, influencing power or coherence 68 

(Benchenane et al., 2010; Xu et al., 2016; Perreault et al., 2017; Gener et al., 2019). 69 

However, this process is still poorly understood. For instance, in a small sample, 70 

Benchenane et al. (2010) reported that local DA administration in the PFC increased 71 

theta coherence without altering theta power in either PFC or HPC. Since coherence is 72 

a measure dependent on power, these findings suggest that there may also be phase 73 

synchrony modulation, i.e., a power-unbiased and volume-conduction-free 74 

synchronization. In this study, we tested the hypothesis that DA modulates HPC-PFC 75 

oscillatory phase synchrony. Here, we investigated the impact of DA administration on 76 

HPC-PFC functional connectivity, and whether these effects are reproduced by selective 77 

D1- and D 2-like DA single-receptor agonism or non-specific apomorphine (APO) agonism. 78 

Our findings reveal that DA induces HPC-PFC theta phase synchrony dose-dependently, 79 

and receptor agonists do not fully reproduce this effect. Therefore, our data suggest that 80 

DA has an essential but complex role in regulating HPC-PFC oscillatory activity, 81 

influencing not only power and coherence but also phase synchrony in theta 82 

frequencies. 83

Surgery and electrode implantation 93 

Rats were anesthetized under urethane (1.2 g/Kg in NaCl 0.15 M, i.p., Sigma-94 

Aldrich) and placed in a stereotaxic frame (Kopf Instruments, EUA) equipped with a 95

AP: + 3.2 mm; medial-lateral, ML: - 0.5 mm; dorsal-ventral, DV: - 2.9 mm) and the left 98

recordings, and the right lateral ventricle (LV; AP: - 0.5 mm, ML: - 1.8 mm, DV: - 3.5 mm) 100

A single stereotrode (two intertwined, 50-μm, Teflon-coated tungsten wires) or 102 

a 64-channel acute silicon probe (E64+R-50-S4-L6-200 NT, ATLAS Neuroengineering, 103 

Belgium) was implanted in the PFC, and a single stereotrode was implanted in the HPC. 104 

Each stereotrode was attached to a silver wire soldered into an 8-channel connector 105 

(Omnectics Connector Corporation, EUA). Silicon probes and Omnetics connectors were 106 

coupled to 32-channel Intan headstage preamplifiers (Intan Technologies, EUA). 107

al., 2017). For recordings with silicon probes, the following parameters were used for 110 

signal acquisition: 1000x gain, 30 kHz sampling rate, 0.5-7603.8 Hz bandpass filtering. 111

Drugs and experimental design 114 

Apomorphine hydrochloride, SKF-38393 hydrochloride, and quinpirole 115 

hydrochloride (Sigma-Aldrich, USA) were dissolved in sterile 0.9% NaCl. Dopamine 116 

hydrochloride (Sigma-Aldrich, USA) was dissolved in 0.04 mg/mL ascorbic acid to 117 

prevent oxidation. Aliquots were stored at −80 °C, and each aliquot was thawed once on 118 

the day of use. The doses were as follows: apomorphine 0.75, 1.5, or 3 mg/kg (i.p.); 119 

dopamine 100 or 500 nmol (i.c.v.); SKF-38393 1 or 10 μg (i.c.v.); quinpirole 1 or 10 μg 120 

(i.c.v.). All drugs except apomorphine were infused at a rate of 0.5 μL/min for a total 121 

volume of 1μL using a Hamilton microsyringe (10 μL) connected to polyethylene tubing 122 

and a 30-gauge cannula. Each animal was given a saline injection followed by a drug 123 

injection. The interval between injections was at least 60 minutes to reduce or avoid the 124 

influence of the previous drug on the next injection (Fig. 1B). 125 

Brain extraction and tissue processing 126 

Rats were submitted to an electrolytic lesion to mark the electrode insertion 127 

sites, then sacrificed with an additional dose of thiopental (25 mg/kg, i.p.). Brains were 128 

extracted and preserved in PFA (4%) for 12 hours and then in sucrose (30%) for 72 hours. 129 

Histological sections were performed in the semi-frozen brains for staining with 130 

hematoxylin and eosin, and subsequent verification of the electrode insertion sites. 131

Spectral power 140 

Power spectral density (PSD) was calculated using Welch’s method via the Fast 141 

Fourier Transform algorithm available in the MATLAB software. We used 3s time 142 

windows with 50% overlap and 2 16 FFT points. We averaged the PSD estimates across 143 

trials and animals, respectively. The relative power was calculated by dividing the PSD 144 

estimate by the integrated power over the frequency range of 0.5-55 Hz. Whole-145 

recording spectrograms and coherograms were computed with the Chronux toolbox 146 

(Bokil et al., 2010) using a 5 s moving window advanced in 2.5 s steps, a multitaper 147 

method (time-bandwidth product = 3, 5 tapers), a sampling rate of 500 Hz, and a 148 

frequency range of 0.5-55 Hz. 149 

Functional connectivity 150 

Spectral coherence and debiased weighted phase lag index (dwPLI - used here to 151 

evaluate oscillatory phase synchrony; Vinck et al., 2011) measures were estimated using 152 

cross PSDs of HPC and PFC calculated using modified Welch’s periodogram method. 153 

Spectral coherence is the same as that obtained by MATLAB’s mscohere function and 154 

indicates how well the signals being compared match each other at each frequency. 155 

dwPLI was calculated as described by Vinck et al. (2011) and quantifies the phase 156 

relationship between oscillations, thereby reducing zero-lag contamination due to 157

Spike sorting and single-unit analysis 161 

Spike sorting was performed using Kilosort 2.5 (Pachitariu et al., 2021). Quality 162 

control of individual units was performed manually using the phy interactive interface 163 

(https://github.com/cortex-lab/phy/). Firing-rate and spike-LFP phase-locking analyses 164 

were performed using custom MATLAB scripts. Mean firing rate was quantified for 165 

baseline and post-injection epochs, and spike-LFP phase locking was estimated as the 166 

phase-locking value between PFC spike times and the phase of the simultaneously 167 

recorded local PFC LFP. For spike-LFP analyses, the LFP was filtered in the 4-10 Hz band 168 

to provide more stable phase estimates at the single-unit level. Statistical analyses were 169 

based on 4-minute windows immediately before and after injection (Fig. 1D). 170

Statistical analysis 187 

We used the Shapiro-Wilk normality test to verify the data distribution. To 188 

compare two experimental groups or two conditions, we used the paired or unpaired 189 

two-tailed Student’s t-test, or the non-parametric Wilcoxon matched-pairs signed-rank 190 

test using the GraphPad Prism 9.0 software (GraphPad Software, EUA). 191 

All effects were tested using linear mixed-effects models (LMM). For DA, we 192 

fitted an LMM including the fixed factors Condition (Sal vs. DA) and DoseGroup (100 vs. 193 

500 nmol), with a random intercept for each subject (animal nested within dose). For 194 

APO, SKF, and QUIN, we modeled Dose (saline plus the respective drug dose) as a fixed 195 

factor, with random intercepts for animal and, when applicable, an additional random 196 

intercept for injection order. Post hoc comparisons were conducted using estimated 197 

marginal means (EMMs) with a Holm adjustment for multiple comparisons. The models 198 

were implemented in R (v4.5.2; R Core Team, 2025) and run in Google Colab (Google, 199 

n.d.), a hosted Jupyter notebook environment (Kluyver et al., 2016). 200

We next tested whether selective activation of D1- or D2-like receptors could 276 

reproduce the DA effects on HPC-PFC oscillatory activity at multiple doses. At theta 277 

frequencies, the selective D1 agonist SKF-38393 (1 or 10 μg) was able to modulate 278 

coherence (SKF vs. Sal - main effect: F (2, 20) =4.64, p=0.02, n rats =7; Fig. 5A, B, right), but 279 

not phase synchrony. However, this effect did not remain significant after adjustment 280 

(Holm-adjusted: p>0.05), suggesting that any differences were inconsistent across doses 281 

and not reliably different from saline. Overall, SKF was associated with non-significant 282 

modulation of theta relative power and HPC-PFC theta synchrony. 283 

The selective D2 agonist quinpirole (QUIN, 1 or 10 μg) similarly failed to induce 284 

reliable changes in HPC-PFC oscillatory activity. No consistent impact on theta relative 285 

power and on interareal synchrony measures was found, indicating no reliable effects 286 

produced by QUIN (Fig. 5C, D). 287 

In contrast to DA, which increased theta power and HPC-PFC synchrony, isolated 288 

stimulation of D 1 or D 2 did not amplify theta relative power or strengthen interareal 289 

synchrony. This may indicate that concurrent D 1/D 2 activation is required to reproduce 290 

the DA-induced effects. 291

Discussion 347 

In this study, we demonstrate that DA activation modulates HPC-PFC oscillatory 348 

dynamics under urethane anesthesia in a dose-dependent manner. Specifically, DA 349 

increases functional theta connectivity, enhances theta phase synchrony, and shifts PFC 350 

spike timing. Similarly, APO modulates the oscillatory activity. However, like the 351 

selective D 1 and D 2 receptor agonists it fails to reproduce the full spectrum of DA-352 

induced effects. 353 

Urethane anesthesia produces alternating activated and deactivated oscillatory 354 

states, whose activity patterns are similar to those observed in natural sleep states 355 

(Clement et al., 2008). During activated states, rapid low-amplitude theta oscillations 356 

and HPC-to-PFC 4 Hz synchronization predominate. In deactivated states, there are high-357 

amplitude slow delta oscillations and a PFC to HPC 1 Hz synchronization predominance 358 

(Ruggiero et al., 2018; Lopes-Aguiar et al., 2020). Experimental studies have shown that 359 

sleep-wake cycles (Oishi & Lazarus, 2017) and urethane anesthesia are both regulated 360 

by D 1- and D 2-like dopaminergic receptors (Monti & Jantos, 2008). Our results 361 

demonstrate that DA and the non-specific agonist apomorphine, but not the selective 362 

D1 or D 2 agonists, elicit dose-dependent alterations in urethane-induced oscillatory 363 

activity. 364 

Both DA 100 nmol and 500 nmol doses induced theta oscillations predominance 365 

for at least 10 continuous minutes, but DA 500 nmol effects were more pronounced, 366

indicating active DA participation in HPC-PFC theta modulation (Benchenane et al., 368 

2010; Ruggiero et al., 2021) and in sleep-wake-like systems (Oishi & Lazarus, 2017; Monti 369 

& Jantos, 2008). Interestingly, it was shown that acute DA depletion abolishes REM 370 

sleep, and REM can be recovered by D 2 receptor stimulation (Dzirasa et al., 2006). 371 

However, D 2-pathway activation is necessary but not sufficient by itself to generate REM 372 

sleep, and hyperdopaminergia is necessary but not sufficient to produce REM-like 373 

oscillations during wake in behaving DAT-KO mice (Dzirasa et al., 2006). This result 374 

indicates that the regulation of oscillatory states may also depend on the DA interaction 375 

with other neurotransmitter systems, such as the cholinergic system (Lester et al., 2010; 376 

Monti & Jantos, 2008). Indeed, cholinergic agonists can produce activated states, while 377 

cholinergic antagonists produce prolonged deactivated states (Clement et al., 2008; 378 

Ruggiero et al., 2018; Schall & Dickson, 2010). These results support the notion that 379 

dopaminergic and cholinergic systems may interact in regulating urethane-induced 380 

oscillatory states and the sleep-wake-like system (Dzirasa et al., 2006; Monti & Jantos, 381 

2008). 382 

We show that non-specific D 1/D 2 APO agonism has a dose-dependent effect on 383 

HPC-PFC oscillatory dynamics. In low doses, APO is known to produce sedative effects 384 

and increase the slow oscillations (Kropf & Kuschinsky, 1991; Monti & Jantos, 2008; Xu 385 

et al., 2011); high doses induce the opposite effect (Monti & Jantos, 2008). Our data 386 

confirm that low dose favored slower frequencies in the delta band and reduced theta 387 

predominance, while high dose induced theta-dominant activity. This effect is likely 388 

mediated by differential receptor mechanisms, with low doses preferentially activating 389 

dopaminergic autoreceptors, inhibiting neuronal firing, and decreasing DA release, 390

reduce dopaminergic firing rate (Assié et al., 2009; Bunney et al., 1973a; 1973b; Mallet 393 

et al., 2008; Xu et al., 2011), whereas higher doses can promote neuronal excitability 394

hyperdopaminergic state. Still, APO is reported to be D 2-biased (Dépatie & Lal, 2001), 396 

which may explain its partial divergence from DA in shaping HPC-PFC oscillations in our 397 

study. However, APO’s behavioral impact depends on intact D1 receptors (Ralph-398 

Williams et al., 2002), highlighting that concurrent D1/D2 agonism can have effects that 399 

depend on which receptors are available. 400 

We found that DA induced HPC-PFC theta synchrony and a phase shift in PFC 401 

neuronal activity. Consistent with this result, Benchenane et al. (2010) showed that DA 402 

infused into the PFC of anesthetized rats could induce HPC-PFC theta coherence and 403 

reorganize the preferred firing phase of PFC neurons relative to local theta cycles. 404 

However, to investigate HPC-PFC synchrony, Benchenane et al. (2010) relied solely on 405 

spectral coherence, which is biased by spectral power. The authors note that despite 406 

the increase in coherence, there is no alteration in spectral power in both HPC and PFC, 407 

suggesting that DA may induce phase synchrony independently of spectral power. By 408 

using dwPLI, a measure that quantifies consistent delays between signals and reduces 409 

zero-lag contamination due to volume conduction (Vinck et al., 2011), we showed that 410 

DA induced genuine HPC-PFC theta synchrony rather than just amplifying shared 411 

sources, and this effect was consistent across many animals following DA 412 

administration. 413

In our study, single-activation of D 1 or D 2 receptors by respective selective 414 

agonists was unable to induce significant effects. One possibility is that full dynamic 415 

dopaminergic modulation of HPC-PFC oscillatory synchrony requires concurrent 416 

activation of both D₁ and D₂ receptors to ensure a synergistic effect that no single-417 

receptor agonist could achieve. For instance, this activation is required by cortical D₁-D₂ 418 

receptor heteromers to drive specific cellular and synaptic responses (Xu & Yao, 2010; 419 

Thompson et al., 2016; Perreault et al., 2014). However, the literature shows the 420 

involvement of specific dopaminergic receptors in modulating HPC-PFC activity. Xu et al. 421 

(2016) demonstrated in anesthetized rats that the administration of a D 1 receptor 422 

antagonist could induce a reduction in HPC-PFC theta phase synchrony and theta power. 423 

Additionally, systemic administration of a D 1 receptor agonist induced HPC-PFC theta 424 

coherence in pharmacological animal models of schizophrenia, but not in control 425 

animals (Perreault et al., 2017). In another study, a reduction in HPC-PFC theta phase 426 

synchrony was observed in freely-moving animals after systemic D 2 receptor agonist or 427 

antagonist infusion (Gener et al., 2019). A likely explanation for why our results diverge 428 

from the literature regarding D 1 and D 2 effects is that the doses we used for selective 429 

agonists were suboptimal, and could mean insufficient receptor engagement at the low 430 

end, or activation of counter-regulatory mechanisms at the high end. Despite Perreault 431 

et al. (2017) and Gener et al. (2019) administering comparable agonist doses, it was 432 

delivered systemically, whereas we used the i.c.v. route, which may be related to the 433 

divergent findings. 434

U-shaped effects on cortical function, such that both hypo- and hyperdopaminergic 436 

states are associated with impaired cognition and only an intermediate range of 437

that urethane anesthesia neuromodulatory tone and state transitions differ from 449 

wakefulness. Thus, follow-up experiments in behaving animals are needed to generalize 450 

the results found in this study, and to better understand DA’s role in interareal 451 

synchronization across wakefulness states. 452 

Our findings link DA to long-range circuit-level communication, showing that DA 453 

can rapidly increase low-frequency synchrony in crucial cognitive networks (HPC-PFC), 454 

beyond simply changing local activity. In Parkinson’s disease, cognitive impairment has 455 

been associated with reduced midfrontal low-frequency rhythms, motivating the 456 

investigation of biomarkers and stimulation-based approaches to restore coordination 457 

(Singh et al., 2021; Narayanan et al., 2024). Our results suggest that DA loss may weaken 458 

not only low-frequency frontal oscillations but also long-range HPC-PFC coupling needed 459 

for cognitive performance. Clinically, this circuit-level interpretation aligns with the use 460 

of apomorphine in patient cohorts reporting improvements in aspects of non-motor 461

tuning the timing of interactions across circuits, making low-frequency coupling and 469 

spike timing potential targets for pharmacology and neuromodulation in DA-related 470 

brain disorders. 471 

Conclusion 472 

We demonstrate that DA dose-dependently reconfigures HPC-PFC network 473 

dynamics under urethane anesthesia, increasing theta power, enhancing theta phase 474 

synchrony, and strengthening prefrontal spike-theta locking. In contrast, selective D 1 or 475 

D2 agonism failed to reproduce these effects, and apomorphine produced a dose-476 

dependent modulation of oscillatory dynamics. Importantly, phase-coupling analyses 477 

that minimize zero-lag effects, thereby reducing volume-conduction and power-related 478 

confounds, support the interpretation that the observed synchrony reflects genuine 479 

interareal coupling, suggesting that DA rapidly regulates hippocampal-prefrontal 480 

communication. 481
