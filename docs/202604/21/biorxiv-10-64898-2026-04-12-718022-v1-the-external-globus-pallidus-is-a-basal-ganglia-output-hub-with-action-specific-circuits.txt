In brief: 28 

The external globus pallidus (GPe), classically considered a relatively homogeneous relay within 29 the indirect basal ganglia pathway, is revealed here as a major basal ganglia output hub. Distinct 30 GPe subpopulations, defined by transcriptomic identity and projection target, form parallel 31 channels to cortical, thalamic and brainstem structures with action-specific functions. These 32 findings uncover a disinhibitory mechanism through which D2-MSNs can facilitate behavior, 33 revising canonical models of basal ganglia function. 34 35

Abstract 42 

The external globus pallidus (GPe) is traditionally viewed as a homogeneous relay in the indirect 43 basal ganglia pathway that broadly suppresses movement. Using whole-brain anterograde axon 44 mapping, rabies tracing, single-neuron reconstruction, and single-nucleus RNA sequencing, we 45 reveal that the GPe is a major basal ganglia output nucleus, composed of anatomically and 46 molecularly distinct populations. Besides the neurons with canonical projections, and 47 projections to striatum and cortex, the GPe contains specific neuronal populations that target the 48 thalamus and brainstem directly, including the parafascicular thalamus (GPe Pf ) and the 49 pedunculopontine nucleus (GPe PPN ). These projection-defined subpopulations exhibit distinct 50 behavioral functions. GPe-PPN neurons are selectively suppressed at locomotor onset, and 51 bidirectional manipulation of this pathway is sufficient to promote or suppress locomotion. 52 Notably, D2-MSN stimulation upstream of these neurons evokes locomotion, and co-activation 53 of GPe PPN pathway blocks this D2-MSN-evoked locomotion, demonstrating a disinhibitory 54 circuit for action stemming from D2-MSNs. In contrast, GPe Pf neurons are not engaged during 55 locomotion but are selectively recruited during skilled forelimb actions, and their activation 56 disrupts forelimb movements without affecting locomotion. Together, these findings establish 57 the GPe as a basal ganglia output hub composed of modules that mediate distinct behaviors. This 58 organization revises canonical models of the indirect pathway by demonstrating that D2-MSNs 59 can also facilitate, rather than only suppress, movement depending on the downstream GPe 60 output channels they engage. 61 62 

Introduction 63 

The ability to execute specific movements in particular contexts is essential for survival. The 64 basal ganglia are essential for this process, and disorders of the basal ganglia result in profound 65 alterations of behavior and movement. Basal ganglia output nuclei, like globus pallidus internal 66 segment (GPi), and substantia nigra pars reticulata (SNr), are thought to inhibit thalamocortical 67 and brainstem motor centers through GABAergic output projections, thereby preventing 68 unwanted movements, and disinhibition of these centers facilitates movement 1-4 . According to 69 the canonical model, the striatum modulates these basal ganglia output nuclei via two major 70 pathways arising from distinct populations of striatal medium spiny neurons (MSN)¹. The D1-71 MSN direct pathway is postulated to facilitate movement by directly inhibiting GPi and SNr and 72 therefore disinhibiting thalamic and brainstem targets, whereas the D2-MSN indirect pathway 73 is proposed to suppress movement by increasing the activity of basal ganglia output nuclei 74 through its indirect polysynaptic projections via the external globus pallidus (GPe). This 75 classical framework assumes that GPe is a relatively homogeneous relay within the indirect 76 pathway 1-3,5 and that it primarily projects within the basal ganglia, to the subthalamic nucleus 77 (STN), the GPi and SNr. 78 However, accumulating evidence has challenged these assumptions. First, although some 79 studies have reported that stimulation of D1-MSNs promotes movement and stimulation of D2-80 MSNs inhibits movement, others have shown that both populations are active during movement 81 and that optogenetic stimulation of D2-MSNs can evoke movement and reinforcement 6-12 .82 Second, the GPe is increasingly recognized as a heterogeneous structure composed of distinct 83 neuron populations with substantial variability in molecular constitution, intrinsic properties and 84 firing dynamics 13-18 . Finally, accumulating evidence suggests that GPe neurons project not only 85 to downstream basal ganglia nuclei (prototypical) and back to the striatum (arkypallidal) but also 86 to targets outside the basal ganglia 19-22 .87

3These observations highlight the need for a comprehensive and unbiased characterization of 88 GPe output organization. To address this, we performed unbiased brain-wide mapping of the 89 output projections of GABAergic GPe neurons that receive input from the striatum. This 90 approach revealed a broad and previously underappreciated set of output targets, including 91 numerous regions outside the basal ganglia in the cortex, thalamus, and brainstem. We then 92 combined projection mapping with single-cell and single-nucleus transcriptomic profiling to 93 resolve the molecular diversity of these neurons, identifying seven transcriptomically defined 94 cell types with distinct soma locations and anatomical projection patterns. To confirm these 95 observations, we performed sparse labeling and complete (full-brain) reconstruction of 96 individual GPe neurons. These reconstructions revealed striking diversity in axonal architecture: 97 some neurons exhibited highly focused, target-specific projections, whereas others collateralized 98 broadly across multiple downstream regions. Importantly, the projection patterns of individual 99 neurons matched the transcriptomic and retrogradely traced subpopulations, providing 100 convergent evidence for the existence of distinct output circuits from the GPe. 101 Building on this framework, we interrogated if different GPe output circuits would have 102 different behavioral functions. We focused on two distinct populations of neurons directly 103 projecting to pedunculopontine nucleus (PPN) of the brainstem and parafascicular nucleus (Pf) 104 of the thalamus, as these regions have been traditionally viewed as being disinhibited by D1 105 MSNs via their direct projections to GPi and SNr. These two GPe output circuits exhibited 106 behaviorally specific engagement: projections to PPN were preferentially modulated during 107 locomotion, whereas projections to Pf were more strongly engaged during learned forelimb 108 actions. Projection-specific optogenetic gain- and loss-of-function experiments demonstrated 109 that GPe projections to PPN bidirectionally modulate locomotion. Critically, stimulation of D2-110 MSNs upstream of these neurons robustly evokes locomotion that is blocked by co-activation of 111 the GPe-PPN projections, uncovering a disinhibitory circuit for action directly emanating from 112 D2-MSNs. In contrast, activation of GPe projections to Pf selectively disrupts forelimb 113 movements without altering locomotion, demonstrating the behavioral specificity of these 114 circuits. 115 Together, these experiments redefine the GPe as a major hub composed of molecularly and 116 anatomically defined distinct cell types that project not only within the basal ganglia but also to 117 regions of the cortex, thalamus and brainstem. These populations specifically and directly 118 modulate motor centers in these regions demonstrating that D2-MSNs can give rise to “direct 119 pathways” that disinhibit motor centers. 120 121

To better understand the function of GPe and how it relates to the canonical model of basal 125 ganglia function, there is a strong need for a comprehensive, unbiased characterization of GPe 126 anatomical projections, in particular for the GPe neurons that receive striatal input. Therefore, 127 to identify and quantify GPe output patterns across the brain, we performed brain-wide 128 projection mapping of GPe neurons. We used an intersectional viral labeling strategy combined 129 with BrainJ 23 , a machine learning/based pipeline for pixel classification and mesoscale axonal 130 mapping. Specifically, we targeted GABAergic GPe neurons receiving input from the 131 dorsolateral striatum (DLS), the sensorimotor region of the striatum, by injecting an anterograde 132 adeno-associated virus (AAV) encoding Flpo recombinase into the left DLS of vGat -Cre mice 133

Transcriptomic heterogeneity and novel GABAergic cell types in GPe 205 Previous studies have suggested that the GPe is composed of different cell types based on their 206 transcriptomic profiles 13,25-27 . We therefore combined unbiased single-nucleus RNA sequencing 207 (snRNA-seq) and projection-specific snRNA-seq (Retro-seq) to define the transcriptomic 208 profiles of GABAergic GPe projection neurons 28,29 . After quality control and exclusion of non-neuronal and 223 cholinergic (ChAT⁺) clusters, we identified seven different GABAergic neuronal clusters in the 224 GPe, more than previously acknowledged, and differentially expressed genes across the clusters 225 (Figures 2B, 2D, and S2B–S2F ). This final GABAergic neuron subset contained 3,634 neurons 226 (173 from 10x Genomics v3.1 snRNA-seq, 879 from 10x Genomics snMultiome, and 2582 from 227 Retro-SMART-seq). 228 We sought to identify new molecular markers emerging from our dataset that could define 229 individual transcriptomic clusters. Differential expression analysis revealed that Etv1 , Cdh18, 230 

Mboat1, Tac2, Meis2, Pappa and Meis2/Cyp26b1/Npy are reliable markers for clusters 1 through 231 7, respectively ( Figures 2D and 2E ), some of which were previously identified 25-27 . These 232 markers can be coupled with established markers for GPe neural cell types to define a variety of 233 cell subgroupings. We then characterized the expression patterns of canonical GPe markers, 234 including Pvalb , Nkx2.1 , Lhx6 , Npas1 , Foxp2 , Grik3 , and Penk (Figure 2F ). Although Pvalb 235 and Npas1 have been described as defining mutually exclusive cell types 13,15,17,20,27 , our data 236 revealed extensive overlap between these markers, particularly in cluster 3_GPe_Mboat1 and 237 6_GPe_Meis2 ( Figures 2F , S2F , and S2G ). Moreover, Pvalb expression varied along a 238 continuum and could be stratified into high, medium, and low levels ( Figures S2F and S2G ). 239 We also observed substantial co-expression of Pvalb with Nkx2.1 and Lhx6 (Figures S2F and 240 

S2G ), further challenging the notion that these markers define strictly segregated molecular or 241 functional GPe subtypes 30 . The classically-defined prototypical neurons fall into cluster 242 1_GPe_Pvalb ( Pvalb+/Nkx2-1+/Npas1-), while the classically-defined arkypallidal neurons 243 likely belong to clusters 6_GPe_Meis2 and 7_GPe_Meis2_Npy ( Figure 2B ). Together, these 244 transcriptomic results define seven distinct GABAergic GPe cell types, including two clusters 245 corresponding to the classically described prototypical and arkypallidal neurons 25 . The 246 increased resolution helped reveal further sub-structure in the other clusters beyond what was 247 previously described 25 . For instance, we subdivide Saunders “subcluster 2-17” into clusters 248 2_GPe_Cdh18 and 3_GPe_Mboat1 and divide “subcluster 2-18” into clusters 4_GPe_Tac2 and 249 5_GPe_Pappa. We do not find support for subclusters 2-16, 2-15, or 2-21 ( Figure S2K ), which 250 fell into our integrated clusters 4 and 7 and are outliers characterized by poor QC characteristics 251 (not shown). Conversely, we assign “prototypical” GPe neurons to one cluster (1_GPe_Pvalb), 252 while Saunders et al. assigned it to subclusters 2-13 and 2-14 ( Figure S2L ). Together, these 253 analyses establish a high-resolution transcriptomic atlas of GPe projection neurons, identifying 254 seven GABAergic cell types, linking projection-defined populations to specific molecular 255 clusters, and revealing greater molecular diversity within the GPe than previously appreciated. 256

GPe projection to PPN but not Pf modulates locomotion 542 Previous studies have identified the PPN as a locomotor command center responsible for 543 initiating and maintaining locomotion, while the Pf has been implicated in the execution of 544 learned forelimb action sequences 38-41 . Classic basal ganglia output structures SNr and GPi are 545 thought to exert tonic inhibitory control over their downstream targets and can disinhibit them 546 by reducing their firing rates 6,42,43 . To investigate how GPe projections to the PPN and Pf are 547 engaged during behavior and whether they decrease their activity to relieve inhibition of their 548 targets, we monitored the activity of GPe Pf and GPe PPN neurons during open field exploration. 549 To achieve this, we expressed axon-targeted GCaMP6f in the GPe and implanted optical fibers 550 above the axonal terminals of GPe projections to the PPN and Pf ( Figures 5A–5C ), enabling 551 projection-specific fiber photometry recordings 44 . We collected continuous inertial sensor data 552 and applied affinity propagation to identify behavioral clusters during open field 45,46 . To quantify 553 how GPe outputs to the PPN and Pf are modulated during locomotion, we aligned calcium 554 signals to the onset of locomotor bouts, defined as 600-ms epochs corresponding to two 555 consecutive locomotion clusters (300 ms per cluster bin) identified through unsupervised 556 behavioral clustering. We observed a robust decrease in the activity of GPe axons projecting to 557 the PPN at the onset of locomotion ( Figure 5D ), consistent with a disinhibitory mechanism 558 facilitating movement initiation. Direct statistical comparison revealed that GPe→PPN axons 559 showed a significantly greater decrease in activity at locomotor onset than GPe→Pf axons 560 (Figures 5E and 5F ). 561

Our imaging and functional perturbation studies revealed a projection- and action-specific role 951 for GPe→PPN outputs in locomotion: only GPe→PPN activity decreased at the onset of 952 locomotion, and only suppression of GPe→PPN (but not GPe→Pf) facilitated locomotion 953 initiation and execution ( Figure 5 ). Prior studies have implicated the Pf in skilled motor 954 behaviors, including the execution of learned forelimb action sequences 38 . We therefore 955 investigated how the activity of GPe axons projecting to Pf and PPN are modulated during the 956 performance of a trained forelimb lever-press sequence, and whether these projections show 957 differential activity patterns aligned to the execution of learned, skilled forelimb behavior. We 958 used the same cohort of mice (n = 5) in which axon-targeted GCaMP6f was expressed in the 959 GPe, and optical fibers were implanted above GPe axonal terminals in the PPN and Pf ( Figure 960 

7A ). Discussion 1058 

Our findings redefine the functional role of the GPe within the basal ganglia circuitry. 1059 Traditionally, the GPe has been viewed as an intermediate node in the indirect pathway, 1060 receiving inhibitory input from D2-MSNs and sending inhibitory projections to the STN as well 1061 as to the basal ganglia output nuclei GPi and SNr. In contrast, our results reveal that the GPe is 1062 a major hub with vast and diverse projections beyond the basal ganglia, targeting a broad array 1063 of cortical, thalamic, and brainstem structures. Furthermore, our results reveal that GABAergic 1064 projection neurons in GPe can be classified into at least seven molecularly distinct cell types, of 1065 which only one completely matches the prototypical neurons of the canonical model. Single-1066 neuron reconstructions further resolved the diverse and distinct projection patterns of GPe 1067 neurons. We show that GPe projections to PPN and Pf directly and specifically modulate 1068 locomotion and forelimb movements. Furthermore, our optogenetic manipulations targeting 1069 different stages of the basal ganglia signal flow, including input alone (D2-MSNs in the DLS), 1070 specific GPe output pathways, or combined manipulation of basal ganglia input and defined GPe 1071 output channels, provide strong evidence for the existence of direct disinhibitory circuits linking 1072 D2-MSNs to brainstem and thalamic targets. These results call for a conceptual revision of basal 1073 ganglia organization, integrating both classical intranuclear relay circuits and novel extrinsic 1074 output modules, where GPe, GPi and SNr can all be output inhibitory circuits, and where both 1075 D1 and D2-MSNs can disinhibit downstream circuits with specific behavioral functions. 1076 1077

Transcriptomic and Anatomical Logic of Modular GPe Organization 1102 

By integrating projection-specific single-nucleus RNA sequencing, retrograde labeling, and 1103 soma localization, we establish a comprehensive molecular and anatomical framework for the 1104 modular organization of the GPe. Single-cell and projection-defined transcriptomic analyses 1105 reveal that GPe GABAergic neurons are molecularly diverse with at least 7 clusters, only one of 1106 which corresponds entirely to the classical prototypical neurons. Distinct transcriptional clusters 1107 align with specific projection patterns and soma localization, forming discrete subpopulations 1108 with distinct projection combinations. The convergence of molecular identity, projection 1109 topology, and soma positioning suggests a genetically determined blueprint for parallel output 1110 channels within the pallidum. 1111 GPe neurons projecting to Pf in thalamus and the PPN in brainstem form largely non-1112 overlapping, spatially segregated populations, consistent with our retrograde tracing, 3D 1113 reconstruction, and soma registration to the Allen Brain Atlas. Single-neuron reconstructions 1114 further confirm that these neurons exhibit highly specific axonal arborization with minimal 1115 collateralization, establishing that GPe outputs are organized into parallel, functionally 1116 specialized inhibitory circuits rather than diffuse networks. This spatial segregation is 1117 complemented by projection-specific gene expression programs, indicating that molecular 1118 identity predicts both connectivity and anatomical position. 1119 Importantly, our transcriptomic analysis reveals that classical molecular markers such as 1120

Projection- and Action-Specific Encoding in GPe Outputs 1153 

Our functional imaging and perturbation experiments reveal that the GPe is organized into 1154 projection- and action-specific modules, each tuned to distinct behavioral domains. Projection-1155 specific calcium imaging and closed-loop optogenetic manipulations show that GPe→PPN 1156 neurons decrease their activity at locomotor onset, and that suppressing their output facilitates 1157 movement initiation through a disinhibitory mechanism. In contrast, GPe→Pf neurons exhibit 1158 minimal modulation during locomotion but display robust decreases during the initiation and 1159 execution of learned forelimb action sequences. Thus, the GPe governs locomotor and skilled 1160 forelimb behaviors through anatomically segregated inhibitory pathways that act as parallel 1161 output channels for behaviorally distinct actions. 1162 Monosynaptic rabies tracing further reveals that these output-defined GPe populations 1163 receive inputs from distinct subsets of D2-MSNs in striatum. This input specificity indicates that 1164 the indirect pathway is not a uniform, movement-suppressing stream but a modular network of 1165 separable input–output channels tuned to different motor functions. Subsets of D2-MSNs 1166 preferentially engage the GPe→PPN circuit to modulate locomotor control, whereas others 1167 target the GPe→Pf circuit to shape the sequencing and precision of forelimb actions. 1168 Together, these findings demonstrate a fine-grained, behavior-specific wiring logic within 1169 the D2-MSNs pathway, establishing that D2-MSNs can facilitate or suppress movement 1170 depending on the output module they engage. A Revised View of D2-MSN Pathway Function 1176 

Our findings support a conceptual revision of the D2-MSN pathway. Rather than a single 1177 inhibitory stream that uniformly suppresses movement, the D2-MSN→GPe circuit comprises 1178 multiple projection-defined subcircuits that shape motor output through target-specific 1179 disinhibition. Within this modular framework, distinct subsets of D2-MSNs recruit discrete GPe 1180 output channels to control separate behavioral domains. The D2-MSN→GPe→PPN route 1181 operates as a disinhibitory loop that facilitates locomotion by transiently relieving GPe-mediated 1182 inhibition of brainstem motor centers, whereas the D2-MSN→GPe→Pf pathway regulates the 1183 precision and sequencing of skilled forelimb actions through dynamic inhibition of 1184 thalamocortical circuits. This division of labor redefines the indirect pathway as a flexible, 1185 context-dependent network capable of both facilitating and constraining movement, depending 1186 on which GPe module is engaged. The discovery of parallel disinhibitory subcircuits thus 1187 reconciles the long-standing paradox that D2-MSNs can both promote and suppress movement, 1188 providing a mechanistic framework for adaptive action selection 6-8,10,11 .1189 Finally, this modular redefinition of the GPe shifts its conceptual role within the basal 1190 ganglia from an intermediate relay to a flexible output control hub where striatal, pallidal, and 1191 subcortical computations converge, and information is then broadcasted out through specific 1192 channels. Future studies should dissect the synaptic and neuromodulatory mechanisms 1193 governing plasticity within D2-MSNs→GPe pathways and determine how dopaminergic tone, 1194 behavioral context, and learning dynamically rebalance activity across distinct GPe output 1195 modules in health, disease, and recovery. 1196 1197 

Implications for basal ganglia disorders 1198 

These insights also have direct relevance to the pathophysiology of Parkinson’s disease. 1199 Differential vulnerability or maladaptive plasticity across GPe output modules could underlie 1200 the heterogeneity of motor symptoms. Hypofunction of the GPe→PPN channel may contribute 1201 to gait freezing and akinesia, whereas disruption of the GPe→Pf pathway could impair fine 1202 motor coordination and action sequencing. Importantly, our transcriptomic analyses further 1203 revealed that these projection-defined pathways correspond to molecularly distinct GPe neuron 1204 populations, linking specific projection targets to defined transcriptional identities. More 1205 broadly, the identification of molecularly and projection-defined GPe modules provides a 1206 blueprint for circuit-level therapeutic strategies, including targeted neuromodulation, 1207 chemogenetic, or optogenetic interventions designed to selectively restore function within 1208 specific disinhibitory channels rather than globally modulate basal ganglia activity. 1209 1210 1211 1212 1213 1214 1215 1216 1217 1218

Image processing, anatomical reconstructions, and analysis 1860 

Automated reconstruction of slide-scanned images was performed in ImageJ/Fiji using BrainJ 1861 as previously described 20 . BrainJ is an ImageJ-based analysis pipeline that enables high-1862 throughput processing, registration, and quantification of whole-brain histological datasets 20 .1863 Briefly, brain sections were aligned and registered using two-dimensional rigid-body 1864 registration 38 . A seven-pixel rolling-ball filter was applied to reduce background fluorescence. 1865 Machine-learning-based pixel classification was performed using Ilastik to identify cell bodies 1866 and neuronal processes 39 . Background-subtracted images from each fluorescence channel were 1867 imported into Ilastik, and representative examples of cell bodies, neurites, and background signal 1868 were manually annotated to train the classifier across all sections. Probabilistic classification 1869 outputs were inspected using live preview to ensure segmentation accuracy. The trained models 1870 were then applied to generate probability maps for each fluorescence channel, which were 1871 subsequently used for segmentation of labeled cells and processes. To assign anatomical 1872 location, three-dimensional registration was performed using Elastix relative to a reference 1873 brain 39 . Coordinates of detected cells and processes were projected into the Allen Brain Atlas 1874 Common Coordinate Framework 40 . Data visualization was performed using ImageJ and 1875 Brainrender 21 , and quantitative analyses were conducted using custom MATLAB and Python 1876 scripts. 1877 To quantitatively define novel GPe projection targets, we calculated projection density for 1878 each annotated brain region as the total GPe axonal projection volume within that region divided 1879 by the region’s total volume. Projection density values were computed for each brain and then 1880 averaged across animals (n = 7 mice). A region was classified as a GPe target if its mean 1881

Calculating the WNM projection matrix 2031 

CCF-registered reconstructions were translated such that all somas were positioned in the left 2032 hemisphere. Determining neuron projection groups and visualizing projection data 2040 

Neurons located within 20μm of the GPe border were excluded from analysis, as were neurons 2041 from Chat, Tle4, and Gnb4 Cre lines due to border proximity, cholinergic characteristic, or 2042 aberrant projection patterns. This left 20 reconstructed neurons with soma localized to the GPe. 2043 Ipsilateral and contralateral axon lengths were summed for each brain region from the branch/tip 2044 masked projection matrix, so as not to count pass-through neurons. Total neuron length was 2045 calculated from the un-masked matrix. The branch/tip projection matrix was normalized by each 2046 neuron’s total axonal length. Principal component analysis (PCA) was performed on the 2047 normalized matrix, and the first 10 components were retained. Neurons were clustered using 2048 hierarchical clustering with Ward’s D2 linkage based on the PCA scores, with the optimal cluster 2049 number (k = 4) determined by inspecting the within-cluster sum of squares elbow plot. Brain 2050 regions were organized at multiple hierarchical levels using the Allen Mouse Brain Common 2051 Coordinate Framework (CCF 2020) parcellation: projection targets within the striatum, 2052 midbrain, and thalamus were represented at the “child” structure level, while other regions were 2053 collapsed to their “parent” structure. Unsupervised behavior clustering 2096 

To unbiasedly identify the natural action repertoire of individual mice, we performed 2097 unsupervised behavioral classification using affinity propagation applied to IMU sensor data, 2098 following a framework similar to previously described approaches 10,35,41 . In contrast to the 2099 original implementation described by Klaus et al. study 10 , which used three behavioral features 2100 derived from accelerometer and video measurements (total body acceleration, gravitational 2101 acceleration along the anterior–posterior axis, and video-derived head–body rotation), our 2102 analysis incorporated four features extracted directly from IMU signals to capture additional 2103 aspects of body dynamics. Four features were extracted from the sensor data: 1) gravitational 2104 acceleration along the anterior–posterior axis for the discrimination of postural changes, GAap; 2105 (2) Raw sensor acceleration along the dorsal–ventral axis to quantify movement momentum, 2106 ACCdv; (3) Dorsal–ventral axis of the gyroscope to extract head head–body rotational 2107 information, GYRdv; (4) Total body acceleration to differentiate the resting state from 2108 movement.
