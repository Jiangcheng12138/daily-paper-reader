Abstract 24 

Background 25 

Neurodevelopmental impairment remains common in neonatal hypoxic –ischemic 26 

encephalopathy (HIE) despite treatment with the standard of care, therapeutic hypothermia (TH). 27 

The complement response activates at reperfusion and is known to exacerbate 28 

neuroinflammation and injury, though its full role and interaction with hypothermia are 29 

incompletely defined. We hypothesized that modulating the complement response could improve 30 

structural and functional outcomes in HIE, and tested a novel complement therapy (CT), 31 

consisting of C3a peptides and the C5a -receptor antagonist PMX205, as both a stand -alone 32 

treatment and as an adjuvant to TH. 33 

Methods 34 

Wistar rat pups were randomized to the following treatment groups: Sham (uninjured control), 35 

NT (uninjured, normothermia/not treated control), or injured and treated with either TH, CT, or 36 

CT+TH. At term -equivalence, mild -moderate hypoxic -ischemic injury was induced by 37 

Vannucci’s method. To capture the short and long -term effects of the treatments, cohorts were 38 

harvested 3 or 66 -72 days post -injury, respectively. Cerebral injury was measured by quantifying 39 

levels of inflammatory markers and cerebral tissue loss, and functional outcomes were assessed 40 

in a series of behavioral tests. The data were stratified to detect sexual dimorphisms. 41 

Results 42 

CT and TH treatments demonstrated test and sex -dependent differences in improvement 43 

compared to untreated, injured rats. In male rats, TH treatment worsened long -term hippocampal 44 

and thalamic brain injury and functional measures of ataxia and attention. CT -treatment 45 

worsened long -term thalamic loss in females. Conclusions 49 

Complement modulation enhances the neuroprotective effects of TH after neonatal hypoxic –50

modulation with the standard of care often demonstrated synergistic improvement in both sexes, 52 

supporting complement -targeted therapy as a promising adjunct to hypothermia in neonatal HIE. 53

Introduction 71 

Neonatal hypoxic -ischemic encephalopathy (HIE) remains a critical global health 72 

challenge 1-4, with therapeutic hypothermia (TH) serving as the sole evidence -based 73 

neuroprotective intervention 5. TH reduces mortality and improves early childhood outcomes 74 

among survivors, yet significant residual disability persists, and no adjunct treatments to TH 75 

have been clinically validated 6,7 . This therapeutic stagnation underscores the urgent need to 76 

unravel HIE's molecular complexity to identify customized novel, mechanistically driven 77 

interventions 8.78 

Recent clinical trials highlight both the limitations of current approaches and the 79 

necessity for mechanistic innovation. The HELIX trial 9 revealed TH's inefficacy in low - and 80 

middle -income countries (LMICs), where 50% of cooled infants still died or developed 81 

disabilities, potentially due to distinct injury patterns from subacute hypoxia in out born 82 

populations. Conversely, the HEAL trial 10 explored erythropoietin as an adjuvant to TH but 83 

faced challenges in demonstrating clear efficacy, reflecting the difficulty of translating preclinical 84 

neuroprotection strategies to heterogeneous clinical populations. These trials collectively 85 

emphasize that improving outcomes requires moving beyond empiric combination therapies 86 

toward interventions grounded in precise pathophysiological insights. 87 

Emerging research on regulated cell death mechanisms —including necroptosis, 88 

pyroptosis, and ferroptosis —further illustrates HIE's biological complexity, suggesting that 89 

combinatorial approaches targeting multiple injury phases may be essential 11,12 . The 90 

complement system exemplifies a mechanistically rational target for development of a 91 

therapeutic adjunct for TH. While complement activation exacerbates blood -brain barrier 92 

disruption and neuroinflammation post -HIE 13 -17 , its dual role in neurodevelopmental processes 93 .CC-BY-NC 4.0 International license available under a (which was not certified by peer review) is the author/funder, who has granted bioRxiv a license to display the preprint in perpetuity. is a potent anaphylatoxin and engagement of C5a with its receptor C5aR1 induces microglial 95 

activation, neuronal apoptosis and sustained neuroinflammation 23 -25 . HIE increases systemic 96

been shown to stimulate neurogenesis and neuroplasticity following ischemic brain injury, and 101 

elevating C3a levels by the intranasal route decreases neurodegeneration and gliosis 30 -33 .102 

Integrating mechanistic discoveries with clinically adaptable delivery systems could 103 

enable therapies that synergize with TH's metabolic suppression while preserving 104 

neurodevelopmental trajectories. We hypothesized that neurological damage can be mitigated in 105 

neonates following hypoxic ischemic insult by concomitantly blocking C5a -C5aR1 interaction 106 

and increasing cranial levels of C3a, and that this combination therapy will synergize with TH to 107 

improve functional and structural outcomes. Utilizing this targeted, nuanced approach in a 108 

rigorous preclinical model that replicate human disease heterogeneity may aid in bridging the 109 

gap between bench and bedside 9.110

Hypoxic -ischemic injury 140 

Mild -moderate HIE was induced at P10 -12 with Vannucci’s method 35 as described 141 

previously 20,36 . Briefly, pups were anesthetized by isoflurane inhalation (3% induction, 1% 142 

maintenance) and ischemia was induced by permanent ligation of the right common carotid 143 

artery (4 -0 silk suture), at two sites 2 -5 mm apart. Sham rats received anesthesia, incision, and 144 

dissection but no ligation. Incisions were sealed with VetBond and animals were allowed to 145 

recover (~10 min) returning to the dam for 1 hour to rest and feed. For hypoxia, the ligated 146 

animals were placed in a custom chamber (BioSpherix) balanced to 8% oxygen with nitrogen gas 147 

at 37°C for 60 min (including a 10 min period where the chamber returns to 8% O 2 following 148 

opening to place the animals inside). To control for this time away from the dam, Sham rats were 149 

placed in an identical chamber at ambient oxygenic conditions at 37°C. 150 

Complement treatment 151 

PMX205 trifluoroacetate salt (Cayman Chemical) was reconstituted in 5% ethanol then 152 

diluted to 0.25 μg/μL in PBS 37 . This solution was injected subcutaneously 37 into the neck scruff 153 

for a total dose of 1 mg/kg. C3a peptides (Complement Technology, Inc.) were serially -diluted to 154 

0.008 μg/μL in PBS and slowly pipetted into each nostril, as by Pekna and colleagues 38 , for a 155 

total dose of 2.56 μg/kg. Both drugs were adjusted for variations in pup weight, warmed to 156 

~37°C, and administered as a one -time dose immediately following recovery from the hypoxia 157 

procedure; Sham, NT, and TH animals received vehicle control inoculations. 158

Long -term brain injury was more pronounced than at short -term, as evidenced in increased tissue 349

Discussion 360 

In this study, we demonstrate that targeted complement modulation enhances the 361 

neuroprotective effects of TH following neonatal HI -injury. Using a clinically relevant rodent 362 

model, we show that complement -targeted therapy (CT), particularly when combined with TH 363 

(CT+TH), reduces early structural brain injury (Fig. 2), attenuates neuroinflammation (Fig. 3), 364 

and improves long -term functional and neuroanatomic outcomes (Figs 4 &5). These findings 365 

identify complement -driven immune signaling as a sustained contributor to HI -brain injury and 366 

support complement modulation as a promising adjunct to hypothermia. 367 .CC-BY-NC 4.0 International license available under a (which was not certified by peer review) is the author/funder, who has granted bioRxiv a license to display the preprint in perpetuity. A principal finding of this work is that the effects of complement activation continue 368 

beyond the acute phase of injury and contribute to secondary tissue loss. At 3 dpi, untreated 369 

animals exhibited substantial hemispheric, cortical, hippocampal, and thalamic area loss (Fig. 2), 370 

accompanied by increased apoptosis, microglial activation, astrogliosis, neuronal loss, and 371 

elevated C5aR1 expression (Fig. 3). Complement -targeted therapy, particularly when combined 372 

with hypothermia, reduced these markers of neural degeneration and inflammation, supporting a 373 

role for complement signaling in amplifying secondary injury cascades, and are consistent with 374 

prior studies implicating C5a –C5aR1 signaling in HI -injury and inflammatory neurodegeneration 375

.376 

TH remains the SOC for neonatal HIE; however, its neuroprotective efficacy is 377 

incomplete, with nearly half of treated infants dying or surviving with adverse 378 

neurodevelopmental outcomes 5,72,73 . Experimental and clinical evidence suggests hypothermia 379 

incompletely suppresses immune and inflammatory signaling, particularly during the subacute 380 

phase of injury 74 -76 . Our data support this concept, as hypothermia alone modestly reduced 381 

injury but did not fully attenuate inflammatory markers or prevent long -term tissue loss (Figs 382 

2,3,5). 383 

CT reduced inflammatory signaling even in the absence of hypothermia, and combined 384 

CT+TH treatment overall produced the most favorable outcomes across multiple measures. 385 

These findings suggest that complement signaling operates through pathways not fully addressed 386 

by temperature reduction alone and that combined targeting of metabolic and immune injury 387 

mechanisms may be necessary to achieve optimal neuroprotection. 388

Functional outcomes further support the neuroprotective benefit of complement 400

demonstrated improvements in motor coordination and complex cognitive tasks such as food 402 

protection (Fig. 4) These behaviors reflect integrated sensorimotor and cognitive function and 403 

correlate with preservation of cortical, hippocampal, and thalamic structures. Prior work has 404 

shown that injury to these regions following neonatal HIE predicts long -term deficits in 405 

executive function, learning, and memory 5,36,65,80 . Notably, functional improvements in our 406 

study were observed weeks after injury, suggesting that early modulation of complement 407 

signaling produces enduring benefits extending into later developmental stages. 408 

Long -term neuroanatomic analysis revealed that untreated HI -injury results in 409 

progressive tissue loss extending into adolescence (Supplementary Fig. 2), consistent with 410 

ongoing tertiary injury mechanisms such as chronic inflammation, gliosis, and impaired synaptic 411 

remodeling 81 -83 . CT reduced chronic cortical, hippocampal, and thalamic atrophy (Fig. 2,5), 412

This study has several limitations. While the rodent model recapitulates key features of 416 

neonatal HIE, species differences in brain maturation and immune development limit direct 417 

extrapolation to human infants. However, the Vannucci model has been extensively characterized 418 

and validated over the past 40 years for neonatal HIE 35 . Additionally, although PMX205 and 419 

C3a peptides were selected based on prior efficacy and mechanistic rationale 31,65 , the optimal 420 

dosing, timing, and duration of complement modulation continues to be refined. Finally, while 421 

behavioral testing provides functional relevance, additional longer -term assessments into 422 

adulthood will be necessary to fully characterize cognitive and neuropsychiatric outcomes. 423 

Despite these limitations, our findings provide evidence that complement -driven immune 424 

dysregulation contributes meaningfully to neonatal HIE injury and that targeted complement 425 

modulation enhances the neuroprotective effects of TH. By attenuating neuroinflammation, 426 

preserving neuronal integrity, and improving long -term functional outcomes, complement -427 

targeted therapy represents a promising adjunctive strategy to current standards of care. Future 428
