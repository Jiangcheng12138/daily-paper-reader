Abstract Patterns of locus coeruleus (LC) activity and norepinephrine (NE) release during 8

non-rapid-eye-movement (NREM) sleep suggest a critical role for the LC–NE system in offline 9

modulation of forebrain circuits. NE transmission promotes synaptic plasticity and is required for 10 

memory consolidation, but the field has only begun to uncover how LC activity contributes to 11 

coordinated forebrain network dynamics. Hippocampal ripples, a hallmark of memory replay, are 12 

temporally coupled with thalamocortical oscillations; however, the circuit mechanisms underlying 13 

systems-level consolidation across larger brain networks remain incompletely understood. Here, 14 

using multi-site electrophysiology, we examined LC firing in relation to hippocampal ripples in 15 

freely behaving rats. LC activity and ripple occurrence were state-dependent and inversely 16 

related: heightened arousal was associated with increased LC firing and reduced ripple rates. At 17 

finer timescales, LC spiking decreased ∼1–2 seconds before ripple onset, with the strongest 18 

modulation during awake ripples but minimal change during ripple–spindle coupling. These 19 

findings reveal state-dependent dynamics of LC-hippocampal interactions, positioning the LC as a 20 

key component of a cortical–subcortical network supporting systems-level memory consolidation. 21 22 

## Introduction 23 

Norepinephrine (NE) modulation of the forebrain circuits supporting cognition has long been con-24 

sidered to play a role during vigilant states ( Sara, 2009 ; Sara and Bouret, 2012 ), whereas its impact 25 

during low arousal (or ‘offline’) states, including sleep, has received less attention. The pioneering 26 

discovery of greatly reduced firing of the locus coeruleus (LC) neurons during sleep ( Aston-Jones 27 

and Bloom, 1981 ), for decades, directed research focus toward the role of LC, as a part of the as-28 

cending arousal system, for ’online’ information processing. At the same time, pharmacological 29 

studies in both animals ( Sara et al., 1999 ; Roullet and Sara, 1998 ; Clayton and Williams, 2000 ; Ga-30 

leotti et al., 2004 ; Gazarini et al., 2013 ) and humans ( Groch et al., 2011 ; Gais et al., 2011 ; Kuffel 31 

et al., 2014 ; Cahill et al., 1994 ) have demonstrated the importance of post-learning NE transmis-32 

sion for memory consolidation. The results of pharmacology studies were consistent with a well-33 

established facilitatory role of NE for synaptic plasticity, which also takes place offline ( Straube and 34 

Frey, 2003 ; Gelinas and Nguyen, 2005 ; Harley, 2007 ; Hansen and Manahan-Vaughan, 2015 ; Hagena 35 

et al., 2016 ; Palacios-Filardo and Mellor, 2019 ). 36 

During offline states such as awake immobility and non-rapid eye movement (NREM) sleep, 37 

hippocampal activity is marked by transient episodes of synchronized firing, detected in CA1 lo-38 

cal field potentials (LFPs) as high-frequency ∼150 Hz oscillations known as ripples Buzsaki (1996 ). 39 

Hippocampal ripples, a hallmark of memory replay, are temporally coupled with thalamocortical 40

In recent years, it became evident that ripples reflect not only a coordinated local activity within 43 

the hippocampus but also indicate the emergence of large-scale functional networks ( Logothetis 44 

et al., 2012 ; Nitzan et al., 2022 ). The ripple-associated cross-regional communication may occur 45 

through a coordinated up/down-regulation of neural activity across cortical and subcortical struc-46 

tures, including neuromodulatory centers ( Skelin et al., 2018 ; Maingret et al., 2016 ; Brodt et al., 47 

2023 ). Several studies have demonstrated temporally coordinated cross-regional neuronal activity 48 

around ripples, involving the amygdala ( Girardeau et al., 2017 ), the ventral tegmental area ( Gom-49 

perts et al., 2015 ), the median raphe ( Wang et al., 2015 ), and the thalamus ( Logothetis, 2015 ; Yang 50 

et al., 2019 ; Varela et al., 2001 ). 51 

While some indirect evidence suggests a link ( Logothetis et al., 2012 ), the relationship between 52 

LC neuron spiking and hippocampal ripples has not been directly characterized. For example, a 53 

study in the murine slice showed that increasing NE concentration increased ripple incidence and 54 

their amplitudes ( Ul Haq et al., 2012 ). Consistently, in behaving rats, we showed that pharmaco-55 

logical suppression of NE transmission decreased the occurrence of ripples and impaired spatial 56 

memory consolidation ( Duran et al., 2023 ). Finally, experimentally induced LC activation affected 57 

the temporal pattern of ripple occurrence ( Novitskaya et al., 2016 ; Swift et al., 2018 ). Earlier stud-58 

ies have shown close temporal relations between LC and sleep spindles ( Aston-Jones and Bloom, 59 

1981 ; Swift et al., 2018 ; Osorio-Forero et al., 2021 ; Kjaerby et al., 2022 ). In our previous work, we 60 

have shown that firing of a subpopulation of LC neurons was temporally coordinated with slow 61 

oscillations ( Eschenko et al., 2012 ; Totah et al., 2018 ), which in turn orchestrate spindle and ripple 62 

activity ( Molle et al., 2006 ). 63 

In the present study, we characterized the ripple-associated LC firing patterns in freely behav-64 

ing rats. We report that LC activity was anticorrelated with the ripple rate at both large and fine 65 

temporal scales. Our results also provide evidence for a state-dependent engagement of the LC in 66 

offline memory processing and hippocampal-cortical communication. 67 

## Results 68 

We analyzed a total of 20 recording sessions (n = 7 rats, 2 to 8 sessions per rat with an average 69 

duration of 6458.48 ± 190.43 sec), including two datasets (n = 1 rat) that contributed to our previous 70 

publication ( Eschenko et al., 2012 ). All datasets contained simultaneously recorded extracellular 71 

spikes of putative LC-NE neurons, local field potentials (LFPs) from the CA1 subfield of the dorsal 72 

hippocampus (HPC), and frontal EEG (Figure 1). The recordings were performed in freely behaving 73 

adult male rats placed in a small chamber (30 x 30 x 40 cm) in dim light during the dark phase 74 

of the animal’s circadian rhythm. Electrode positioning was fine-tuned using movable microdrives 75 

to ensure optimal detection of hippocampal ripples and LC spikes. The recordings from putative 76 

LC-NE neurons were verified using multiple electrophysiological criteria (see Methods for details), 77 

and the recording sites were additionally confirmed by histological examination (Figure 1B). The 78 

average firing rate of LC single units was 1.70 ± 0.21 Hz during wakefulness, 0.51 ± 0.07 Hz during 79 

NREM sleep, and 0.014 ± 0.01 Hz during REM sleep, differing significantly across arousal states (one-80 

way ANOVA: F(2,38) = 39.8, p < 0.0001; Supplementary Figure 1). This firing pattern is characteristic 81 

of LC-NE neurons and is consistent with existing literature ( Aston-Jones and Bloom, 1981 ; Eschenko 82 

et al., 2012 ; Eschenko and Sara, 2008 ; Hayat et al., 2020 ; Takahashi et al., 2010 ). 83

The LC multiunit activity (LC-MUA) was extracted by thresholding a high-pass filtered (300 Hz – 93 

8 kHz) extracellular signal recorded from the LC (Figure 1C). To directly compare the ripple-related 94 

LC activity dynamics, for each recording session, we computed the LC-MUA firing rate in a [-6, 6] 95 

sec window centered on the ripple onset, z-scored, and averaged across all detected ripples. This 96 

analysis revealed a prolonged and consistent suppression of LC activity both before and after ripple 97 

onset, with peak suppression < -2 SDs in all sessions (Figure 2C). In six out of twenty recording 98 

sessions, we could reliably isolate spikes from a total of 15 single units (n = 4 rats). The peri-ripple 99 

(± 6 s) firing suppression of LC single-unit activity (LC-SUA) exceeding the significance threshold of 100 

2 SDs was observed in 13 of 15 cases (Figure 2D). 101 

To quantify the peri-ripple LC activity change, we extracted the modulation onset time, dura-102 

tion, and magnitude, as illustrated in Figure 2D. The modulation magnitude (through on the peri-103 

event histogram) was greater for LC-MUA (-6.83 ± 0.84 SDs) compared to LC-SUA (-4.01 ± 0.65 SDs). 104 

Despite the difference in the modulation magnitude (Kolmogorov–Smirnov test, p = 0.019), the 105 

temporal profile of both LC-MUA and LC-SUA around ripples was remarkably similar (Figure 2D). 106 

No significant differences were found in the onset of spiking suppression (MUA: -4.08 ± 0.71 sec 107 

vs. SUA: -2.90 ± 0.50 sec, p = 0.138), duration (MUA: 8.28 ± 1.37 sec vs. SUA: 5.16 ± 0.92 sec, p = 108 

0.069), or peak time (MUA: -0.29 ± 0.04 sec vs. SUA: -0.35 ± 0.04 sec, p = 0.561). Together, these 109 

findings indicated that the majority of LC-NE neurons showed a stereotypic activity pattern around 110 

hippocampal ripples. Therefore, we used the LC-MUA datasets for subsequent analyses. 111 

LC firing and ripple occurrence are state-dependent and inversely related 112 

The LC activity around rippled was modulated at multiple temporal scales. In the previous section, 113 

we described a relatively sharp drop in the LC firing rate ∼ 2 s before the ripple onset. To capture 114 

LC dynamics at longer temporal scales, we tested a range of time windows and found that a 12-115 

s peri-event interval adequately represents LC activity dynamics. When computing peri-ripple LC 116 

firing over a [–12, 12] sec time window, we observed a decrease in LC activity beginning as early as 117 

∼10 s before the ripple onset (Supplementary Figure 2). We hypothesized that slower LC dynamics 118 

might be related to fluctuations of the global brain state. Indeed, transient spectral changes in the 119 

EEG coincided with the occurrence of hippocampal ripples (Figure 2B). 120 

We thus examined the temporal relationships between the ripple and LC activity and ongoing 121 

cortical state. To this end, we computed the ripple and LC-MUA rates within 4-s windows and quan-122 

tified the corresponding cortical state using a synchronization index (SI), calculated as a power ratio 123

To account for the potential influence of global brain state on peri-ripple LC activity, we gener-134 

ated surrogate events for each session by jittering the timestamps of detected ripples (see Meth-135 

ods). We first verified that hippocampal CA1 LFPs (140–250 Hz) triggered on these surrogate events 136 

lacked the ripple-specific frequency component (Figure 3C) and that the shuffled events adequately 137 

captured the cortical state dynamics associated with ripples (Figure 3D). Thus, LC activity aligned 138 

to surrogate events captured its state-dependent dynamics (Supplementary Figure 2). To isolate 139 

peri-ripple LC modulation from state-dependent fluctuations of the LC activity, we subtracted the 140 

LC-MUA around shuffled events from the peri-ripple LC-MUA for each session. The resulting traces 141 

provided a state-corrected estimate of ripple-associated LC activity, largely free from confounding 142 

effects of cortical state transitions. This analysis revealed a consistent suppression of LC activity at 143 

a finer temporal scale (1–2 s), although the temporal profile and magnitude of modulation varied 144 

across sessions (Figure 3E). 145 

To quantify the degree of peri-ripple LC activity modulation, we extracted the onset time and 146 

the duration as illustrated in Figure 2D. In addition, we calculated the modulation index (MI) as the 147 

area under the curve of the session-averaged peri-ripple LC rate within 1 second preceding the 148 

ripple onset (Figure 3F, see Methods for details). Because high-quality recordings from the LC in 149 

behaving rats are technically challenging and relatively rare, we included all valid sessions in this 150 

study. The average MI, calculated per animal and per session, fell within a consistent range despite 151 

variability in the number of recording sessions (2–8 sessions per rat) across (Supplementary Figure 152 

3). On average, LC activity significantly decreased 1.41 ± 0.06 sec (range: 1.02 – 1.88 sec) before 153 

the ripple onset and returned to the baseline after 1.70 ± 0.08 sec (range: 1.10 – 2.58 sec). Notably, 154 

maximal LC activity suppression occurred approximately 200 ms before the ripple onset (0.24 ±155 

0.04 sec; range: 0.03 – 0.73 sec). For all 20 datasets, the MI exceeded a 95% confidence interval 156 

(CI) of the MIs calculated around the shuffled time series, confirming a substantial decrease in LC 157 

activity around ripples. 158 

Differential LC modulation across ripple subsets 159 

The overall transient suppression of LC activity around hippocampal ripples, reported above, does 160 

not preclude differential LC modulation depending on ripple subtype. We therefore examined 161 

potential heterogeneity in LC modulation within a ± 6-sec time window around ripples. To this end, 162 

we randomly selected 20% of ripples from each dataset, calculated the subset modulation index 163 

(subMI) for this subset, and repeated the procedure to generate 5000 ripple subsets (537.6 ± 53.0 164 

ripples per subset). SubMI values showed substantial variability across subsets (range: –45.70 to 165 

1.38 a.u.; Figure 4A). On average, more than half of the subsets (65.99 ± 7.08%) exhibited significant 166 

peri-ripple LC modulation (subMI >95% CI), whereas the change of LC activity in the remaining 167 

subsets did not exceed 95% CI. Importantly, ripple intrinsic properties did not account for this 168 

variability: no significant differences were found in peak amplitude (Wilcoxon signed-rank test, Z 169 

= 0.63, p = 0.53), duration (Z = –0.52, p = 0.60), or ripple oscillation frequency (Z = 1.47, p = 0.14) 170 

between ripples in the 10th and 90th percentiles of the subMI distribution. 171

Peri-ripple LC modulation depends on the cortical-hippocampal interaction 177 

Building on our findings, we predicted that the LC-ripple interactions might vary with arousal. Us-178 

ing a previously established sleep scoring procedure ( Novitskaya et al., 2016 ; Yang et al., 2019 )179 

(Supplementary Figure 4), we classified rat behavior as awake (37.4% ± 2.9% of total recording 180 

time), NREM sleep (53.1% ± 3.2%), or REM sleep (1.2% ± 0.9%). Since ripples were extremely rare 181 

or essentially absent during REM sleep, we limited further analysis to ripples occurring during 182 

awake (awRipple) or NREM sleep (NREM-Ripple). Expectedly, LC activity was overall higher during 183 

awake than during NREM sleep (Figure 5A). The LC suppression occurred around both awRipples 184 

and NREM-Ripples, although the dynamic range was bigger during wakefulness. Moreover, dur-185 

ing awRipples, LC activity did not decrease to the levels observed during NREM sleep (Figure 5A). 186 

Furthermore, peri-ripple LC modulation occurred at slower and faster temporal scales, regardless 187 

of the behavioral state. Figure 5B illustrates a slow downward drift in the LC firing rate preceding 188 

ripples, likely reflecting the ongoing brain state, as described in the previous Results section. In 189 

contrast, event-specific LC modulation had faster dynamics (Figure 5B, highlighted interval). To 190 

minimize the influence of global state fluctuations and emphasize event-related dynamics, we ap-191 

plied state correction and z-score normalization for further analysis. 192 

For each data set and each behavioral state, we extracted the percent of ripple subsets with 193 

significant LC modulation (subMI >95% CI). We then correlated the subMIs with the total time spent 194 

in each behavioral state. The time spent awake, but not in NREM sleep, was significantly correlated 195 

with the proportion of ripples associated with LC suppression (r = 0.48, p = 0.03), indicating a more 196 

consistent LC modulation around awRipples. 197

## Discussion 262 

Using multi-site electrophysiology in freely behaving adult male rats, we characterized the dynam-263 

ics of LC neuronal activity and hippocampal ripples across two temporal scales. Both LC firing 264 

and ripple occurrence were strongly state-dependent, yet inversely related: periods of heightened 265 

arousal were marked by increased LC activity and reduced ripple rates. At a finer temporal resolu-266 

tion, LC spiking consistently decreased approximately 1–2 seconds before ripple onset. The mag-267 

nitude of peri-ripple LC modulation varied across ripple subsets but was not correlated with ripple 268 

intrinsic properties. Notably, the strongest LC modulation occurred around ripples in the awake 269 

state, whereas LC activity remained largely unchanged around ripples coupled with sleep spin-270 

dles during NREM sleep. Together, these findings provide novel insight into the state-dependent 271 

dynamics of cross-regional interactions and highlight the LC as a key component of a large-scale 272 

cortical–subcortical network supporting offline information processing. 273

and lower during sleep ( Aston-Jones and Bloom, 1981 ). This pioneering work has promoted exten-276 

sive research that has established the critical engagement of the LC-NE system in many cognitive 277 

functions dependent on real-time processing of incoming information ( Berridge and Waterhouse, 278 

2003 ; Sara and Bouret, 2012 ; Sara, 2009 ). However, only recently has the field begun to uncover 279

and the degree of cortical arousal as indicated by SI. Furthermore, LC activity during NREM sleep 286

result is in agreement with inverse relationships between NE release and the power of spindle os-288 

cillations ( Osorio-Forero et al., 2021 ). Collectively, these observations suggest the existence of a 289 

large-scale coordinated network regulating the NREM sleep microstructure and possibly mediating 290 

offline information processing. 291 

In our earlier study, by recording LC spiking activity in naturally behaving rats, we observed a 292 

delayed time window of enhanced LC neuron firing, specifically during NREM sleep episodes after 293 

learning ( Eschenko and Sara, 2008 ). Recent studies in mice uncovered infra-slow ( ∼0.02 Hz) fluctu-294 

ations of cortical and thalamic NE release during NREM sleep and related the LC activity dynamics 295 

to the regulation of sleep microstructure and state transitions ( Osorio-Forero et al., 2021 ; Kjaerby 296 

et al., 2022 ). Furthermore, optogenetic LC stimulation at 2Hz during NREM sleep after exposure 297 

to a spatial memory task, whilst not affecting ripple occurrence, decreased the stability of replay 298 

of hippocampal place cells and caused a memory deficit ( Swift et al., 2018 ). This finding aligns 299 

with our previous results demonstrating that phasic LC activation time-locked to ripples during 300 

post-learning sleep disrupts spatial memory consolidation ( Novitskaya et al., 2016 ). 301 

While the mechanisms and functional significance of LC activity patterns during NREM sleep 302 

remain to be fully understood ( Foustoukos and Lüthi, 2025 ; Sara, 2017 ; Poe, 2017 ), these obser-303 

vations align with pharmacological evidence showing that experimental manipulation of NE trans-304 

mission following learning can alter memory strength ( Przybyslawski et al., 1999 ; Sara et al., 1999 ;305 

Roullet and Sara, 1998 ; Miranda et al., 2009 ; Gibbs et al., 2010 ; Gazarini et al., 2013 ; Gais et al., 306 

2011 ). Our recent work suggested a potential network mechanism by which the LC–NE system con-307 

tributes to sleep-dependent memory consolidation ( Duran et al., 2023 ). Specifically, we demon-308 

strated that both 𝛼 2- and 𝛽 -adrenoceptors are involved in the generation of hippocampal ripples. 309 

Moreover, pharmacological manipulation of noradrenergic transmission after learning altered all 310 

major NREM sleep oscillations and their coupling, potentially leading to less efficient spatial mem-311 

ory consolidation ( Duran et al., 2023 ). The present findings extend this work by providing new 312 

insights into how LC neuronal activity contributes to the expression of normal sleep microarchitec-313 

ture. 314 

Coerulear-hippocampal interactions for memory consolidation 315 

Hippocampal ripples are broadly recognized as a central mechanism of memory consolidation 316 

(Klinzing et al., 2019 ; Skelin et al., 2018 ). Emerging work has begun to reveal the circuit mecha-317 

nisms by which hippocampal ripples engage a larger brain network that includes the prefrontal 318 

cortex, thalamus, amygdala, and ventral tegmental area (VTA) for offline information processing 319 

(Girardeau et al., 2017 ; Latchoumane et al., 2017 ; Gomperts et al., 2015 ; Wang and Ikemoto, 2016 ;320 

Yang et al., 2019 ). Our findings expand this memory-supporting circuit to the LC-NE system. By 321 

characterizing LC and hippocampal activity at a fine temporal scale, we revealed differential pat-322 

terns of coerulear-hippocampal interactions. First, we observed consistent transient suppression 323 

of LC activity occurring 1-2 seconds before the ripple onset. This cessation of LC neuron spiking 324 

and the corresponding depletion of NE might have enabled a network to shift to a transient state 325 

of enhanced synchronization, allowing ripple generation. This view is indirectly supported by an 326 

increase in the EEG delta power immediately preceding the ripple onset, when LC activity is sup-327 

pressed. 328 

Second, we showed that the degree of LC modulation varied across different subsets of ripples. 329

The strongest and most consistent peri-ripple LC inhibition was observed during the awake state. 330 

Interestingly, ripples occurring during wakefulness have been associated with stronger and more 331 

structured neuron ensemble reactivation, which was correlated with recent experience ( Tang et al., 332 

2017 ). Therefore, a transient silence of LC might be beneficial for the ripple-associated reactivation 333 

of recent memory traces. Similarly, coordinated activity of reward-related VTA neurons was greater 334 

around ripples that were associated with reactivation of neuronal ensembles that relayed recent 335 

learning experience ( Gomperts et al., 2015 ). Conversely, LC activation induces arousal, shifts the 336 

network to a state incompatible with ripple generation, and thereby might cause interference for 337 

the consolidation of recently acquired information ( Novitskaya et al., 2016 ; Swift et al., 2018 ). 338 

During NREM sleep, despite substantially reduced LC activity, we observed a fine-tuned LC ac-339 

tivity dynamics around ripples. Specifically, LC neuron discharge was only mildly reduced around 340 

isolated ripples, and it was largely preserved during ripples co-occurring with sleep spindles. Dif-341 

ferential firing patterns for spindle-coupled and -uncoupled ripples have been reported for cor-342 

tical and thalamic neurons ( Peyrache et al., 2011 ; Varela et al., 2001 ; Yang et al., 2019 ). During 343 

NREM sleep, hippocampal ripples occur in coordination with cortical slow oscillations and sleep 344 

spindles ( Maingret et al., 2016 ). The precise temporal correlation between the oscillatory patterns 345 

expressed during NREM sleep has been causally linked to memory consolidation by mediating the 346 

transfer of newly encoded information from the hippocampus to the cortex for long-term storage 347 

(Latchoumane et al., 2017 ; Buzsaki, 1996 ; Molle et al., 2006 ; Maingret et al., 2016 ; Klinzing et al., 348 

2019 ). 349 

At first glance, the suppression of LC activity around ripples that we observed in the present 350 

study may seem inconsistent with the established role of NE in facilitating ripple-mediated synap-351 

tic plasticity ( Norimoto et al., 2018 ; Sadowski et al., 2016 ). Remarkably, LC activity was indeed 352 

preserved around some subsets of ripples. In our previous work, we showed that LC activation 353 

during the time windows marking hippocampal-cortical communication interferes with the corti-354 

cal dynamics and is detrimental for spatial memory ( Novitskaya et al., 2016 ). 355 

Differential modulation of LC activity that was observed in the present study supports the idea 356 

that reduced NE release in the hippocampus might bias the content of memory trace during the 357 

subsequent ripples and weaken the reactivation of irrelevant experiences. In general, our results 358 

highlight the importance of preserved NE transmission at times of cross-regional information trans-359 

fer. An overall higher LC firing rate during states of elevated arousal may require a larger dynamic 360 

range of LC activity modulation ( Kjaerby et al., 2022 ). Therefore, in the awake state, a more pro-361 

nounced suppression of LC activity may be required for the network shift permitting ripple gener-362 

ation. 363 

In summary, our results demonstrate a state-dependent noradrenergic influence on the thala-364 

mocortical–hippocampal circuit and position the LC as part of an extended brain network whose 365 

coordinated activity supports system-level memory consolidation. Conducting behavioral assays 366 

before electrophysiological recordings, along with spatially and temporally precise modulation of 367 

LC activity during recording sessions, will be essential for achieving a mechanistic understanding of 368 

network dynamics and its functional role for memory consolidation in future investigations. Our 369 

present results suggest that during wakefulness, transient suppression of noradrenergic trans-370 

mission might facilitate the generation of hippocampal ripples and sleep spindles by enhancing 371 

network synchrony. We propose that reduced peri-ripple LC activity during the awake state may 372 

preferentially promote the replay of recent memory traces by limiting interference from remote 373 

memories, which are more likely to be reactivated in more alert states. In contrast, during NREM 374 

sleep, preserved LC activity during ripples coinciding with sleep spindles suggests a role for NE in 375 

facilitating cross-regional communication underlying memory-related information transfer. These 376 

results provide new insight into how the LC–NE system shapes memory processing across brain 377 

states and open the way for future causal investigations using modern circuit-level tools. 378

hippocampal ripples, the electrode was mounted on a self-made movable microdrive and inserted 404 

above the CA1 subfield of the dorsal hippocampus (dHPC, -3mm/2mm/ 2mm). For LC recordings, 405 

single platinum–iridium electrodes (FHC, Bowdoin, ME; n = 1), microwire brush electrodes (Micro-406

Classification of behavioral states 431 

We classified the rat’s spontaneous behavior into awake and NREM sleep using frontal EEG or cor-432 

tical LFPs by applying a previously established sleep scoring algorithm ( Novitskaya et al., 2016 ;433 

Yang et al., 2019 ). Briefly, animal movement speed was extracted from the video recording syn-434 

chronized with a neural signal. The theta/delta ( 𝜃 ∕𝛿 ) ratio was calculated from the artifact-free EEG 435 

in 4-sec epochs. The epochs of the awake state were identified by the presence of active locomo-436 

tion and above 𝜃 ∕𝛿 threshold; the epochs of NREM sleep were identified by the absence of motor 437 

activity and below 𝜃 ∕𝛿 threshold. The minimal duration of the same behavioral state was set to 20 438 

sec; data segments with less steady behavioral states were excluded from further analysis. 439 

Event detection 440 

For detection of the hippocampal ripples, a broadband (0.1 Hz - 8 kHz) extracellular signal recorded 441 

from the dHPC (pyramidal layer, CA1 subfield) was band-pass (120 – 250 Hz) filtered, rectified, 442 

and low-pass filtered (25 Hz). The resulting signal was z-score normalized, and ripple oscillations 443 

were detected by signal thresholding at 5 standard deviations (SDs). Ascending and descending 444 

crossings at 1 SD defined the ripple onset and offset, respectively. Clustered and isolated ripples 445 

were classified based on the inter-ripple interval (IRI) as described in detail elsewhere ( Selinger 446 

et al., 2007 ). Briefly, IRIs were extracted, and the log(IRI) distribution was analyzed. A bimodal 447 

log(IRI) distribution indicated ripple occurrence with shorter and longer IRI. A crossing point of 448 

two distributions was used for classifying the ripple type. Ripples with short IRI (< 0.33 sec) were 449 

classified as ‘clustered’ and treated as a single ripple event; the onset time was defined by the first 450 

ripple in a cluster. Ripples with IRI > 0.33 sec were classified as ‘isolated’. 451 

For the detection of sleep spindles, the EEG signal was band-pass (12 - 16 Hz) filtered, down-452 

sampled (200 Hz), and the root mean square (0.2 s smoothing) was calculated. The spindle detec-453 

tion threshold was defined at 3 SD of the signal amplitude during NREM sleep episodes. Ascending 454 

and descending signal crossings at 1SD defined the spindle on- and offset, respectively. The spin-455 

dle duration was extracted as the time between the spindle onset and offset; the minimal sleep 456 

spindle duration was set to 0.5 sec. Coupled oscillatory events were defined as ripples occurring 457 

between spindle onset and offset. 458 

Analysis of peri-event neural activity 459 

For analysis of LC activity modulation around ripples, we used a previously established method 460 

(Yang et al., 2019 ). First, the spike times of LC multiunit activity (LC-MUA) were extracted by high-461 

pass (600Hz) filtering of the broadband extracellular signal recorded from LC and thresholding at 462 

-0.05 mV. The LC-MUA was triggered on ripple/spindle onset, and the averaged peri-event spike 463 

histogram (PETH) was generated either ± 6 sec or ± 3 sec around all detected ripples and ± 5 sec 464 

around sleep spindles (5-ms bins, Gaussian smoothed with a 10-ms window). To compensate for 465 

possible state-dependent fluctuations of LC-MUA, a ‘surrogate’ event sequence was generated by 466 

randomly distributing the same number of time points within a 4-second time window around 467 

each event; the jittering procedure was repeated 100 times. The averaged PETH was generated 468 

around jittered events. Time series of LC MUA around the jittered events were subtracted from 469 

the corresponding values around real events and the resulting (‘corrected’) PETHs were z-score 470 

normalized. To further smooth the PETH, we extracted the area above/below the curve of each 471 

PETH every 100ms and z-scored the modulation response. To quantify peri-event dynamics of LC-472 

MUA, a modulation index (MI) was calculated by modulation response within 1 sec before the event 473 

onsets. To determine the significance of modulation, we calculated the modulation responses 474
