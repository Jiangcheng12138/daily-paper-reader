Abstract 26 

27 

The ovarian reserve, a finite pool of long-lived non-growing oocytes established at birth, 28 

determines female reproductive lifespan, yet how these oocytes establish long-term quiescence 29 

while retaining the capacity for future growth and embryogenesis remains poorly understood. 30 

Here, we define a regulatory logic by which Polycomb repressive complexes shape stage-31 

specific active chromatin remodeling during ovarian reserve formation and early oocyte growth. 32 

During ovarian reserve formation, H3K27ac, an active promoter- and enhancer-associated mark, 33 

undergoes extensive genome-wide redistribution. A key feature of this transition is CpG island 34 

promoter remodeling, in which many loci lose H3K27ac while gaining PRC1-dependent 35 

H2AK119ub, a repressive mark. This early reprogramming is followed during oocyte growth by 36 

acquisition of PRC2-dependent H3K27me3, de novo establishment of bivalent promoters, and 37 

protection of promoter regions from de novo DNA methylation. Oocyte growth is also 38 

accompanied by broad gains in both H3K27ac and H3K4me3, an active promoter-associated 39 

mark. Analyses of PRC1- and PRC2-deficient oocytes reveal unequal Polycomb contributions: 40 

PRC2 broadly constrains H3K27ac, whereas PRC1 more selectively shapes genome-wide 41 

H3K27ac redistribution and restricts H3K4me3 accumulation at bivalent promoters. Together, 42 

these findings identify staged active chromatin remodeling as an integral feature of perinatal 43 

oocyte development and reveal that Polycomb shapes chromatin state transitions as oocytes enter 44 

quiescence and become poised for future growth. 45

Introduction 46 

The ovarian reserve is a finite pool of oocytes that sustains female fertility across reproductive 47 

lifespan ( 1-3 ). Established around birth, this pool consists of long-lived non-growing oocytes 48 

(NGOs) enclosed within primordial follicles ( 3-8 ). Although often viewed as a dormant 49 

reservoir, the ovarian reserve represents a more demanding developmental state: oocytes must 50 

acquire long-term quiescence while preserving the capacity to resume growth, complete 51 

oogenesis, and ultimately support fertilization and early embryogenesis. A central question in 52 

female germline biology is how oocytes establish an epigenomic state that sustains quiescence 53 

while preserving competence for future growth and embryogenesis. 54 

> 55

In mice, formation of the ovarian reserve is followed by recruitment of a subset of primordial 56 

follicles with initiation of oocyte growth, giving rise to growing oocytes (GOs) that later mature 57 

into fertilizable eggs ( 9, 10 ). These transitions are accompanied by extensive transcriptional 58 

reprogramming ( 11-14 ), indicating that progression from embryonic oocytes to NGOs and then 59 

to GOs is governed by a coordinated developmental program rather than by simple entry into and 60 

release from quiescence. Defining chromatin mechanisms that operate across these transitions is 61 

therefore essential for understanding how the ovarian reserve is established, maintained, and 62 

linked to subsequent oocyte growth. 63 

> 64

Following genome-wide DNA demethylation in primordial germ cells, the oocyte genome 65 

remains globally hypomethylated until postnatal growth, when de novo DNA methylation and 66 

specialized chromatin landscapes are progressively established ( 15-18 ). Full-grown oocytes 67 

(FGOs) display unusual epigenomic features, including broad H3K4me3 domains ( 19, 20 ), a 68 

histone modification typically enriched at active promoters, noncanonical H3K27ac patterns ( 18, 69 

21 ), a mark associated with active promoters and enhancers, and widespread H3K27me3 ( 22 ), a 70 

repressive histone modification deposited by PRC2 that persists into early embryogenesis and 71 

contributes to DNA methylation–independent imprinting ( 23-25 ). The developmental origins of 72 

these distinctive chromatin states, however, remain poorly understood, particularly during the 73 

perinatal period, when oocytes transition from embryonic meiotic prophase I into the ovarian 74 

reserve and subsequently into the growth phase ( 26 ). 75 

> 76

Polycomb repressive complexes are central regulators of developmental chromatin states ( 27-77 

29 ). In the female germline, PRC1-mediated H2AK119ub-dependent repression of the meiotic 78 

prophase I (MPI) program underlies proper ovarian reserve formation and is linked to later 79 

establishment of H3K27me3 during oocyte growth ( 14, 30 ). Together, these studies established 80 

Polycomb-mediated chromatin programming as a key feature of perinatal oogenesis but left 81 

unresolved whether Polycomb acts only to impose repressive chromatin or also shapes chromatin 82 

features associated with future activation competence. This question is especially relevant in the 83 

context of active chromatin, because H3K4me3 and H3K27ac are generally associated with 84 

transcriptional competence, whereas Polycomb is classically linked to repressive chromatin 85 

marked by H2AK119ub and H3K27me3. 86 

> 87

Here, by analyzing PRC1- and PRC2-deficient oocytes, we define the molecular logic by which 88 

Polycomb repressive complexes shape stage-specific remodeling of active chromatin during 89 

ovarian reserve formation and oocyte growth. Our findings identify promoter remodeling and 90 

subsequent bivalent promoter priming as integral features of perinatal oocyte development and 91 

suggest that Polycomb shapes chromatin state transitions as oocytes enter quiescence and 92 

become poised for future growth. 93

Results 94 

Dynamic active chromatin remodeling during perinatal oogenesis 95 

In mice, perinatal oocytes undergo two key developmental transitions: the perinatal oocyte 96 

transition (POT), during which oocytes exit MPI and establish the ovarian reserve ( 14 ), and the 97 

primordial-to-primary follicle transition (PPT), during which a subset of primordial follicles 98 

initiates growth ( 13 ). To define active chromatin dynamics across the POT and PPT, we profiled 99 

H3K4me3 and H3K27ac at four key stages of mouse perinatal oogenesis: E18.5 oocytes in MPI 100 

(E18O), oocytes transitioning to dictyate arrest at postnatal day 1 (P1O), NGOs within 101 

primordial follicles at P6 (NGO), and GOs in primary follicles at P7 (GO) (Fig. 1A). To quantify 102 

stage-specific changes, we performed low-input cleavage under targets and tagmentation 103 

(CUT&Tag) ( 31, 32 ) with spike-in controls normalized to cell number, enabling quantitative 104 

comparison across stages (fig. S1A). After confirming dataset quality and reproducibility (fig. 105 

S1B), biological replicates were pooled for downstream analyses. 106 

> 107

Hierarchical clustering revealed two major epigenomic transitions in active chromatin 108 

corresponding to the POT and PPT (Fig. 1B), accompanied by dynamic changes in the oocyte 109 

transcriptome (Fig. 1B, right) and in expression of enzymes that regulate active histone marks 110 

(fig. S1C). During POT, H3K4me3 levels remained relatively constant (Fig. 1, C and D), 111 

whereas H3K27ac underwent extensive redistribution from E18O to NGO (Fig. 1, C and D). By 112 

contrast, during PPT, both H3K27ac and H3K4me3 increased more broadly, consistent with 113 

broad transcriptional activation during early oocyte growth (Fig. 1, C and D). 114 

> 115

Peak-based analyses corroborated these stage-specific changes. H3K4me3 peaks displayed 116 

relatively limited changes during POT but broadened and accumulated more extensively during 117 

PPT (fig. S2, A to D). In contrast, H3K27ac peaks showed more prominent changes in peak 118 

number, genomic distribution, and differential enrichment during POT (fig. S2, E to H), 119 

indicating dynamic H3K27ac remodeling during ovarian reserve establishment. Notably, gains of 120 

H3K27ac peaks in NGOs were observed in intergenic regions and gene bodies, suggesting 121 

acquisition of distal regulatory elements during POT (fig. S2, E and H). 122 

> 123

These dynamic epigenomic transitions were further supported by ChromHMM analysis ( 33 ), 124 

which integrated both active and Polycomb-mediated repressive chromatin features across these 125 

developmental stages (Fig. 1E). By classifying genomic regions into 12 chromatin states, we 126 

identified dynamic, region-specific epigenomic changes during perinatal oogenesis. Notably, 127 

states 1, 5, and 12 were characterized by high levels of H3K4me3 and H3K27ac and enrichment 128 

for CpG islands (CGIs), consistent with promoter-like regions. States 1 and 12 were additionally 129 

enriched for the repressive marks H2AK119ub and H3K27me3, consistent with putative bivalent 130 

promoters. ChromHMM further confirmed genome-wide redistribution of H3K27ac during POT, 131 

particularly the extensive loss of state 12 signal at CGI-enriched promoters and gains of states 3 132 

and 4 outside putative promoters. 133 

> 134

Together, these data indicate that ovarian reserve formation is marked by H3K27ac redistribution 135 

during POT, followed by broader increases in both H3K27ac and H3K4me3 during subsequent 136 

oocyte growth. Furthermore, the abundance of active marks in NGOs suggests that these cells are 137 

not simply dormant but retain active regulatory elements despite remaining quiescent. 138

In mammals, a large group of promoters contains CGIs that are generally constitutively 141 

unmethylated and enriched for PRC1 and PRC2, as well as the H2AK119ub and H3K27me3 142 

marks they mediate, respectively ( 34, 35 ). Following our observation of H3K27ac loss at CGI-143 

enriched promoter regions, we compared histone marks at 14,040 CGI-associated genes (CGI 144 

genes, including 11,399 active genes and 2,641 inactive genes in perinatal oocytes) and 39,755 145 

genes without CGIs (non-CGI genes, including 8,694 active genes and 31,061 inactive genes in 146 

perinatal oocytes), and found extensive promoter reprogramming at CGI genes (Fig. 2A). 147 

Overall, promoters of active genes were associated with high levels of active marks, whereas 148 

inactive genes were enriched for repressive marks. Strikingly, a sharp loss of H3K27ac was 149 

observed at ~62% (n = 8,615) of CGI gene TSSs during POT regardless of gene expression 150 

status (Fig. 2B), whereas H3K4me3 levels remained relatively constant (Fig. 2, A and B). These 151 

changes were unexpected given the extensive genome-wide overlap between H3K27ac and 152 

H3K4me3 peaks (fig. S2I). Consistent with our recent report ( 30 ), H2AK119ub increased 153 

extensively and peaked in NGOs, whereas H3K27me3 enrichment followed this pattern and 154 

peaked in GOs (Fig. 2A). In particular, gain of H3K27me3 at CGI promoters in GOs appeared to 155 

coincide with substantial de novo bivalent promoter formation, defined by the coexistence of 156 

H3K4me3 and H3K27me3. In addition, H3K4me3 and H3K27ac increased during PPT, 157 

consistent with global gene activation in GOs. 158 

> 159

To examine the relationship between chromatin state and gene expression during POT and PPT, 160 

we analyzed differentially expressed genes (DEGs) across these transitions using publicly 161 

available data ( 11 ) (fig. S3A). Genome-wide analysis revealed that global loss of H3K27ac and 162 

gain of H2AK119ub at promoters during POT were frequently associated with both up- and 163 

down-regulated genes (fig. S3, B and C). Meioc is a representative down-regulated CGI gene and 164 

a critical regulator of MPI ( 36 ) that is expressed in MPI and repressed during POT and PPT (fig. 165 

S3A). At the Meioc promoter, loss of H3K27ac and gain of H2AK119ub were observed during 166 

POT, followed by gain of H3K27me3 in GOs (Fig. 2C). Gdf9 is a representative up-regulated 167 

CGI gene and an essential regulator of folliculogenesis ( 37 ) whose expression increases during 168 

POT and PPT (fig. S3A). During POT, loss of H3K27ac and gain of H2AK119ub were observed 169 

at the Gdf9 promoter, whereas H3K4me3 and H3K27ac increased across the gene body as 170 

expression increased (Fig. 2D). Together, these findings indicate that promoter reprogramming 171 

occurs at both up- and down-regulated genes, suggesting that a relatively repressive promoter 172 

chromatin state is established after POT and may contribute to the long-term stability of the 173 

ovarian reserve. 174 

> 175

Notably, CGI promoter reprogramming precedes DNA methylation patterning associated with 176 

transcription-dependent de novo DNA methylation in GOs ( 38 ). Reanalysis of previous whole-177 

genome bisulfite sequencing data from FGOs ( 15 ) showed that, at CGI genes, DNA methylation 178 

increased globally across gene bodies while TSSs remained unmethylated (Fig. 2A). Thus, 179 

promoter reprogramming at CGI genes during POT, characterized by loss of H3K27ac and gain 180 

of H2AK119ub, together with subsequent gain of H3K27me3 during PPT, may contribute to 181 

protection of TSSs from de novo DNA methylation in GOs and to later DNA methylation 182 

patterning in FGOs. 183 

> 184

In contrast to CGI genes, non-CGI genes displayed distinct gene-body-associated chromatin 185 

regulation. Within gene bodies of non-CGI genes, H3K4me3 and H3K27ac signals were 186 

generally sparse, whereas H2AK119ub increased globally in NGOs, followed by gain of 187 

H3K27me3 in GOs (Fig. 2A). Sohlh1 is a representative non-CGI gene required for oogenesis 188

> 198

H3K27ac reprogramming during POT is accompanied by gain of H2AK119ub 199 

H3K27ac reprogramming during POT was not unidirectional, but instead involved selective loss, 200 

gain, and maintenance of H3K27ac across the genome. Differential peak analysis between E18O 201 

and NGOs identified three major classes of H3K27ac peaks (Fig. 3A): 17,481 peaks that lost 202 

H3K27ac during POT (“POT loss” peaks), 33,667 peaks that gained H3K27ac (“POT gain” 203 

peaks), and 62,127 peaks that remained relatively stable (“POT stable” peaks). These three 204 

classes included both proximal and distal elements, with proximal peaks notably enriched among 205 

“POT loss” peaks (Fig. 3B). “POT loss” peaks were enriched for YY1 and MAFK/MAFB 206 

motifs, whereas “POT gain” peaks were enriched for motifs recognized by TCF3 (E2A), PTF1A, 207 

and TCF12 (HEB) (Fig. 3C). YY1 functions as a Polycomb-associated factor ( 42 ) and is 208 

essential for follicle expansion ( 43 ), whereas TCF3 and TCF12 are critical regulators of postnatal 209 

oogenesis and folliculogenesis ( 18 ). Thus, H3K27ac reprogramming during POT is associated 210 

with developmental rewiring of gene regulatory elements. 211 

> 212

Notably, POT loss peaks gained H2AK119ub but lost chromatin accessibility during POT (Fig. 213 

3D). At these peaks, H3K4me3 intensity remained relatively high, likely reflecting the 214 

enrichment of proximal elements (fig. S4A). By contrast, “POT gain” and “POT stable” peaks 215 

showed only modest gains in H2AK119ub and accessibility in NGOs. These patterns raised the 216 

possibility that Polycomb contributes to H3K27ac reprogramming during POT. Consistent with 217 

this idea, PRC2-mediated H3K27me3 is mutually exclusive with H3K27ac because the two 218 

modifications occupy the same residue, whereas PRC1 may influence H3K27ac through 219 

interactions with histone acetyltransferase ( 44 ) or deacetylase activities ( 45 ). 220 

> 221

PRC1 regulates H3K27ac reprogramming during ovarian reserve formation 222 

To determine how Polycomb complexes contribute to H3K27ac reprogramming, we used two 223 

germ cell-specific conditional knockout mouse models that disrupt PRC1 or PRC2 function. Our 224 

previously established PRC1 loss-of-function model (PRC1cKO) and PRC2 loss-of-function 225 

model (PRC2cKO) ( 14, 30, 46, 47 ) use Ddx4 -Cre, which is expressed in germ cells from E15 226 

(48 ), to inactivate RNF2, the catalytic E3 ubiquitin ligase of PRC1, in a genetic background 227 

lacking the partially redundant paralog RING1 (RING1A) ( 49, 50 ), or EED, an essential 228 

component of PRC2 ( 51 ), respectively (Fig. 4, A and B). As reported previously, these models 229 

result in depletion of H2AK119ub or H3K27me3 in NGOs, respectively ( 14, 30 ). We therefore 230 

examined H3K27ac profiles in PRC1- and PRC2-deficient NGOs (fig. S5). 231 

> 232

Loss of PRC1 and PRC2 affected H3K27ac in distinct ways across the three classes of POT 233 

peaks, revealing that Polycomb regulation of H3K27ac is not uniform across the genome. In 234 

PRC1cKO NGOs, H3K27ac levels were markedly elevated at “POT loss” peaks, whereas 235 

H3K27ac levels were reduced at “POT gain” peaks (Fig. 4C and fig. S4B). By contrast, in 236

PRC2cKO NGOs, H3K27ac levels increased across all peak classes, consistent with the 237

> 242

In PRC1cKO NGOs, 17,460 H3K27ac peaks were differentially gained relative to PRC1ctrl 243 

NGOs, and these peaks were enriched for YY1 and MAFK motifs (Fig. 4, G and H). These 244 

motifs were also enriched among “POT loss” peaks in wild-type oocytes (Fig. 3C), supporting 245 

the idea that PRC1 facilitates developmental loss of H3K27ac during POT. Conversely, 4,749 246 

H3K27ac peaks were differentially lost in PRC1cKO NGOs (Fig. 4G), and these peaks were 247 

enriched for TCF3 (E2A), PTF1A, and TCF12 (HEB) motifs (Fig. 4H), which were likewise 248 

enriched among “POT gain” peaks in wild-type oocytes (Fig. 3C). These findings support a 249 

second role for PRC1 in facilitating developmental gain of H3K27ac during POT. Thus, PRC1 250 

influences both attenuation and acquisition of H3K27ac, indicating that it functions as more than 251 

a general suppressor of active chromatin. 252 

> 253

Compared with PRC1 loss, PRC2 loss altered fewer peaks and produced a more uniform 254 

increase in H3K27ac signal (Fig. 4, D to G, and fig. S4B), consistent with the antagonism 255 

between H3K27me3 and H3K27ac at lysine 27. In PRC2cKO NGOs, both gained and lost 256 

H3K27ac peaks were similarly enriched for TCF3/12 motifs (Fig. 4H). Thus, PRC2 appears to 257 

contribute less selectively to H3K27ac redistribution during POT and primarily acts to restrain 258 

H3K27ac levels. The contrasting effects of PRC1cKO and PRC2cKO therefore reveal distinct 259 

Polycomb functions: PRC1 facilitates both developmental loss and gain of H3K27ac during 260 

POT, whereas PRC2-mediated H3K27me3 broadly antagonizes H3K27ac (Fig. 4I). 261 

> 262

A prominent feature of FGOs is the presence of noncanonical, distal-rich broad H3K27ac 263 

domains ( 18, 21 ). To determine when and how these domains emerge, we examined H3K27ac 264 

enrichment across perinatal oocytes at genomic loci corresponding to all broad peaks (>10 kb) 265 

identified in FGOs (fig. S6A). Broad H3K27ac domains could already be detected in NGOs 266 

before growth, although they were relatively limited in early perinatal oocytes and became more 267 

evident during oocyte growth. Notably, in both PRC1- and PRC2-deficient NGOs, loci 268 

corresponding to broad H3K27ac domains in FGOs showed elevated H3K27ac relative to 269 

controls (fig. S6, A and B). These findings suggest that Polycomb normally restrains premature 270 

expansion of broad H3K27ac domains during ovarian reserve formation, further supporting a 271 

role for Polycomb in limiting untimely acquisition of active chromatin features in perinatal 272 

oocytes. 273 

> 274

Together, these results indicate that PRC1 and PRC2 do not contribute equivalently to active 275 

chromatin remodeling during ovarian reserve formation. PRC2 broadly antagonizes H3K27ac, 276 

whereas PRC1 plays a more prominent and selective role in shaping developmental H3K27ac 277 

transitions during POT. 278

Promoter bivalency is developmentally reconfigured during PPT 280 

We next examined how promoter-associated chromatin states, particularly promoter bivalency, 281 

are regulated during perinatal oogenesis. Promoter bivalency, defined by the coexistence of 282 

H3K4me3 and H3K27me3, is typically associated with genes poised for later activation ( 52 ) and 283 

plays important roles in development and differentiation, particularly in the germline ( 53, 54 ). To 284

define promoter bivalency, we classified promoters in E18O, NGOs, and GOs into four 285 

categories based on H3K4me3 and H3K27me3 status: bivalent, H3K27me3-only, H3K4me3-286 

only, and other (fig. S7A). This analysis revealed that bivalent promoters were abundant at all 287 

three stages but underwent extensive developmental reconfiguration (Fig. 5A). 288 

> 289

A substantial fraction of bivalent promoters was shared across E18O, NGOs, and GOs, but many 290 

were newly established in GOs. We identified 4,908 and 5,461 bivalent promoters in E18Os and 291 

NGOs, respectively, with substantial overlap (3,751 in common), indicating that promoter 292 

bivalency is broadly maintained during POT (Fig. 5B and fig. S8A). By contrast, a substantial 293 

fraction of bivalent promoters detected in GOs (10,906 in total) arose from promoters that were 294 

not bivalent at earlier stages, particularly from H3K4me3-only promoters (Fig. 5B and fig. S8B), 295 

coincident with genome-wide H3K27me3 reprogramming during PPT ( 30 ). Because this 296 

reprogramming is directed by PRC1-H2AK119ub ( 30 ), H2AK119ub was already highly 297 

enriched at GO-specific bivalent promoters in NGOs (Fig. 5C), suggesting that H2AK119ub in 298 

NGOs primes the later formation of bivalent promoters in GOs. 299 

> 300

Notably, virtually all classes of bivalent promoters were accompanied by H2AK119ub 301 

enrichment (Fig. 5C), indicating that these loci are more appropriately viewed as H2AK119ub-302 

containing trivalent chromatin states and implicating PRC1 in regulation of promoter bivalency 303 

in oocytes. Among them, 3,227 promoters remained bivalent throughout all developmental 304 

stages and were marked by persistently high levels of H3K4me3 and H3K27me3 together with 305 

strong H2AK119ub (Fig. 5C). These constitutively bivalent promoters were associated with 306 

developmental regulators, canonical Polycomb targets, and morphogenesis-related processes 307 

(fig. S7B). By contrast, GO-specific bivalent promoters showed high H3K4me3 but relatively 308 

weak H3K27me3 and H2AK119ub enrichment, consistent with their higher expression levels 309 

than other classes of bivalent promoters (Fig. 5C and fig. S8B). Despite the relatively weak 310 

H3K27me3 enrichment, H3K27me3 deposition during PPT was still associated with reduced 311 

expression of these otherwise active genes (Fig. 5C and fig. S8B). Among these genes, those 312 

involved in translation, autophagy, and apoptosis were significantly down-regulated during PPT 313 

(fig. S7C). 314 

> 315

Together, these findings indicate that promoter bivalency is dynamically reconfigured during 316 

perinatal oogenesis, with a major wave of de novo establishment during PPT. The presence of 317 

H2AK119ub at these promoters further suggests that many represent trivalent promoter states 318 

and implicates PRC1 in their regulation. 319 

> 320

Distinct roles of PRC1 and PRC2 at bivalent promoters during quiescence and early oocyte 321 

growth 322 

Given the developmental reconfiguration of bivalent promoters during PPT, we next asked how 323 

Polycomb complexes regulate both the maintenance of preexisting bivalent promoters and the de 324 

novo establishment of new bivalent promoters during perinatal oogenesis. Because bivalent 325 

promoters are defined by the coexistence of the active mark H3K4me3 and the repressive mark 326 

H3K27me3, they provide a useful framework for dissecting how PRC1 and PRC2 intersect with 327 

promoter-associated active chromatin during POT and PPT. 328 

> 329

To address this, we profiled H3K4me3 in PRC1cKO and PRC2cKO NGOs by quantitative 330 

CUT&Tag (fig. S9A). Unexpectedly, PRC1 and PRC2 deficiency had opposite effects on 331 

H3K4me3. PRC1 depletion significantly increased global H3K4me3 levels, particularly at peaks 332

6B). These results suggest that, in NGOs, H3K27me3 and H3K4me3 do not exhibit the 341 

antagonistic relationship observed in ESCs ( 56 ), and that PRC1 is the predominant regulator of 342 

bivalent promoters at the quiescent stage, consistent with the H2AK119ub-associated trivalent 343 

state observed in NGOs (Fig. 5C). 344 

> 345

This functional distinction between PRC1 and PRC2 was also evident in GOs. Genes associated 346 

with bivalent promoters in GOs, including both promoters maintained from NGOs and those 347 

newly established in GOs, were more strongly derepressed in PRC1-deficient GOs than in 348 

PRC2-deficient counterparts (Fig. 6, C and D). Notably, although loss of PRC2-H3K27me3 had 349 

little effect on expression of bivalent genes in NGOs (Fig. 6B), these genes tended to be up-350 

regulated in GOs upon PRC2 loss, including genes shared between NGOs and GOs as well as 351 

newly established GO-specific bivalent genes (Fig. 6D). Thus, whereas PRC1 dominates 352 

restraint of bivalent promoters in NGOs, PRC2 begins to contribute more substantially to 353 

repression as oocytes enter growth. 354 

> 355

Together, these findings reveal stage-dependent roles between PRC1 and PRC2 at bivalent 356 

promoters. PRC1 plays the stronger role in restraining H3K4me3 accumulation and transcription 357 

in NGOs, whereas PRC2 contributes more prominently after growth begins. More broadly, these 358 

results suggest that Polycomb-dependent regulation in perinatal oocytes helps couple long-term 359 

quiescence to future developmental competence by differentially controlling promoter-associated 360 

active chromatin states across the transition from the ovarian reserve to oocyte growth. 361 

> 362

Discussion 363 

The ovarian reserve is the key determinant of female reproductive lifespan, yet the chromatin 364 

principles that stabilize NGOs while preserving competence for later growth have remained 365 

incompletely understood. Our previous work established that Polycomb-mediated repressive 366 

chromatin is integral to ovarian reserve formation and to later oocyte development ( 14, 30 ). 367 

Building on that framework, the present study uncovers an additional layer of Polycomb-368 

dependent regulation centered on active chromatin (Fig. 7). H3K27ac is extensively redistributed 369 

during ovarian reserve formation, whereas both H3K27ac and H3K4me3 increase more broadly 370 

during subsequent oocyte growth. Moreover, PRC1 and PRC2 contribute unequally to these 371 

transitions, with PRC1 exerting the stronger influence on H3K27ac redistribution and promoter-372 

associated H3K4me3 restraint. These findings indicate that the ovarian reserve is not an 373 

epigenetically inert state but is established through coordinated programming of both repressive 374 

and active chromatin. 375

A central implication of our data is that CGI promoter reprogramming during ovarian reserve 378 

formation may help preconfigure promoter states for later oocyte growth and embryogenesis. 379 

During this period, many CGI promoters lose H3K27ac while gaining PRC1-mediated 380 

H2AK119ub, regardless of whether the associated genes are subsequently up- or down-381 

regulated. This promoter-centered remodeling is followed during oocyte growth by acquisition of 382 

PRC2-mediated H3K27me3 and de novo establishment of bivalent promoters, supporting a 383 

model in which early promoter reprogramming creates a chromatin framework for later 384 

promoter-state transitions. This model is especially relevant in the context of DNA methylation 385 

establishment during oocyte growth. In growing oocytes, de novo DNA methylation is deposited 386 

broadly across transcribed regions, whereas CGI promoters generally remain protected from 387 

methylation ( 38 ). Loss of this protection causes persistent maternal CGI hypermethylation, 388 

impaired embryonic gene expression, and preimplantation lethality ( 57 ). In this context, our 389 

findings raise the possibility that early CGI promoter remodeling in NGOs, characterized by loss 390 

of H3K27ac and gain of Polycomb-associated marks, helps establish a promoter chromatin state 391 

that is less permissive to inappropriate de novo DNA methylation during later oocyte growth, 392 

thereby contributing to formation of the maternal epigenome inherited by the next generation. In 393 

this sense, perinatal oogenesis is better viewed not as simple entry into quiescence, but as a 394 

developmental window in which promoter states are reorganized in preparation for future oocyte 395 

development. 396 

Our data further suggest that these later poised promoter states extend beyond the classical 397 

PRC2-centered concept of bivalency. Rather than representing classical bivalent domains 398 

marked only by H3K4me3 and H3K27me3, a substantial fraction of these poised promoters in 399 

oocytes may be more appropriately viewed as trivalent promoter states because they are also 400 

enriched for H2AK119ub. This is notable because it places PRC1-H2AK119ub directly within 401 

the chromatin architecture of poised developmental promoters in oocytes. Consistent with this 402 

interpretation, PRC1 loss had a stronger effect than PRC2 loss on promoter-associated H3K4me3 403 

accumulation, indicating that PRC1-H2AK119ub is an important determinant of H3K4me3 404 

restraint during establishment and maintenance of these bivalent/trivalent promoter states. Thus, 405 

PRC1 appears to act not only upstream of later H3K27me3 accumulation, but also as a key 406 

regulator of promoter chromatin balance during ovarian reserve formation and subsequent 407 

growth. 408 

> 409

Our perturbation analyses further show that PRC1 and PRC2 do not act equivalently in the active 410 

chromatin remodeling process. PRC2 broadly antagonizes H3K27ac, consistent with the 411 

mutually exclusive nature of H3K27ac and H3K27me3 ( 58-63 ). PRC1, by contrast, acts more 412 

selectively, facilitating both developmental loss and gain of H3K27ac at distinct loci. Previous 413 

studies have shown that PRC1 can counteract H3K27ac by inhibiting acetyltransferase 414 

recruitment ( 44 ) or recruiting deacetylases ( 45 ), but in some contexts it can also support gene 415 

activation and H3K27ac accumulation ( 64-67 ). Our data are consistent with such context-416 

dependent regulation and suggest that PRC1 functions not simply as a general suppressor of 417 

active chromatin, but as a selective organizer of promoter remodeling during ovarian reserve 418 

formation. We further find that broad H3K27ac domains characteristic of FGOs can already be 419 

detected in NGOs and become more prominent during growth, while both PRC1 and PRC2 420 

restrain their premature expansion during ovarian reserve formation. Together, these 421 

observations support a broader role for Polycomb in delimiting the timing and extent of active 422 

chromatin acquisition. 423

This interpretation also helps unify our recent studies. Our previous work showed that epigenetic 425 

priming in NGOs shapes later chromatin states during oocyte growth and the oocyte-to-embryo 426 

transition, and that PRC1-dependent H2AK119ub prepatterns later H3K27me3 organization and 427 

developmental competence (14, 30). The present study adds the missing promoter-level logic to 428 

this framework: PRC1-dependent CGI promoter remodeling during the perinatal stage, 429 

especially removal of H3K27ac coupled to gain of H2AK119ub, appears to create chromatin 430 

states that are later elaborated into bivalent/trivalent promoters and protected from inappropriate 431 

DNA methylation during growth. In this way, the current study supports a broader model in 432 

which epigenetic priming in the female germline is established through staged Polycomb-433 

dependent remodeling of both active and repressive chromatin. 434 

> 435

More broadly, our findings place perinatal oocytes within an emerging view of Polycomb 436 

regulation in which poised promoter states are not simply inherited, but developmentally 437 

assembled and reconfigured. In pluripotent cells, bivalent promoters have classically been 438 

interpreted through a PRC2-centered framework, in which H3K27me3 counterbalances 439 

promoter-associated H3K4me3 to maintain developmental genes in a poised state ( 52-54 ). Our 440 

data suggest that perinatal oocytes deploy a germline-adapted version of this logic, in which 441 

PRC1 plays a disproportionately strong role at an earlier stage by remodeling CGI promoters 442 

during ovarian reserve formation and limiting H3K4me3 accumulation and transcriptional 443 

leakage at later poised promoters. At the same time, although the H3K27ac and H3K4me3 444 

changes described here are tightly associated with developmental progression and gene 445 

expression changes, our study does not support a generalized conclusion that such chromatin 446 

changes directly determine all aspects of later developmental competence. Resolving causality 447 

will require targeted functional dissection of individual loci and regulatory elements. 448 

> 449

In summary, our study identifies perinatal oogenesis as a developmental window in which 450 

Polycomb-mediated repressive programming and active chromatin remodeling are tightly 451 

integrated (Fig. 7). During this period, PRC1-dependent CGI promoter reprogramming is 452 

coupled to H3K27ac redistribution and bivalent promoter priming, thereby helping establish 453 

promoter states that support quiescence while preserving future growth potential. These findings 454 

extend the current framework of ovarian reserve biology beyond Polycomb-dependent 455 

repression, establish active chromatin remodeling as an integral component of perinatal oocyte 456 

development, and provide a molecular logic for how epigenetic priming is established in the 457 

female germline. 458

Oocyte collection 480

mice were isolated by FACS and the early GOs (P7) were manually collected under the 482

Ovaries at different developmental stages were harvested by carefully removing oviducts and 488

for bigger postnatal ovaries) with gentle agitation. After incubation, the ovaries were dissociated 492

visible tissue pieces. Two ml DMEM/F-12 medium (Gibco, 11330107) supplemented with 10% 494 

FBS (HyClone, SH30396.03) were then added to the suspension to stop enzyme reaction. 495

subjected to centrifugation at 300 × g for 5 min. The supernatant was decanted and the cells 498 

resuspended in FACS buffer and filtered into a 5 ml FACS tube with a 35 μm nylon mesh cap 499 

(Falcon, 352235). The cells were analyzed after removing small and large debris in FSC-A 500 

versus SSC-A gating, and doublets in FSC-W versus FSC-H gating. Then, the desired small 501 

oocyte population (enriched GFP+ cells with uniform size) was collected based on both GFP 502 

signal gating and FSC-A versus SSC-A size backgating. 503

CUT&Tag data processing 522 

Upstream processing . We basically followed the online tutorial posted by the Henikoff Lab 523 

(https://protocols.io/view/cut-amp-tag-data-processing-and-analysis-tutorial-bjk2kkye.html) with 524 

some modifications. Briefly, after trimming by Trim-galore 525 

(https://github.com/FelixKrueger/TrimGalore) (version 0.6.10), raw paired-end reads were 526 

aligned with the mouse genome (GRCm38/mm10) using Bowtie2 ( 70 ) (version 2.4.5) with 527 

options: --end-to-end --very-sensitive --no-mixed --no-discordant --phred33 -I 10 -X 700. D. 528 

melanogaster DNA delivered by Drosophila S2 cells was used as spike-in DNA for CUT&Tag. 529 

For mapping D. melanogaster spike-in fragments, we also use the --no-overlap --no-dovetail 530 

options to avoid cross-mapping. PCR duplicates from all mapped bam files were removed using 531 

the ‘MarkDuplicates’ command in Picard tools (version 3.0.0) 532 

(https://broadinstitute.github.io/picard/ ). To compare replicates, Pearson correlation coefficients 533 

were calculated and plotted by ‘multiBamSummary bins’ and ‘plot correlation’ commands from 534 

deepTools ( 71 ) (version 3.5.5). Biological replicates were pooled using the ‘merge’ command in 535 

samtools for visualization and other analyses after confirming reproducibility (Pearson 536 

correlation coefficient > 0.85). Spike-in normalization was implemented using the exogenous 537 

scaling factor computed from the dm6 mapping files (scale factors = 10,000 / aligned spike-in 538 

reads). 539

Downstream analysis and visualization . Spike-in normalized genome coverage tracks with 1 bp 541 

resolution in BigWig format were generated using ‘bamCoverage’ from deepTools with the 542 

parameters ‘--binSize 1 --extendReads --samFlagInclude 64 --normalizeUsing RPKM --543 

scaleFactor $scale_factor’. Track views were visualized and exported from Integrative Genomics 544 

Viewer ( 72 ) (version 2.14.1). Average scores (sRPKM: scaled RPKM) per region [10kb bins or 545 

custom intervals, e.g., peaks, promoters (TSS±1kb)] were calculated from scaled BigWig files 546 

using the ‘multiBigwigSummary bins’ or ‘multiBigwigSummary BED-file’ functions from 547 

deepTools with adding parameter ‘--outRawCounts’. Blacklisted regions were excluded from 548 

analyses by applying the parameter ‘--blackListFileName mm10-blacklist.v2.bed’ while using 549 

deepTools. This output matrix from deepTools is used for the following comparative analyses. 550 

Hierarchical clustering analysis was conducted in Morpheus using sRPKM values of each 10kb 551 

bin across the entire genome as input. Sum sRPKM values of all bins from each replicate track 552 

were used to calculate relative global levels of histone modifications. Following a previous study 553 

(73 ), average sRPKM values of 10kb bins or custom intervals are transformed to integers and 554

Fig. 7. Model for Polycomb-directed crosstalk between active and repressive chromatin during perinatal oogenesis 

Schematic model summarizing stage-specific crosstalk between Polycomb-mediated repressive chromatin and active chromatin during ovarian reserve formation and subsequent oocyte growth. During the perinatal oocyte transition, H3K27ac undergoes extensive redistribution, including CpG island promoter remodeling characterized by loss of H3K27ac and gain of PRC1-dependent H2AK119ub. This early promoter reprogramming is proposed to establish a repressive yet developmentally competent promoter framework in non-growing oocytes. During subsequent oocyte growth, broader gains in H3K27ac and H3K4me3 accompany acquisition of PRC2-dependent H3K27me3, de novo establishment of bivalent promoters, and formation of H2AK119ub-containing trivalent promoter states. PRC1 and PRC2 contribute unequally to these transitions: PRC2 broadly antagonizes H3K27ac, whereas PRC1 plays the stronger role in shaping developmental H3K27ac remodeling and restraining promoter-associated H3K4me3. Together, these processes establish promoter states that support quiescence while preserving future growth potential and developmental competence.
