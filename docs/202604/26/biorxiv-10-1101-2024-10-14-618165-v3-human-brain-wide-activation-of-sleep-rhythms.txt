Abstract 16 

During sleep, our brain undergoes highly synchronized activity, orchestrated by distinct neural 17 

rhythms. Little is known about the associated brain activation during these sleep rhythms, and even 18 

less about their functional implications. In this study, we investigated the brain-wide activation 19 

underlying human sleep rhythms by employing simultaneous electroencephalography (EEG) and 20 

functional magnetic resonance imaging (fMRI) in 107 participants during nocturnal nap (first half of 21 

the night). We identified robust coupling between slow oscillations (SOs) and fast spindles during 22 

deep non-rapid eye movement (NREM) sleep (N2/3 stages), with spindle peaks consistently occurring 23 

just before the SO UP-state. This SO-spindle coupling was linked to elevated activation in both the 24 

thalamus and hippocampus, alongside increased functional connectivity from the hippocampus to the 25 

thalamus and from the thalamus to the medial prefrontal cortex (mPFC). An open-ended cognitive 26 

state decoding analysis suggested that these activations may relate to episodic memory processes, yet 27 

were distinct from task-related networks. Together, these findings highlight the thalamus as a key 28 

coordinator of hippocampal-cortical communication during sleep and provide new insights into the 29 

mechanisms by which synchronized sleep rhythms may support memory consolidation. 30 

Keywords : slow oscillation (SO), spindle, hippocampus, thalamus, simultaneous EEG-fMRI 31 32

Introduction 33 

During sleep, the human brain engages in highly synchronized activity driven by distinct neural 34 

rhythms. In non-rapid eye movement (NREM) sleep, the emergence and coordination of slow 35 

oscillations (SOs) and sleep spindles are key features (Hahn et al., 2020; Helfrich et al., 2018; Ngo, 36 

Fell, & Staresina, 2020; Schreiner et al., 2022; Staresina et al., 2015; Staresina et al., 2023). However, 37 

the brain activation patterns associated with these rhythms, and especially their coordination, remain 38 

largely unexplored, and their functional implications are even less understood. 39 

SOs (~1 Hz) are characterised by fluctuations in neuronal membrane potential between periods of 40 

silence ('hyperpolarization', or ‘DOWN’ state) and excitation ('depolarization', or ‘UP’ state) (Amzica 41 

& Steriade, 2002; Steriade, McCormick, & Sejnowski, 1993). These oscillations originate in 42 

neocortical regions, particularly the medial prefrontal cortex (mPFC), during NREM sleep (Dang-Vu 43 

et al., 2008; Massimini et al., 2004; Nir et al., 2011). The depolarization phase of SOs is believed to 44 

trigger sleep spindles in the thalamus, producing 11–16 Hz oscillations (Fernandez & Lüthi, 2020; 45 

Mak-McCully et al., 2017). Thalamic spindles tend to align with the excitable UP-states of SOs and 46 

can synchronize with hippocampal ripples (Buzsáki, 2015; Helfrich et al., 2019; Joo & Frank, 2018; 47 

Ngo, Fell, & Staresina, 2020). Rodent optogenetics studies have shown that only thalamic spindles 48 

phase-locked to the UP-state of cortical SOs enhance memory consolidation, while out-of-phase 49 

spindles do not (Latchoumane et al., 2017). This finding is consistent with intracranial evidence from 50 

human epilepsy patients, suggesting the thalamus acts as a key relay between the hippocampus and 51 

cortex, coordinating their interaction (Coulon, Budde, & Pape, 2012; Ferraris et al., 2021; Schreiner et 52 

al., 2022; Staresina et al., 2015). 53 

The triple coupling of SOs, spindles, and ripples is believed to facilitate memory consolidation by 54 

synchronizing neuronal activity across brain regions and replaying memory traces from the 55 

hippocampus to the neocortex (Diekelmann & Born, 2010; Latchoumane et al., 2017; Liu et al., 2022; 56 

Singh, Norman, & Schapiro, 2022; Staresina et al., 2015; Staresina et al., 2023), possibly through 57 

relay from thalamic spindles. Consistent with this view, prior human EEG-fMRI studies have reported 58

spindle-related activation not only in the thalamus, but also in the hippocampus and adjacent 59 

parahippocampal gyrus (Bergmann et al., 2012; Schabus et al., 2007). Spindle-related activity has also 60 

been linked to striatal engagement, suggesting a broader network that may support memory-related 61 

processing during sleep (Fogel et al., 2017). However, the exact mechanisms of this inter-regional 62 

communication during coordinated sleep rhythms remain unclear. Understanding the brain-wide 63 

activation associated with these rhythms is crucial, as it reveals how information flows among brain 64 

regions, especially during the critical time windows when these rhythms align. 65 

Investigating these sleep-related neural processes in humans is challenging because it requires 66 

tracking transient sleep rhythms while simultaneously assessing their widespread brain activation. 67 

Recent advances in simultaneous EEG-fMRI techniques provide a unique opportunity to explore these 68 

processes. EEG allows for precise event-based detection of neural signal, while fMRI provides insight 69 

into the broader spatial patterns of brain activation and functional connectivity (Horovitz et al., 2008; 70 

Huang et al., 2024; Laufs, 2008; Laufs, Walker, & Lund, 2007; Spoormaker et al., 2010). Previous 71 

EEG-fMRI studies on sleep have examined both global sleep characteristics (Hale et al., 2016; 72 

Moehlman et al., 2019) and the neural correlates of specific waves, including slow oscillations and 73 

spindles. These studies have generally shown that slow oscillations are associated with widespread 74 

cortical and subcortical BOLD changes (Czisch et al., 2009; Ilhan-Bayrakcı et al., 2022; Picchioni et 75 

al., 2011), whereas spindles have been linked not only to thalamic activation but also to cortical and 76 

paralimbic regions, including the hippocampus and parahippocampal gyrus (Bergmann et al., 2012; 77 

Caporro et al., 2012; Fogel et al., 2017; Schabus et al., 2007). Although these findings provide 78 

important insight into the BOLD correlates of sleep rhythms, most previous studies have focused on 79 

individual oscillatory events rather than explicitly modelling their temporal interaction (Bergmann et 80 

al., 2012; Caporro et al., 2012; Fogel et al., 2017; Picchioni et al., 2011). Only a few recent studies 81 

have begun to examine coupling between rhythms directly, for example Huang et al. (2024). 82 

Understanding sleep rhythm-related brain activation is also essential for uncovering its potential 83 

cognitive functions. Since performing cognitive tasks during sleep is limited, most neuroimaging 84 

studies on human sleep rely on the targeted memory reactivation (TMR) paradigm, where participants 85

are re-exposed to sensory cues during sleep to trigger associated memory reactivation (Cousins et al., 86 

2016; Hu et al., 2020; Oudiette & Paller, 2013; Siefert et al., 2024). For instance, fMRI studies have 87 

shown significant hippocampal activation in response to odour re-exposure during sleep (Rasch et al., 88 

2007), while EEG studies suggest that this reactivation is more likely during the UP-state of SOs 89 

(Cousins et al., 2016; Göldi et al., 2019; Xia et al., 2023). However, while TMR provides insight into 90 

externally induced memory processing during sleep, it offers limited understanding of the endogenous 91 

neural mechanisms underlying natural sleep rhythms. 92 

To address this gap, our study investigates brain-wide activation and functional connectivity patterns 93 

associated with SO-spindle coupling, and employs a cognitive state decoding approach (Margulies et 94 

al., 2016; Yarkoni et al., 2011) - albeit indirectly - to infer potential cognitive functions. In the current 95 

study, we used simultaneous EEG-fMRI recordings during nocturnal naps (detailed sleep staging 96 

results are provided in the Methods and Table S1 ) in 107 participants. Although directly detecting 97 

hippocampal ripples using scalp EEG or fMRI is challenging, we expected that hippocampal 98 

activation in fMRI would coincide with SO-spindle coupling detected by EEG, given that SOs, 99 

spindles, and ripples frequently co-occur during NREM sleep. We also anticipated a critical role of the 100 

thalamus, particularly thalamic spindles, in coordinating hippocampal-cortical communication. 101 

We found significant coupling between SOs and spindles during NREM sleep (N2/3), with spindle 102 

peaks occurring slightly before the SO peak. This coupling was associated with increased activation in 103 

both the thalamus and hippocampus, with functional connectivity patterns suggesting thalamic 104 

coordination of hippocampal-cortical communication, in line with prior EEG-fMRI studies of spindle-105 

related activity (Bergmann et al., 2012; Caporro et al., 2012; Fogel et al., 2017; Schabus et al., 2007). 106 

These findings highlight the key role of the thalamus in coordinating hippocampal-cortical 107 

interactions during human sleep and provide new insights into the neural mechanisms underlying 108 

sleep-dependent brain communication. A deeper understanding of these mechanisms may contribute 109 

to future neuromodulation approaches aimed at enhancing sleep-dependent cognitive function and 110 

treating sleep-related disorders. 111 112

Results 113 

Simultaneous EEG-fMRI data during nocturnal sleep were collected from 138 healthy subjects (age, 114 

22.56 ± 0.30; 81 females). After excluding subjects due to excessive head motion (mean framewise 115 

displacement > 0.5) or insufficient scanning sleep duration (<10 min), 107 participants (age, 22.40 ± 116 

0.34; 63 females) were retained for subsequent analysis, with an average scanning time of sleep of 117 

3.01 ± 0.14 hours (see Methods and Table S1 ). Since NREM sleep predominantly occurs in early 118 

sleep (Carskadon & Dement, 2005), we conducted our scans during the first half of the night to ensure 119 

sufficient coverage of NREM sleep, this is also the period when sleep rhythms are most consistently 120 

observed and studied in relation to memory consolidation (Maingret et al., 2016; Molle et al., 2011; 121 

Schreiner et al., 2021; Staresina et al., 2015; Staresina et al., 2023). 122 123 

Sleep stages and sleep rhythms 124 

The primary objective of the EEG analysis was to examine the intrinsic characteristics of sleep 125 

rhythms and to identify the timing of these SO and spindle events for subsequent fMRI analyses. 126 

We first removed MRI gradient noise from the EEG signal (detailed in Methods, see Fig. S1 ) and then 127 

applied an automated sleep staging algorithm (Vallat & Walker, 2021). The staging results were 128 

manually reviewed by two sleep experts to ensure the accurate classification of each sleep stage ( Fig. 129 

1b ). Upon validation, we confirmed that N2/3 stages predominated in the dataset, with a significantly 130 

higher proportion of N2/3 sleep (75.77% ± 1.50%) compared to N1 (14.57% ± 1.06%, t(106) = 25.04, p131 

< 1e-4, paired-samples t test) and REM sleep (9.66% ± 0.90%, t(106) = 29.60, p < 1e-4, Fig. 1d, Table 132 

S1 ). 133 

Each sleep stage is characterised by distinct spectral properties and rhythmic waveforms, serving as 134 

physiological markers ( Fig. 1c ). Because SO and spindle detection relies on amplitude-based 135 

percentile thresholds, we avoided estimating separate thresholds within each sleep stage. Instead, for 136 

each participant, the SO and spindle thresholds were defined from N2/3 sleep only, where these 137

SO-spindle coupling 174 

SO-spindle coupling is considered important for sleep-dependent memory consolidation. In the 175 

current study, using the same N2/3-derived detection thresholds described above, we found that SO-176 

spindle coupling occurred most frequently during N2/3 sleep (2.46 ± 0.06 events/min). Coupling 177 

density was significantly lower in N1 (0.75 ± 0.05 events/min, t(106) = 23.54, p < 1e-4) and REM sleep 178 

(0.43 ± 0.04 events/min, t(106) = 31.24, p < 1e-4; Fig. 2b, Table S2-S4 ), consistent with the expected 179 

predominance of SO-spindle coupling in NREM sleep (Ngo et al., 2013; Staresina et al., 2015). As 180 

with the individual SO and spindle detections, coupling events detected in N1 and REM were retained 181 

only for descriptive stage-wise reporting (see Table S2, S4 ). They were not used to support 182 

physiological claims about SO-spindle coupling in these stages, and they were not entered into the 183 

EEG-informed fMRI analyses. All subsequent fMRI GLM and PPI analyses were restricted to N2/3 184 

sleep. 185 

After extracting all N2/3 EEG epochs in which SO-spindle coupling occurred, we analysed their 186 

spectral and phase characteristics. The spindles were most likely to occur slightly before the UP-state 187 

peak of SOs ( Fig. 2a, e ), aligning with results from both animal studies (Maingret et al., 2016) and 188 

human research (Staresina et al., 2015). In our data, this pattern was consistent across subjects ( Fig. 189 

2d , Rayleigh test: z = 9.51, p < 1e-4), with the peak of the spindle aligned at an SO phase of −41.61 ± 190 

0.86° (the SO UP-state peak is 0°). 191 

Interestingly, spindle events coupled with SOs had higher amplitudes (7.69 ± 0.26 μV) compared to 192 

those not coupled (7.33 ± 0.25 μV, t(106) = 4.65, p < 1e-4, Fig. 2c , left). Similarly, SO events coupled 193 

with spindles exhibited higher amplitudes (26.28 ± 1.34 μV) than those not coupled (24.56 ± 1.33 μV, 194 

t(106) = 6.86, p < 1e-4, Fig. 2c , right). These findings suggest coordinated neural processing during 195 

SO-spindle coupling. Next, we explore the brain-wide activation associated with these sleep rhythms. 196 197

Sleep rhythms associated brain activation 218 

To investigate brain-wide activity during sleep rhythms, we modelled the fMRI blood oxygen level-219 

dependent (BOLD) signal based on EEG-identified timing of SOs and spindle events during N2/3 220 

sleep stages. The design matrix included the main effects of SOs, spindles, and their coupling ( Fig. 3a ,221 

detailed in Methods). This method is validated in our previous work to effectively capture replay-222 

aligned brain activation during rest (Huang et al., 2024). We found that SOs were associated with 223 

positive activation in the thalamus (ROI analysis, t(106) = 2.38, p = 0.0096, one-sample t test) and 224 

significant deactivation in the neocortex ( Fig. 3b , see also Fig. S5 ), particularly in the default mode 225 

network (DMN), which includes the mPFC and posterior cingulate cortex (PCC), consistent with 226 

previous research (Dang-Vu et al., 2008; Picchioni et al., 2011; Tagliazucchi et al., 2013). This 227 

deactivation of the neocortex, particularly the DMN, at the trough of SOs is intriguing, given that the 228 

DMN is thought to encode the internal model of the world or cognitive map (Baldassano, Hasson, & 229 

Norman, 2018; Constantinescu, O ’Reilly, & Behrens, 2016; Park, Miller, & Boorman, 2021). 230 

Spindles were correlated with positive activation in the thalamus (ROI analysis, t(106) = 15.39, p < 1e-231 

4), the anterior cingulate cortex (ACC), and the putamen, alongside deactivation in the DMN (Fig. 3c ). 232 

Notably, SO-spindle coupling was linked to significant activation in both the thalamus (ROI analysis, 233 

t(106) = 3.38, p = 0.0005) and the hippocampus (ROI analysis, t(106) = 2.50, p = 0.0070, Fig. 3d ). 234 

However, no decrease in DMN activity was found during SO-spindle coupling, and DMN activity 235 

during SO was significantly lower than during coupling (ROI analysis, t(106) = -4.17, p < 1e-4). For 236 

more detailed activation patterns, see Table S5-S7 .237

series of control analyses. First, we excluded participants with less than 10 minutes of N2/3 sleep. 239 

Only three participants met this criterion, and their exclusion did not change the main results. For 240 

example, hippocampal activation during SO-spindle coupling remained significant ( t(103) = 2.50, p =241 

0.0071), comparable to the full sample ( t(106) = 2.50, p = 0.0070). Second, because the absolute 242 

number of detected SO-spindle coupling events depends on the SO detection threshold, we examined 243

To understand the functional implications of these sleep rhythm-related brain activation, we 251 

performed open-ended cognitive state decoding using the NeuroSynth database (Yarkoni et al., 2011), 252 

examining associations between topic terms and regions of interest derived from the activation 253 

patterns of the sleep rhythms. Topic terms were ranked by their z-scores across the rhythms to assess 254 

overall functional relevance (see Methods for details). For positive activation patterns ( Fig. S7a ), SOs 255 

were associated with declarative ( z = 6.31, p < 1e-4) and episodic memory ( z = 5.08, p < 1e-4), while 256 

spindles aligned most with working memory ( z = 4.08, p < 1e-4). SO-spindle coupling showed the 257 

strongest similarity to episodic ( z = 3.66, p = 0.0003) and declarative memory ( z = 2.21, p = 0.0271). 258 

For negative (deactivation) patterns ( Fig. S7b ), SOs were linked to declarative memory ( z = 7.61, p <259 

1e-4), while spindles resembled task-related activations ( z = 7.05, p < 1e-4). SO-spindle coupling had 260 

the highest similarity to task-related ( z = 9.40, p < 1e-4) and working memory ( z = 9.15, p < 1e-4). 261 

These findings suggest that internal memory-related regions are more likely to reactivate during SO-262 

spindle coupling, whereas task-related activations, like working memory, are less prominent. 263 264

Functional connectivity during SO-spindle coupling 278 

Previous studies have indicated that the hippocampal-cortical dialogue during sleep memory 279 

consolidation relies on the thalamus (Coulon, Budde, & Pape, 2012; Ferraris et al., 2021; 280 

Latchoumane et al., 2017). To explore this in the human brain, we examined how functional 281 

connectivity (FC) changes when spindles couple (versus not) with SOs during sleep, using 282 

psychophysiological interaction (PPI) analysis (Friston et al., 1997). Building on the previous GLM 283 

(Fig. 3a ), this model incorporated two additional regressors: the BOLD signal of the ROI seed and its 284 

interaction with SO-spindle events ( Fig. 4a , detailed in Methods). 285 

We first examined whole-brain FC (physiological term) in three ROIs: the hippocampus (bilateral, 286 

anatomically defined), thalamus (bilateral, anatomically defined), and mPFC (bilateral, functionally 287

Discussion 317 

Using simultaneous EEG and fMRI during nocturnal naps in 107 subjects, we identified robust 318 

coupling between spindles and the UP-state of SOs in deep NREM sleep (N2/3 stages). This coupling 319 

was linked to distinct neural activation patterns, including significant thalamic activation during 320 

spindle occurrences, reduced activity in the DMN during SOs trough, and selective hippocampal 321 

activation when spindles and SOs coupled. Furthermore, functional connectivity analyses revealed 322 

increased connectivity from the hippocampus to the thalamus and from the thalamus to the mPFC, 323 

only during SO-spindle coupling, but not isolated SOs or spindle events alone. These results shed 324 

light on how the thalamus may coordinate hippocampal-cortical interactions during NREM sleep. 325 

Our data provide insights into the neurobiological underpinnings of these sleep rhythms. SOs, 326 

originating mainly in neocortical areas such as the mPFC, alternate between DOWN- and UP-states. 327 

The thalamus generates sleep spindles, which in turn couple with SOs. Our finding that spindle peaks 328 

consistently occurred slightly before the UP-state peak of SOs (in 83 out of 107 participants), concurs 329 

with prior studies, including Schreiner et al. (2021). Yet it differs from some results suggesting 330 

spindles might peak right at the SO UP-state (Hahn et al., 2020; Helfrich et al., 2018). Such 331 

discrepancies could arise from differences in detection algorithms, participant age (Helfrich et al., 332 

2018), or subtle variations in cortical-thalamic timing. Nonetheless, these results underscore the 333 

importance of coordinated SO-spindle interplay in supporting sleep-dependent processes. 334 

When modelling the timing of these sleep rhythms in the fMRI, we observed hippocampal activation 335 

selectively during SO-spindle events. This suggests the possibility of triple coupling (SOs–spindles– 336 

ripples), even though our scalp EEG was not sufficiently sensitive to detect hippocampal ripples—key 337 

markers of memory replay (Buzsáki, 2015). Recent iEEG evidence indicates that ripples often co-338 

occur with both spindles (Ngo, Fell, & Staresina, 2020) and SOs (Staresina et al., 2015; Staresina et 339 

al., 2023). Therefore, the hippocampal involvement during SO-spindle events in our study may reflect 340 

memory replay from the hippocampus, propagated via thalamic spindles to distributed cortical regions. 341

The thalamus, known to generate spindles (Halassa et al., 2011), plays a key role in producing and 342 

coordinating sleep rhythms (Coulon, Budde, & Pape, 2012; Crunelli et al., 2018), while the 343 

hippocampus is found essential for memory consolidation (Buzsáki, 2015; Diba & Buzsáki, 2007; 344 

Singh, Norman, & Schapiro, 2022). The increased hippocampal and thalamic activity, along with 345 

strengthened connectivity between these regions and the mPFC during SO-spindle events, 346 

underscores a hippocampal-thalamic-neocortical information flow. This aligns with recent findings 347 

suggesting the thalamus orchestrates neocortical oscillations during sleep (Schreiner et al., 2022). The 348 

thalamus and hippocampus thus appear central to memory consolidation during sleep, guiding 349 

information transfer to the neocortex, e.g., mPFC. 350 

An intriguing aspect of our findings is the reduced DMN activity during SOs when modelled at the 351 

SO trough (DOWN-state). This reduced DMN activity may reflect large-scale neural inhibition 352 

characteristic of the SO trough. The DMN is typically active during internally oriented cognition (e.g., 353 

self-referential processing or mind-wandering) and is suppressed during external stimuli processing 354 

(Yeshurun, Nguyen, & Hasson, 2021). It is unlikely, however, that this suppression of DMN during 355 

SO events is related to a shift from internal cognition to external responses given it is during deep 356 

sleep time. Instead, it could be driven by the inherent rhythmic pattern of SOs, which makes it 357 

difficult to separate UP- from DOWN-states (the two temporal regressors were highly correlated, and 358 

similar brain activation during SOs events was obtained if modelled at the SO peak instead, Fig. S5 ). 359 

Since the amplitude at the SO trough is consistently larger than that at the SO peak, the neural 360 

activation we detected may primarily capture the large-scale inhibition from DOWN-state. 361 

Interestingly, no such DMN reduction was found during SO-spindle coupling, implying that coupling 362 

may involve distinct neural dynamics that partially re-engage DMN-related processes, possibly 363 

reflecting memory-related reactivation. Future research using high-temporal-resolution techniques 364 

like iEEG could clarify these possibilities. 365 

To explore functional relevance, we employed an open-ended cognitive state decoding approach using 366 

meta-analytic data (NeuroSynth: Yarkoni et al. (2011)). Although this method usefully generates 367 

hypotheses about potential cognitive processes, particularly in the absence of a pre- and post-sleep 368

memory task, it is inherently indirect. Many cognitive terms showed significant associations (16 of 369 

50), such as “episodic memory,” “declarative memory,” and “working memory.” We focused on 370 

episodic/declarative memory given the known link with hippocampal reactivation (Diekelmann & 371 

Born, 2010; Staresina et al., 2015; Staresina et al., 2023). Nonetheless, these inferences regarding 372 

memory reactivation should be interpreted cautiously without direct behavioral measures. Future 373 

research incorporating explicit tasks before and after sleep would more rigorously validate these 374 

potential functional claims. 375 

Despite providing new insights, our study has several limitations. First, our scalp EEG did not directly 376 

capture hippocampal ripples, preventing us from conclusively demonstrating triple coupling. Second, 377 

the combination of EEG-fMRI and the lack of a memory task limit our ability to parse fine-grained 378 

BOLD responses at the DOWN- vs. UP-states of SOs and link observed activations to behavioral 379 

outcomes. Third, sleep oscillation detection was based on a single frontal electrode. This choice 380 

improved signal stability and event timing in the prolonged simultaneous EEG-fMRI setting, but it 381 

did not exploit the full multichannel EEG information and cannot characterise the full spatial 382 

distribution of SOs and spindles. In particular, F3-based detection may be more sensitive to frontal 383 

SOs and frontal sigma activity than to the centro-parietal fast spindle component. We therefore 384 

interpret the EEG-informed fMRI results as reflecting BOLD activity associated with frontal-channel 385 

detected SOs, spindles, and their coupling during N2/3 sleep. Future studies using multichannel or 386 

source-informed detection strategies, with separate treatment of slow and fast spindles, will be better 387 

suited to capture the spatial dynamics of these sleep oscillations. Fourth, the use of large anatomical 388 

ROIs may mask subregional contributions of specific thalamic nuclei or hippocampal subfields. 389 

Finally, without a memory task, we cannot establish a direct behavioral link between sleep-rhythm-390 

locked activation and memory consolidation. Future studies combining ultra-high-field fMRI or iEEG 391 

with cognitive tasks, as well as multichannel or source-informed detection strategies that separately 392 

characterize slow and fast spindles, will be better suited to refine our understanding of subregional 393 

network dynamics and the functional significance of sleep oscillations. 394

In summary, we found that SO-spindle coupling during deep NREM sleep is associated with distinct 395 

activation in both the thalamus and hippocampus, accompanied by increased connectivity from the 396 

hippocampus to the thalamus and from the thalamus to the mPFC. These results emphasize the central 397 

role of the thalamus in orchestrating hippocampal-cortical interactions and highlight potential neural 398 

substrates underlying the complex interplay of SOs, spindles, and, possibly, ripples. Our open-ended 399 

decoding analyses suggest that these coordinated sleep events may be relevant for memory-related 400 

processes; however, future experiments incorporating explicit behavioral measures are necessary to 401 

confirm their functional significance. By revealing how these rhythms modulate widespread brain 402 

networks, our findings open avenues for developing targeted neuromodulatory strategies to enhance 403 

sleep-dependent cognitive functions and potentially address sleep-related disorders. 404

EEG sleep rhythms analysis 551 

In line with other studies examining the coordination between SOs and spindles, particularly their 552 

spatial distribution (Massimini et al., 2004; Molle et al., 2011), EEG data from the F3 electrode were 553 

used to quantify the occurrence of SOs and spindles. Using the established detection algorithms 554 

(detailed below), we identified SOs and sleep spindles for each subject. 555 

It is worth noting that the primary aim of EEG rhythm detection was to identify reliable event times 556 

for EEG-informed fMRI modelling. Detection was performed on the F3 electrode because this 557 

channel provided stable signal quality during prolonged nocturnal EEG-fMRI recordings. In our MR-558 

compatible EEG setup, FCz was used as the online reference. Central electrodes close to this 559 

reference, and electrodes near the vertex that were in prolonged contact with the head coil during 560 

supine sleep, were more susceptible to reduced signal contrast, impedance drift, and pressure-related 561 

degradation of electrode-scalp contact. We therefore used F3 as a pragmatic choice to maximize 562 

reliable event timing in N2/3 sleep. This choice was not intended to characterize the full scalp 563 

topography of SOs or spindles, and it may underrepresent the centro-parietal fast spindle component. 564 

As a sensitivity analysis, we repeated the main EEG-informed fMRI GLM using Fz, a midline frontal 565 

electrode, with the same detection and modelling procedure. 566 

Detection of SOs. Data were first bandpass-filtered between 0.16 and 1.25 Hz (Butterworth filter, 567 

order 3, bidirectional filtering for zero phase). After identifying all positive-to-negative zero crossings, 568 

potential SOs were defined based on the interval between consecutive zero crossings, ranging from 569 

0.8 s to 3 s. For each potential SO, we calculated the amplitude range as the peak minus the trough. 570 

For each participant, the amplitude threshold was defined as the 75th percentile of candidate SO 571 

amplitude ranges observed during N2/3 sleep. This fixed N2/3-derived threshold was then applied 572 

unchanged across the recording for descriptive stage-wise summaries. Detected events were assigned 573

fMRI GLM analysis 626 

All fMRI GLM analyses were conducted using only data from the N2/3 stages of NREM sleep. 627 

Segments of sleep data from these stages were concatenated for each participant. After pre-processing, 628 

the fMRI data was smoothed with an 8 mm FWHM kernel. All images underwent a temporal high-629 

pass filter (width of 100 s), and the autocorrelation of the hemodynamic response was modelled via an 630 

AR(1) model. We then conducted General Linear Model (GLM) analyses to identify whole-brain 631 

activations associated with sleep rhythms and their coupling. We incorporated nuisance regressors 632 

estimated during the preprocessing with fMRIprep: six rigid-body motion-correction parameters 633 

identified during realignment (comprising three translation and rotation parameters), the mean White 634 

Matter, and the mean Cerebral Spinal Fluid signals. The cosine drift model was employed to detect 635 

and eliminate data fluctuations, thereby enhancing the accuracy of actual experimental effects. The 636 

GLMs were designed to evaluate the influence of experimental conditions on BOLD responses. All 637 

whole-brain analyses were thresholded using whole-brain FWE corrected at the cluster level ( p <638 

0.05), with a cluster-forming voxel threshold of punc. < 0.001, unless otherwise specified. 639 

The fMRI GLM analysis was designed to look for brain activations pertinent to SO, spindle, and SO-640 

spindle coupling. Drawing from the EEG results concerning the timing of SO and spindle, we 641 

identified the trough of the SO as the onset for SO events and the peak of the spindle as the onset for 642 

spindle events. We chose the trough of the SO and the peak of the spindle as the onset points, 643 

primarily due to the larger amplitudes observed at these moments (thereby more robust to noise). This 644 

was also the common choice in previous EEG studies on SO and spindles (Ngo, Fell, & Staresina, 645 

2020; Staresina et al., 2015; Staresina et al., 2023). To model SO-spindle coupling in fMRI, we 646 

followed Schreiner et al. (2021), and defining SO-spindle complex as the peak of spindle appeared 647 

within 1.5 seconds after SO trough. When such a peak was found, that particular SO was labelled as 648 

an SO-spindle event, with its SO trough labelled as the event onset. After convolving these events 649 

with the canonical Hemodynamic Response Function (HRF) of SPM and downsampling them to align 650 

with the fMRI time resolution, they were integrated as regressors in the design matrix for GLM 651
