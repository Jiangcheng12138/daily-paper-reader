# Abstract 1

To survive and reproduce, living organisms need to maintain an efficient balance be-2

tween energy intake and energy expenditure. Changes in environmental conditions 3

can disrupt previously efficient energy allocation strategies, and organisms are required 4

to change their behaviour, physiology, or morphology to cope with the new environ-5

ment. However, how multiple phenotypic traits interact with one another and with 6

environmental conditions to shape energy allocation remains poorly understood. To 7

better understand this type of phenotype-environment interactions, we develop a pre-8

dictive framework, grounded in energetic and biophysical principles that allows us to 9

make predictions on how metabolic rate and movement speed should change in response 10 

to environmental temperature and resource supply, differentiating between short-term, 11 

acute exposure to novel conditions and longer-term exposure that allows acclimation 12 

or adaptation. We tested these predictions by exposing axenic populations of the ciliate 13 

Tetrahymena pyriformis to different combinations of temperature and resource availability. 14 

We measured population growth, cell size, respiration, and movement. Acute increases 15 

in temperature led to higher movement speeds and respiration rates, consistent with 16 

expectations from physical scaling relationships such as the Boltzmann-Arrhenius equa-17 

tion and the viscous drag acting on movement. However, by around 3.5 days after the 18 

introduction of Tetrahymena into a novel environment, all measured traits shifted toward 19 

values closer to those of the original environment. These changes likely reflect pheno-20 

typic acclimation responses that restored a more efficient energy allocation under the 21 

new conditions. Changes in cell size played a key role in this process, by simultaneously 22 

affecting multiple phenotypic traits, including metabolic rate and the energetic costs of 23 

movement. In small microbial consumers like Tetrahymena , body size can change rapidly, 24 

relative to ecological and seasonal timescales. Changes in body size can therefore be ef-25 

fectively leveraged - alongside physiological and biochemical regulations – to cope with 26 

environmental changes. 27

# Introduction 28 

To survive and reproduce, living organisms need to obtain energy from their environ-29 

ment, and to allocate it efficiently to competing processes of growth, movement, and 30 

reproduction (Alexander, 1996). Different strategies of energy allocation manifest, in 31 

practice, as different phenotypes: having a larger or smaller body size, or a different 32 

growth rate and movement patterns, for example. When the environment changes, the 33 

change can affect the organisms directly (e.g. by increasing the metabolic rate when tem-34 

perature rises) and indirectly (e.g. if resources become scarce). As a consequence, energy 35 

allocation strategies that used to be efficient in the original environment may become in-36 

efficient, and organisms are required to adapt to the new environment by changing their 37 

behaviour, or their phenotype, or they will incur a fitness cost. 38 

An extensive body of scientific literature has identified general patterns of phenotypic 39 

variation in relation to environmental variables such as temperature and the availability 40 

of resources. These empirically observed patterns are described in terms of ‘rules’ of 41 

phenotypic change such as the temperature-size rule – the observation that ectotherms 42 

grow to a larger adult size when they develop in a colder environment (Atkinson and 43 

Sibly, 1997; Angilletta Jr et al., 2004; Walters and Hassall, 2006), or the relation between 44 

metabolic rate, environmental temperature and body size observed across different taxa 45 

(Gillooly et al., 2001; Brown et al., 2004). Phenotypic traits such as the movement and 46 

the behaviour of organisms are also modulated in consistent ways by environmental 47 

variables, with temperature, again, playing an important role (e.g. Gibert et al., 2016; 48 

Abram et al., 2017). 49 

Understanding all these general patterns of phenotypic change is important because 50 

they not only affect the survival of individual organisms, but also ecological variables: 51 

population growth rate (DeLong and Hanson, 2009), carrying capacity (Bernhardt et al., 52 

2018), and predation risk (Gibert et al., 2017), with cascading effects beyond a single 53

However, it is also important to understand the ultimate causes, and particularly the 62 

adaptive value, of commonly observed phenotypic changes in relation to the environ-63 

ment. This type of understanding can help make general predictions about the direction 64 

of phenotypic change: if a phenotypic shift in a particular direction brings an adaptive 65 

benefit – we could argue – this is the kind of shift that is more likely to be produced and 66 

to persist in a population on the long-term, whether mediated by plasticity or by evolu-67 

tion. Phenomena that appear to be maladaptive, like non-adaptive reaction norms could 68 

then be interpreted in terms of limited previous selective pressure when organisms are 69 

exposed to environmental conditions outside the range of those historically experienced 70 

(Ghalambor et al., 2007). Transient phenotypic changes that are different over short and 71 

long timescales (Dupont et al., 2024; DeLong et al., 2017) can also be interpreted in rela-72 

tion to adaptive value benchmarks, for instance in terms of mechanistic constraints that 73 

could prevent the implementation of plastic adaptive responses over short timescales, 74 

such as temporal lags for genetic expression and morphological changes. 75

strategies are ‘optimal’ for a given set of environmental parameters, has been provided 77

number of different phenotypic traits, ranging from the size and shape of an organism 79 

(Cooper and Denny, 1997; West et al., 2001), to the arrangement of its constituent units, 80

In the present work, we adopt a related framework, which involves considering the 91 

energetic costs and benefits of different phenotypic and ‘behavioural’ traits explicitly, to 92 

study how a model microorganism, the ciliate Tetrahymena pyriformis (henceforth Tetrahy-93 

mena ) responds to changes in environmental conditions characterised by differences in 94 

temperature and concentration of food. We make predictions on the direction of change 95 

of phenotypic traits (i.e. whether they are expected to increase or decrease) for four main 96 

traits: rate of population increase, cell size (expressed as cell volume), respiration (as a 97 

proxy for metabolic rate), and movement speed. While the first three of these phenotypic 98 

traits have been extensively considered in the scientific literature on thermal adaptation, 99 

movement speed is a ‘behavioural’ trait that hasn’t yet systematically and quantitatively 100 

been put in relation with the other three. 101 

We test these predictions empirically, by measuring how the phenotypic traits of 102 

Tetrahymena change under different culture conditions and different timescales. We in-103 

terpret our results based on the idea that the physical parameters of the environment af-104 

fect the organisms directly (for example, through the effect of temperature on metabolic 105 

rate) and in so doing they alter the energetic costs and benefits of different phenotypic 106

Below, we analyse the model by asking questions such as: how should the swimming 178 

speed of Tetrahymena change with temperature if the energy that Tetrahymena allocated 179 

to movement was a fixed proportion of its total metabolic energy expenditure? And 180 

how should the swimming speed change as a function of temperature and food supply 181 

if Tetrahymena , instead, optimized the proportion of energy allocated to movement in 182 

each environment? To structure the range of possible theoretical questions, we focus 183 

in particular on formulating three testable predictions accompanied by a theoretically-184 

based consideration. 185 

Prediction 1: Exposure to high temperature and sufficient resources 186 

should lead to higher metabolic rate and faster population growth, un-187 

less compensated by other phenotypic changes 188 

The fact that metabolic rate increases with temperature is a direct implication of the 189 

Arrhenius-type dependence of metabolic rate on environmental temperature (eq. 6). 190 

As for population growth, we can reason as follows. When resources are abundant, 191 

feeding F will approach the maximum rate ( F ≃ fmax , eq. 4), and will mainly be limited 192 

by prey handling and digestion time (changes of movement speed U, and of the asso-193 

ciated prey encounter rate, have a comparatively smaller impact on the actual feeding 194 

in these conditions of abundant resources). This means that under abundant resources, 195 

the high metabolic rate induced by the higher temperature is also likely to speed up the 196 

digestion process: both the F and the B term in equation 1 increase in similar propor-197 

tion, and so also does their difference, resulting in faster per capita growth rate of the 198 

population. 199

Prediction 2: Cells exposed to a higher temperature over short timescales 205

If cells do not modulate the proportion of metabolic energy that they allocate to move-208

of increased environmental temperature, their movement speed should also increase: a 210 

faster metabolism can power a faster movement. However, speed should increase less 211 

than metabolic rate with increasing temperature, i.e. speed should have a lower activa-212

low Reynolds number, the power P dissipated for moving in water at speed U increases 214 

proportionally to the square of speed ( P ∝ U2, eq. 3), meaning that a 4-fold increase in 215 

the metabolic power invested into movement would lead only to a two-fold increase of 216 

swimming speed. This non-linear relation between power consumption and movement 217 

speed leads to a predicted activation energy for speed approximately 50 % the activation 218 

energy for respiration (this relation between metabolic power and movement speed can 219

Prediction 3: Short-term changes of movement speed and metabolic rate 226 

are unlikely to be sustainable in the long-term. 227 

The changes of metabolic rate and movement speed with environmental temperature 228 

predicted above reflect simple physico-chemical changes in the rate of metabolic reac-229 

tions, and in the physical forces acting on the microorganism from the environment 230 

(such as the drag) but they are unlikely to reflect efficient energy allocation strategies for 231 

the fitness of the individual organism. 232 

For example, an increase of movement speed at high temperature, and the associated 233 

increase of metabolic power P allocated to movement (as in eq. 1) is only beneficial if it 234 

is also matched by a corresponding increase in energy intake from feeding F. However, 235 

this is unlikely to be the case because the energetic gains from feeding are expected to 236 

increase more slowly than the energetic costs of movement. In simple encounter-based 237 

models, the volume searched for food – and thus the attack rate – scales at most linearly 238 

with speed (at best, F ∝ U in eq. 4), while the energetic cost of movement increases 239 

faster than speed ( P ∝ U2). 240 

This mismatch between feeding energy acquisition rate and power consumption of 241 

movement is robust to the details of model formulation: any mechanism for which feed-242 

ing increases sub-quadratically with speed will lead to the same qualitative outcome. 243 

For example, models that account for the diffusion of food molecules (or for prey move-244 

ment, in case of living prey), predict that an increase of predator speed leads to less than 245 

proportional gains in food intake (Karp-Boss et al., 1996). Digestion time can further 246 

limit the uptake of resources independently of the rate at which they are encountered. 247

Consideration: Changes in cell volume and morphology can modulate 294 

metabolic rate and movement speed 295 

Phenotypic changes that affect the total metabolic rate of an organism, and the alloca-296 

tion of metabolic energy to maintenance, movement, growth, and reproduction, could 297 

involve changes in its physiology, e.g. biochemical regulations related to enzyme struc-298 

ture, changes in the concentration of regulatory intracellular molecules, density of mito-299 

chondria etc. (see e.g. Angilletta, 2009 for a review). However, also morphological traits 300 

such as cell volume and elongation can affect metabolic rate and movement speed. We 301 

see this in equation 2, where metabolic rate scales with cell volume and in equation 3, 302 

where power dissipation is dependent on parameters s which is itself proportional to cell 303 

radius. For this reason, we can expect that modulations of metabolic rate and movement 304 

speed can be mediated by changes in cell volume and shape, in addition to changes in 305 

physiology. 306

to empirical measurements on how Tetrahymena pyriformis responds to changes of envi-308

Design of the experiment 319 

Cells were initially maintained in the exponential growth phase at 20 °C and 100 % 320 

medium concentration for approximately 50 days (under constant temperature and in 321 

the absence of light) to acclimate to our laboratory conditions in preparation for the 322 

experiment. At the beginning of the experiment, cultures were split across 9 different 323 

treatments (3 temperature conditions × 3 medium concentrations), and were exposed to 324 

these stable growth conditions for at least three weeks (corresponding to a minimum of 325 

20 generations), in four replicates for each condition (see fig. 2 for a schematic diagram of 326 

the experiment and Appendix section 1 for a detailed description of the culture protocol). 327 

We chose a treatment duration of at least 3 weeks because previous studies had shown 328 

that after such duration Tetrahymena develop detectable phenotypic changes (Fussmann 329 

et al., 2017). Throughout the entire study, exponential growth was maintained by adopt-330 

ing a serial transfer regime, which involved the transfer of approximately 1 mL of culture 331 

into approximately 24 mL of fresh growth medium every 1-4 days, as required to keep 332 

population densities within the exponential growth range (details in the Appendix, sec-333 

tion 1). At the end of the main acclimation period to different temperature and resources, 334 

cultures were further split across a wide range of temperature conditions (keeping the 335 

same medium concentration at which they were acclimated) to measure thermal re-336 

sponse curves for different phenotypic traits (respiration rate, cell volume, movement 337 

speed, and population growth rate). Specifically, we measured acute thermal responses 338 

for movement speed and respiration. For movement speed, measurements were taken 339 

1.5–3 minutes after transfer to the test temperature. For respiration, measurements were 340 

taken between approximately 30 minutes to three hours after transfer. We refer to re-341 

sponses measured over timescales of minutes as acute, corresponding to a ‘short-term, 342 

transient exposure’. We also measured medium-term responses, over timescales of a few 343 

days (2 to 6 generations after the change of environment, corresponding to 3.5 days on 344

Population growth 347 

Population densities were estimated at the time of each subculture into fresh medium 348 

(i.e. every 1 to 4 days) by counting cells under the microscope. Assuming exponen-349 

tial growth throughout the experiment, the per capita growth rate of the population (or 350 

‘growth rate’, for brevity), expressed in number of generations per day, was calculated as 351 

log 2(N(t)/N(0))/d, where N(0) is the density at the beginning of a subculture, N(t) is the 352 

final density of the subculture, and d is the length of the subculture period in number of 353 

days. Note that we use the logarithm base 2, rather than the natural logarithm, because 354 

in the case of organisms that reproduce by binary divisions like Tetrahymena this has an 355 

intuitive interpretation as the number of generations per day (Reiss and Schmid-Araya, 356 

2010). 357

Feeding rate 391 

One additional variable that we consider in interpreting our results in terms of the en-392 

ergy budget of Tetrahymena is its feeding rate. We do not measure the feeding rate of 393 

Tetrahymena directly in our experiments, although we have indirect evidence about feed-394 

ing from other experimental variables such as population growth rate. We model feeding 395 

rate based on a Holling type II functional response function in which movement speed 396 

determines the ‘attack rate’ (as described in the ‘Theoretical framework’ section, eq. 4). 397 

Statistical analyses 398 

We used linear mixed-effects (LME) modeling (Bolker et al., 2009; Zuur et al., 2009) to 399 

test for the effects of temperature and growth medium concentration (included as fixed 400 

categorical factors) on population growth rate and individual cell volume using the lme4 401 

package in R v.3.0.2 (R Core Team, 2022). Culture identity was added as a random effect 402 

to account for the multi-level structure of our data (i.e. cultures nested within treat-403 

ments) and its unbalanced nature (e.g. there is variation in the number of measurements 404 

made for different culture lines). Significance of fixed factors (temperature and medium 405 

concentration) and their interactions were determined using Satterthwaite’s method for 406 

approximating degrees of freedom for the t and F tests from the lmerTest package in R.407

Temperature responses for respiration and movement 488 

Focusing first on the effects of the transient conditions at which cells are tested, metabolic 489 

rate (respiration) was expected to increase with the temperature at which cells are tested 490 

(prediction 1), and we predicted that movement speed would also increase with in-491 

creasing temperature, but with a less strong effect of temperature than for respiration, 492 

i.e. with a lower activation energy (prediction 2). In our data, the metabolic rate of 493 

Tetrahymena – measured from oxygen consumption – showed a clear dependence on the 494 

temperature at which cells were tested, with values of activation energy E measured 495 

from respiration broadly in the range of commonly reported values for metabolic en-496

1). 498

# Discussion 590 

Based on well-established bio-physical principles and energetic considerations, we pre-591 

dicted distinct patterns of phenotypic changes in unicellular consumers over short and 592 

over long timescales, particularly in relation to their movement speed. 593 

Short-term effects of environmental conditions 594 

We assumed that, over short-term, transient exposure to novel environmental conditions, 595 

the direct effects of temperature and resource availability dominate over physiological 596 

regulations and morphological phenotypic changes. This assumption allowed us to pre-597 

dict that the movement speed of swimming ciliates should have a lower activation energy 598 

than their metabolism, due to the quadratic scaling of power consumption with move-599 

ment speed for swimmers in a viscous medium (prediction 2). The thermal response 600 

curves experimentally measured on Tetrahymena broadly confirmed this prediction, as 601 

the values of activation energy measured for movement speed under a short-term, tran-602 

sient exposure were lower than those measured for respiration (a proxy for metabolic 603

Conclusion 728 

Our study highlights the multiple changes associated with the response of microorgan-729 

isms to changes in their environment. Short-term, acute responses to changes in the 730 

environment are characterised by rapid and marked changes in metabolic activity and 731 

movement speed. These short-term changes mainly reflect simple physico-chemical pro-732 

cesses, such as the effects of temperature on the rates of metabolic reactions (Brown et al., 733 

2004; Arroyo et al., 2022) and the temperature dependence of water viscosity (Beveridge 734 

et al., 2010). Yet, movement and respiration are not affected in the same way by changes 735

Temperature affects the solubility of oxygen and can also affect the readings of the 1070 

Sensor Dish Reader directly. Because of this, we had to take a series of precautions to 1071 

minimize the changes of temperature during the measurements of respiration, in spite of 1072 

the fact that, necessarily, there is a change of temperature associated with moving cells 1073 

from their incubation temperature to the temperature at which they needed to be tested 1074 

for respiration. 1075 

In advance of any respiration measurement, we filled small disposable flasks each 1076 

with approximately 5 mL of sterile culture medium at the desired concentration (50 %, 1077 

100 %, 200 %) and stored them at the same temperature at which we would later measure 1078 

respiration. This was done so that we had fresh culture medium already at the right 1079 

temperature. We also placed the empty oxygen sensor vials into their holding wells in 1080 

the Sensor Dish Reader, and we placed the whole kit in the incubator to acclimate at the 1081 

desired temperature. 1082 

Just before the respiration measurement started, we took cells from their incubation 1083 

temperature, we counted their density in cell-counting slides under the microscope and 1084 

we transferred the appropriate amount of cell suspension into the previously prepared 1085 

small disposable flasks with culture medium at the testing temperature, so as to obtain 1086 

a density of approximately 8000 cells/mL. We then mixed gently by swirling the flasks 1087 

and we used this diluted suspension to fill the oxygen sensor vial. The vials prepared in 1088 

this way were then placed back onto the Sensor Dish reader and in the thermal cabinet, 1089

2.11 Impact of cell size on movement speed 1470 

Larger organisms move typically faster than smaller ones. They also have a faster per 1471 

capita metabolic rate, simply because of their size. The observation that, in our exper-1472 

iments, Tetrahymena cells changed their volume rapidly and substantially, depending 1473 

on the experimental conditions, begs for the question: to what extent do the observed 1474 

changes in cell volume alone account for the other observed changes in metabolic rate 1475 

and movement speed? 1476 

To better understand the possible relation between cell volume and speed, we can 1477 

consider the example of an isogeometric unicellular organism (an organism that could 1478 

have different sizes but which preserves the same shape): its metabolic rate – and total 1479 

available power – would scale as the volume of the cell, or as the third power of the 1480 

linear dimension (assuming a linear scaling between metabolic rate and cell volume in 1481 

unicellular eukaryotes; DeLong et al., 2010), and this metabolic power would have a 1482 

positive impact on speed; the viscous drag, having a negative impact on speed, would 1483 

scale as the linear dimension (e.g. eq. 3, expanded in Appendix eq. 13). If we further 1484 

assume that a constant fraction of metabolic energy is allocated to movement, then speed 1485 

U depends on the invested power P as P = sU 2 (eq. 3). Here, P is proportional to V and 1486 

s is proportional to the linear cell dimension ( s ∝ V1/3 ). Combining these relationships 1487 

leads to the prediction: U ∝ Vα with α = 1/3. (If, instead of assuming a linear scaling of 1488 

metabolic rate with cell volume we assumed a 3/4 power scaling (as in Kleiber’s law), 1489 

the same reasoning would lead to a predicted relationship between movement speed 1490 

and cell volume as U ∝ Vα, with α = 5/24). 1491 

Based on these scaling arguments, we could predict that a change by a factor 2 in 1492 

the cell volume would translate into a factor 2 1/3 ≈ 1.26 in the change of speed (or 1493 

25/24 ≈ 1.16 if we assumed a 3/4 power scaling of metabolic rate with cell volume). 1494 

Inevitably, this isogeometric unicellular organism is only a very crude approximation 1495 

of our Tetrahymena . Among the limitations of this approximation we could mention the 1496
