drivers and providing a reliable tool to monitor phenological shifts in response to climate 47

Background 53 

Hibernation in mammals is a seasonal physiological adaptation that allows animals to survive 54 

periods of cold temperatures and limited food availability by significantly reducing metabolic 55 

activity  (1) . The timing of entering and exiting a hibernation den  is affected  by several factors 56 

including  environmental conditions, physiological state, and energetic constraints  (2 –4) . Shifts 57 

in  the  duration  of  dennin g can have major  impacts on  body condition, reproduction, and 58 

individual survival, and  ultimately  on  population dynamics  (5 –7) . In the context of climate 59 

change, phenological shifts  in denning behavior  have been observed in several species  (4,8,9) .60 

For example,  as winters are getting shorter  in  Alaska , USA,  female arctic ground squirrels 61 

(Urocitellus parryi i) stop  hibernation  10 days  earlier  than they used to  25  years ago  (4) .62 

Accurate e stimati on of  denning  timing  and  long -term  monitor ing  of changes in this important 63 

behaviour  is essential for assessing  the potential  effects  of climate change on hibernating 64 

species . Thus, r eliable methods to estimate dates  of key phenological events  are required .65 

Several methods have been developed to estimate den entry and exit dates. For example, den 66 

exit  can be inferred from direct visual observations of emergence events or from the presence 67 

of tracks at the hibernaculum entrance,  e.g. in  rodents  (9,10) . Other approaches include the use 68 

of antenna systems placed at the entrance of the  den  to record entry and exit events of 69 

individuals equipped with tags, as applied in bats  (11,12) , or the deployment of camera traps, 70 

as for the Eur asian  badger ( Meles meles ) (13) . Overall, these methods can provide relatively 71 

fine -scale d estimates of denning phenology.  However, these approaches are difficult to apply 72

In hibernating bears ( Ursus arctos , U. thibetanus , and  U. americanus ), although some studies 75 

have relied on observations of tracks or direct observations of bears to estimate den entry and 76 

exit dates  (14,15) , VHF and GPS data are by far the most commonly used sources of 77 

information  (2,16 –20) . The advent of VHF and GPS tracking has allowed for more refined 78 

estimates, either by using the loss of VHF or GPS signals as an indicator of den entry  (18 –20) ,79 

by identifying periods when signals become stationary  (16 –18) , or by analyzing changes in 80 

movement speed and direction  (2) . Nevertheless, these approaches rely on indirect indicators 81 

of hibernation and may lack precision, as bears often reduce their movements gradually over 82 

several days or even weeks before fully retreating into their dens  (16,17,21) . Other studies have 83 

used body temperature thresholds recorded with implanted sensors to determine den entry and 84 

exit dates  (22,23) , as is sometimes done in other species, although such methods are generally 85 

applied to study hibernation physiology rather than denning phenology per se  (4,13) .86 

Activity data recorded by accelerometers  mounted on collars have also been used to infer den 87 

entry and exit dates  (21,24 –26) . An activity index  as well as  a threshold  below which the animal 88 

is assumed to be inactive  can be calculated based  on acceleration values  to identify prolonged 89 

periods  of  inactivity corresponding to hibernation  (21,25 –28) . However, activity thresholds can 90 

be difficult to determine in wild‐living animals because their validation requires direct 91 

behavioral observations, leading studies on wild bears to rely on thresholds derived from 92 

observations of captive individuals.  For example, a n activity threshold  was defined based on 93 

observations made on two captive  brown  bears  during their sleep and resting periods  (29) , and 94 

later applied to wild individuals  (21,25,26) . However,  threshold s based on captive individuals 95 

may  not necessarily reflect conditions in the wild  because  classification models trained on 96 

captive animals do not always transfer reliably to free‑ranging individuals  (30) , and 97

can vary among individuals and across seasons  (31) . However, collar activity data are more 99 

informative than GPS alone for studying denning behavior because they capture fine -scale 100 

changes in movement patterns, including subtle shifts associated with pre -denning and early 101 

den exit , as well as short forays outside the den  (32) . As shown by Archer et al.  (2025)  for 102 

denning polar bears (U. maritim us), activity measurements closely matched camera 103 

observations of den exit, whereas GPS data provided only coarse location information and could 104 

miss brief emergence events, potentially biasing estimates of den departure timing  (32) .105 

In this study, we used data from a brown bear population in south -central Sweden to develop a 106 

method to estimate the timing of denning, defined by the start and end of the inactive period 107 

derived from accelerometer -based activity data, rather than relying solely on movement patterns 108 

inferred from GPS locations. We used day -to -day changes in activity to define adaptive, 109 

individual -based thresholds that account for inter -individual variation in baseline activity, 110 

thereby avoiding the use of fixed cut -off values.  Then, w e compared the estimates obtained 111 

with this activity -based method to those derived from commonly used GPS -based approaches 112 

that infer inactivity from movement data. Finally, because denning phenology in brown bears 113 

varies with sex , age  and reproductive status  (16,17,20) , we assessed variation in denning 114 

behavior across demographic groups to evaluate whether our method yields den entry and exit 115 

chronologies consistent with those reported in previous studies using alternative methods.  In 116 

addition, because denning is a phenological behavior  that may be  affected  by temporal 117 

environmental variation, including climate change  (4,9) , we evaluated interannual trends to 118 

assess whether  shift s over time could affect denning timing and  consequently,  influence 119 

comparisons with estimates reported in previous studies. 120

Jämtland  County , south -central Sweden  (~61°N, 15°E ). The area is  covered by heavily 125

Determination of  start and end  dates  of the inactive period 175 

We  determined  start and end  dates  using  six  steps :176 

1)  We selected  all  bear -winters , defined as  one denning period for a given individual  with 177 

activity data for each day of the potential denning period, which  ranges  from  September 178 

until May  (17) .179 

2)  We  calculated a 5 -minute  activity ind ex  ranging from 0 to 510  by summing the mean 180 

acceleration values from the two orthogonal axes  (21) .181 

3)  We used the  5-minute  activity indices  to  calculate  mean daily  activity  values  for each 182 

individual  bear -winter .183 

4)  For each  bear -winter , we  carried out  a Hidden Markov Model (HMM)  (39)  with three 184 

behavioral  states (active, transition al,  inactive)  based on mean daily activity  values .185 

HMM s are  flexible model s used to  analys e time series  data  generated by underlying 186 

hidden processes or states  (40) . They are  commonly  used to identify  different behavioral 187 

states and  evaluate  the factors influencing transition s from one state to another  (41) . We 188 

used a model with  three  behavioral  activity levels  to balance  model accuracy  and  state 189 

interpretability  (42) . The lowest activity level corresponds to an  “inactive state ”, the 190 

highest activity level to an  “active  state ” and  the  intermediate  level to  a “transitional 191 

state ”. We used a  gamma distribution , because  it  characterizes  positive continuous 192 

random variable s with  asymmetry near zero.  We added a zero mass parameter, which 193 

combines  a binomial distribution  with  the gamma distribution to account for the 194 

inflation of zero counts  (43) . Since  we  did  not expect  that  the active and transitional 195 

state s exhibit zero activity,  we  constrain ed  the  zero -mass  parameter to zero for these 196 

two  states , which improve d model likelihood.  We fitted HMMs using numerical 197

Determination of  dates of arrival and departure from den site 224 

We identified the location of each den in every  bear -winters  using GPS data  to  determine the 225 

arrival and departure dates  of an individual bear  from the den site. Because bears may 226 

occasionally switch dens during winter  (21,47) , our first step was to identify the  location of  den 227 

sites  used at both the  start  and the end of the inactivity period  identified by activity -based 228 

HMMs . Since GPS  position  intervals  can be irregular, we applied the  crawlWrap  function from 229 

the  momentuHMM  package to interpolate temporally regular daily locations  (43) . We then 230 

performed a sequential clustering of the GPS data  with  the GPSeqClus package  (48) . We 231 

aggregated locations into  spatio -temporal  clusters based on three criteria: a 50 m search radius, 232 

a 3 -day temporal window, and a minimum of three  GPS  locations . For each cluster identified 233 

as a den, we calculated centroid coordinates using the median of  x and y coordinates  and added 234 

a 50m buffer to define  a den site  (21) .235 

To minimize the risk of misclassifying temporary movements, we applied strict criteria to define 236 

arrival and departure dates.  The arrival date at the den site was defined as the last day of a period 237 

of at least seven consecutive days spent outside the  50m  den site buffer prior to the inactivity 238 

period, a threshold chosen arbitrarily to ensure sustained site use rather than transient visits. 239 

The departure date was defined as the first day of a period of at least seven consecutive days 240 

spent outside the den site  buffer  following the inactivity period. 241 

Lastly, w e studied the sequential occurrence of  the start and end dates of the inactivity period 242 

identified using HMMs and the arrival and departure dates at the den site estimated from GPS 243 

locations  using  descriptive statistics . The correlation between  arrival date and start of inactivity 244 

period  and  between  departure date and end of inactivity period  was assessed  using  a Pearson 245 

linear correlation coefficient with its 95% confidence .246

Variation in denning behavior across demographic groups  and years 248 

We compared  start and end  dates  of the inactivity period  across demographic  groups  by 249 

calculating median dates and through graphical representations.  To assess whether denning 250 

phenology differed among demographic groups, we used linear mixed -effects models fitted 251 

with the lme rTest  (49)  package in R  (44) . Separate models were built for  start  and  end  dates  of 252 

the inactivity period  (expressed as Julian day). In each model, demographic group  and year 253 

(centered on the mean sampling year)  were  included as fixed effect s. Individual identity  was 254 

included as random intercepts to account for repeated measures of the same individuals across 255 

years. Models were fitted using restricted maximum likelihood (REML). Model assumptions 256 

were assessed by visually inspecting residual plots and Q –Q plots to verify homoscedasticity, 257 

normality of residuals, and the absence of strong outliers or influential points. Significance was 258 

set at  α = 0.05  for all tests. 259 

Estimated marginal means (LS -means) were computed for each demographic group using the 260 

emmeans  package  (50) . LS -means provide adjusted group means that account for the random 261 

effects structure of the model and potential unbalanced sample sizes. Pairwise contrasts 262 

between  demographic  groups  were then performed using Tukey’s post hoc tests to control for 263 

Type I error inflation associated with multiple comparisons. 264

Discussion 331 

In this study, we  develop and test  a flexible and reproducible framework for identifying the 332 

onset and termination of inactivity, as well as estimating den entry and exit dates.  The HMM -333 

based method successfully identified periods of inactivity for 360 out of 388 bear -winters. 334 

Among the 28 cases where classification failed, one involved a pregnant female, for whom 335 

elevated activity levels during gestation were misclassified as a transition period rather than as 336 

part of the inactivity period. Notably, this issue did not occur in the remaining 144 bear -winters 337 

of pregnant females included in the analysis.  We compared these dates obtained from 338 

accelerometer -derived activity data with the dates of arrival at and departure from the den site 339 

derived from GPS data and observed a significant correlation between the two sets of 340 

measurements.  This discrepancy indicates that the two approaches do not capture  the same 341 

behavioral phenomenon, and that  den site occupancy includes active phases that may occur 342 

both before and after the actual denning period. 343

Discrepancy between GPS and activity -based methods 345 

The estimation of den  entry and exit dates  solely based on  GPS data  has  several limitations. A 346 

stationary  GPS position indicates only that  an individual  remains  at  the same location , however, 347 

does not  necessarily represent  inactivity  (2,20) . Moreover, the accuracy of GPS signals 348 

generally does not allow  for the detection of complete immobility  (2,20,25) . Finally, GPS units 349 

may fail to record locations for extended periods, thereby reducing data quality and tracking 350 

precision, a limitation that frequently occurs when bears are inside their dens  (20,32) . These 351 

limitations are reflected in our dataset:  we were able to confidently identify the first and last 352 

den site locations for only 75% and 57% of cases, respectively, resulting in a substantial 353 

reduction in the number of den -site arrival and departure dates that could be identified . For one 354 

bear -winter, the arrival date occurred two days after the identified start of inactivity. This 355 

discrepancy likely results from the bear using its first den for fewer than three days before 356 

switching, which prevented proper identification and localization using our method. 357 

In contrast, activity data derived from accelerometers are not subject to many of the limitations 358 

affecting geolocation systems and are typically recorded at a finer temporal resolution, 359 

providing a large number of observations per day. Furthermore, our methodology accounts for 360 

individual variability in activity patterns by operating at the individual level rather than 361 

applying a fixed activity threshold across all bears, thereby reducing bias and improving 362 

detection reliability. In addition, bear metabolism slows substantially during hibernation  (2) ,363 

and the space available inside dens is closely related to individual body size  (51) . Movements 364 

within the den are therefore constrained, likely resulting in the very low activity levels detected 365 

by accelerometers. For these reasons, we consider the start and end dates of the inactivity period 366 

to be relevant indicators of den entry and exit, respectively, whereas GPS data are more suitable 367 

for estimating general presence  at a den site .368

of  Evans et al.  (2) . These discrepancies were particularly pronounced at den exit, sometimes 374 

lasting several days or even weeks , which  may  also  explain why studies  do  not consistently 375 

detect clear relationships  between climatic factors and den entry and exit dates . For instance, 376 

Evans et al.  (2) , who defined den exit dates based on movement patterns detected by GPS data, 377 

found no significant climatic effect on den e xit . In contrast , Pigeon et al.  (20) , who identified 378 

exit dates  based on GPS signal recovery after total loss following entry into the den , found an 379 

effect of temperature on den exit as well as an effect of winter precipitation on den entry. 380

Denning phenology in our study area 382 

Our results indicate that bears may arrive at the den site up to several weeks before the onset of 383 

inactivity  with variation among  demographic group . This delay  was longer for subadults than 384 

for solitary adults. This pattern is consistent with the findings of Sahlén et al.  (21) , who reported 385 

a decrease in the time spent at the den site prior to entry with increasing age, possibly reflecting 386 

greater experience and more efficient  den construction and  denning behavior in older 387 

individuals.  From a management perspective, this pre -denning period may represent a critical 388 

window of increased human –bear interactions. In Sweden, many incidents involving humans, 389 

particularly hunters, occur during this period, often in close proximity to den sites  (21,52) .390 

Changes in bear behavior at that time, including reduced activity and a lower propensity to flee, 391

brown bears emerging progressively earlier by approximately 7 days. This shift in denning 413 

phenology may reflect plastic responses to interannual environmental variation, such as warmer 414 

spring temperatures or changes in food availability but that remains to be assessed. 415

Conclusion 429 

In conclusion, our study highlights the value of activity -based analyses for accurately 430 

identifying den entry and exit in brown bears. By distinguishing true inactivity from simple 431 

den -site presence, this approach overcomes key limitations of GPS -only methods and reveals 432 

substantial lags between arrival and den entry, as well as between den exit and final departure. 433 

These results emphasize the complexity of denning transitions and are essential for correctly 434 

interpreting links between denning phenology and environmental drivers, particularly under 435 

climate change. Overall, individual -level activity data provide a robust framework for 436 

improving phenological estimates and advancing our understanding of hibernation ecology in 437 

bears and other hibernating species. 438
