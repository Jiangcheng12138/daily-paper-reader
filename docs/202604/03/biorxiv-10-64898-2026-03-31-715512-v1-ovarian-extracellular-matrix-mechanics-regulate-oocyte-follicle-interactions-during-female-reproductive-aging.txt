Abstract 18 

19 

Female reproductive aging is associated with ovarian functional decline, leading to infertility. 20 During aging, biochemical and biophysical changes in the ovarian extracellular matrix (ECM) 21 occur, yet how these properties affect follicle growth and oocyte quality remains poorly 22 understood. Here we describe spatiotemporal changes in the ovarian ECM with age using mass 23 spectrometry, immunohistochemistry, and nanoindentation. While follicle stiffness remains 24 unchanged, stromal matrix remodeling is associated with a ~2.5-fold increase in stiffness. To 25 understand how this increase in stromal stiffness affects age-related follicular dysfunction, 26 isolated young follicles were cultured in soft and stiff hydrogels mimicking young and aged 27 ovarian stromal stiffness, respectively. Higher stiffness leads to a decrease in granulosa cell 28 (GC) proliferation, oocyte quality, and GC-oocyte interactions mediated via transzonal 29 projections (TZPs). RNA-seq revealed TGF-b signaling as a major pathway affected by 30 stiffness, and activation of TGF-b signaling through Mongersen treatment rescued TZP 31 formation and oocyte quality in stiff matrix. These findings provide mechanistic insight into 32 how changes in ECM mechanics contribute to ovarian aging functional decline and reveal 33 potential therapeutic targets to counter fertility loss associated with tissue aging and fibrosis. 34

Introduction 38 

39 

Female fertility declines progressively with age, resulting in an increased rate of spontaneous 40 abortion and a reduced rate of conception ( 1, 2). This age-related fertility decline is primarily 41 attributed to a decrease in follicular quantity and quality ( 3). As the ovarian reserve pool is 42 finite and unable to self-renew, developing strategies to preserve or restore follicular quality is 43 a main approach to address age-associated fertility loss. 44 45 A multitude of factors at both the cell and tissue level contribute to follicular dysfunction during 46 aging ( 4). In aged follicles, granulosa cells (GCs) exhibit reduced proliferation and increased 47 apoptosis, whereas oocytes more frequently produce eggs with abnormal spindle morphology 48 and chromosome number, as well as exhibit mitochondrial and metabolic dysfunction leading 49 to reduced developmental potential ( 3, 5). Exchange of metabolites and growth factors between 50 oocytes and GCs are essential for proper follicle function; however, during aging, GC-oocyte 51 communication is impaired, characterized by a reduction in transzonal projections (TZPs). This 52 reduction in cell-cell interactions limits metabolic flux and causes a decline in oocyte-secreted 53 factors that regulate cell proliferation, differentiation, and steroidogenesis ( 6, 7). At the tissue 54 level, the extracellular matrix (ECM) plays vital roles in ovarian function, including in follicle 55 development and ovulation. Alterations to ECM composition, structure, and mechanics are 56 widely acknowledged as a hallmark of ovarian aging ( 8–10 ), resulting in increased expression 57 of fibrosis-associated factors and higher tissue stiffness ( 11 , 12 ). 58 59 Recent studies have demonstrated that antifibrotic drug treatment can ameliorate ovarian 60 fibrosis and extend reproductive lifespan in aged mice, highlighting the therapeutic potential 61 of ECM modification to enhance fertility ( 13 –15 ). Indeed, culturing young follicles in 62 hydrogels with tunable stiffness revealed that increased matrix stiffness impairs follicular 63 growth and hormone secretion ( 16 , 17 ), establishing a causal link between mechanical 64 properties and follicular development. However, the underlying mechanisms by which matrix 65 stiffness results in follicular dysfunction remain unknown. 66 67 To understand compartment-specific regulation in the ovary, we spatially characterized ECM 68 organization and mechanical alterations in fresh murine tissues during reproductive aging. We 69 then applied in vitro hydrogel systems mimicking young and aged stromal stiffness for isolated 70 follicle culture to understand how age-related mechanical cues direct follicle growth and oocyte 71 quality. Our findings reveal that altered TZP formation, regulated by TGF-b signaling, is a key 72 downstream process affected by stiff ECM. By perturbing TGF-b signaling with Mongersen, 73 a SMAD7 antisense oligonucleotide, oocyte quality and function improved despite the stiff 74 surrounding matrix. These findings have important implications in reproductive decline and 75 provide a potential target for preserving follicle fitness in a stiff microenvironment that is 76 characteristic of aged ovarian tissue. 77

RESULTS 81

Global and regional ECM remodeling during ovarian reproductive aging 83 

84 

We first carried out quantitative mass spectrometry (MS) to characterize global ECM 85 alterations during ovarian aging in tissues from reproductively young (6-8 weeks) versus aged 86 (14-16 months) mice. Because ECM proteins constitute only ~1-1.5% of the total proteome 87 (18 ), we applied an enrichment protocol that retains ECM proteins while reducing cellular 88 protein components ( 19 , 20 ). MS identified > 4,000 proteins in total, and after annotation using 89 the Matrisome Database (MatrisomeDB 2.0) ( 21 , 22 ), we identified 99 ECM proteins. In aged 90 ovarian ECM, vitronectin (VTNC), collagen type VI (COL6A5/6), collagen type IV (COL4A4), 91 and laminin (LAMA3) were all found to increase compared to young ECM (Fig. 1a). 92 Downregulated ECM proteins with age include Fibrillin-2 (FBN2), collagen type II (COL2A1), 93 and zona pellucida glycoproteins 1/2/3 (ZP1/2/3) (Fig. 1a). 94 95 While bulk MS identified overall age-related changes in tissue composition, follicular and 96 stromal ECM serve distinct structural and functional roles ( 23 , 24 ). Therefore, identifying 97 where matrix remodeling occurs is essential to understand how these changes may influence 98 follicle development. From our immunohistochemistry (IHC) images of collagen I, collagen 99 IV, and laminin, which were identified as differentially regulated in our MS data, we 100 characterized regional ECM abundance in follicular versus stromal compartments (Fig. 1b). 101 With age, collagen I significantly increased in the stroma, collagen IV in the follicle, and 102 laminin in both (Fig. 1c-e). As these proteins are known to affect ECM stiffness ( 25 , 26 ), their 103 compartment-specific alterations with age likely contribute to spatially distinct mechanical 104 properties within the ovary. Indeed, recent reports found age-related mechanical changes across 105 ovarian compartments, specifically in the corpora lutea ( 27 ), and we next sought to understand 106 the contribution of matrix to follicular versus stromal mechanics. 107 108 

Ovarian stromal but not follicle stiffness increases with age 109 

110 To understand how follicular versus stromal ECM alterations correlate to age-specific 111 mechanical properties, we carried out nanoindentation on both isolated follicles and fresh 112 ovarian tissue sections. Atomic force microscopy (AFM) on isolated primary to secondary 113 stage follicles from both young and aged mice was used to quantify follicle stiffness (Fig. S1a). 114 We found no stiffness difference between young and aged follicles (Young’s modulus, E ~1 115 kPa) nor a dependence on follicle size/stage (Fig. 1f, g). These findings are consistent with our 116 IHC staining, where limited ECM remodeling was observed in the follicular regions (Fig. 1c-117 e). 118 119 For stromal mechanics, we used nanoindentation on fresh ovarian tissue sections that were live 120 stained with CNA35-GFP, a collagen hybridizing peptide ( 28 ). This enabled us to correlate 121 stromal stiffness with ECM organization by comparing indented regions with captured images 122 of stained collagen (Fig. S1b). Matrix stiffness impairs follicle growth, GC proliferation, and oocyte quality 132 

133 

To understand how stromal mechanics impact follicle development, we produced alginate 134 hydrogels mimicking young versus aged stromal stiffness for 3D in vitro culture of isolated 135 secondary follicles from young mice (Fig. 2a, b). Consistent with previous work ( 16 ), follicles 136 cultured in soft hydrogels over 7 days grew larger and had more proliferating Ki-67 + GCs than 137 those cultured in stiff hydrogels (Fig. 2c-f). This reduction in proliferating GCs is a hallmark 138 of aged ovaries, indicating the stiff hydrogel culture can recapitulate some aspects of aging 139 dysfunction in young follicles (Fig. S2a, b). Next, oocyte quality was assessed and we observed 140 a ~3-fold reduction in maturation and a ~2-fold increase in MII spindle abnormalities in stiff 141 hydrogels (Fig. 2g-j). Taken together, these data show that stiffness alone can impair follicle 142 growth, GC proliferation, and oocyte quality. 143 144 

Stiffness alters both GC and oocyte gene expression 145 

146 

To understand how stiffness impairs follicle function, we performed RNA sequencing (RNA-147 seq) on separated GC and oocytes isolated from young follicles cultured in soft or stiff 148 hydrogels (Fig. S3a). Differentially expressed gene (DEG) analysis of GCs identified a total of 149 1,160 DEGs, with 872 upregulated and 288 downregulated, in the stiff versus soft hydrogels 150 (Fig. S3b, c). In stiff hydrogels, Gene Ontology (GO) analysis identified ‘ECM organization’, 151 ‘response to mechanical stimulus’, ‘cell adhesion mediated by integrin’, ‘ERK1/2 cascade’, 152 and ‘cellular response to transforming growth factor beta (TGF-b) stimulus’ as upregulated 153 biological processes, while downregulated cellular components include ‘actin-based cell 154 projections’, ‘adherens junctions’, and ‘cell projection membranes’ (Fig. S3d, e). DEG analysis 155 of isolated oocytes from follicle culture in stiff versus soft hydrogels showed that global 156 transcriptional differences were not as pronounced as those observed in the GCs (Fig. S3f); 157 however, Gene Set Enrichment Analysis (GSEA) identified metabolic processes, including 158 ‘mitochondrial gene expression’ and ‘NADH dehydrogenase complex assembly’, as 159 downregulated with stiffness (Fig. S3g). 160 161 High matrix stiffness has been associated with the upregulation of specific mechanosensitive 162 pathways, including the Hippo pathway, which functions to transduce mechanical signals to 163 biochemical responses ( 30 ). Thus, we examined our GC RNA-seq data for Yap1 and 164 downstream Hippo pathway genes but found no differential regulation related to hydrogel 165 stiffness (Fig. S4a). Consistently, immunostaining of YAP in follicles cultured in the soft and 166 stiff hydrogels showed no significant difference in YAP nuclear translocation in the GCs (Fig. 167

Matrix stiffness reduces TZP density between GCs and the oocyte 172 

173 Further analysis of the GC RNA-seq data using GO terms ‘adherens junctions’ and ‘actin-based 174 cell projections’ found that Cdh2 (encodes N-cadherin), Fmn2 (encodes Formin 2), and Myo7a 175 (encodes myosin-VIIA) were significantly downregulated in stiff hydrogels (Fig. 3a-e). This 176 led us to hypothesize that ECM stiffness affects TZPs, which are the cytoskeleton-based 177 extensions from GCs to the oocyte that form both adherens and gap junctions between the two 178 cell types. For follicles cultured in stiff hydrogels, we found a ~28% reduction in TZPs per 179 oocyte versus in soft hydrogels (Fig. 3f, g), in line with recent reports in other systems ( 31 ). 180 This reduction in TZPs is attributed to a reduction in TZP density (defined as number of TZPs 181 per 10 μm) rather than to oocyte diameter or the number of oocyte-adjacent GCs (Fig. 3h-j). 182 Next, to determine whether these in vitro changes resemble those observed in vivo during aging, 183 we quantified TZPs in directly isolated follicles from reproductively young and aged mice. 184 Aged follicles had ~50% fewer TZPs per oocyte, while oocyte diameter and oocyte-adjacent 185 GC number also remained unchanged (Fig. 3k-o). Thus, this stiffness-induced TZP loss 186 recapitulates the age-associated decline in TZP density. 187 188 

Impaired TGF-

b signaling underlies stiffness-induced TZP and follicle decline 189 

190 GO analysis of GC RNA-seq data from follicles cultured in stiff versus soft hydrogels 191 identified the ‘cellular response to TGF-b stimulus’ pathway as significantly upregulated (Fig. 192 4a). This pathway has previously been shown to regulate TZP formation and is 193 mechanosensitive, although its mechanosensitive activation has not been demonstrated in 194 ovaries ( 32 –35 ). Thus, we investigated downstream regulators, specifically Smad7, which is a 195 negative regulator of TGF-b signaling and competes with the Smad 2/3 and Smad1/5/8 196 complexes that translocate into the nucleus with co-mediator Smad4 to control gene 197 transcription ( 36 , 37 ). Smad7 was found to be upregulated in stiff hydrogels (Fig. 4b), 198 suggesting that the TGF-b signaling pathway may be inhibited by mechanics. Indeed, we 199 observed a decrease in Smad2/3 nuclear translocation in GCs within the stiff hydrogel (Fig. 4c, 200 d). When compared to in vivo tissues from reproductively young mice, Smad2/3 nuclear 201 translocation was only observed in the GCs of large follicles, which was correlated to an 202 increase in TZP density, consistent with activation of the TGF-β signaling pathway that has 203 been reported to promote TZP formation (Fig. S5a-d). 204 205 To test if Smad7 knockdown could restore TZPs and follicle growth in stiff hydrogels, we 206 treated our cultured follicles with Mongersen, a SMAD7 antisense oligonucleotide ( 38 , 39 ). 207 Treatment increased Smad2/3 nuclear translocation in the stiff hydrogel, to the same level as 208 in the soft hydrogels, suggesting TGF-b pathway activation (Figure 4c, d). Further, we found 209 that Mongersen treatment rescued TZP number and density, follicle growth, and oocyte 210

Discussion 226 

227 

In this study, we showed that age-associated stiffening of the ovarian stroma is sufficient to 228 compromise follicle development and oocyte quality by disrupting soma-germLine 229 communication. Through biochemical and biomechanical characterization of the aging ECM, 230 combined with tunable hydrogels that recapitulate age-related stromal stiffness, we provide 231 direct evidence that a stiff microenvironment alone can reduce follicular development and 232 disrupt TZPs at the GC-oocyte interface. Importantly, we have identified TGF-β signaling as a 233 mechanoregulated pathway linking matrix stiffness to impaired TZP formation. We further 234 demonstrated that restoring this pathway can rescue follicular function despite a persistent stiff 235 microenvironment. 236 237 Our results using spatial nanoindentation of ovarian tissue sections and atomic force 238 microscopy on isolated follicles are consistent with recent follicular elastography 239 measurements on whole ovaries ( 27 ). In young ovaries, follicles are almost two times stiffer 240 than their surrounding stroma. However, ECM accumulation during aging causes a mechanical 241 imbalance, with the stroma becoming stiffer than the follicles. Although the exact mechanisms 242 underlying this age-related mechanical shift between follicles and stroma remain unclear, 243 increased collagen production and reduced ECM turnover in ovarian stromal fibroblasts, 244 coupled with minimal alterations to the ECM surrounding GCs, may contribute to this 245 relationship ( 40 ). Consequently, aged follicles are exposed to higher mechanical stress 246 compared to young follicles, and we found that this stiffer microenvironment restricts follicle 247 activation and growth, consistent with previous studies showing that proper follicle 248 development requires a soft matrix ( 41 , 42 ). It is important to note that aging-associated stromal 249 ECM dysregulation not only alters stiffness, but also matrix composition and organization, and 250 these cues likely cooperatively influence follicle function in vivo . For example, laminin, which 251 increases in aged ovaries, could modulate follicle development via enhanced integrin-mediated 252 adhesion formation ( 43 , 44 ). How cells integrate biochemical, architectural, and mechanical 253 cues to regulate follicle function require further investigation using approaches such as 254

bioengineered materials that can decouple contributions from these interconnected matrix 255 signals in tissues ( 45 ). 256 257 Our results showed that a target of stromal stiffening is TZPs, structures that mediate GC-258 oocyte communication. Whereas intrinsic changes in GCs or oocytes have been shown to 259 regulate TZPs ( 5, 46 , 47 ), aging and mechanics can also play a role ( 7, 17 ). Our data further 260 demonstrated that TZP impairment due to higher ECM stiffness disrupts GC-oocyte 261 connectivity. Such disruption could limit the transfer of essential metabolites, such as pyruvate 262 and lactate, that support oocyte mitochondrial function ( 48 ). Oocyte maturation is an energy-263 intensive process, and the reduced ATP availability and mitochondrial membrane potential that 264 we observed in stiff hydrogels could directly contribute to defective meiotic progression and 265 abnormal spindle organization. Together, these findings support a model in which age-266 associated stromal stiffening disrupts TZPs and impairs soma-germLine communication during 267 ovarian aging. 268 269 Our work further uncovered the TGF-β signaling pathway as a key link between stiff matrix 270 and impaired follicle development. Although previous studies established TGF-β family 271 growth factors such as GDF9 and BMP15 as essential regulators of TZP maintenance ( 6, 49 ), 272 our work advances this understanding by demonstrating that the mechanical microenvironment 273 inhibits TGF-β signaling by upregulating Smad7, thereby suppressing Smad2/3 nuclear 274 translocation. We showed that restoration of TGF-β activity via Smad7 knockdown using 275 Mongersen was sufficient to rescue TZP density, follicle growth, and oocyte metabolic 276 competence, suggesting that stiffness-induced follicle dysfunction is at least partially reversible. 277 Our results implicate mechanoregulation of TGF-β signaling as a potential therapeutic target 278 for improving follicle growth in aging or fibrotic ovarian environments, such as in polycystic 279 ovary syndrome (PCOS) ( 50 –52 ). 280 281 Future studies are required to understand how matrix stiffness is sensed by follicular cells 282 leading to increased Smad7 expression ( 53 , 54 ). Inhibiting Smad7 only partially restored 283 stiffness-mediated follicle and oocyte dysfunction, indicating that additional mechanisms may 284 act in parallel. We also note that Smad7 inhibition in this study was carried out on young 285 follicles cultured in stiff microenvironments. Therefore, further investigation is required to 286 determine whether Smad7 inhibition in aged follicles will restore TZP density and follicle 287 competence. Despite these open questions, our results establish stromal ECM stiffness as an 288 important regulator of oocyte-follicle crosstalk and provides new insights into the mechanisms 289 of follicle decline during female reproductive aging. 290

Alginate hydrogel synthesis 312 

313 

Alginate powder (Sigma-Aldrich, 71238) was dissolved in ultrapure water at 2% (w/v) by 314 continuous stirring overnight in a 50 mL conical tube. Activated charcoal (Sigma-Aldrich) was 315 added at a ratio of 0.5 g charcoal per 1 g alginate and mixed for 30 min. The suspension was 316 filtered through a 0.22 μm filter (Millipore Express), frozen at −80 °C for 24 h and lyophilized 317 for ~1 week. Lyophilized alginate was stored at −80 °C until use. Prior to use in experiments, 318 alginate was reconstituted in 1X DPBS lacking Ca²⁺ and Mg²⁺ to 2% (w/v), briefly vortexed, 319 and placed on an orbital shaker until fully dissolved. 320 321 Hydrogel stiffness was tuned by varying CaCl₂ concentration and crosslinking duration. For 322 soft hydrogels, 4.5 μL alginate droplets were crosslinked in 15 mM CaCl₂ and 140 mM NaCl 323 for 30 s, then washed a single time in maintenance medium. For stiff hydrogels, droplets were 324 first crosslinked in 15 mM CaCl₂ and 140 mM NaCl for 30 s, then transferred to 100 mM CaCl₂ 325 for an additional 3 min and washed three times in maintenance medium. 326 327 

Follicle encapsulation and culture 328 

329 Ovaries were isolated from young virgin C57BL/6J mice. Secondary follicles were then 330 mechanically isolated in dissection medium consisting of Leibovitz’s L-15 (Thermo Fisher 331 Scientific, 11415064) supplemented with 1% fetal bovine serum (FBS) and 100 U/mL 332 penicillin-streptomycin. Isolated follicles were transferred to maintenance medium (αMEM; 333 Thermo Fisher Scientific, 32561102) supplemented with 5% FBS and 100 U/mL penicillin-334 streptomycin for 2 h prior to encapsulation. After incubation, follicles with similar size were 335 selected under a stereomicroscope. Individual follicles were encapsulated in 4.5 μL beads of 2% 336 (w/v) alginate followed by a single wash in the maintenance medium. Encapsulated follicles 337 were cultured individually in 96-well plates in 100 μL growth medium composed of a 1:1 338 mixture of αMEM GlutaMAX and F-12 GlutaMAX, supplemented with 5% FBS, 100 U/mL 339 follicle-stimulating hormone (FSH; Sigma-Aldrich), and 5 μg/mL each of insulin, transferrin 340 and selenium. Cultures were maintained at 37 °C and 5% CO₂, with 50% medium exchanged 341 every 2 days for up to 7 days. Follicle diameter is tracked every 2 days. 342

Mass spectrometry 344 

345 

Ovaries were collected from young and aged mice (n = 3, with each replicate consisting of 346 ovaries from 4–5 mice). For each replicate, 8–10 ovaries were pooled to yield ~20 mg fresh 347 tissue. ECM enrichment was performed on the ovaries using the Compartmental Protein 348 Extraction Kit (Merck Millipore), as previously described ( 19 ). Briefly, ovaries were flash-349 frozen in liquid nitrogen, pulverized with a mortar and pestle under liquid nitrogen, and 350 resuspended in Lysis Buffer C (Merck Millipore) supplemented with 1X protease inhibitor 351 (Merck Millipore)) at a final concentration of ~20 mg tissue per 400 μL lysis buffer. Cytosolic, 352 nuclear, membrane, and cytoskeletal fractions were sequentially extracted using the supplied 353 buffers with centrifugation-based separation. The remaining ECM-enriched pellet was washed 354 twice in Buffer W and three times in 1X PBS prior to preparation for mass spectrometry. All 355 buffers were supplemented with 1X protease inhibitor (Merck Millipore), and Buffer N was 356 supplemented with 3.5 μL Benzonase nuclease (Merck E1014) to digest genomic DNA and 357 RNA. 358 359 Samples were resuspended in 100 μL of 8 M urea in 100 μM triethylammonium bicarbonate 360 (TEAB) and lysed by sonication (15 s pulse followed by 45 s cooling, repeated three times). 361 Proteins were precipitated by addition of four volumes of cold acetone and incubated at −80 °C 362 for 2 d. Samples were centrifuged at 20,000 × g for 15 min at 4 °C, and the supernatant was 363 removed. Pellets were resuspended in 50 μL of 8 M urea in 100 μM TEAB, and protein 364 concentration was determined using the Pierce 660 nm protein assay. For reduction, 50 μg of 365 protein from each sample was treated with 20 μM tris(2-carboxyethyl)phosphine (TCEP) in 366 100 μM TEAB for 30 min at room temperature. Alkylation was performed using 367 chloroacetamide (CAA) to a final concentration of 55 μM in 100 mM TEAB for 30 min in the 368 dark. Proteins were digested with Lys-C (1:25, enzyme:protein) for 4 h, followed by dilution 369 to 1 M urea with 100 μM TEAB and overnight digestion with trypsin (1:50, enzyme:protein). 370 Peptides were desalted using 1 mL C18 Empore SPE cartridges. For quantitative analysis, 5 μg 371 of peptides per sample were labelled using TMT 6-plex reagents according to the 372 manufacturer’s instructions. Labelled peptides were fractionated using high-pH reversed-phase 373 fractionation prior to LC–MS/MS analysis. MS data were acquired on a Q Exactive HFX mass 374 spectrometer (Thermo) using data-dependent mode. 375 376

Image analysis and quantification 528 

529 

Follicle diameter was measured in FIJI using the line tool by recording the major and minor 530 axes of each follicle at day 0 and day 6. The mean diameter was defined as the average of the 531 major and minor axis lengths. Follicle growth rate was calculated as Growth Rate = (Diameter 532 at day 6 – Diameter at day 0) / 6 days. 533 534 All oocyte spindle images were processed using ZEN 3.8, and three-dimensional 535 reconstructions of tubulin and DNA channels were generated from z-stacks encompassing the 536 entire spindle. Spindles were classified as normal if they exhibited a compact bipolar structure 537 with focused poles and a clear axis, whereas abnormal spindles showed one or more of the 538 following features: multipolarity (≥3 poles), unfocused or broadened poles, disorganized 539 microtubules (splayed, fragmented, or irregular bundles with loss of bipolar architecture), or 540 bent/asymmetric morphology. Chromosome alignment was evaluated on the DNA channel. 541 Chromosomes were considered aligned when a compact metaphase plate at the spindle equator 542 was formed, and misaligned when at least one chromosome was located outside the metaphase 543 plate or dispersed along the spindle axis ( 55 ). The spindle abnormality rate was calculated as 544 the percentage of abnormal oocytes among the total analyzed. 545 546 Ovary ECM IHC images were analyzed using FIJI (ImageJ). Images were acquired under 547 identical exposure settings across groups. Fluorescence intensity of ECM markers (collagen I, 548 collagen IV, and laminin) was quantified from manually defined regions of interest (ROIs) 549 corresponding to follicles and stromal areas. ROIs were delineated based on the phalloidin 550 channel to define follicular architecture. Fluorescence intensity was normalized to ROI area 551 and expressed as the mean gray value. Sections processed in parallel without primary 552 antibodies served as background controls, and background-corrected intensities were 553 calculated by subtracting the mean control value from each corresponding measurement. All 554 analyses were performed using identical processing parameters. Mean CNA35 intensity was 555 quantified as the mean gray value within the ovary tissue region.
