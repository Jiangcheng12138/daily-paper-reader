# Abstract 10 

Spontaneous neural activity reflects sensory experience yet occupies a lower-dimensional 11 

subspace than evoked responses. However, the principle governing which components of 12 

experience are incorporated into this intrinsic activity has remained unknown. Here, we 13 

propose that temporal predictability is one of the organizing principles that selects which 14 

evoked dimensions enter spontaneous neural manifolds. We show that local predictive 15 

synaptic plasticity provides a circuit mechanism that selectively embeds components of 16 

evoked activity that are predictable on the intrinsic timescale of recurrent dynamics, while 17 

excluding unpredictable fluctuations. As a consequence, the dimensionality of spontaneous 18 

activity is not fixed but depends on environmental timescales: rapidly fluctuating inputs are 19 

excluded, whereas slowly varying components are retained as contextual dimensions. This 20 

framework reproduces on- and off-manifold coding observed in visual cortex, and reconciles 21 

apparently conflicting developmental observations that spontaneous and evoked activity 22 

become both more similar and more geometrically distinct over maturation. Together, these 23 

results identify temporal predictability as a key principle linking environmental statistics to 24 

the geometry of intrinsic population dynamics. 25

Here, we propose that temporal predictability is a fundamental selection principle governing 69

manifolds. Prior work has shown that predictive learning can extract low-dimensional latent 71 

structure from the complex environment (Recanatesi et al., 2021; Levenstein et al., 2024; 72

show that predictive plasticity selectively incorporates into spontaneous manifolds those 76 

components of input that are predictable on the intrinsic timescale of recurrent dynamics, 77 

while excluding fluctuations that cannot be recurrently reconstructed. This filtering principle 78 

explains why spontaneous and evoked activity can become more similar within the 79 

predictive subspace yet more geometrically separated in the full neural state space, thereby 80 

resolving the developmental paradox noted above. More broadly, our results reframe 81 

spontaneous population geometry as a direct readout of the brain's implicit model of 82 

environmental timescales. 83

# Results 85 

Predictive learning selectively incorporates evoked dimensions into 86 

spontaneous manifolds 87 

Spontaneous neural activity resembles stimulus-evoked responses yet occupies a lower-88 

dimensional subspace, suggesting that it reflects only a subset of sensory experience. We 89 

hypothesized that this dimensional reduction arises from predictive learning, which 90 

selectively embeds predictable structure into recurrent dynamics while filtering out 91 

unpredictable fluctuations (Fig. 1A). 92

To test this hypothesis, we trained a rate-based recurrent neural network (RNN; Fig. 1B) 93 

using a local predictive plasticity rule (Asabuki & Clopath, 2025). The network consists of a 94 

fixed, strong random connectivity that generates high-dimensional chaotic dynamics, and a 95 

plastic recurrent component that is modified during learning. External inputs drive stimulus-96 

evoked responses, and synaptic updates minimize the mismatch between these responses 97 

and recurrent predictions (Fig. 1C). 98 

More precisely, synaptic changes are driven by the product of a prediction-error term (the 99 

difference between a neuron’s current activity and its recurrent prediction) and a presynaptic 100 

activity term. Importantly, both signals are locally available at each synapse: the prediction 101 

error is computed at the postsynaptic neuron based on its own activity and the recurrent 102 

inputs it receives, while the presynaptic activity reflects the input from the corresponding 103 

afferent neuron. Both signals undergo temporal filtering prior to influencing plasticity, 104 

analogous to postsynaptic and presynaptic integration in biological neurons. The recurrent 105 

prediction integrates recent recurrent input over a short period, and the presynaptic activity 106 

is converted into an eligibility trace. This learning rule is fully local, consistent with biological 107 

constraints on synaptic plasticity. 108

Geometric separation enables multiple predictable manifolds to coexist with 198 

minimal interference 199 

Having shown that predictive plasticity selects which evoked dimensions are incorporated 200 

into a spontaneous manifold, we next asked whether the same principle can organize 201 

multiple predictable structures within a single recurrent circuit. If predictability indeed 202 

organizes spontaneous population geometry, then distinct predictable structures should be 203 

stored as separable manifolds whose retrieval depends on their geometric separation. To test 204 

this idea, we trained the network on two distinct trajectories presented in alternating blocks. 205 

One input traced a circular trajectory confined to a specific two-dimensional subspace (z ₁, z ₂), 206 

and the other followed a different trajectory confined to a separate, orthogonal subspace (z 4,207 

z5) (Fig. 4A). Each trajectory was combined with an unpredictable component along z 3 or z 6,208 

respectively. During training, these two input patterns alternated across epochs (Fig. 4B). By 209 

embedding the dynamics in orthogonal subspaces, we hypothesized that the learning rule 210 

would modify largely independent sets of synaptic weights, thereby minimizing interference 211 

between the two learned internal models. 212

# Discussion 327 

Spontaneous neural activity is not a passive echo of prior sensory input, but a geometrically 328 

organized internal model of the subset of experience that recurrent circuits can 329 

autonomously sustain. Here we show that predictive synaptic plasticity provides a circuit-330 

level principle that sculpts this geometry. By selectively stabilizing temporally predictable 331 

components of sensory input, recurrent networks compress high-dimensional experience 332 

into low-dimensional manifolds that can be autonomously sustained. Rather than 333 

minimizing dimensionality for efficiency alone, predictive learning allocates population 334 

activity into a self-consistent predictive subspace while excluding unpredictable fluctuations. 335 

Importantly, the exclusion of unpredictable components is not a design choice but an 336 

inevitable consequence of predictive learning operating on finite timescales. Thus, 337 

population geometry reflects not the full structure of sensory inputs, but the subset that can 338 

be internally predicted and sustained by recurrent dynamics. 339 

This dependence arises because spontaneous activity can internalize only those components 340 

of past input that are predictable on the intrinsic timescale of recurrent dynamics. The 341 

dimensionality of spontaneous manifolds is therefore not fixed, but selected by the temporal 342 

statistics of environmental variability. Rapid fluctuations are excluded because they cannot 343 

be recurrently reconstructed, whereas slowly varying inputs are retained as contextual 344 

dimensions. Spontaneous manifold geometry thus reflects not only the complexity of past 345 

inputs but also the temporal structure of uncertainty present during learning, consistent with 346 

theoretical views linking neural dynamics to prediction and environmental statistics (Huang 347 

& Rao, 2011; Chalk et al., 2018; Koren & Denève, 2017). In this sense, population geometry 348 

encodes predictability structure rather than stimulus identity per se. 349 

Our framework makes experimentally tractable predictions about how environmental 350 

statistics shape spontaneous population geometry. If spontaneous manifold dimensionality 351 

depends on the temporal structure of sensory noise encountered during learning, then 352

Our framework reconciles seemingly contradictory developmental observations. Berkes et al. 365 

(2011) reported that spontaneous activity increasingly resembles evoked responses over 366 

development, whereas Avitan et al. (2021) found that the two become more orthogonal. We 367 

propose that predictive plasticity selectively embeds temporally predictable components of 368 

sensory experience into recurrent connectivity. Within the low-dimensional predictive 369 

subspace defined by these stable regularities, spontaneous activity converges toward evoked 370 

structure. At the same time, unpredictable and fast-varying components are excluded from 371 

recurrent dynamics, producing geometric divergence in the full neural state space. Cortical 372 

development therefore might reflect geometric refinement of predictive structure, with 373 

predictable components stabilized and unpredictable variability progressively excluded. 374 

Our framework also provides a geometric account of on- and off-manifold coding reported 375 

in cortex. Because spontaneous activity reflects a stabilized predictive subspace, behavior-376 

related signals that can be internally generated remain largely confined within this manifold. 377

necessarily recruits additional dimensions. This separation arises as a consequence of 379

Analysis of on- and off-manifold coding 492 

We quantified how the network represented predictable and unpredictable information 493 

across on- and off-manifold subspaces. The input signals consisted of three components: 494 

z1(predictable behavioral dynamics), z2 (predictable sensory component of visual stimuli), 495 

and z3 (unpredictable sensory fluctuations). 496 

After training, we simulated the network in two conditions—spontaneous (SWS-like) with 497 

all inputs set to zero, and evoked (awake-like) with all inputs active. We defined the 498 

spontaneous manifold by applying principal component analysis (PCA) to spontaneous 499 

population activity. The top principal components explaining 99% of cumulative variance 500 

were defined as the on-manifold subspace, and an equal number of components from the tail 501 

of the variance spectrum were defined as the off-manifold subspace, matched in 502 

dimensionality. 503 

We projected evoked activity onto these two subspaces and used ridge regression 504 

(regularization coefficient = 10) to decode each input signal from the neural population 505 

activity. Decoding accuracy was quantified by the coefficient of determination ( 𝑅 ") for (i) the 506 

behavioral signal ( z1) and (ii) the sensory signal, computed as the average decoding accuracy 507 

for z2 and z3. We compared decoding performance between on- and off-manifold subspaces 508 

using Welch’s two-tailed t-tests. 509
