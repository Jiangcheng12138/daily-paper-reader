ABSTRACT 14 

Upon sperm and oocyte fertilization, the embryo undergoes drastic histone modification 15 

reprogramming during pre-implantation development. In this study, we investigated the erasure and 16 

re-establishment of H3K4me2 in mouse GV, MII, and embryos using an improved approach called 17 

Cleavage Under Targets and Release Using Nuclease (CUT&RUN) with high-throughput sequencing. 18 

We found that H3K4me2 extensively occurs as a non-canonical pattern in mouse GV oocytes and 19 

early embryos, and parental H3K4me2 was erased by maternal LSD2 during forming the pronucleus in 20 

the zygote. The H3K4me2 was erased from GV to MII oocyte and was re-established in the late two-21 

cell stage, removing this epigenetic burden is crucial for ZGA. We then revealed the H3K4me2 locates 22 

widespread in the CpG-rich and hypomethylated regulatory regions in the four-cell stage, and the 23 

eight-cell stage, but dramatic changes occurred in the inner cell mass (ICM) in mouse embryos. Then, 24 

these CpG-rich H3K4me2 regulatory regions became either activated or repressed. To summarize, in 25 

this study, we elucidated the pattern of H3K4me2 transition from parent to zygote and H3K4me2 26 

profile during early embryo development. Our findings might provide deeper insights into epigenetic 27 

reprogramming during early development and in vitro fertilization in mammalian. 28 29

INTRODUCTION 39 

Drastic epigenetic reprogramming occurs during early mammalian embryo development. Epigenetic 40 

marks, such as DNA methylation and histone modification, play important roles in the epigenome 41 

reprogramming process 1,2 . Epigenome reprogramming during the pre-implantation embryo 42 

development stage erases the epigenetic memory of oocytes and sperm cells and allows the zygote to 43 

establish a new epigenetic landscape, which is relevant for early development and is essential for 44 

maternal zygotic transition (MZT) and differentiation 1,3 . Deficiencies in the reconstruction of the 45 

epigenome can result in developmental defects, such as embryonic arrest 3. Histone modifications are 46 

fundamental epigenetic regulators that control gene expression and many key cellular processes 4-6 .47 

Different types of histone modifications can occur in specific genomic regions and perform distinct 48 

functions 3. Most histone modifications occur around transcription start sites (TSSs) in the embryos; this 49 

feature is shared by all somatic cells 7-9 . A similar pattern of histone modification commonly occurs in 50 

many species and cell lines 10-12 , and thus, this distribution pattern is known as the ‘canonical’ pattern 13 .51 

Highly pervasive H3K27me3 is found in regions depleted of transcription and DNA methylation in 52 

oocytes, whereas extensive loss of promoter H3K27me3 occurs at Hox and other developmental 53 

genes after fertilization, accompanied by global erasure of H3K27me3 from sperm but inheritance of 54 

distal H3K27me3 from oocytes; both H3K4me3/H3K27me3 bivalent promoter marks are restored at 55 

developmental genes 14 .56 

The K-residues at the N-terminus of histone H3 undergo mono-(H3K4me1), di-(H3K4me2), or tri-57 

methylation (H3K4me3), catalyzed by a family of proteins that contain a SET domain; the methylation 58

H3K4me2 can coordinate the recovery of protein biosynthesis and homeostasis following DNA 71

housekeeping genes 35 . Many regions of the sperm epigenome bearing H3K4me2 are present in 84 

spermatogonia, suggesting an early establishment of H3K4me2 in spermatogenesis, which is a highly 85 

conserved process 36 .86

Results 94 

Genome-wide profiling of H3K4me2 in mouse oocytes and early embryos 95 

The epigenome underwent significant reprogramming during early development. To determine how 96 

H3K4me2 is inherited from the sperm and oocyte by the zygote during early development, we 97 

investigated H3K4me2 in mouse gametes and pre-implantation embryos using CUT&RUN methods. 98 

We optimized the CUT&RUN protocol (CUT&RUN; Methods) using H3K4me2 antibody. We also 99 

acquired high-quality data using only 50 cells. Most peaks could be retrieved using only 50 cells 100 

(Figures S1A and S1B). Then, we collected GV oocytes, MII, PN5, early two-cell embryos, late two-cell 101 

embryos, four-cell embryos, eight-cell embryos, and the inner cell mass (ICM) of mice. Mouse 102 

embryonic stem cells (mESC) were used as control. We profiled H3K4me2 in oocytes and early 103 

embryos using CUT&RUN and immunofluorescence (IF) techniques. The results of all replicates were 104 

highly consistent (Figure 1A; Figures S1A, B, and C). We identified 57,455, 25,073, 110,517, 126,318, 105 

62,575, and 69,154 H3K4me2 peaks in the GV oocytes, late two-cell embryos, four-cell embryos, 106 

eight-cell embryos, ICM, and mESC, respectively. Most of the H3K4me2 was erased from GV to MII 107 

during oocyte development. The paternal H3K4me2 was also erased after fertilization, and it was re-108 

established in the late two-cell stage (Figures 1A-B). We also demonstrated the H3K4me2, ATAC-seq, 109 

and mRNA expression of several developmental key genes (Figure 1C). We found that H3K4me2 110 

positively correlated with H3K4me3 and chromatin accessibility in early embryos (Figures S1D-E). The 111 

H3K4me2 genome-wide pattern of four-cell embryos showed a strong association with eight-cell 112 

embryos: the H3K4me2 of four-cell and eight-cell embryos was in one cluster, and the correlation was 113 

high (R = 0.93) (Figures S1F-G). 114

Resetting H3K4me2 in pre-implanted embryos 148 

Significant epigenetic reprograming and chromosome remodeling associated with H3K4me3, 149 

H3K27me3, H3K36me3, and H3K27ac occurs during early mammalian development 44,48,49 . In another 150 

study, we found that epigenome reboot occurred during the early developmental stage in humans 50 .151 

We investigated how parental histone mark H3K4me2 at regulatory elements are reprogrammed after 152 

fertilization. We found that GV and sperm H3K4me2 peaks were almost erased in PN5 and re-153 

established along with zygotic genome activation (ZGA). This result was further confirmed by IF 154 

analysis, which showed that the reconstruction of H3K4me2 might start from the late two-cell stage 155 

(Figures 1B and 3B). It is worth noting that previous studies using different immunofluorescence 156 

parameters reported detectable H3K4me2 signals at the zygote and two-cell stages 51 . This discrepancy 157 

may arise from differences in confocal microscope settings, antibody sensitivity, or sample processing. 158 

In our study, we used consistent parameters across all stages from GV to blastocyst; when we 159 

specifically optimized the acquisition parameters for zygotes and two-cell embryos, weak signals 160 

could be observed. The H3K4me2 demethylases KDM1B (LSD2) were also highly expressed in growing 161 

oocytes but showed decreased expression in MII oocytes (Figure S3A, see also revised heatmap). We 162 

have re-analyzed the expression data and corrected the heatmap normalization; the revised Figure 163 

S3A now accurately shows that Kdm1b is highly expressed in growing oocytes, with lower levels in 8-164 

week and MII oocytes, consistent with its role in maternal imprint establishment. The CUT&RUN data, 165 

which showed small H3K4me2 peaks (related to other stages), reappeared at the late two-cell stage 166 

(Figure 3A). We concluded that the IF analysis could not detect the newly established weak H3K4me2 167 

signal in the late two-cell stage. H3K4me2 was correlated with the activation of Prdm9 and Kmt2c at 168 

the four-cell stage (Figure S3A), and the signal was strong enough to be detected at the four-cell and 169 

eight-cell stages (Figures 3A-B) using both the CUT&RUN and IF techniques. The four/eight-cell 170 

H3K4me2 targets only partially overlapped with those in sperm or oocytes, and these overlapped 171 

targets were mainly enriched in development-associated pathways or processes (Figure 3C). 172 

Additionally, gamete-specific H3K4me2 targets were involved in the development and generation of 173 

gametes, whereas four/eight cell-specific targets were associated with cell communication and 174

The inhibition of LSD2 increased the expression of H3K4me2, which impaired the development 181 

of embryos in vitro 182 

Histone post-translational modifications are coordinated by histone methyltransferases and 183 

demethylases 52 . KDM1B, also known as LSD2, is the only demethylase homologous to LSD1 in 184 

mammals 29 . Although they share the H3K4me1/2 demethylase function, our analysis showed that 185 

Kdm1a was rarely expressed during mouse embryonic development (Figure 4A). While, LSD2, which is 186 

required to establish the maternal imprinted gene 53 , was highly expressed before the early two-cell 187 

stage and decreased after the late two-cell stage (Figures 4A and S3A). 188 

Tranylcypromine (TCP) can inhibit the expression of LSD2 54 . To investigate the effect of the expression 189 

of H3K4me2 on embryonic development, mouse PN5 embryos were cultured in vitro with three 190 

different TCP treatment groups (3.6 mM, 5 mM, and 10 mM) and a control group. The inhibition in the 191 

presence of 3.6 mM TCP was not prominent, and some embryos could develop to the eight-cell stage 192 

at 65 h. When 10 mM TCP was present, embryonic development was strongly inhibited, and the 193 

embryo could not develop to the two-cell stage (Figure S4A). When 5 mM TCP was present, the 194 

embryos could pass through the two-cell stage with a significant developmental delay. Some embryos 195 

developed till the four-cell stage, and then, were completely arrested. These results showed that TCP 196 

affected embryonic development in a dose-dependent manner and caused developmental arrest. As 197 

no prior study has reported the precise effective concentration of TCP for inhibiting LSD2 in mouse 198 

embryos, our dose-response experiment empirically determined that 5 mM TCP was sufficient to 199 

cause significant H3K4me2 accumulation and developmental arrest without immediate lethality (see 200 

Methods).We concluded that the TCP concentration of 5 mM in the experimental group (5 mM TCP 201 

group) could be used to determine whether the abnormal expression of H3K4me2 affects the 202 

development of embryonic cells, especially the occurrence of ZGA. 203

H3K4me2 (Figure S5E). We found promoters with constantly high H3K4me2 that are preferentially 297 

CpG-rich, whereas promoters with constantly low H3K4me2 were generally CpG-poor (Figures 7F and 298 

S5E). A group of genes showed dynamic promoter H3K4me2 that correlated with gene expression 299 

(Figure S5E). These genes played key roles in development, differentiation, and morphogenesis. 300 

Therefore, our results indicated that H3K4me2 of the promoter during early embryonic development 301 

correlated with gene activities and CpG density. 302 

Next, we investigated distal H3K4me2 signals and found that four/eight-cell embryos also showed 303 

widespread distal H3K4me2 signals (Figure 7C). We first identified the distal H3K4me2 regions in 304 

mouse gametes and pre-implantation embryos. These regions were relatively CpG-rich (although 305 

lower than promoters) and hypomethylated (Figures S5B and S5C). However, compared to promoters, 306 

distal H3K4me2 was correlated with CpG density in a continuous and monotonic manner (Figure S5A). 307 

To summarize, these results indicated that widespread distal H3K4me2 occurred in CpG-rich and 308 

hypomethylated regions in mouse four/eight-cell embryos. 309 310

DISCUSSION 343 

Histone modifications are fundamental epigenetic marks. The reprogramming of histone marks plays 344 

an important role in early development 9. In this study, we profiled the H3K4me2 landscape in mESC, 345 

oocytes, and early embryos using a modified low-input, high-sensitivity CUT&RUN method. We found 346 

the H3K4me2 showed differential patterns in sperms and oocytes and exhibited non-canonical 347 

patterns in GV oocytes but canonical patterns in the sperm in mice 36 . Additionally, the GV H3K4me2 348

indicated that maternal H3K4me2 was lost during oocyte maturation. The non-canonical H3K4me2 in 350 

mammalian embryos and pervasive H3K4me2 in CpG-rich regulatory regions were found in four-cell 351 

and eight-cell embryos and in ICM. Hence, we described the first landscape and elucidated the 352 

potential function of H3K4me2 during early mammalian development. We also proposed a novel 353 

model, known as “Epigenetic Burden”, according to which parental H3K4me2 exhibits an “Epigenetic 354 

Burden” of maternal-zygote-transition. This burden is erased by maternal factor LSD2 before ZGA. 355 

H3K4me2 plays a regulatory role after ZGA (Figure 8 X). H3K4me2 undergoes a transition that starts 356 

from an “Epigenetic Burden”, and then becomes an “Epigenetic regulator” during early histone 357 

modification reprogramming. 358 

The paternal epigenetic inheritance is important for development and disease in offspring. Significant 359 

epigenome reprogramming occurs and is associated with key processes during early mammalian 360 

development 1,3,9,14,39-41,60-64 . Genome-wide H3K4me3 exhibited a non-canonical pattern (ncH3K4me3) in 361

establishment of the embryo epigenome, which leads to epigenetic ‘‘rebooting’’ 9. We found that 370 

H3K4me2 was erased by LSD2 after fertilization and re-established at the late two-cell stage. 371

H3K4me3 is an active gene expression marker, which mainly marks the gene promoter region. 383 

H3K4me1 and H3K4me2 mark enhancer and promoter regions. They have multiple functions for gene 384

factor binding regions and motifs. In another study, we found large-scale chromatin accessibility in 395 

pre-ZGA human embryos, and these regions contained many transcription factors 3. Around 90% of the 396 

transcription factor binding regions overlap with the H3K4me2-enriched regions. Stronger signals of 397 

H3K4me2 enrichment indicate a greater chance of overlap with the transcription factor binding 398 

regions 34 . In this study, we identified the binding motifs for transcription factors enriched in these distal 399 

regions, and the CCAAT transcription factor (CTF) was found to be specifically enriched in eight-cell 400 

embryos. The GATA and KLF families were enriched in ICMs. GATA is a key regulator of primitive 401 

endoderm (PE), which is a part of the ICM 75 , and KLF factors support naive pluripotency 76 . These results 402 

showed that stage-specific distal H3K4me2 involves transcription circuitry during mammalian 403 

embryonic development. 404

Animal embryos in the early stages undergo extensive histone modification reprogramming, and 408

Dynamic histone modification plays an important role during mammalian maternal-to-zygotic 416

negatively correlated with DNA methylation and becomes confined to transcriptional-start-site (TSS) 418

scores were calculated based on them. A Shannon entropy-based method 3 was used to identify 589 

stage-specific peaks. Peaks with entropy less than 2 were selected as candidates and were further 590 

screened using the following criteria: the distal peak had high enrichment at this stage (normalized 591 

RPKM > 1) and positive enrichment (normalized RPKM > 0) at no more than two additional stages. 592 

Minor/major ZGA genes and stage-specific genes were screened using the RNA-seq data from mouse 593 

germ cells and embryos. ZGA genes were defined as those unexpressed or underexpressed in oocytes 594 

(FPKM < 5) but highly upregulated (FPKM > 5, at least three-fold upregulation) in either the one-cell 595 

or early two-cell embryo stage (minor ZGA genes) or the late two-cell embryo stage (major ZGA 596 

genes). The genes that were highly expressed in oocytes and considerably upregulated (over five-fold 597 

upregulation) during the late two-cell embryo stage were also defined as major ZGA genes. 598 

For stage-specific genes in eight-cell embryos and ICMs, a Shannon entropy-based method 3 was 599 

used to identify stage specific-genes, as previously described. Maternally expressed genes, defined as 600 

those expressed in GV or MII oocytes (FPKM ≥ 1), were excluded from this analysis to avoid the 601 

confounding effects of lingering maternal RNAs. 602 

Clustering analysis 603 

We conducted k-means clustering of gene expression levels at various stages using Cluster 3.0 with 604 

the parameters –g 7 (Euclidean distance). Heat maps were generated using Java Treeview 3.0. We 605 

conducted hierarchical clustering analysis for H3K4me3 at various stages using the R package “ape” 606 

based on Pearson’s correlation between each pair of stages. The distance between the stages was 607 

calculated as 1 − Pearson’s correlation. 608
