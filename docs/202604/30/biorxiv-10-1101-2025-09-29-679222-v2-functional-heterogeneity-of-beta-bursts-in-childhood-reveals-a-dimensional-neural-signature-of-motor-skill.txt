Abstract 

Activity in the beta frequency band appears to play a critical role in sensorimotor 

processing, with transient ‘bursts’ exhibiting distinct waveform motifs that may reflect 

different neural computations. However, how these dynamics relate to motor 

developm ent and neurodevelopmental conditions remains unclear. Here, we used EEG 

to investigate sensorimotor beta bursts in children with and without developmental 

coordination disorder (DCD). We characterized beta bursts during action execution and 

observation of gross and fine motor actions. Conventional spectral features and overall 

burst rates did not differ between groups. In contrast, children with DCD showed altered 

rate modulation of specific burst waveform motifs, including atypical hemispheric 

lateralizat ion and disrupted interhemispheric connectivity. Critically, motif -specific burst 

rates were systematically related to motor performance across individuals, with similar 

relations observed across groups. These findings indicate that waveform -specific beta 

burst dynamics track graded variation in motor performance, rather than mapping 

selectively onto diagnostic status. Beta burst variability may therefore reflect the 

functional expression of motor performance, rather than a disorder -specific neural 

mechanis m underlying DCD. This work highlights the importance of beta burst waveform 

diversity for understanding variability in sensorimotor function across development.

1. Introduction 1

Sensorimotor beta activity (13 -30 Hz in adults) has long been associated with 2

motor control and is traditionally conceptualized as a sustained oscillatory rhythm that 3

modulates in power prior to, during, and after movement  (Pfurtscheller, 1981; 4

Pfurtscheller et al., 1996) . However, more recent work indicates that beta activity occurs 5

in transient, burst -like events, rather than as a continuous oscillation  (Feingold et al., 6

2015; Sherman et al., 2016; Little et al., 2019) . These beta bursts are temporally sparse 7

and behaviorally relevant, predicting response time, motor errors, and stopping 8

performance in adults  (Little et al., 2019; Wessel, 2020; West et al., 2023) , and are 9

modulated not only during action execution, but also during observation of others’ 10 

movements  (Rayson et al., 2023) , implicating them in motor simulation and internal model 11 

engagement  (Jeannerod, 2001; Press et al., 2011) . Developmental work further suggests 12 

that beta activity occurs as bursts from infancy, with age -related shifts in peak frequency, 13 

timing, duration, and hemispheric lateralization  (Gaetz et al., 2010; Cheyne et al., 2014; 14 

Rayson et al., 2022, 2023; Rier et al., 2024; Wilkinson et al., 2024) . These findings 15 

indicate that maturation of sensorimotor function may involve progressive refinement of 16 

transient beta events rather than simple changes in sustained oscillatory power. Although 17 

the precise computational role of beta bursts remains unresolved, their developmental 18 

tuning and behavioral relevance suggest that burst dynamics provide a useful framework 19 

for understanding how transient beta activity contributes to sensorimotor processing 20 

(Rayson et al., 2025) .21 

Most studies implicitly treat beta bursts as homogenous events, yet recent work 22 

demonstrates that individual bursts vary systematically in their time -domain waveform 23 

shape. Rather than defining burst types using spectral features (e.g., peak frequency, 24 

power, or duration), waveform -based approaches represent each burst as a vector of 25 

voltage (or magnetic field strength in the case of magnetoencephalography) across time 26 

and apply dimensionality reduction (e.g., PCA) to identify axes of shape variability 27 

(Rayson et al., 2023; Szul et al., 2023; Papadopoulos et al., 2024; Agouram et al., 2025) .28 

A “motif” in this context refers to the characteristic waveform shape at the extreme ends 29 

of a given principal component, that is, bursts with high positive or negative loadings that 30 

share reproducible configurations of peaks, troughs, asymmetries, or temporal skew 31 

relative to the mean burst waveform. Motifs are therefore not discrete physiological 32 

generators, but statistical patterns of shape variation within the burst population. These 33 

motifs differ in their rate dynamics and lateralization, and accumulating evidence suggests 34 

that they index distinct functional processes  (Rayson et al., 2023; Szul et al., 2023; 35 

Agouram et al., 2025)  such as behavioural error  (Moreau et al., 2026) .36 

Developmental findings support the functional relevance of this variability. Infant 37 

bursts share a similar canonical waveform with adults but are temporally longer; after 38

accounting for duration differences, their waveforms align closely in shape  (Rayson et al., 39 

2023) . Moreover, variability around the mean waveform is structured and conserved 40 

across age, while motif -specific rate modulation becomes increasingly precise from 41 

infancy to adulthood  (Rayson et al., 2023) , consistent with progressive specialization of 42 

sensorimotor computations. Examining waveform -defined motifs across children 43 

spanning a range of motor abilities, including those with developmental coordination 44 

disorder  (Wilson et al., 2017)  therefore provides a principled framework for testing 45 

whether specific dimensions of burst shape variability track individual differences in motor 46 

skill and interhemispheric organization. 47 

Recent evidence indicates that beta bursts with distinct waveform shapes exhibit 48 

dissociable patterns of rate modulation and hemispheric lateralization during movement, 49 

with some motifs showing increasingly precise lateralization across early development 50 

(Rayson et al., 2023) . These findings suggest that waveform -defined burst types may 51 

reflect distinct computational roles within distributed sensorimotor networks, potentially 52 

including interhemispheric coordination. Motor ability varies substantially across 53 

development, and individual differences in coordination are associated with variability in 54 

hemispheric specialization and functional connectivity within sensorimotor systems 55 

(Querne et al., 2008; Tallet et al., 2013; Brown -Lum and Zwicker, 2015; McLeod et al., 56 

2016) . Studying children with a wide range of motor proficiency, including those meeting 57 

criteria for developmental coordination disorder  (Wilson et al., 2017) , therefore provides 58 

a principled way to test whether specific waveform -defined beta burst motifs scale with 59 

motor performance and interhemispheric dynamics. While DCD is defined categorically 60 

at the clinical level, variation in motor performance and its neural substrates may 61 

nonetheless be continuous across individuals. Under this framework, DCD therefore 62 

serves as a model of pronounced motor difficulty within a broader continuum, enabling us 63 

to examine how variation in burst motif dynamics relates to motor coordination. 64 

Here, we characterized sensorimotor beta burst dynamics in children spanning a 65 

broad range of motor abilities during action execution and observation, including children 66 

with and without DCD. We explicitly tested for differences between groups in the 67 

modulation, lateralization, and interhemispheric connectivity of waveform -defined burst 68 

motifs, and additionally examined whether motif -specific burst rates were related to 69 

individual differences in motor performance. We show that specific burst motifs display 70 

altered rate modulation and hemispheric asymmetry during movement in children with 71 

DCD, and that motif -specific burst rates are systematically related to motor performance 72 

across individuals. Notably, although these effects differentiate groups, the relationship 73 

between burst dynamics and motor performance is continuous across the full sample. 74 

These findings suggest that variability in waveform -specific beta burst dynamics reflects 75 

graded differences in the organization of interhemispheric sensorimotor communication, 76 

along a continuum of motor performance. 77

2. Methods 78 

2.1 Participants 79 

Twenty -three 8 - to 12 -year -old children with DCD ( M = 10.88, SD = 1.56; 18 male) 80 

were recruited via social media and the Dyspraxia Foundation, and 33 typically 81 

developing children ( M = 9.85, SD = 1.30; 17 male) were recruited via social media as a 82 

comparison group. Participants in the DCD group were required to have received a 83 

clinical diagnosis of DCD, confirmed for this study via parent report and converted total 84 

score on the Movement Assessment Battery for Children 2nd Edition  (MABC -2; 85 

Henderson et al., 2007)  at or below the 16 th percentile at the time of the testing session. 86 

In line with the Leeds Consensus Statement  (2006) , participants with a diagnosis of DCD 87 

who had co -occurring neurodevelopmental conditions were not excluded from 88 

participating, but additional diagnoses were noted. In the final sample, co -occurring 89 

conditions were autism ( n = 3), ADHD ( n = 1), dyslexia ( n = 1), and autism, dyslexia, 90 

developmental language disorder and learning difficulty ( n = 1). Children were not 91 

recruited to the comparison group if they had a diagnosis of a neurodevelopmental 92 

condition or developmental delay. 93 

Nine children from the comparison group were excluded from the analyses of 94 

group differences as they scored below the 25 th percentile on the MABC -2. This was to 95 

ensure we were not including children in the comparison group who did not have 96 

normative motor development  (French et al., 2018) . A further six participants were 97 

excluded during EEG processing due to insufficient data (DCD n = 4; comparison n = 2). 98 

The final sample therefore included 19 children with DCD ( Mage = 11.17 years, SD = 1.28; 99 

14 male; 14 right -handed) and 22 children without DCD ( Mage = 9.78 years, SD = 1.40; 100 

12 male; 21 right -handed). Full testing and demographic details are available in Keating 101 

et al.,  (2023) ; however we note here that the comparison group had significantly higher 102 

IQ scores ( p = .011)  (assessed by the Weschler Abbreviated Scales of Intelligence, 2nd 103 

edition [WASI -II]; Weschler, 2011)  and were significantly younger than the DCD group ( p104 

= .002). Written informed consent was obtained from the participant’s caregiver before 105 

the start of the experiment. Each child also provided written assent to take part. Ethical 106 

approval was granted by the Ethics Committee at BLINDED FOR REVIEW. Participants 107 

received travel expenses and a small prize. 108

2012) . The assessment consists of eight tasks organized into three subsets: manual 113 

dexterity, aiming and catching, and static and dynamic balance. Age -adjusted standard 114 

scores were calculated for each subtest, and a total difficulty score was created from 115 

which percentiles can be derived. A total score below the 5 th percentile indicates 116

2.3 Task and Stimuli 120 

Video stimuli for the EEG motor tasks were filmed using a handheld camera fixed 121 

to a tripod. The camera was positioned overlooking the model’s shoulder while she 122 

performed the actions to best match the participant’s perspective of the actions as if they 123 

were performing them, while also providing a clear view of the entire action. We chose an 124 

ego -centric perspective because previous studies have suggested this elicits greater 125 

sensorimotor activity  (Jackson et al., 2006; Fu and Franz, 2014; Drew et al., 2015) .126 

All videos were trimmed to fit within a 10 -second period, with three types of videos 127 

in total (gross motor, fine motor, and non -biological movement). The gross motor video 128 

displayed a model sitting with the hand starting at rest on the edge of a table, extending 129 

the arm to reach out and tap a red square, and then returning the arm to rest. This action 130 

took approximately 2 seconds and was repeated 5 times. The fine motor video displayed 131 

a hand holding a red pen and tracing a flower figure  (based on the Movement Assessment 132 

Battery for Children; Henderson et al., 1992) . The tracing lasted approximately 20 133 

seconds and was split into two separate videos; therefore, half of the fine motor action 134 

observation trials began with an empty flower and showed the hand tracing halfway 135 

around and the other half began with the hand already halfway around the flower and 136 

continued tracing to the end of the flower. The non -biological movement videos were of 137 

kaleidoscope patterns taken from Pixabay (www.pixabay.com). All videos of actions were 138 

performed by a right -handed person and were mirrored to make the actions appear to be 139 

performed by a left -handed model for left -handed participants (n = 6). All videos were 140 

presented on the center of the screen, subtending a visual angle of ~28° (horizontal) and 141 

~14° (vertical). Example stimuli are displayed in Figure 1 and are available at 142 

https://osf.io/vnup9/ .143 

The EEG task consisted of five conditions adapted from Hobson and Bishop  (2016; 144 

Figure 1) . Two conditions were execution trials: Execution Gross motor (ExG) and 145 

Execution Fine motor (ExF). These began with an instruction screen describing the action 146 

with an image of the action stimulus (i.e. a red square for ExG/the flower for ExF). Once 147 

the participant was ready, the experimenter initiated the trial with a button press. During 148 

the trial, the command ‘GO’ was displayed beneath the action stimulus for 10 seconds 149 

while participants performed the action. A ‘STOP’ command was then displayed for 2 150 

seconds. Three conditions consisted of observation trials using the videos described 151 

above. These began with a fixation cross. Once the participant was ready, the 152 

experimenter initiated the trial with a button press. During the trial, either the Observation 153 

Gross motor (ObG), Observation Fine motor (ObF), or the non -biological movement 154 

(kaleidoscope; Kal) video played for 10 seconds. Participants completed 40 trials of each 155

2.5 EEG Acquisition 186 

Continuous EEG was recorded with the BrainVision actiCHamp Plus system (Brain 187 

Products GmbH, Gilching, Germany) from 32 channels placed according to the 188 

International 10 -20 System, and referenced online to the vertex electrode (Cz). ECI 189 

Electro -Gel (GVB geliMED GmbH, Germany) was used to reduce impedances, which 190 

were kept below 20 kΩ. Data were sampled at 500 Hz. Video recordings of all EEG 191 

sessions were coded to identify trials in which participants were not watching a video or 192 

completing the required action. Any trials where participants were visibly not attending or 193 

were making off -task movements were removed prior to analysis (Observation: Mdn = 4, 194 

range 0 -13 trials; Execution: Mdn = 2, range 0 -8 trials). 195 

2.6 Data preprocessing 196 

EEG data were preprocessed in MATLAB (R2018a) using a custom version of the 197 

MADE pipeline  (Debnath et al., 2020) , augmented with artifact -detection routines from 198 

the NEAR pipeline  (Kumaravel et al., 2022) . Data were high -pass filtered at 1 Hz and low -199 

pass filtered at 100 Hz using EEGLAB  (v2021.1; Delorme and Makeig, 2004)  Hamming -200 

window FIR filters (high -pass cutoff at 0.5 Hz; low -pass cutoff at 105 Hz, reflecting the 201 

transition -band specification in the filter design). Prior to cleaning, discontinuous 202 

segments outside the task were removed by trimming the continuous recording to 1.5 s 203 

before the first event and 1.5 s after the last event. 204 

Bad channels were identified using NEAR’s local outlier factor (LOF) procedure, 205 

with an initial LOF cutoff of 2.5 and adaptive thresholding enabled (the cutoff was 206 

incremented when >10% of channels were flagged); flat -line detection was also enabled 207 

(5 s tolerance), while the periodogram -based detector was disabled (comparison: 0 - 3208 

channels, M = 0.24, SD = 0.66; DCD: 0 - 12 channels, M = 0.70, SD = 2.51). Artifact 209 

Subspace Reconstruction (ASR) was then applied to correct non -stereotyped artifacts 210 

(correction mode, burst criterion = 13). Stereotyped artifacts were subsequently removed 211 

using ICA, computed on a copy of the dataset segmented into 1 s epochs; epochs 212 

exceeding ±1000 μV were rejected prior to ICA, and channels with >20% rejected 1 s 213 

epochs were removed from the ICA copy and correspondingly excluded from the original 214 

data before transferring ICA weights. Artifactual independent components were identified 215 

using the Adjusted -ADJUST plugin with default parameters  (Mognon et al., 2011; Leach 216 

et al., 2020)  and removed from the original continuous dataset (comparison: 1 - 19 217 

components, M = 8.03, SD = 4.66; DCD: 1 - 14 components, M = 7.74, SD = 2.83). 218

2.7 Data -Driven Definition of the Beta Band 232 

All analyses focused on the C3 and C4 electrodes, overlying left and right 233 

sensorimotor cortex, which are the canonical loci of movement -related beta modulation 234 

(Pfurtscheller, 1981; Pfurtscheller et al., 1996) . To define the frequency limits of the beta 235 

band in a data -driven manner, power spectral densities (PSDs) were computed for each 236 

participant from 0.5 to 40 Hz using Welch's method (1 s Hann windows, 50% overlap, 10× 237 

zero -padding). PSDs were parameterized using specparam  (Donoghue et al., 2020)  to 238 

separate periodic (oscillatory) and aperiodic components. 239 

Periodic components were averaged across C3 and C4 electrodes and then 240 

across participants from both groups. Spectral peaks were identified using an iterative 241 

peak -fitting procedure in which the frequency -domain periodic spectrum was fit with a 242 

Gaussian at its global maximum, subtracted, and re -evaluated until no additional peaks 243 

remained. Peak frequency bands were defined using the full width at half maximum 244 

(FWHM) of each Gaussian, retaining only peaks with a minimum FWHM of 1 Hz below 245 

10 Hz or 3 Hz above 10 Hz. This procedure yielded a beta range of 14 -31.25 Hz, which 246 

was used in all subsequent analyses. 247 

2.8 Conventional Beta Power Analysis 248 

To characterize canonical beta modulation, time -frequency (TF) representations 249 

of each trial were computed using an adaptive superlet transform  (Moca et al., 2021) , with 250 

varying central frequencies (1 -50 Hz) and a fixed number of cycles (4 cycles) within a 251 

Gaussian envelope. The wavelet order (i.e., the multiplier for the number of cycles in the 252 

wavelet) varied linearly from 1 to 40 over the frequency range. Time -frequency 253 

representations were averaged across the defined beta band and baseline -corrected 254 

relative to -7 to -1 s using percent signal change. Group -level time courses were 255 

compared using cluster -based permutation testing (Maris & Oostenveld, 2007), with 256

2.9 Lagged Hilbert Autocoherence 271 

To assess whether beta activity reflected sustained rhythmic oscillations or 272 

transient burst -like dynamics, we computed lagged Hilbert autocoherence (LHaC) over 273 

the 1 -40 Hz range during the 0 -10 s execution interval. LHaC quantifies the degree to 274 

which oscillatory amplitude fluctuations remain temporally coherent across lags; lower 275 

values in the beta band relative to neighboring frequencies indicate reduced sustained 276 

rhythmicity and greater burst -like structure  (Zhang et al., 2025) . For each participant and 277 

hemisphere, LHaC was computed across temporal lags (0.1 -4.5 cycles). Group -level 278 

LHaC spectra were obtained by averaging across participants within hemisphere. 279 

2.10 Beta Burst Detection Algorithm 280 

The lagged Hilbert autocoherence analysis indicated that beta activity over 281 

sensorimotor cortex was weakly rhythmic and therefore better characterized as transient 282 

burst events rather than sustained oscillations. We therefore analyzed beta activity at the 283 

level of individual burst events using a recently developed adaptive burst detection 284 

algorithm  (Rayson et al., 2023; Szul et al., 2023)  designed to capture transient oscillatory 285 

events over a wide amplitude range without relying on a fixed global threshold (Figure 2). 286 

For each trial, time -frequency (TF) representations were computed using the superlet 287 

transform  (Moca et al., 2021) , which optimally balances time and frequency resolution, 288 

and is thus particularly suited for detecting transient bursts within narrow frequency 289 

bands. As for conventional power analyses, an adaptive superlet transform was used, 290 

with varying central frequencies (1 -50 Hz) and a fixed number of cycles (4 cycles) within 291 

a Gaussian envelope. The wavelet order (i.e., the multiplier for the number of cycles in 292 

the wavelet) varied linearly from 1 to 40 over the frequency range. 293

Figure 2. Iterative beta burst detection and waveform extraction pipeline. Raw EEG time series are 295 

transformed into the time –frequency domain using the superlet transform. Power spectral densities are 296 

computed and parameterized to separate periodic and aperiodic components, allowing subtraction of the 297 

aperiodic fit from the TF representation. Burst detection proceeds iteratively by identifying the global TF 298 

maximum, fitting a two -dimensional Gaussian, subtracting it from the residual TF matrix, and repeating until 299 

no peaks exceed a noise threshold (2 SD). For each detected burst, a 260 ms time -domain waveform is 300 

extracted, phase -aligned using the Hilbert transform, checked for temporal consistency with the TF peak, 301 

DC -corrected, and retained for further analysis. 302 

To isolate oscillatory burst activity, PSDs were derived from each trial’s TF 303 

decomposition and parameterized using specparam to estimate the aperiodic (1/f -like) 304 

component of the spectrum. This step is critical because aperiodic activity contributes 305 

broadband power that can vary across frequency and time, and if left unaccounted for, 306 

can bias burst detection by inflating apparent oscillatory peaks or distorting their 307 

frequency and duration estimates. The aperiodic fit was therefore subtracted from the TF 308 

representation, yielding a residual TF matrix that more selectivity reflects band -limited 309 

power  (Brady and Bardouille, 2022) . Burst detection was then performed directly in this 310 

residual TF space. At each iteration, the algorithm identified the global maximum in the 311 

residual TF matrix, defined jointly over the time and frequency dimensions. A two -312 

dimensional Gaussian was then fit to this peak, with one dimension capturing the burst’s 313 

temporal extent (duration) and the other capturing its spectral extent (frequency span). 314 

The fitted Gaussian provides estimates of peak time, duration, peak frequency, 315 

frequency span, and peak amplitude, and was subsequently subtracted from the residual 316 

TF matrix. The algorithm then proceeded to the next iteration on the updated residual. 317 

Iteration continued until no remaining TF peak exceeded a noise floor defined as 2 318 

standard deviations above the mean residual power across all time and frequency bins, 319 

recomputed in every iteration. To minimize edge effects, burst detection was applied to 320 

TF data spanning 11 -34.25 Hz, and only bursts with peak frequencies within the 321 

predefined beta band (14 -31.25 Hz) were retained for further analysis. 322

2.8 Burst analysis 334 

We first compared beta burst features (duration, peak amplitude, peak frequency, 335 

and frequency span) between the comparison and DCD groups to ensure that any 336 

subsequent group differences were not driven by conventional time -frequency 337 

characteristics. Statistical significance was assessed using nonparametric permutation 338 

tests (N = 100,000), in which group labels were randomly shuffled and the observed 339 

difference in mean feature values was evaluated against the resulting null distribution. 340 

Overall burst rate was then computed separately for contralateral and ipsilateral 341 

sensorimotor electrodes during fine (ExF) and gross (ExG) motor execution. For each 342 

participant and condition, burst occurrences were binned in 0.2 s intervals from -8 to 15 343 

s, converted to rates (Hz), and normalized by the number of trials. The resulting time 344 

series were smoothed with a Gaussian kernel (σ = 2 bins) and baseline -corrected by 345 

subtracting the mean rate during the pre -task interval ( -7 to -1 s). Group differences in 346 

burst -rate dynamics were evaluated using cluster -based permutation testing (1,000 347 

permutations) on the baseline -corrected time series, controlling for multiple comparisons 348 

across time. 349 

We then used Principal Component Analysis (PCA) to characterize variability in 350 

burst waveform shape and to determine whether this variability differed between the 351 

comparison and DCD groups. Unlike conventional time -frequency features (e.g., peak 352 

frequency or amplitude), which are derived from spectral representations, waveform 353 

analysis operates directly on the raw time -domain signal of each detected burst. PCA 354 

identifies orthogonal axes that capture the dominant dimensions of waveform variability 355 

across bursts. These components represent statistical directions of variance and are not 356 

assumed to correspond to discrete physiological generators. Throughout the manuscript, 357 

we use the term “motif” as a descriptive shorthand for the waveform shapes associated 358 

with extreme loadings on a given component. 359

Burst waveforms were extracted during the detection procedure and pooled across 360 

conditions for PCA. We specified 20 components to ensure that the decomposition 361 

captured the full range of observable waveform variability. This dimensionality was 362 

chosen conservatively: across multiple datasets analyzed with this framework, 363 

permutation -based significance testing has consistently identified no more than 364 

approximately 10 -12 components as exceeding chance levels of explained variance 365 

(Rayson et al., 2023; Szul et al., 2023; Papadopoulos et al., 2024; Agouram et al., 2025) .366 

Specifying 20 components therefore provides a stable upper bound that exceeds the 367 

number of meaningful components without constraining the solution a priori. 368 

We first conducted separate PCAs for each group (after robust scaling), treating 369 

each time point as a feature and defining the mean burst waveform as the origin of a 20 -370 

dimensional space. Each burst was represented by its scores along each principal 371 

component, indicating its deviation from the mean waveform along that dimension. To 372 

assess whether the structure of waveform variability differed between groups, we 373 

compared eigenvectors using Spearman correlation and observed high correspondence 374 

across groups. A single global PCA was therefore fit to all bursts pooled across 375 

participants, enabling direct comparison within a shared representational space. 376 

To determine which components captured meaningful waveform variance, we 377 

compared the variance explained by each component in the real data with null 378 

distributions obtained from 100 iterations of temporally shuffled data, in which time points 379 

within each waveform were permuted to destroy temporal structure while preserving 380 

marginal distributions  (Vieira, 2012) . P -values were defined as the proportion of shuffled 381 

solutions explaining greater variance than the empirical component. After Bonferroni 382 

correction (α = 0.0025), PCs 1 -11 were retained for further analysis. 383 

To identify waveform motifs that were modulated during movement, we compared 384 

principal component (PC) scores between baseline ( -7 to -1 s) and task (1 to 9 s) windows 385 

within each execution condition (ExF, ExG). For each subject, bursts were assigned to 386 

100 ms time bins, and mean PC scores were computed per bin, normalized by trial count, 387 

and smoothed (Gaussian kernel, σ = 2.5 bins). This yielded a time -resolved estimate of 388 

the average contribution of each PC to the burst population over the course of the trial. 389 

For each PC, we then computed the difference between baseline and task window means 390 

separately for each subject and group. Statistical significance was assessed using paired 391 

permutation tests (sign -flipping across subjects; 1,000 permutations). PCs showing a 392 

significant baseline -task difference ( p < 0.05) were considered task -modulated. This 393 

analysis identified PCs 3, 6, and 8 as significantly modulated during execution. Because 394 

changes in mean PC score may reflect altered prevalence of bursts at different positions 395 

along that dimension rather than purely within -burst shape changes, we next quantified 396

2.10 Granger Causality Analysis 453 

To test whether group differences in waveform -specific burst dynamics were 454 

accompanied by altered directed inter -hemispheric interactions, we estimated Granger 455 

causality (GC) from motif -specific burst -count time series rather than from the continuous 456 

beta envelope. This choice was motivated by the aim of quantifying directed coupling 457 

specifically between discrete burst events stratified by waveform motif (PC -score bins), 458 

whereas beta -envelope signals pool across motifs and can conflate changes in burst 459 

probability with changes in within -burst amplitude. 460 

GC was computed only for the PC -condition pairs that showed robust motif -specific 461 

group effects in the burst -rate time series (PC6 during ExG; PC8 during ExF). For each 462 

analysis (PC6 -ExG; PC8 -ExF), bursts were first stratified into PC tertiles (T1 -T3) using 463 

global PC -score percentiles. Within each subject, bursts were then converted into trial -464 

wise point -process count series by binning burst peak times into 10 ms bins over the 465 

movement period (0 -10 s post -onset), separately for ipsilateral and contralateral 466 

sensorimotor electrodes. This yielded six units per subject (ipsi T1 -T3; contra T1 -T3), 467 

each represented as an array of shape units × trials × time bins .468 

Directed connectivity was estimated using a point -process GC framework 469 

implemented as a negative -binomial GLM with constant dispersion (α = 1.0), following the 470 

logic of point -process Granger methods  (Kim et al., 2011; Agouram et al., 2025) . For each 471

4. Discussion 760 

This study applied waveform -based beta burst analyses to investigate how 761 

sensorimotor burst motifs relate to motor performance in childhood. Conventional time -762 

frequency measures, including beta power and burst spectral features, did not differ 763 

between groups, and the overall structure of burst waveform variability was highly similar 764 

in children with and without developmental coordination disorder. Instead, differences 765 

emerged only when examining burst dynamics along specific waveform dimensions. 766 

Movement execution was associated with systematic redistribution of bursts along these 767 

dimensions, and the prevalence and lateralization of particular motifs co -varied with motor 768 

coordination. Children with DCD were disproportionately represented at one end of this 769 

motif -defined axis, but the relation between waveform -specific burst rate and MABC -2770 

scores followed a similar slope across groups. These findings indicate that beta bursts 771 

comprise functionally heterogeneous events  (Rayson et al., 2023, 2025; Szul et al., 2023; 772 

Zich et al., 2023; Moreau et al., 2026)  whose motif -specific dynamics track continuous 773 

variation in motor competence, with DCD providing a clinically informative model of 774 

extreme motor difficulty rather than a categorically distinct beta phenotype. 775 

Our results further suggest that different beta burst waveform motifs contribute 776 

differently to sensorimotor processing during action execution. Rather than reflecting 777 

discrete burst classes, the principal components derived from waveform analysis capture 778 

continuous dimensions of burst shape variability along which bursts can be redistributed 779 

depending on task demands and motor performance. Group differences emerged 780 

selectively at the extremes of these dimensions: during gross motor execution, bursts 781 

with low versus high PC6 scores showed opposing changes in rate across groups, while 782 

during fine motor execution a similar divergence was observed for bursts occupying the 783 

highest tertile of PC8. Importantly, these effects did not involve global changes in burst 784 

incidence or spectral burst features. Instead, motor -related differences reflected shifts in 785 

the relative prevalence of bursts occupying specific regions of the waveform variability 786

A notable feature of these effects was their localization to the ipsilateral 790

contralateral motor control, ipsilateral sensorimotor cortex plays an important role in 792 

coordinating activity across hemispheres during movement  (Serrien et al., 2006) . The 793 

motif -specific redistribution observed here may therefore reflect differences in how 794 

transient beta events participate in interhemispheric sensorimotor communication 795

revealed motif -specific differences in interhemispheric interactions, including changes in 797 

the sign of influence for particular burst tertiles. These findings suggest that the 798 

coordination of activity between hemispheres depends not only on the timing of beta 799 

bursts but also on the waveform motifs through which these events are expressed. 800 

The relation between burst dynamics and motor performance further supports a 801 

dimensional interpretation at the level of brain –behavior mapping. Although children with 802 

DCD exhibited poorer motor performance overall, the association between waveform -803 

specific burst rate and MABC -2 score was comparable across groups, indicating that 804 

similar neural -behavioral relationships are expressed across the full sample. This pattern 805 

suggests that variation in sensorimotor beta burst dynamics may index sensorimotor 806 

performance, rather than an underlying process specific to DCD. 807 

The present findings therefore complement existing accounts of motor impairment 808 

in DCD that emphasize atypical sensorimotor connectivity and hemispheric specialization 809 

(Tallet et al., 2013; McLeod et al., 2014, 2016) . Previous work has linked these differences 810 

to alterations in corpus callosum -mediated motor coordination and interhemispheric 811 

inhibition. The motif -specific redistribution observed here provides a potential 812 

physiological substrate for these network -level differences. If distinct beta burst motifs 813 

reflect different modes of sensorimotor processing, then altered recruitment of these 814 

motifs could influence how information is exchanged between hemispheres during action 815 

planning and execution. Such differences may ultimately contribute to the motor planning 816 

and adaptation difficulties often observed in DCD  (Subara -Zukic et al., 2022) . More 817 

broadly, these findings suggest that the functional role of beta bursts in motor control may 818 

depend on the distribution of waveform motifs within the burst population rather than on 819 

overall burst incidence alone. 820

The present study has several strengths. The inclusion of execution, observation, 834 

and non -biological motion conditions allowed us to isolate sensorimotor -specific effects 835 

and demonstrate that motif -related differences were not simply driven by visual or 836 

attentional factors. Moreover, this study represents the first application of waveform -837 

based beta burst analysis to a neurodevelopmental population, demonstrating the utility 838 

of this approach for characterizing subtle differences in sensorimotor network dynamics 839 

that are not apparent using conventional spectral measures. Nevertheless, several 840 

limitations should be noted. The sample size, while comparable to previous EEG studies 841 

in DCD, was relatively modest, and replication in larger cohorts will be important. In 842 

addition, the present analyses focused on central electrodes overlying sensorimotor 843 

cortex; beta bursts arising from associative or prefrontal regions may also contribute to 844 

motor planning and control  (Wessel, 2020; Diesburg et al., 2021; Liljefors et al., 2024) .845 

Finally, because the experimental design used relatively long task epochs, the temporal 846 

relation between individual movements and burst events could not be examined with high 847 

precision. Future studies using event -locked designs will be important for understanding 848 

how motif -specific bursts relate to individual movement components and error signals. 849 

In conclusion, our findings demonstrate that sensorimotor beta burst dynamics in 850 

childhood vary systematically along waveform -defined dimensions that relate to motor 851 

performance. Rather than reflecting global differences in beta activity, motor coordination 852 

differences were associated with motif -specific redistribution of bursts within the broader 853 

population of beta events. These results support emerging models in which the functional 854 

heterogeneity of beta bursts plays a central role in sensorimotor computation. By linking 855 

waveform -defined burst motifs to motor competence across development, this work 856 

provides new insight into the neural mechanisms underlying motor coordination 857 

differences and highlights the potential of burst -based approaches for studying atypical 858 

motor development. 859
