# Abstract 12 

Overgeneralization of negative experiences, in which aversive responses spread to otherwise safe 13 

stimuli, often co -occurs with sleep disruption , and both are central features of anxiety and 14 

posttraumatic stress disorder (PTSD). Here , we show that sleep shifts emotional generalization away 15 

from the negative and toward the positive. Across three experiments combining behavior, fMRI , and 16 

sleep electrophysiology, participants learned to associate three faces with positive, negative, or 17 

neutral outcomes. Participants were tested using morphed faces that blended the original stimuli in 18 

varying proportions . Immediat e generalization was assessed post -learning, and delayed 19 

generalization was assessed after overnight sleep or daytime wake fulness . Behaviorally, we found 20 

that sleep selectively promotes positive generalization , whereas prolonged daytime wakefulness 21 

favors generalization of the negative face. In the fMRI scanner, amygdala and limbic activity during 22 

outcome processing predict ed stronger immediate generalization of the negative face , and 23 

subsequent shift from negative to positive face generalization that occurred only after sleep .24 

Overnight sleep spindle activity , extracted from high -density sleep EEG, positively correlated both 25 

with positive face generalization and with the shift from negative to positive generalization following 26 

sleep. These findings reveal a potential neural mechanism by which sleep attenuates negative bias 27 

and enhanc es positive representations , suggesting a potential method to buffer against maladaptive 28 

generalization in anxiety and PTSD. 29

# Introduction 31 

Imagine walking down the street to your favorite café when a dog suddenly jumps out from around a 32 

corner, knocking you to the ground. Upon cautiously returning to the same street, you see a large 33 

dog; even if only vaguely resembling the original, you may believe it is the same dog and instinctively 34 

retreat . This response reflects emotional generalization : an adaptive process that allows individuals 35 

to extend past experiences to similar, novel situations 1,2 . Generalization can occur consciously or 36 

unconsciously , often influencing how we interpret current stimuli based on past outcomes 2,3 . It is 37 

particularly sensitive to emotionally salient events, with a stronger bias toward negative 38 

associat ions . For example , tones paired with negative reinforcement produce broader 39 

generalization gradients than those linked to positive reinforcement, which in turn produce broader 40 

generalization than neutral stimuli 3,4 .41 

While generalization is considered adaptive in many contexts, overgeneralization, especially of 42 

negative experiences, can become maladaptive . For example, a single dog attack may lead to fear 43 

of all dogs or even avoidance of outdoor settings altogether . Such behavioral patterns are implicated 44 

in psychiatric disorders , including major depression, phobias, generalized anxiety disorder, and 45 

posttraumatic stress disorder (PTSD) 1,4,5 .46 

Sleep disturbances are also a hallmark of psychiatric disorders , reflecting the bidirectional 47 

relationship between sleep and mental health 6–9. Sleep disruption can impair emotional regulation 48 

and increase anxiety, while anxiety itself can interfere with sleep quality 10 . This raises a critical 49 

question: What role does sleep play in shaping emotional generalization? 50 

Research increasingly shows that sleep facilitates the consolidation of both rewarding 11 –15 and 51 

aversive 16,17 emotionally salient memories , and supports the extraction of patterns from 52 

experience 18 –20 . Th us, sleep may help to regulate emotional generalization by limiting the spread of 53 

negative associations. Accordingly, w e hypothesize d that sleep serve s a corrective function, 54 

reducing negative overgeneralization while enhancing the generalization of positive events .55

# Experimental tasks 66 

Face stimuli used in the tasks: Twenty -six participants (14 women) rated faces with neutral 67 

expression sourced from an online database (the siblings DB) 22 , arranging them along a continuum 68 

from most malicious to most kind. Three males with similar average ratings as well as similar skin 69 

tones, hair, and eye colors were selected as the original faces (Figure 1A ). These faces were 70 

positioned as vert ices of a triangle space comprising 66 faces that were morphed composite s of the 71 

three original (vertex) faces (FantaMorph software ). The proximity of each face to each vertex 72 

corresponded to the proportion of the original face in the morph. Thus, the face positioned in the 73 

center of the triangle was an average representation derived from all three original faces (Figure 1A ). 74 

The assignment of original faces to negative, positive, or neutral valence vertices was 75 

counterbalanced across participants. Twenty -nine (18 women) participants completed a n online 76 

discrimination experiment to ensure no bias in perceiv ing morphed faces as similar to the original 77 

faces (see also 'Discrimination Task' below). 78 

Learning task. We used a probabilistic learning task (two -armed bandit 23 ) to associate valence with 79 

the original face stimuli. After a short training to ensure understanding, participants were instructed 80 

to choose one face in each trial and maximize their points according to feedback. During each trial, 81 

participants were presented with two out of three original faces simultaneously on the screen, with 82 

the left and right positions randomized for each trial. All possible pairs of faces were presented 83 

throughout the task. Each original face was probabilistically assigned (p=0.9) to either positive, 84 

negative, or neutral feedback (gain +10, loss -10, or zero points), and participants learned these 85 

associations as the task progressed. After their selection, the chosen face was highlighted by a 86 

yellow frame on the screen. Feedback regarding the participant 's response (gain +10, loss -10, or 87 

zero points) was then provided based on the selected face (see Figure 1B and Supplementary Figure 88 

1A-C). Because face –feedback associations were highly p redictable (p =0.9), participants quickly 89 

learned the value of each face and consistently chose the more rewarding option. This might lead to 90 

disproportionate exposure to positive feedback and limited exposure to negative feedback. To 91 

overcome this potential bias, we included 'helpless' trials in which both presented stimuli were 92 

identical faces associated with negative or neutral feedback, ensuring balanced exposure across 93 

feedback types . Trial composition included a total of 132 trials comprising 30 positive -negative, 30 94 

positive -neutral, 30 negative -neutral, 18 neutral -neutral, and 24 negative -negative face pairs trial. 95

# Generalization analysis 288 

Generalization assessment: We analyzed each participant 's response patterns separately for each 289 

session. We calculated the proportion of responses classifying each morphed face as most similar 290 

to one of the original faces, organizing them into three triangular spaces based on valence: positive, 291 

negative, or neutral. As face –valence pairings were counterbalanced, triangles were labeled by 292 

valence rather than face identity (Figure 1E, top left). We interpolated each triangle to generate 293 

continuous response gradients (Figure 1E, top right). Perceptual boundaries were defined using a 294 

50% threshold, such that a morphed face was considered to belong to a given original face if it was 295 

classified as resembling that face on at least 50% of its presentations. The area within each triangle 296 

meeting this criterion was defined as the generalization region. The size of these areas served as a 297 

measure of generalization (Figure 1E, bottom left; for visualization, all three are shown on one 298 

triangle). To assess changes over time, we computed two metrics: (1) immediate generalization : the 299 

area change from Session 1 to Session 2 (after learning), and (2) delayed generalization : the change 300 

from Session 2 to Session 3 (after sleep or wake; Figure 1E, bottom right). 301 

Note: While a 66% threshold was used in the figures for clearer visualization, all analyses were 302 

conducted using the 50% threshold. 303 

Repeated face presentations: Although the discrimination task was repeated across sessions, it 304 

could, in principle, introduce additional learning through repeated exposure to the faces, these trials 305 

were conducted without immediate feedback. Thus, repeated exposure would be expected to 306 

promote extinction or weakening of learned valence associations rather than reinforcement, 307 

potentially reducing the magnitude of generalization effects. Therefore, the observed generalization 308 

patterns are unlikely to be explained solely by repeated task exposure .309 

Generalization score: In the fMRI experiment, we computed a single score to capture the balance 310 

between generalization of positive and negative faces. This generalization score was calculated by 311 

subtracting negative face generalization from positive face generalization. Scores above zero 312 

indicate a bias toward generalizing the positive face, while scores below zero indicate greater 313 

generalization of the negative face. This measure reflects the idea that increased generalization of 314 

one emotional valence occurs at the expense of the other. 315

Overgeneralization Calculation : Overgeneralization was defined as instances where participants 321 

incorrectly classified morphed faces as resembling one of the original faces, beyond the boundaries 322 

of the correct generalization zone. In the generalization task, such incorrect responses resulted in 323 

point loss and were considered maladaptive. Regions of the triangular face space that extended 324 

beyond the adaptive generalization boundaries were designated as overgeneralization areas . See 325 

Supplementary Figure 7A, B for illustration. 326 

Statistics: Group -level effects in generalization were assessed using mixed -model ANOVAs. 327 

Significant interactions were followed by paired or independent -samples two -tailed t -tests, 328 

depending on the comparison. To examine whether brain ROI activity predicted generalization, we 329 

applied both linear regression and LASSO (least absolute shrinkage and selection operator) 330 

regression. All predictors were z -scored prior to LASSO to ensure comparability. The regularization 331

# Sleep reverses amygdala -associated generalization of negative stimuli 450 

Next, we set out to investigate the neural correlates associated with generalization of positive and 451 

negative faces and their dynamics using fMRI, employing a modified version of the learning task 452 

performed in the scanner (Methods). We could not replicate the group -level behavioral differences 453

# Discussion 664 

In this study, we tested the hypothesis that sleep reduces emotional generalization of negative 665 

stimuli while boosting the generalization of positive stimuli. Using a face discrimination task with 666 

morphed faces, we found that overnight sleep selectively promote d generalization of positive 667 

associations, whereas daytime wakefulness was linked to generalization of negative associations 668 

(Figure 2). fMRI analysis revealed that greater limbic responses to emotional feedback during 669 

learning (gains or losses) were associated with broader immediate generalization of the negative 670 

face . This activity was associated with a reversal of this pattern after sleep: participants exhibited 671 

greater generalization of positive faces following overnight sleep, but not after a day awake (Figure 672 

3). Amygdala activity consistently emerged as a main predictor of both immediate and delayed 673 

generalization , for both positive and negative emotional valence (Figure 4). Participants with high 674 

amygdala activation during learning exhibited the strongest immediate generalization of the negative 675 

face and showed the greatest sleep -associated renormalization. Finally, we found that sleep spindle 676 

activity was significantly correlated with a shift from negative to positive generalization (Figure 5). 677 

Data from the behavioral experiment revealed a clear divergence in delayed generalization profiles 678 

following overnight sleep or daytime wakefulness. Participants who remained awake after learning 679 

tended to generalize the negatively associated face , reinforcing a negative bias , while those who 680 

slept were more likely to generalize the positively associated face. These findings align with previous 681 

research showing that negative stimuli often elicit broader generalization curves than neutral or 682 

positive stimuli 1,3,4 , a pattern thought to reflect an adaptive threat -detection mechanism. Our results 683 

extend this literature by showing that sleep does not merely preserve these biases but actively 684 

reshapes the generalization landscape, attenuating negative overgeneralization and enhancing 685 

positive generalization 686 

fMRI data showed that greater amygdala activity during processing of either positive or negative 687 

outcomes was linked to broader generalization of negative stimuli immediately after learning. After 688 

sleep, this pattern reversed: higher amygdala activity was now associated with greater generalization 689 

of positive faces, suggesting that sleep supports a renormalization of amygdala -related emotional 690 

memory. This effect was absent in the wake group, reinforcing its sleep dependency. Notably, 691

To investigate the role of sleep -specific neurophysiology in this reorganization, we recorded high -700 

density EEG during overnight sleep. We found that both sigma power and the number of fast spindle 701 

events during NREM sleep were positively correlated with the generalization of positively associated 702 

faces. Additionally, we observed a trend suggesting that individuals who showed greater negative 703 

generalization immediately after learning exhibited increased spindle activity during subsequent 704 

sleep, pointing to a possible compensatory or recovery -related renormalization mechanism . Both 705 

measures of spindle activity were significantly correlated with the renormalization in positive face 706 

generalization , reflecting a shift from negative to positive bias after sleep . These findings extend our 707 

understanding of the association between sleep spindles and memory consolidation to show that 708 

spindles play a role in emotionally adaptive reorganization of generalized learned associations. In 709 

addition to modulating emotional memory, our results suggest that sleep spindles may contribute 710 

to the selective suppression of emotionally neutral information. Specifically, greater fast spindle 711 

activity was associated with reduced generalization of neutral faces, which were not linked to reward 712 

or punishment. This pattern suggests that spindles may serve a dual function , supporting the 713 

generalization of emotionally salient memories while promoting forgetting of behaviorally irrelevant 714 

content. This interpretation extends prior work proposing that sleep supports the prioritization of 715 

salient or goal -relevant information during consolidation 12,16,58 .716

relationship between NREM spindle activity and arousal systems. During NREM sleep, spindle 718 

activity is anti -correlated with markers of arousal, including locus coeruleus –norepinephrine (LC –719 

NE) activity, corticotropin -releasing hormone (CRH) signaling, heightened autonomic tone, and 720 

increased microarousals 59 –62 . Thus, beyond their role in memory consolidation, increased spindle 721 

activity may also index a state of reduced noradrenergic tone. Such a “silent” noradrenergic 722

Some limitation s of this study should be explicitly acknowledged. First, circadian factors may 728 

influence the observed group differences. Because testing sessions occurred at opposite times of 729 

day (morning vs. evening), it is difficult to fully disentangle the effects of sleep from those of time of 730 

day. In the behavioral experiment, participants in the day group reported higher subjective 731 

sleepiness in the evening session than the night group did in the morning, although objective 732 

vigilance (PVT) scores did not differ . Previous research has linked fatigue to more negative 733 

interpretation bias es 9,57 , which may in principle partly explain the negative generalization in the day 734 

group. However, neither SSS nor PVT scores were significantly correlated with generalization 735 

measures, arguing against a major role for circadian factors. Further arguing against circadian 736 

confounds, robust correlations between individual sleep spindle activity in the sleep EEG 737 

experiment and shifts in emotional generalization support the interpretation that sleep plays an 738 

active, corrective role in modulating emotional overgeneralization . Such renormalization may be 739 

mediated in part via spindle -related mechanisms operating during NREM sleep. Second, group -level 740 

generalization differences in the behavioral experiment were not replicated in the fMRI experiment, 741 

where no group differences in subjective sleepiness were found. While we can only speculate about 742 

the source of differences between the two experiments, the learning in the fMRI experiment was 743 

much longer with extended inter -stimulus and inter -trial intervals (a total of over 1 hour compared 744

a protective mechanism . Sleep spindles may not only reduc e negative amygdala -related 751
