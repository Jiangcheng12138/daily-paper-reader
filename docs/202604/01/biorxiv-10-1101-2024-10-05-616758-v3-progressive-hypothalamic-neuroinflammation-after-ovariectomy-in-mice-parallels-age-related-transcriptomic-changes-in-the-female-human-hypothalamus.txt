Abstract 34 The hypothalamic changes that occur after the loss of ovarian estrogen remain poorly 35 characterized. Here, we performed a comprehensive temporal characterization of the mouse 36 hypothalamus following ovariectomy (OVX), combining physiological measurements with bulk 37 RNA-sequencing of the posterior hypothalamus (PH) and preoptic area (POA) at short-term (14 38 days) and long-term (4 months) post-OVX. Serum LH levels rose progressively and then 39 declined, while core temperature peaked early and subsequently normalized, recapitulating the 40 endocrine and thermoregulatory dynamics of reproductive aging in humans. Transcriptomic 41 analysis revealed time-dependent activation of inflammatory pathways, glial markers, and KNDy 42 neuron-related gene networks, with the most pronounced changes emerging at 4 months post-43 OVX, particularly in the PH. Immunofluorescence confirmed increased NKB release, declining 44 KNDy neuronal activity, and heightened astrocytic reactivity in the arcuate nucleus after 45 prolonged estrogen withdrawal. To contextualize these findings, we analyzed publicly available 46 human hypothalamic RNA-seq data across chronological age. Age-related transcriptomic 47 patterns in women, including progressive inflammatory signaling, glial activation, and altered 48 KNDy gene expression, showed significant correlation with the OVX mouse model, particularly 49 at the pathway level. These findings establish a temporal framework for hypothalamic molecular 50 changes after estrogen withdrawal, identify conserved neuroinflammatory signatures across 51 species, and provide a preclinical platform for testing interventions targeting menopausal-52 associated hypothalamic dysfunction. 53

Introduction 55 Menopause represents a significant life transition for women, characterized by physiological 56 changes extending beyond reproductive function 1 . Understanding the neurobiological basis of 57 these changes is crucial for advancing women’s health and developing targeted interventions 58 for menopausal symptoms 2 . While much attention has been focused on ovarian function, 59 research focused on characterizing the changes that happen at the level of the brain, 60 particularly the hypothalamus, has lagged. The hypothalamus contains neuroendocrine centers 61 that both control and respond to estrogens 3,4 , and hypothalamic changes are implicated in 62 disrupted thermoregulation, sleep patterns, and increased mood disorders during menopause 5-63 12 .64 Hypothalamic KNDy neurons (expressing kisspeptin, neurokinin B, and dynorphin A) have 65 emerged as central players in vasomotor symptoms (VMS), including hot flashes and night 66

sweats, which are among the most common and disruptive menopausal symptoms 13,14 . The 67 identification of KNDy neurons as VMS triggers has opened new avenues for understanding and 68 treating these symptoms 13-15 , ultimately leading to the FDA approval of NK3R antagonists for 69 VMS 16 . However, our knowledge of broader hypothalamic changes during and after estrogen 70 withdrawal remains incomplete, compounded by limitations in current animal models 17 .71 A critical gap in the field has been the absence of a well-characterized temporal model of 72 hypothalamic changes following estrogen loss. Most ovariectomy (OVX) studies in rodents 73 examine a single short-term timepoint, typically 1–3 weeks post-surgery, which captures the 74 acute response to estrogen withdrawal but fails to model the progressive changes that develop 75 over months to years in women. This is particularly relevant because VMS and other 76 menopausal symptoms show distinct temporal trajectories. VMS typically peak during the 77 perimenopausal period and gradually resolve years after menopause, while inflammatory and 78 neurodegenerative processes may continue to progress 18 . Whether these clinically distinct 79 phases correspond to discrete molecular programs in the hypothalamus has not been 80 systematically investigated. 81 Here, we address this gap by performing a comprehensive temporal analysis of the mouse 82 hypothalamus at multiple timepoints after OVX, integrating endocrine measurements, core body 83 temperature, bulk RNA-sequencing of two hypothalamic subregions, and protein-level 84 validation. We then leverage publicly available human hypothalamic transcriptomic data from 85 the GTEx consortium to assess whether the mouse molecular signatures parallel age-related 86 changes observed in women across the adult lifespan, while acknowledging the inherent 87 limitations of the human observational dataset. By combining rigorous experimental data with 88 complementary human observations, this study provides both mechanistic insight and a89 preclinical platform for developing interventions targeting hypothalamic dysfunction after 90 estrogen withdrawal. 91

Progressive endocrine and thermoregulatory changes after ovariectomy in mice. 93 To model the time-dependent effects of estrogen withdrawal on the hypothalamus, we assessed 94 a series of timepoints post-OVX in wild-type female mice ( Fig. 1a ). Serum luteinizing hormone 95 (LH) concentrations increased progressively, with levels peaking at 2 months post-OVX before 96 declining at 4 months post-OVX ( Fig. 1b ). This biphasic LH pattern, initial rise followed by 97 decline, parallels the endocrine trajectory observed during human reproductive aging, in which 98 gonadotropin levels increase during perimenopause and subsequently decline in late 99 postmenopause 19 . To characterize changes in thermoregulation, core temperature was 100 measured across timepoints, revealing a peak at 14 days post-OVX that progressively 101 normalized by 4 months ( Fig. 1c ). Together, these physiological data indicate that the 14-day 102 post-OVX model captures the acute neuroendocrine response to estrogen withdrawal, 103 characterized by elevated gonadotropins and disrupted thermoregulation, while the 4-month 104 post-OVX model reflects a more chronic, adapted state with declining gonadotropins and 105 normalized temperature. 106

Discussion 291 This study provides the first systematic temporal characterization of hypothalamic transcriptomic 292 changes following estrogen withdrawal, revealing progressive neuroinflammation that develops 293 over months in the mouse and whose molecular characteristics parallel age-related changes in 294 the female human hypothalamus. 295 A central finding is that the hypothalamic response to estrogen loss is markedly time-dependent. 296 While physiological changes (i.e. elevated LH and core temperature) manifest rapidly after OVX, 297 the full transcriptomic inflammatory signature does not emerge until 4 months post-OVX. This 298 dissociation between acute neuroendocrine responses and delayed transcriptomic remodeling 299 has important implications since short-term OVX studies, which constitute the majority of the 300 literature, may capture the initial endocrine disruption but miss the progressive 301 neuroinflammatory processes that could underlie the chronic sequelae of estrogen withdrawal. 302 The delayed onset of robust inflammatory pathway activation, particularly TNF-alpha signaling 303 via NF-κB and interferon responses, suggests that neuroinflammation is not a direct 304 consequence of estrogen loss but rather develops as a secondary process, possibly driven by 305 cumulative glial activation and loss of estrogen’s anti-inflammatory protective effects. 306 Our protein-level validation in the arcuate nucleus provides cellular resolution to complement 307 the transcriptomic data. The finding that NKB peptide stores are depleted while Tac2 mRNA is 308 increased is consistent with a state of hyperactivated neuropeptide release in KNDy neurons, 309 particularly at 14 days post-OVX. The subsequent ~50% decline in KNDy neuron activation (c-310 Fos/NKB co-localization) at 4 months aligns with the normalization of core temperature at this 311

Materials and Methods 390 

Mouse studies 391 

Study approval and animals 392 The animal studies were approved by the Brigham and Women’s Hospital Institutional Animal 393 Care and Use Committee (IACUC) in the Center for Comparative Medicine. Adult wild-type 394 (WT) C57/BL6 female mice were group housed under constant conditions of temperature (22– 395 24°C) and light (12:12-hour light:dark cycle), fed with standard mouse chow and ad libitum 396 access to tap water. 397 

Experimental design 398 To investigate the effects of sex steroid withdrawal on LH levels, core temperature (Tc), and 399 hypothalamic transcriptome profile, intact WT female mice were bilaterally ovariectomized 400 (OVX) to generate models at progressive timepoints after estrogen withdrawal. Blood LH levels 401 and Tc were measured at baseline (intact), at 7-, 14- and 21-days post-OVX and at 1-, 2- and 4-402 months post-OVX. Additional females were euthanized before OVX in diestrus (intact group), 403 after 14 days of OVX (short-term post-OVX group) or after 4 months of OVX (long-term post-404 OVX group) and their posterior (PH) and preoptic area (POA) of the hypothalamus were 405 collected for transcriptome profile analysis by bulk RNA-seq. 406

RNA-seq data processing and analysis 455 We performed all analyses using mouse genome build mm39. To pseudoalign reads to the 456 transcriptome annotation and estimate expression levels, we used kallisto 66 in the tximport 457 package 67 (v1.24.0). We imported the resulting count data into R for analysis in DESeq2 25 458 (v1.36.0). To adjust for unknown, unmodeled or latent sources of noise, we performed surrogate 459 variable analysis (SVA) using the smartSVA package 29 (v0.1.3) and used SV1 as a covariate in 460 the differential gene expression analysis. Pseudoalignment and DESeq2 were performed on the 461 entire transcriptome annotation, with downstream analysis in some cases restricted to protein-462 coding genes as annotated in GENCODE version M30 68 . GSEA was performed on pre-ranked 463 ordered gene lists using the fgsea package 21 (v1.22.0). 464

Sex and Gender Reporting 478 All human hypothalamic samples from the GTEx database were from donors recorded as 479 female (biological sex). This study examines transcriptomic changes across the female 480 reproductive lifespan. Sex was determined by GTEx based on donor records. We use the term 481 “women” to reflect the clinical population most directly affected by natural menopause, while 482 acknowledging that individuals of diverse gender identities with intact ovarian function may also 483 experience menopausal transition. 484 

Human data analysis 485 After sub-setting the GTEx RNA expression file for our samples, we adjusted for latent sources 486 of noise using SVA with the smartSVA package 29 (v0.1.3). Differential expression analyses 487 across age bins were performed using DESeq2 25 (v1.36.0) and GSEA was performed on pre-488 ranked ordered gene lists using the fgsea package 21 (v1.22.0). Gene expression trajectories 489 across age were performed on protein-coding genes using LOESS similarly to Piehl et 490 al. 2022 69 . Gene level output was subjected to expression trajectory correlation analysis; genes 491 were ranked based on their correlation with TACR3/Tacr3 expression pattern. STRING 492 interaction analyses were performed as described above. 493 Given the unavailability of hormone measurements in GTEx, we used chronological age bins as 494 proxies, informed by epidemiological data from the Study of Women’s Health Across the Nation 495 (SWAN) where median age at final menstrual period is 51–52 years 31 . Samples were grouped 496 into five age ranges: <45 years, 46–50 years, 51–55 years, 56–60 years, and 61–65 years. We 497 acknowledge that individual variation in menopausal timing means our age-based classification 498 represents chronological categories, and we cannot definitively assign clinical menopausal 499 staging or vasomotor symptom status to individual donors. The GTEx consortium did not collect 500 serum samples, precluding direct hormone measurements. 501
