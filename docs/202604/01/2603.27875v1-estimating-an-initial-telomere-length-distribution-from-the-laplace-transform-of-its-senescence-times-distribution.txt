# Estimating an initial telomere length distribution from the Laplace transform of its senescence times distribution 

Jules Olayé †

Abstract 

This work follows from a previous study on the estimation of an initial distribution of telomere length from a senescence times distribution done in [27]. In this previous study, we have presented an estimation method based on the fact that our telomere shortening model can be approximated by a transport equation. This method has encouraging results, but fails to provide a good estimation when the variability of the initial telomere length distribution is too small. We improve here this method by approximating our model with an advection-diffusion equation, which allows us to better take into account the randomness of the shortening values. We show that under this approximation, there exists a simple link between the Laplace transform of the initial telomere length distribution and that of the senescence times distribution. Then, by using a numerical method for inverting Laplace transforms called Gaver-Stehfest algorithm , we exploit this link to construct a new estimator. 

Keywords: Inverse problem, advection-diffusion equation, Laplace transform, Gaver-Stehfest algorithm, telomeres 

## 1 Introduction 

Motivation and presentation of the problem. Telomeres are DNA sequences located at the end of chromosomes that shorten during cell divisions. When one of the telomeres of a cell becomes critically short, which is around 27 base pairs for the yeast [32], this cell enters in a state called senescence , where it stops to divide [2, 9, 24, 42]. The study of this phenomenon has gained in interest over the last few years, due to its link with the apparition of cancer [33]. This has led to the development of mathematical models representing the phenomenon, studied with both theoretical and numerical approaches [3, 6, 7, 9, 30, 32]. Due to the fact that a cell becomes senescent when it has a too short telomere, the initial distribution of telomere lengths and the distribution of senescence times are deeply linked. We are interested here in better understanding this link, by solving the inverse problem which consists in estimating the initial telomere length distribution from a senescence times distribution in a single-telomere model. 

Context of the study and previous results. This work is the direct continuation of the article [27]. In this previous study, we have developed a telomere shortening model representing cells with one telomere. This model corresponds to a system of integro-differential equations representing the evolution of the telomere length distribution and the senescence times distri-bution, and is given here in (2.1). Then, we have proposed a first method to solve the inverse problem presented in the previous paragraph, based on a model approximation by a transport

The main limitation of the estimation method presented in this previous article is that it does not work very well when the initial telomere length distribution has a small variability, see [27, Section 5.1.2]. In the current work, our aim is to improve our estimation results on such initial distributions. The estimation strategy presented in [27] is inspired by the following works in protein de-polymerisation [4, 15]. In these two articles, the context is similar: a trait progressively degrades to 0, and the authors aim to retrieve the initial distribution from the density of the trait at 0.In [4], the inverse problem has been solved thanks to a model approximation by a transport equation, obtained through a scaling limit. This type of approximation is called first-order approximation , and is the type of approximation used in [27]. In order to obtain better esti-mation results, this approximation has been refined in [15] by approximating their model by a transport-diffusion equation. This type of approximation is called second-order approximation ,and its main idea is to add a diffusion term to take into account the fluctuations around the scaling limit related to the central limit theorem. After having obtained this model approxi-mation, the authors used an estimation method based on a Kalman observer to retrieve the initial distribution. 

Estimation strategy. The main reason that the estimator developed in [27] does not work when the initial distribution has a small variability is that the approximated model used to construct it does not take into account well the variability of the shortening values at each division. To manage this issue, in the current work, we develop an estimation strategy that better takes into account this variability. This strategy is inspired by the one followed in [27], and consists in the three following steps. First, we refine the first-order model approximation obtained in [27], by obtaining a second-order model approximation, as done in [15]. Then, we show that there exists a simple link between the Laplace transform of the senescence times distribution and that of the initial telomere length distribution. Finally, we use a numerical method for inverting Laplace transforms called Gaver-Stehfest algorithm [18, 36, 37] to retrieve the initial distribution of telomere length. 

Contributions. Our first contribution is to provide precise bounds for the second-order ap-proximation. As mentioned before, the first-order and second-order approximations are obtained by a scaling limit of our model. In [15], the authors proved on a similar discrete model that, de-noting by N > 0 the scaling parameter used to obtain the approximation, the error in L2-norm between their model and a transport-diffusion model is at most of order 1/N 3/2. They also obtained a bound that increases linearly with time. We improve here this result by obtaining, on more restrictive assumptions, a bound of order 1/N 2 for the pointwise approximation error, that can be extended to all the Lp-norm, where p > 0. We also prove that the bound on the error decreases exponentially fast after a certain time. The bounds we have obtained on the second-order approximation error allow us to provide a first theoretical justification that telomere shortening models can be approximated by transport-diffusion equations. Indeed, such approximations have already been studied and justified from a modelling perspective in previous works [31, 39], but no theoretical results related to it has been obtained. Our second contribution is to present and rigorously analyse on simulations a new method for solving our inverse problem based on a Laplace transform inversion. This new method improves the estimation results presented in [27] when the initial distribution has a small coefficient of variation. By presenting this new method, we also complete what is done in [15] by providing an alternative method for estimating the initial distribution of advection-diffusion equations on R+ from the observation of its value at the boundary of the space. Compared to [15], the 2advantage of our new method is that it is based on a simple link between the initial distribution and the trait density at the boundary. As a result, our new method is easier to understand and better highlights the fact that there is a deep link between the initial distribution and the distribution of the trait at the boundary of the space on such models. 

Difficulty for the model approximation. The main difficulty in obtaining a bound of order 1/N 2 is that the third derivative in space of the density of telomere lengths in the ap-proximated model explodes when N → +∞. This derivative is the main term that describes the error between the original model and the approximated model due to a Taylor expansion, see (2.5). Thus, it is necessary to control carefully the explosion of this derivative as N → +∞

to obtain a qualitative bound on the approximation error. Compared to [15], we take into ac-count here the evolution of the impact of the third-derivative with the time. This allows us to improve the bound on the model approximation. To do this, we first rewrite the approximation error as the integral of several sub-errors, as done in [27]. Then, we show that these sub-errors dissipate over time by using a maximum principle. What changes compared to [27] is that we cannot use the method of characteristics to obtain our bounds when we prove the dissipation of the sub-errors. We use here instead that the second derivative in space of our approximated model can be explicitly written thanks to the heat kernel. This explicit representation allows us to simplify the use of a maximum principle to bound the derivatives in space of the telomere length density by the eigenfunctions of the operator associated to their equations, and to exploit classical heat kernel estimates, see Sections 3.2 and 3.3. 

Plan of the article. The paper is organised as follows. First, we present the model, its approximation, and the main results of the paper in Section 2. Then, we prove that our model can be approximated by an advection-diffusion equation in Section 3. Thereafter, we present our estimation method based on the Gaver-Stehfest algorithm in Section 4. Finally, in Section 5, we discuss our results and the perspectives of this work. The appendices A-D are devoted to the proofs of certain statements given during the paper, and to additional informations about the way we have done our simulations.

4.1.3 Difficulties of estimation for a very small initial variability 

In [27, Section 5.1.2], we have shown that the estimation with bn(old ,N )0 does not work well when n0 corresponds to the density function of a distribution with a small variability and N = 40 . What we called small variability in this article was when n0 is associated to a coefficient of variation of the order of 0.15 − 0.2. Then, in the current work, we have shown in Figures 2c and 2d that when n0 has a coefficient of variation of this order, see Remark 4.3, our new estimator bn(N,K )0 allows us to manage this issue when K ∈ N∗ is large. It thus remains to check how the quality of estimation evolves when n0 has a coefficient of variation of a smaller order than the one studied in [27, Section 5.1.2], notably to study the limitations of our esti-mator. To check this, we have done exactly the same protocol as in Section 4.1.2, this time for the initial distribution n0 ∈ { h100 ,120 , h 225 ,200 , h 324 ,330 , h 400 ,500 }. In view of Remark 4.3, these cases corresponds to initial distribution with coefficient of variation 1/10 = 0 .1, 1/15 ≈ 0.067 ,

1/18 ≈ 0.056 and 1/20 = 0 .05 , so are of the order of 0.05 − 0.1. The estimation results we have obtained with this protocol are plotted in Figure 4. What we observe is that the estimation obtained with bn(N,K )0 are less satisfactory than in Figure 2, specially in Figures 4c and 4d. Specifically, we observe in Figure 4 that the smaller the coefficient of variation of the initial distribution is, the poorer the estimation of the position of the mode and the asymmetry of the curve becomes. In addition, a part of the estimated curve is negative for the estimations 20 with bn(N,K )0 in Figure 4, and the negative part of increases when the coefficient of variation of the distribution associated to n0 decreases. In Figures 4a and 4b, the error on the estimation of the mode of the curve is not dramatically poor, the negative part of bn(N,K )0 is very small, and the estimations with bn(N,K )0 clearly improve the estimations obtained with bn(old ,N )0 . Our estimator stays therefore useful in this context (coefficients of variation of 0.1 and 0.067 ), even it is not perfect. In Figures 4c and 4d, which corresponds to estimation of initial distribution with coefficients of variation 0.056 and 0.05 , the estimation of the mode of the curve is not satis-factory at all, and the negative part quite large. Our new estimator seems thus not very reliable when the coefficient of variation of the initial distribution is very small. We however mention that this limitation of our estimator is not critical. 28 5 Conclusion and perspectives 

The objective of this work was to improve the estimation method presented in [27]. To do this, we first proved that the error between (2.2) and (2.7) tends to zero when N → +∞,with a higher speed than the one of the error between (2.2) and the approximated model in [27] (see Theorem 2.4 and [27, Proposition 3.2]). The latter and the fact that the Laplace transform of n0 can be computed using the Laplace transform of u(N ) 

> ∂

for any N > 0 sug-gested that it is possible to perform a Laplace transform inversion to improve our estimation method. Our estimations done in Figure 2 showed that, when we observe n(N ) 

> ∂

without noise, this Laplace transform inversion works perfectly well on most biologically relevant initial dis-tribution. The estimations done in Figures 8, 9 and 10 have also shown that when there is noise on n(N ) 

> ∂

related to sampling, the Laplace transform inversion still improves the estimation obtained with the estimator constructed in [27]. We thus have several satisfactions with this new estimation method. Despite these encouraging results, our estimation method have also several limitations. The first one is that when the initial distribution is too much concentrated, the estimation seems to completely fail, as shown in Figures 4c and 4d. The second one is that when there is noise on n(N ) 

> ∂

related to sampling, the improvement obtained with the Laplace transform inversion is not as satisfying as the one we have when we observe noise-free data, see Figures 8, 9a-9c and 10. However, the type of initial distribution studied in Figures 4c and 4d is not biologically relevant, since the variability of the initial distribution is too much small. In addition, the second limitation is not critical in practice, since this is unavoidable that the estimation loses in quality when there is noise on the observation of n(N ) 

> ∂

. The estimation method developed in this work is therefore already useful in practice, despite these limitations. The main results we still need to achieve now is to obtain theoretical results providing bounds on the error between bn(N,K )0 and n0 for all N > 0 and K ∈ N∗, and providing bounds on the error between bn(N,K,α )0 and n0 for all N > 0, K ∈ N∗ and α > 0. 33 D Why we do not take K very large in Section 4.2.3? 

When we use (4.13) to estimate n0 in Figures 8 and 9, we do not take K very large. This may seem contradictory with (4.3) at first sight, which states that the Gaver-Stehfest algorithm converges as K → +∞. In fact, it has been proved in [23, Theorem 1] that the Gaver-Stehfest algorithm converges exponentially fast as K → +∞ when the inverse Laplace transform is regular enough, so that the speed of convergence is very quick. Hence, taking K ≈ 20 in Figures 8 and 9 seems sufficient to obtain a good approximation. Taking a larger parameter K for the estimations in Figures 8 and 9 can even create big estimation errors related to numerical instability. That is why, we do not take it larger in the estimations presented in Figures 8 and 9. To illustrate the issues related to taking K very large, we have represented in Figure 11 exactly the same estimations than the one done in Figures 8a and 8c, except that we now take K = 36 in both estimations. We observe in Figure 11 that for K = 36 , both estimations are now very poor. Specifically, oscillations with large amplitude appears at the estimated curve. These oscillations correspond in fact to numerical errors. Indeed, as the Gaver-Stehfest algorithm corresponds to the sum of large terms with alternating sign (see (4.3)), round-off errors appear when we use this algorithm. The absolute value of the terms we sum in the Gaver-Stehfest algorithm increases when its parameter K increases. Hence, increasing K also increases the impact of the round-off errors on the estimation, and that is why we do not take it larger in Figures 8 and 9. 0.0 0.5 1.0 1.5 2.0 2.5 3.0 3.5
