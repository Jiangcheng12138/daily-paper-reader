Abstract 12 

Background: Mitochondrial dysfunction is a leading contributor to the decline in oocyte quality 13 

associated with maternal aging. Prior investigations of mitochondrial function in the ovarian 14 

follicle have largely treated the mitochondrial pool as a homogeneous population, reporting 15 

aggregate values that may obscure biologically meaningful differences between distinct 16 

mitochondrial subpopulations. The present study addresses this limitation by characterizing 17 

mitochondrial subpopulation dynamics in oocytes and cumulus granulosa cells at single -organelle 18 

resolution using fluorescence -activated mitochondria sorting (FAMS). Results: Analysis of the 19 

aggregate mitochondrial population in mouse oocytes revealed no significant age -associated 20 

differences in mitochondrial DNA copy number or membrane potential, a result that would 21 

previously have been interpreted as evidence of minimal age -related mitochondrial change. 22 

Subpopulation analysis revealed this conclusion to be incomplete: aged oocytes showed 23 

significantly elevated mitochondrial DNA copy number specifically within the high membrane 24 

potential and small mitochondrial subpopulations, with no significant differences in the low 25 

membrane potential or large subpopulations. NMN supplementation normalized mitochondrial 26 

DNA copy number in the high membrane potential and small subpopulations toward young levels 27 

while producing an opposing effect in large mitochondria, demonstrating subpopulation -specific 28 

rather than uniform rejuvenation. In cumulus cells, significant age -associated changes were 29 

detectable at the aggregate level, including a reduction in mitochondrial DNA copy number and 30 

an elevation in membrane potential, and subpopulation analysis further resolved these findings. 31 

The age -associated reduction in cumulus cell mitochondrial DNA copy number was driven 32 

predominantly by the high membrane potential subpopulation. NMN supplementation exerted 33 

opposing effects on small and large cumulus cell mitochondrial subpopulations, increasing 34 

mitochondrial DNA copy number above both young and aged levels in small mitochondria while 35 

further reducing it below aged levels in large mitochondria. Conclusions: Viewing the 36 

mitochondrial pool as a heterogeneous mixture of functionally distinct subpopulations rather than 37 

a uniform population reveals age -associated alterations in oocytes and cumulus cells that are 38 

undetectable by aggregate analysis. NMN supplementation exerts subpopulation -specific effects 39 

in both cell types, identifying specific mitochondrial subtypes as more precise targets for future 40 

mechanistic investigation of age -associated infertility than the mitochondrial pool considered in 41 

aggregate. 42

The ovary is one of the first organs to fail with age, well out of sync with the chronological 48 

lifespan of women. This decline in ovarian function is tightly associated with a decline in both the 49 

quantity and quality of oocytes available for fertilization [1,2]. Of the many mechanisms proposed 50 

as being responsible for driving deterioration of egg quality with age, mitochondrial dysfunction 51 

appears central to the process [3]. Prior studies have shown that there is a marked decrease in 52 

mitochondrial number and function in oocytes with age, associated with abnormal mitochondrial 53 

distribution patterns [4 -9]. Importantly, the mitochondrial role in ovarian function with age is not 54 

restricted to the oocyte, as mitochondria play a major role in other aspects of ovarian function 55 

including steroid hormone biosynthesis in follicular somatic cells [10], and even ovarian stromal 56 

cells have been reported to demonstrate compromised mitochondrial bioenergetics with age [11]. 57 

It is important to note that mitochondria are not a homogeneous pool of organelles; they 58 

display a wide degree of diversity not only between cell types, but even within individual cells. 59 

Mitochondria have been shown to vary in both form, including size, ultrastructure, cristae density, 60 

physical networking, and mitochondrial DNA (mtDNA) content, and function, including 61 

membrane potential (ΔΨ M), apoptotic priming, hormone biosynthesis, and modulation of nuclear 62 

gene expression through retrograde signaling [12 -14]. This variation can be studied by classifying 63 

mitochondria into subpopulations on the basis of these characteristics. While previous studies 64 

lacked the technology to discern these nuances on an intracellular level, the development of 65 

fluorescence -activated mitochondria sorting (FAMS) allows for the identification of mitochondrial 66 

characteristics on a single -organelle basis [13,14,15]. This increased resolution provides a means 67 

to define clear subpopulations of mitochondria on the basis of physical and functional differences. 68 

Given the direct link between a mitochondrion's physical characteristics and its role within the 69 

cell, defining the mitochondrial subpopulations present within the ovarian follicle and evaluating 70

Recent studies have shown that age -associated infertility can be lessened by nicotinamide 73

including improved mitochondrial distribution within oocytes, improved hormone production by 79 

granulosa cells, reduced reactive oxygen species levels, increased cristae density, and improved 80 

mitophagy and mitochondrial biogenesis [9,23,24,18]. Given that mitochondrial dysfunction is a 81 

leading cause of age -associated infertility, the improved mitochondrial function that results from 82 

NMN supplementation is likely to contribute to its restorative effects on overall ovarian function. 83 

However, prior investigation of NMN supplementation on mitochondrial function in the context 84 

of age -associated infertility has been conducted at the whole -cell level, leaving the subpopulation -85 

specific effects of NMN on individual mitochondrial subtypes uncharacterized. Beyond its 86 

potential as a fertility intervention, NMN supplementation represents a useful pharmacological 87 

tool for probing the plasticity of the aged mitochondrial pool, since its ability to alter mitochondrial 88 

characteristics in aged individuals provides a means to assess whether age -associated changes are 89 

reversible or fixed. 90

For NAD+ repletion, 0.5 g/L of nicotinamide mononucleotide (NMN) was added as a supplement 116 

to the water supply of 12 -month -old female C57BL/6 mice for 28 days (4 weeks). The NMN -117

Collection of Cumulus -Oocyte -Complexes 121 

Ovulation was induced by intraperitoneal injection of pregnant mare serum gonadotropin (PMSG; 122 

5 IU for 2 -month -old mice and 10 IU for 12 -month -old mice; ProspecBio) followed by human 123 

chorionic gonadotropin (hCG; 5 IU for 2 -month -old mice and 10 IU for 12 -month -old mice; 124 

ProspecBio) 46 -48 h later. Mice were sacrificed by cervical dislocation 1 3-14 h post -hCG 125 

injection, and their oviducts were collected by dissection. COCs were released from the oviducts 126 

as an intact clutch by gently tearing the ampulla with an insulin syringe. 127 

Isolation of Cumulus Cells and Oocytes 128 

For experiments where cumulus cells and oocytes were analyzed independently, COCs were 129 

incubated for 2 min in 80 IU/mL of hyaluronidase (Sigma Aldrich) at 37°C to separate the cumulus 130 

cells from the oocytes. After the removal of all oocytes by mouth pipette, the dish, which then 131 

contained only cumulus cells, was flooded with 1.5 mL of PBS and transferred to a microcentrifuge 132 

tube. The cumulus cell suspension was then centrifuged at 500 g for 5 min. Denuded oocytes were 133 

washed three times with HEPES -buffered M2 medium (Sigma Aldrich) supplemented with 0.4% 134 

BSA at 37°C. 135

MitoTracker Green FM and JC -1 Labeling for Fluorescence -activated Mitochondria 136 

Sorting (FAMS) 137 

For intracellular mitochondrial samples, oocytes and cumulus cells were either transferred to 200 138 

μL of ice -cold lysis buffer (10 mM NaCl, 1.5 mM MgCl₂, 10 mM Tris -HCl pH 7.6, 1X Halt 139 

Protease Inhibitor Cocktail in PBS) by a glass capillary tube (individual oocytes) or brought to a 140 

single cell suspension in 200 μL ice -cold lysis buffer (cumulus cells). To lyse the cells, samples 141 

were vortexed for 1 min and centrifuged at 12,000 g for 5 min at 4°C. After removal of supernatant, 142 

all samples were resuspended in PEB buffer [0.5% BSA, 2 mM EDTA in PBS; [13]] and stained 143 

with 100 nM MitoTracker Green (MTG; Invitrogen) and 1 μM JC -1 (Marker Gene Technologies) 144 

for 15 min at room temperature, protected from light. Samples were washed with PEB buffer, 145 

centrifuged at 12,000 g, and resuspended in cold sheath fluid before transferring to polystyrene 146 

FACS tubes for FAMS analysis and sorting. 147 

Calibration of Flow Cytometer for FAMS 148 

All analyses were completed using a special -order research product BD FACS Aria III, fitted with 149 

a PMT detector for forward light scatter (FSC) of a 488 nm laser, allowing for an increased 150 

dynamic range of small particle detection over standard photodiode detectors. To achieve high -151 

resolution detection of subcellular -sized calibration beads (<1 μm), the instrument detection 152

calibration of laser area scaling and delay, a mixture of size calibration beads (Spherotech and Life 154 

Technologies) was run to optimize light scatter voltages for visualization of events between 0.22 155 

and 4 μm. For all analyses, at least 3 × 10⁴ events were analyzed per sample. Data were acquired 156

Determination of Mitochondrial DNA (mtDNA) Copy Number 159 

To determine mtDNA copy number on a per -organelle basis, individual mitochondria were sorted 160 

from oocyte and cumulus cell samples into 96 -well PCR plates containing 1 μL of mitochondrial 161 

lysis buffer (10 mM EDTA, 0.5% SDS, 0.1 mg/mL Proteinase K in qPCR grade water). After the 162 

sorting of one mitochondrion into each well of the 96 -well plate was complete, all wells were 163 

immediately overlaid with mineral oil to prevent evaporation and were incubated at 37°C for 1 h 164 

before being transferred to a -80°C freezer until preparation for single molecule polymerase chain 165 

reaction ( sm PCR). Lysed samples were diluted across 32 PCR wells to prevent inhibitory effects 166 

of mitochondrial lysis buffer on amplification, and to ensure templates were individually 167 

segregated into separate wells. smPCR was performed using Ex Taq DNA Polymerase, Hot Start 168 

Version (Takara) with LA PCR buffer (Takara) to generate 331 base pair amplicons. For primer 169 

information, please see MacDonald et al. 2019 [13]. Following gel electrophoresis, mtDNA copy 170 

number was determined by manually counting the number of bands present in each 32 -well 171 

sample, since the smPCR method has been optimized in such a way that each band represents one 172 

mtDNA copy. 173

Results 186 

Mitochondrial subpopulations are present in both oocytes and cumulus cells 187 

To establish whether mitochondrial subpopulations are present within oocytes and cumulus 188 

cells, FAMS was employed to analyze the size, ΔΨ M, and mtDNA copy number of individual 189 

organelles. Typical size gates for the sorting of mouse oocyte and cumulus cell mitochondria were 190 

drawn to encompass size calibration beads ranging in size from 0.22 to 2 μm (Figure 1), consistent 191 

with transmission electron microscopy -based evaluation of oocyte mitochondrial size range in situ 192 

[13,30]. Mitochondria were identified by size and by positive fluorescence using the 193 

mitochondrial -specific dye MTG. For the MTG gate, an unstained sample was used to establish a 194 

negative population with respect to MTG (FITC) -positive events, and a gate was drawn to exclude 195 

all but up to ~0.5% of autofluorescent events to correct for experimental variation (Figure 2). 196 

Compensation was not applied to samples labeled with JC -1, which measures ΔΨ M, due to the 197 

emission properties of the dye. 198 

Variations in size, ΔΨ M, and mtDNA copy number were identified in both oocytes and 199 

cumulus cells, confirming the presence of distinct mitochondrial subpopulations in both cell types. 200

Discussion 384 

Aging differentially affects distinct mitochondrial subpopulations in oocytes and cumulus 385 

granulosa cells in ways that aggregate approaches cannot detect, and the effects of NMN 386 

supplementation are similarly subpopulation -specific rather than uniform across the mitochondrial 387 

pool. A recurring and conceptually important theme across both cell types is the discordance 388 

between aggregate and subpopulation -level findings. In oocytes, neither mtDNA copy number nor 389 

ΔΨ M differed significantly with age when the mitochondrial population was evaluated as a whole, 390 

which would previously have been interpreted as evidence that oocyte mitochondria are not 391 

meaningfully altered during reproductive aging. Subpopulation analysis revealed this conclusion 392 

to be incomplete: significant and opposing changes in mtDNA copy number were present within 393 

specific mitochondrial subpopulations, and these changes were obscured at the aggregate level 394 

precisely because they did not occur uniformly across the pool. In cumulus cells, significant 395 

differences were detectable at the aggregate level, yet subpopulation analysis further resolved these 396 

changes, demonstrating that the age -associated reduction in mtDNA copy number was driven 397 

predominantly by the ΔΨ M-positive subpopulation and that NMN supplementation acted 398 

differentially across size -defined subpopulations in ways the aggregate data could not capture. The 399 

implication is straightforward: bulk mitochondrial assays, including quantitative PCR -based 400 

mtDNA quantification and population -level membrane potential measurements, report on the 401 

average behavior of a heterogeneous organelle pool and may therefore systematically misrepresent 402 

the biology of specific functionally distinct subpopulations. Resolving individual mitochondrial 403 

subpopulations is not merely a technical refinement but an important consideration for accurately 404 

characterizing how aging affects the mitochondrial landscape of the ovarian follicle. The 405

Within the high ΔΨ M oocyte mitochondrial subpopulation, aged individuals displayed a 408 

significantly elevated mtDNA copy number relative to young individuals, a finding that is at first 409 

glance at odds with whole -cell studies reporting decreased oocyte mtDNA content with age [25 -410 

27]. However, these observations are not necessarily contradictory. It is plausible that a global age -411 

associated reduction in total mitochondrial number drives the whole -cell decline in mtDNA 412 

detected by qPCR -based approaches, while within the surviving organelles, particularly those 413 

maintaining high ΔΨ M, mtDNA copy number is paradoxically elevated. Several non -mutually 414 

exclusive mechanisms could account for this elevation. Compensatory mtDNA replication may 415 

occur within functionally active mitochondria in response to an overall decline in mitochondrial 416 

mass, as has been proposed in other aging contexts [29]. Alternatively, impaired mitophagy 417 

clearance could result in the retention of mtDNA -rich, high ΔΨ M mitochondria that would 418 

otherwise be targeted for degradation. This possibility is of particular interest given recent work 419 

from our laboratory demonstrating that PINK1/Parkin -mediated mitophagy is preferentially 420 

associated with high ΔΨ M mitochondria and that mitophagy -targeted organelles carry significantly 421 

elevated mtDNA content [14]. A third possibility is that age -associated changes in mitochondrial 422 

fission dynamics lead to altered mtDNA distribution across the mitochondrial pool, with small 423 

mitochondria accumulating disproportionate mtDNA as a consequence of asymmetric fission 424 

events. Distinguishing between these mechanisms will require direct assessment of mitophagy flux 425 

and mtDNA replication rates at the single -organelle level and represents an important direction for 426 

future investigation. 427

than entirely independent processes. The convergence of elevated mtDNA across two 434 

independently defined subpopulations therefore strengthens the conclusion that age -associated 435 

mtDNA dysregulation in oocytes is subpopulation -specific and selectively concentrated in the 436 

more metabolically active organelles. 437 

To assess whether aged mitochondrial subpopulations retain the capacity for change, NMN 438 

supplementation was used as a pharmacological probe of mitochondrial plasticity rather than as a 439 

candidate therapeutic intervention. The ability of NMN to alter specific mitochondrial 440 

subpopulations in aged individuals therefore reflects the reversibility of age -associated changes 441 

rather than simply the efficacy of NMN as a fertility treatment. NMN supplementation normalized 442 

mtDNA copy number in both the high ΔΨ M and small oocyte mitochondrial subpopulations toward 443 

young levels, consistent with the broader rejuvenative effects of NMN on oocyte mitochondrial 444 

dynamics reported previously [9,18]. Notably, NMN did not significantly affect the low ΔΨ M or 445 

large mitochondrial subpopulations in the same manner, indicating that its effects are not uniform 446 

across the mitochondrial pool. In large oocyte mitochondria specifically, NMN supplementation 447 

resulted in a mtDNA copy number that was significantly reduced compared to young individuals, 448 

though not significantly different from aged individuals. While this pattern does not fit a 449 

straightforward rejuvenation narrative, it may reflect NMN -driven promotion of mitochondrial 450

primed for mitophagy are not being efficiently cleared. The concurrent reduction in mtDNA copy 508 

number within this subpopulation, which diverges from the elevated mtDNA copy number 509

aged CC mitochondria exhibit a disrupted relationship between membrane potential, mtDNA 511 

maintenance, and mitophagy. This dissociation between membrane potential and mtDNA copy 512 

number in aged CCs is particularly notable when compared to the oocyte findings, where elevated 513 

ΔΨ M and elevated mtDNA copy number co -occurred within the same subpopulation. That the two 514 

cell types show divergent subpopulation -level responses to aging despite being metabolically 515 

coupled suggests that the mechanisms driving mitochondrial dysfunction with age may be cell 516 

type -specific, even within the same follicular microenvironment. The partial restoration of both 517

Conclusion s523 

Aging differentially affects distinct mitochondrial subpopulations in oocytes and cumulus 524 

granulosa cells in ways that are not detectable when mitochondria are assessed in aggregate, 525 

demonstrating that subpopulation -level analysis is necessary for an accurate characterization of 526 

mitochondrial dynamics during reproductive aging. The divergent subpopulation -level responses 527 

to aging observed between oocytes and cumulus cells, despite their metabolic interdependence, 528 

suggest that the mechanisms driving mitochondrial dysfunction with age are cell type -specific 529 

even within the same follicular microenvironment. The plasticity of aged mitochondria 530 

demonstrated here, particularly their capacity to respond selectively to NAD+ repletion, supports 531 

the hypothesis that age -associated mitochondrial dysfunction in the ovarian follicle is driven by 532 

the cellular environment rather than by irreversible intrinsic changes to the organelles themselves. 533 

Future studies directly assessing mitophagy flux, mtDNA replication rates, and fission and fusion 534 

dynamics within individual mitochondrial subpopulations will be essential to fully characterize the 535 

mechanisms identified here, and to determine whether targeted modulation of specific 536 

subpopulations represents a viable strategy for preserving or restoring fertility in aged individuals. 537
