Abstract 24 

Understanding the functions of maternal effect genes during oocyte growth is essential for elucidating 25 

the mechanisms of oogenesis and early embryonic development. However, conventional gene knockout 26 

and conditional knockout approaches require extensive breeding and are time-consuming. Here, we 27 

present a rapid in vitro gene functional analysis system that combines microinjection of mRNA, siRNA 28 

and plasmid DNA into mouse secondary follicles with a two-step oocyte growth culture system. Mouse 29 

secondary follicles were subjected to microinjection of mCherry mRNA and subsequently cultured for 30 

15 days to produce fully grown oocytes. mCherry fluorescence persisted throughout the oocyte growth 31 

period but declined rapidly after fertilization. Despite minor cellular damage occasionally caused by 32 

microinjection, injected follicles developed normally and retained developmental competence. To 33 

evaluate the efficiency of gene suppression, we introduced siRNA targeting Dnmt3l , which is abundantly 34 

expressed during oocyte growth phase. Although Dnmt3l deficiency is known not to affect oocyte 35 

growth, we observed that oocyte growth was maintained normally despite a marked reduction in 36 

endogenous Dnmt3l mRNA levels in our knockdown model. These results demonstrate that this method 37 

enables efficient manipulation of gene expression specifically during oocyte growth while preserving 38 

developmental competence, providing a versatile platform for rapid functional screening of maternal 39 

effect genes in vitro .40

Introduction 42 

In mammals, dormant oocytes in primordial follicles are maintained throughout reproductive age and 43 

parts of them are activated and grown to ovulate in response to endogenous sex hormone. During the 44 

growth phase, oocytes undergo various events including follicle growth, acquirement of epigenetic 45 

modification, and maternal factor accumulation [1-5]. In mice, gene knockout (KO) and germline-46 

specific conditional knockout (CKO) strategies are commonly used to study gene functions involved in 47 

these processes [6-8]. However, there has been an issue that generation and maintenance of KO/CKO 48 

mice is time-consuming and needed multi-generational breedings. 49 

To address this, we previously developed a novel oocyte-specific conditional gene knockdown (KD) 50 

system [9]. The CRISPR-mediated targeted insertion of artificial microRNA sequences into the intronic 51 

region of zona pellucida glycoprotein 3 ( Zp3 ) gene enabled the generation of transgenic mice with an 52 

oocyte-specific KD effect, allowing us to analyze the functions of maternal effect genes more efficiently 53 

than with the traditional methods. Exogenous microRNA precursors are driven under control of Zp3 54 

transcription and processed into mature forms in oocytes entering to the growth phase, where they 55 

silence target genes complementary to artificial microRNA sequences. 56 

In addition to the in vivo model, we also developed an in vitro approach involving low-invasive 57 

mRNA transfer to fully grown oocytes at the germinal vesicle stage [10]. Enclosed cumulus-oocyte 58 

complexes (COCs) were electroporated with mRNA and/or small interfering RNA (siRNA), then 59

matured and fertilized, retaining developmental potential to the blastocyst stage. To disrupt maternal 60 

effect genes in vitro , it is necessary to suppress gene expression at earlier stages of oocyte growth, since 61 

siRNA introduction to fully grown oocytes are not able to degrade accumulated maternally translated 62 

proteins. Another in vivo electroporation method applied to whole ovaries does not deliver exogenous 63 

DNA into the growing oocytes but rather targets the oocyte-surrounding granulosa cells [11]. 64 

Other research groups also reported that microinjection of mRNA and/or siRNA directly into 65 

immature growing oocytes in secondary follicles and further simple follicle culture facilitated the 66 

understanding of maternal effect gene functions during oocyte growth [12-14]. However, it has been 67 

unclear whether their follicle culture systems could produce fertile oocytes developing to term. 68 

We previously established a robust in vitro oocyte growth (IVG) culture system using growing 69 

oocytes in the secondary follicles to produce fertile oocytes. [15-17]. In this study, we demonstrate a 70 

novel in vitro gene screening method which is consisted of mRNA and siRNA microinjection into the 71 

mouse secondary follicles and IVG. This technique facilitates time-efficient functional analysis of 72 

oocyte-associated genes while preserving developmental competence, all within a 16-day culture period. 73

cytoplasmic mRNA microinjection is more straightforward and less invasive than the nuclear injection. 88 

Consequently, we adopt cytoplasmic microinjection for all subsequent experiments. 89

preserving the developmental competence of oocytes. Nevertheless, because the current data are 108 

preliminary, further studies will be required to rigorously assess the extent of cellular damage associated 109 

with the microinjection procedure. 110

Next, we suppressed DNA methyltransferase 3-like ( Dnmt3l ) gene to evaluate whether the KD 111 

efficiency was sufficient to silence a highly expressed endogenous gene. Dnmt3l is expressed in oocytes 112 

during oocyte growth phase as well as in several embryonic stages and interacts with other DNA 113 

methyltransferases to facilitate de novo DNA methylation [22, 23]. Maternal Dnmt3l KO oocytes 114 

normally grow to the fully grown stage and can be fertilized; however, the resulting embryos exhibit 115 

lethality during mid-gestation accompanied by global loss of DNA methylation across the genomes, 116 

including differentially methylated regions of maternal imprinted genes. Cultured oocytes microinjected 117 

with siRNAs targeting Dnmt3l together with mCherry mRNA grew normally to the fully grown stage 118 

and exhibited mCherry fluorescence throughout the oocyte growth period ( Fig. 3A–B ). Only mCherry-119 

positive oocytes were collected under a fluorescence microscope and analyzed as Dnmt3l KD oocytes. 120 

A key advantage of this method is that co-injecting fluorescent reporter mRNA with siRNA allows for 121 

the selective exclusion of oocytes that failed to be injected properly due to technical errors or insufficient 122 

volume of the mixture. This screening process ensures that only successfully and adequately 123 

microinjected oocytes are used for subsequent analysis, thereby enhancing the reliability of the 124 

knockdown experiments. Most importantly, endogenous Dnmt3l mRNA expression was markedly 125 

reduced in Dnmt3l KD oocytes ( Fig. 3C ; siNegative = 100% vs. si Dnmt3l = 5.5%). These results 126 

indicate that this method efficiently suppressed endogenous gene expressions throughout the oocyte 127

In the CKO strategy using mice using mice expressing Cre recombinase specifically in female germ 129 

cells, the target gene is irreversibly disrupted during oogenesis, meaning its function cannot be restored 130 

after fertilization. In contrast, our strategy is designed to inhibit gene function specifically during the 131 

oocyte growth phase. Since the introduced siRNAs are gradually diluted and/or degraded through 132 

subsequent embryo development and cell proliferation, the target gene expression is expected to recover 133 

to normal levels from the intact genome. 134 

The present study introduces another approach that enable rapid in vitro evaluation of maternal effect 135 

gene function without interfering with early embryonic development, providing a significant advantage 136 

for functional analysis of maternal factor within a 16-day culture period. Furthermore, this system 137 

facilitates the analysis of combinatorial gene functions through the introduction of multiple siRNAs into 138 

growing oocytes, making it a versatile platform for studying not only oogenesis but also fertilization as 139 

well in vitro .140
