Abstract 14 Enteroendocrine (EE) cells in the Drosophila gut produce and release multiple factors, 15 including Allatostatin A (AstA), Allatostatin C (AstC), neuropeptide F (NPF), tachykinin 16 (Tk), Diuretic hormone 31 (Dh31), Bursicon, CCHamide 1 (CCHa1) and CCHamide 2 17 (CCHa2), and short neuropeptide F (sNPF). Collectively, these peptides ensure that 18 physiology (e.g., fat storage, fluid balance) and behavior (e.g., feeding, sleep) are 19 coordinated with environmental factors such as nutrient quantity and quality. Despite 20 notable sex differences in physiology and behavior, it remains unclear whether the 21 regulation and function of these EE cell-derived factors is shared between males and 22 females. Given that recent data identified sex-biased physiological effects of two EE 23 cell-derived hormones on Drosophila food intake and energy mobilization, we performed 24 a detailed characterization of these hormones in male and female flies. Despite an 25 overall male bias in mRNA levels of AstA , AstC , Tk , NPF , Dh31 in whole-body and head 26 samples, we observed a strong female bias in mRNA levels of AstC , Tk , and NPF in the 27 gut. To determine whether this sex-biased regulation was physiologically significant, we 28 monitored triglyceride levels in flies with gut-specific loss of EE cell-derived hormones. 29 In 5-day-old flies, loss of either EE cell-derived AstC or Tk reduced fat storage in 30 females with no effect in males. These female-specific effects on fat storage were 31 reproduced in flies with neuron-specific loss of the AstC (AstC-R2) and Tk receptors 32 (TkR99D). Together, these data uncover strongly sex-biased regulation of EE cell-33 derived hormones, and show that gut-specific loss of two of these hormones had a 34 female-specific effect on body fat. 35

1. Introduction 43 In Drosophila , females store more fat than males [1–7]. Greater female fat storage has 44 been observed in both mated and unmated females compared with age-matched males 45 [1,3–7]. In flies, as in other animals, triglyceride is the main form of stored fat. Although 46 triglyceride is present in many cell types and organs ( e.g. , oenocytes, glia, neurons, gut) 47 [5,6,8–14], the majority of triglyceride is stored in an organ called the fat body [3,12,15]. 48 In females, high levels of triglyceride in the fat body play a key role in supporting 49 reproduction and physiology [5,16]. Indeed, triglyceride from the fat body is a key 50 energy source for the developing embryo [17]. Increased fat storage in adult females 51 also supports prolonged survival during nutrient deprivation compared with males [4,6]. 52 Despite these clear benefits of greater fat storage for female fertility, excess fat 53 accumulation in males adversely affects their reproductive output. Specifically, males 54 carrying a mutation that promotes excess triglyceride accumulation show reduced testis 55 size, defects consistent with delays in spermatogenesis, and ultimately a reduction in 56 sperm number [8]. The sex difference in fat storage therefore likely reflects the fact that 57 males and females differ in how much whole-body triglyceride storage supports optimal 58 fertility. 59 Recent studies have identified genes and pathways that contribute to sex 60 differences in fat storage [5–7,18]. In mated females, the steroid hormone ecdysone 61 acts on neurons to promote food intake, which is associated with increased body fat [5]. 62 In unmated adult females, the insulin/insulin-like growth factor signaling pathway (IIS) 63 plays a key role in maintaining an elevated level of triglyceride storage compared with 64 adult males [18]. While the specific cell type targeted by these EE cell-derived hormones to 122 influence fat storage remains unclear, this reveals a female-specific contribution of EE 123 cell-derived hormones in regulating body fat. 124 125

3. DISCUSSION 251 EE cell-derived hormones regulate body fat in single- and mixed-sex animal groups; 252 however, it has been unclear whether the regulation and function of these peptides 253 differ between the sexes. The goal of our study was to perform a detailed comparison of 254 EE cell-derived hormones between the sexes, and to test if these hormones contribute 255 to the sex difference in fat storage. Our assessment revealed profound female-biased 256 expression of EE cell-expressed hormones within the gut. This differential expression 257 was physiologically significant, as we showed that EE cell-derived Tk and AstC promote 258 female fat storage. Interestingly, these effects were not mediated by the IPC or APC, 259 cells that we have previously shown contribute to the sex difference in fat storage. 260 Taken together, our data provide additional insight into the highly complex 261 mechanism(s) by which unmated female flies achieve higher fat storage than male flies 262 (Figure 5). 263 While it was not the main goal of our study, our survey of EE cell-expressed 264 hormones in Drosophila revealed that the sex bias in expression was not uniform across 265 tissues. In the gut, mRNA levels of AstC , Tk , and NPF were higher in females than in 266 males. In the brain, mRNA levels of these hormones showed a significant male bias, in 267 line with data from previous reports on Tk [57] and NPF [64]. While it remains unclear 268 whether the tissue-specific sex bias in expression is physiologically significant, peptides 269 derived from the gut and the brain have been shown to mediate distinct effects on 270

Figure 5 – Model of sex differences in fat storage. 1058 Schematic representation of complex mechanisms that promote higher fat storage in 1059 

Drosophila females. Profound female-biased EE cell-derived Tk and AstC promote 1060 female fat storage, an effect mediated by neurons but independent of the IPC or APC. 1061 EE cell-derived factors therefore act alongside a greater insulin/insulin-like growth factor 1062 signaling pathway activity, lower Adipokinetic hormone (Akh) signaling, and lower 1063 expression of triglyceride lipase brummer (bmm ) to specify higher adiposity in female 1064 flies. In males, we detected no contribution of gut-derived factors to fat storage, which 1065 we previously showed is kept at a lower level than in females by higher bmm expression 1066 and Akh signaling. Thus, multiple complex mechanism specify higher fat storage in 1067 unmated female flies compared with males.
