2Wei et al., Vgll2 and Tead1 govern hypothalamic sleep neurons SUMMARY 38 The hypothalamic hypocretin (Hcrt; aka orexin) neuropeptide neurons are crucial for 39 wakefulness and their malfunction results in narcolepsy; a devastating disorder without current 40 cures. Transplantation of mouse Hcrt cells into mouse narcolepsy models is pointing to cell 41 therapy as a potential treatment for narcolepsy. However, progress in this area requires decoding 42 the developmental pathways generating human HCRT neurons and a deeper understanding of 43 their diversity and physiology. Here, we identify the hypothalamic progenitor domain generating 44 mouse Hcrt neurons and propose a conserved mouse/human genetic cascade driving Hcrt 45 specification. This cascade involves the Vgll2 and Tead1 transcription factor partners, which we 46 find are crucial for mouse Hcrt neuron generation. Conversely, co-misexpression of VGLL2 and 47 TEAD1 in human stem cell derived hypothalamic organoids can induce HCRT neuron 48 differentiation. These findings provide an important element for the development of human 49 induced HCRT neurons for physiological studies and for cell therapy in narcolepsy. 50 51

3Wei et al., Vgll2 and Tead1 govern hypothalamic sleep neurons INTRODUCTION 52 The hypothalamus is a key regulator of many bodily functions, including energy and fluid 53 balance, thermoregulation, sleep-wake states, stress responses, growth and reproduction. 1 The 54 pervasive homeostatic role of the hypothalamus relies upon its staggering neuronal diversity, 55 with extensive neuropeptide expression, intricate neurotransmitter/neuropeptide co-expression, 56 widespread connectome and elaborate neuroendocrine secretion. 2 Unsurprisingly, single 57 cell/nuclei and spatial transcriptomic analyses has uncovered hundreds of transcriptionally 58 distinct cell types in the mouse, macaque and human hypothalamus, pointing to, arguably, the 59 greatest cell diversity per volume in the mammalian body. 3-38 While substantial progress has 60 been made regarding the spatio-temporal patterning of the developing hypothalamus, 2,3,31,39-48 the 61 subsequent genetic pathways dictating the terminal cell identity of the hundreds of identified cell 62 types remain poorly understood. 63 Viewed from anterior to posterior, the hypothalamus contains four main regions: 64 preoptic, anterior, tuberal and mammillary, with each region further subdivided into several 65 nuclei. 2,46 The Hypocretin (Hcrt; aka Orexin) neuropeptide neurons are chiefly located in the 66 tuberal region and the lateral hypothalamus nucleus (LH). 49,50 Hcrt neurons are crucial for 67 maintaining wakefulness, but are also involved in regulating feeding, emotional behaviour and 68 reward seeking. 51-53 Studies mainly in rodents have revealed that the activity of Hcrt neurons is 69 controlled by a number of other neurotransmitters and circulatory factors, 54 but the relevance of 70 these findings to human HCRT neurons is less clear. Early single cell RNA sequencing (sc-71 RNA-seq) analysis pointed to gene expression and putative lineage relatedness between mouse 72 Hcrt neurons and another dorso-tuberal neuropeptide cell group, the Npvf neurons, 17 involved in 73 gating fertility 55,56 and circadian motor activity. 57 Recent sn/sc-RNA-seq studies, involving 74 orders of magnitude more cells, have confirmed the notion of relatedness between Hcrt and Npvf 75 neurons and placed them within a distinct cluster of glutamatergic neurons, denoted C66-76 18:Rfx4.GLU-4, identified in adult mouse, 5 or excitatory neuron cluster 21 (EN21), identified 77 during developmental stages in mouse, macaque and human. 30 We will chiefly refer to this 78 cluster by its developmental definition, EN21. 79 Hcrt or Hcrt receptor knockout, as well as Hcrt cell loss, by genetic manipulation or 80 autoimmunity, results in narcolepsy in mice, dogs and humans. 58-64 In humans, narcolepsy is a 81 devastating disease without current cure, affecting ~1/2,000 individuals. 65 Transplantation of 82 purified mouse Hcrt-GFP neurons into Hcrt cell-deficient mice resulted in partial rescue of 83 narcoleptic phenotypes, 66,67 providing proof-of-principle for the development of cell-based 84 therapies for human narcolepsy. 68 Human induced pluripotent stem cells (hiPSCs) can be driven 85 into hypothalamic-like 2D and 3D (organoid) differentiation. 69 These cultures can contain HCRT 86 neurons, but as anticipated from their scarcity in the hypothalamus, in low proportions. 70 The 87 generation of hiPSC-derived HCRT neurons sets the stage for cell transplantation development, 88 pre-clinically and subsequently clinically, but requires a deeper understanding of HCRT cell 89 specification to more readily generate large numbers of these neurons. 90

Three transcription factors (TFs) have been genetically demonstrated to be important for 91 mouse Hcrt neuron generation: Ebf2, Dbx1 and Lhx9. 8,71-73 However, none of these three mouse 92 mutants show a complete loss of Hcrt cells. Moreover, gene expression analysis has not 93 identified Dbx1 or Ebf2 expression in mature mouse or human Hcrt neurons, 26,29,74 pointing to 94 that their effect on Hcrt neurons may be a consequent of patterning or lineage defects in the 95 developing hypothalamus. By contrast, Lhx9 is expressed by mature Hcrt neurons, 26,29 and 96 ectopic Lhx9 expression in the developing mouse hypothalamus can trigger extra Hcrt cells. 72 97 However, because terminal cell fates is often controlled by combinatorial codes of TFs, 75 98 additional TFs are likely involved in the specification of Hcrt neurons. The Vestigial like TF co-99 factor gene Vgll2 is expressed in the developing mid-tuberal mouse hypothalamus, generating 100 the ventro-medial hypothalamus (VMH), 12 but is also selectively expressed in the dorso-medial 101 (DMH) and LH regions, specifically in adult Hcrt and Npvf neurons. 7,8 VGLL factors 102 (Vgll1/2/3/4 in mammals) physically and genetically act with TEAD TFs (Tead1/2/3/4 in 103 mammals). 76,77 Gene expression analysis 19 pointed to Tead1 as the most likely Vgll2 partner in 104 Hcrt and Npvf neurons, suggesting that the Vgll2-Tead1 pair could play important roles in Hcrt 105 and Npvf cell specification. 106 To address the role of Vgll2 and Tead1 in Hcrt and Npvf cell specification, we analysed 107 their expression profiles in the developing mouse hypothalamus, using a combination of newly 108 generated and publicly available expression data. Integrating Vgll2 and Tead1 expression with a 109 number of hypothalamic patterning TFs resulted in the identification of a specific progenitor 110 domain in the developing dorso-tuberal hypothalamus, proposed as pT1-1 (progenitor Tuberal 1-111 1), and a putative genetic pathway towards the EN21/Hcrt/Npvf neurons. This pathway 112 incorporated the known Hcrt determinants Dbx1 and Lhx9 and proposed Vgll2 and Tead1 as 113 additional determinants. To functionally test this notion, we generated CNS-conditional mouse 114 mutants for Vgll2 and Tead1 (Vgll2 cKO and Tead1 cKO ). We observed a complete/near-complete 115 loss of both Hcrt and Npvf neurons in both mutants, evident by immunostaining and sn-RNA-seq 116 analysis. Vgll2 cKO/+ ;Tead1 cKO/+ trans-heterozygotes show significantly reduced Hcrt and Npvf 117 cell numbers, when compared to either heterozygote, indicating combinatorial involvement. 118 Neither mutant displayed altered gene expression of Ebf2 , Dbx1 and Lhx9 , nor loss of the EN21 119 neuron cluster, indicating that Vgll2 and Tead1 act in parallel to and/or downstream of these 120 regulators during Hcrt/Npvf cell specification. We analysed gene expression for the proposed 121 pT1-1/EN21/Hcrt/Npvf pathway in published human sn/sc-RNA-seq datasets, which suggested a 122 similar pathway to that observed in mouse. To begin testing this notion, we addressed the 123 sufficiency of VGLL2 and TEAD1 in human cells, by misexpressing them in human induced 124 pluripotent stem cell (hiPSC) derived hypothalamic organoids. We find that VGLL2 -TEAD1 co-125 misexpression could induce HCRT neurons (iHCRT), which displayed transcriptional profiles 126 related to several human HCRT subtypes. These findings place Hcrt neuron specification within 127 a conserved spatio-temporal developmental framework within the dorso-tuberal hypothalamus, 128 paving the way for hiPSC-derived iHCRT neurons for physiological studies and for the 129 development of cell therapy in narcolepsy. 130 131

Vgll2 and Tead1 mouse mutants display loss of Hcrt and Npvf expression 215 To probe the role of Vgll2 and Tead1 during hypothalamus development we knocked them out in 216 the mouse. While constitutive Vgll2 mutants survive until adulthood, 84 constitutive Tead1 217 mutants die at E12.5. 85 Therefore, we knocked out Vgll2 and Tead1 conditionally in the CNS, by 218 crossing floxed alleles generated by EUCOMM 86 to Sox1-Cre ; an early, pan-CNS deleter 219 strain. 87 Conditional Vgll2 (Vgll2 cKO ) and Tead1 (Tead1 cKO ) both survived postnatally. Neither 220 

Vgll2 cKO nor Tead1 cKO mutants displayed apparent gross brain anatomical abnormalities during 221 developmental or early postnatal stages, but hydrocephaly was apparent in Tead1 cKO at P20. 222 To uncover effects upon terminal differentiation we first analysed postnatal stage P20 by 223 immunostaining. In Vgll2 cKO we observed a 78% loss of Hcrt neurons and a complete loss of 224 Npvf neurons, while Tead1 cKO showed a complete loss of both Hcrt and Npvf neurons (Figure 225 2A-J, Table S1). To determine if Hcrt and Npvf neurons were generated during embryogenesis 226 but lost, due to cell death or de-differentiation, we analysed P0 and E14.5–the earliest stage of 227 robust Hcrt and Npvf immunostaining. At P0, the phenotypes were similar to P20, while at E14.5 228 the effects were even stronger, with a complete absence of both Hcrt and Npvf neurons in both 229 mutants (Figure 2K-T, S6A-L). 230 Because of the well-known genetic and molecular interaction between VGLL and TEAD 231 factors, 76,77 and the overlap in expression and function between Vgll2 and Tead1 in the 232 hypothalamus identified herein, we decided to probe for possible genetic interaction. Tead1 cKO/+ heterozygotes at P20 and observed that while Vgll2 cKO/+ heterozygotes did not display 235 loss of Hcrt or Npvf, Tead1 cKO/+ heterozygotes showed a reduction of both neuronal cell types 236 (Figure S7A-F). The pT1-1 progenitor domain 378 Previous studies, using in situ hybridization, identified the region generating mouse Hcrt neurons 379 as located in the dorsal-most Nkx2-1 region in the developing tuberal hypothalamus, in an 380 anterior-posterior stripe of Lhx9 expression and dorsally bordered by Lhx6 , Lhx8 and Arx . 18 381 Building upon this mapping we analysed gene expression in the tuberal hypothalamus, using a 382 number of different gene expression data sets and immunostaining, and integrated Vgll2 and 383 

Tead1 into a spatio-temporal framework for dorso-tuberal development. Building upon recent 384 progenitor definitions, which identified nine progenitor domains in the developing 385 hypothalamus, including two for the tuberal hypothalamus, pT1 and pT2, we propose a sub-386 division of the pT1 domain into pT1-1 (dorsal-most) and pT1-2 (intermediate), with pT1-1 being 387 

Nkx2-2 + /Six6 - and pT1-2 being Nkx2-2 - /Six6 + /Tbx3 - , and with pT2 ventral-most and Tbx3 +388 (Figure 1M). Given the immense cellular complexity of the hypothalamus, with hundreds of 389 transcriptionally distinct differentiated cell subtypes, further sub-division of the nine progenitor 390 domains is arguably anticipated. We propose that the pT1-1 progenitor domain generates the 391 EN21/Hcrt/Npvf glutamatergic neuron cluster, which contributes neurons into the two mature 392 dorso-tuberal nuclei, the DMH and LH. iHCRT neurons for human physiology studies and cell therapy 500 There are currently 115 ongoing regulatory approved clinical trials involving hiPSC cell therapy, 501 20 out of which involve cell transplantation into the human CNS. 103 While these studies are still 502 ongoing, recent updates on the progress of e.g., hiPSC-derived dopamine cells into Parkinson’s 503 Disease (PD) patients are encouraging. 104,105 However, the case of cell transplantation into PD 504 illustrates the challenges emerging from the cellular complexity in the CNS. Specifically, several 505 different subtypes of midbrain dopaminergic neurons have been identified in vivo , with some 506 subtypes apparently more affected than others in PD. 106 It is unclear if this in vivo and disease 507 complexity will constitute a future hurdle in cell therapy treatments. 508 Similar issues may be important to consider regarding cell therapy for narcolepsy. 509 Previous studies have pointed to different HCRT subtypes, 90-93 and our sn/sc-RNA-seq meta-510 analysis herein identified 12 transcriptionally distinct HCRT sub-clusters in humans. These 511 findings indicate that the different Hcrt neuron subtypes evident from previous functional studies 512 are underpinned by distinct gene expression profiles. We speculate that one main driver of this 513 sub-clustering may relate to axon/dendrite projections, as recent studies in mouse reveal that 514 different subsets of Hcrt neurons project to different CNS regions, identifying four major 515 projection subtypes. 92 Indeed, our GO analysis of the DEGs defining the 12 different HCRT cell 516 clusters revealed many GO terms involving axon pathfinding and synapse formation, hence 517 lending some support for this notion. We find that VGLL2-TEAD1 co-misexpression can 518 generate iHCRT neurons in HypoOrgs, but that these do not overlap fully with all the in vivo 519 sub-clusters. However, how/if the sn/sc-RNA-seq clusters directly correspond to different in vivo 520 defined subtypes, as defined by e.g., pharmacology, electrophysiology and connectome, is 521 currently difficult to assess. 522 HCRT immunostaining and in situ hybridisation studies of narcoleptic individuals have 523 indicated that symptoms begin to occur at an extensive, but partial, loss of HCRT neurons. 90 524 However, it is not clear if the HCRT cells lost belong to a particular subtype(s). Previous studies 525 involved transplanting Hcrt-GFP cells from one mouse strain into Hcrt cell-ablated mouse 526 hosts. 66,67 These cell injections were made into one of the key target areas of Hcrt projections, the 527 Dorsal Raphe nucleus, and resulted in some alleviation of narcoleptic symptoms, specifically 528 cataplexy. However, these studies presumably involved mixtures of Hcrt subtypes. The 529 combination of further analysis of human HCRT subtypes, including the potential selective 530 subtype loss in narcoleptic patients, improved methods for generating iHCRT neurons, a deeper 531 understanding of the physiology of human HCRT neurons, and comprehensive testing of cell 532 injection target regions will likely all be necessary for the development of cell therapy for 533 narcolepsy. 534
