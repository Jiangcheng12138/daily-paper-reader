Summary 23 

Excessive daytime sleepiness (EDS) is a heterogeneous phenotype with little known of its 24 

genetic basis. Large-scale genome-wide association studies (GWAS) have reported genomic 25 

loci associated with EDS, though since most of these are non-coding, the causal gene(s) 26 

underlying the association are not known. Additionally, the cell types in which these genes exert 27 

their effects on sleep have not been functionally explored in vivo . Here, we employed a 28 

chromatin-based variant-to-gene mapping approach to first implicate candidate effector genes 29 

at EDS GWAS loci in human-derived neural and glial cell lines. Subsequent cell type-specific 30 

RNAi knockdown of orthologous genes using neural and glial GAL4 drivers in Drosophila 31 

confirmed cell-specific regulation of sleep by these GWAS-implicated effector genes. Among 32 

these, ruby (ortholog to AP3B2 ), a component of the AP-3 vesicular trafficking complex 33 

emerged as a robust sleep regulator. Targeted knockdown in flies localized ruby function to 34 

astrocyte-like glia, where loss of ruby increased sleep duration. The conserved role of ruby/ 35 

ap3b2 was validated in zebrafish where CRISPR-mediated loss increased daytime sleep. 36 

Together, these findings show that physical variant-to-gene mapping predicted cell-type– 37 

specific gene function for complex sleep traits and revealed ruby/AP3B2 as a conserved glial 38 

regulator of sleep and arousal. This work provides a generalizable framework for connecting 39 

non-coding GWAS variants and their corresponding effector genes to identify novel and highly 40 

conserved regulators of sleep. 41

Introduction 43 

Dysregulation of sleep duration, timing, and quality is associated with increased risk for negative 44 

health outcomes including cardiometabolic disease and mortality [1,2]. Given a strong heritable 45 

component [3], there is an unmet need to identify the causal genetic and molecular drivers of 46 

poor sleep to improve overall health. Significant efforts have been made to define the underlying 47 

genetic drivers of sleep disorders, including insomnia, sleep apnea, and excessive daytime 48 

sleepiness (EDS) through genome wide association studies (GWAS) in large population 49 

cohorts[3-10]. However, few candidate genes identified through GWAS have been functionally 50 

validated, and the genetic and cellular bases of sleep disorders remain poorly understood. 51 

52 

While the field of sleep genetics has primarily focused on insomnia, EDS is also associated with 53 

negative health outcomes including poor metabolic and cognitive health [11,12]. Relative to 54 

other sleep disorders, EDS has received little attention given it is widely considered a55 

heterogeneous symptom rather than a single disorder. Previous GWAS efforts showed that 56 

defined broadly EDS can be genetically subdivided into two biologically distinct subtypes, sleep 57 

propensity and sleep fragmentation [3]. While the genetic associations for each of these 58 

biological subtypes of EDS point to distinct underlying mechanisms, these observations have 59 

not been functionally validated. Mechanistic characterization of these loci should reveal causal 60 

effector genes contributing to EDS and in turn improve our understanding of the etiology. 61 

62 

Many GWAS efforts have reported genetic loci associated with sleep regulation. However, 63 

defining the underlying causal effector genes at these loci remains a central challenge for the 64 

field [13]. Given the complex genetic architecture regulating sleep and its polygenic nature [14], 65 

mechanistic testing of multiple candidate genes in animal models can be costly and challenging. 66 

Further complicating interpretation, more than 90% of GWAS loci reside in non-coding regions 67 

of the genome [13,15]. Consequently, genes selected for functional validation using the 68

Because the majority of GWAS signals reside in non-coding regions, many variants localize to 71

cellular contexts[13,22]. Cell-specific chromatin-based variant-to-gene mapping provides a75

apply chromatin capture techniques across five neural and glial cell types to identify three-78

candidate effector genes, thereby nominating gene regulatory interactions that contribute to the 80 

heritability of EDS. 81 

To functionally validate genes implicated in this variant-to-gene mapping from EDS GWAS, we 82 

employed a cross-species screening approach. Non-mammalian genetic model organisms have 83 

provided critical insight into the genetic regulation of sleep [27,28]. Both Drosophila 84 

melanogaster (fruit fly) and Danio rerio (zebrafish) exhibit the canonical behavioral hallmarks of 85 

sleep, and genetic perturbation studies in these systems demonstrate that core sleep-regulatory 86 

mechanisms are highly conserved with those of mammals [27,28]. While neuronal genes have 87 

been the principal focus of sleep regulation, there is growing evidence that multiple glial cell 88 

types also play critical and conserved roles in sleep and circadian control [29-35]. 89 

Consequently, mechanistic insight into the coordinated contributions of sleep-regulating neurons 90 

and glia is essential for understanding the etiology of human sleep disorders. Informed by our 91 

cell-specific variant-to-gene mapping, we performed cell-type-specific RNAi knockdown in 92 

Drosophila , revealing unique neural and glial roles for each human GWAS-implicated gene. We 93

Results 97 

3D genomic mapping links sleep-associated variants to distal target genes 98 

To implicate effector genes at EDS GWAS loci, we leveraged sentinel signals from a previous 99 

GWAS report for self-reported daytime sleepiness [3] ( Fig. 1A ). This previous GWAS effort 100 

clustered variants into two biological subtypes of EDS using accelerometry data, one marked by 101 

high sleep propensity (e.g. hypersomnia) and one defined by sleep fragmentation. Given EDS is 102 

a consequence of poor sleep quality and is associated with significant morbidity, we elected to 103 

focus our variant-to-gene efforts on this biological subtype, effectively uncoupling this trait from 104 

the distinct genetic architecture of intrinsic hypersomnia. 105 

106 

From the 27 sentinel signals associated with the EDS-sleep fragmentation cluster [3], we 107 

performed LD expansion for proxy variants ( r2>0.8, details in Methods), then applied variant-to-108 

gene mapping by intersecting variants with cell-type specific cis-regulatory elements (cREs) – 109 

defined as genomic regions exhibiting open chromatin (ATAC-seq peaks) and simultaneously 110 

contacted by a chromatin interaction (Hi-C or Capture-C loops) as previously described [23] 111 

(Fig. 1B, Supplementary Fig. 1, and Supplementary Table 1 ). We performed this analysis 112 

across five different cell types including embryonic stem cell-derived hypothalamic neural 113 

progenitors and mature hypothalamic neurons, resting and activated HMC3 microglia, and 114 

astrocytes. This approach yielded 45 unique candidate effector gene promoters (33 of which are 115 

protein-coding) across all 5 cell types contacted by 35 proxies residing in cREs resulting from 10 116 

lead sentinel signals ( Figure S1 and Supplementary Table 1 ). Mature hypothalamic neurons 117 

yielded the most unique candidate effector genes, with 12 promoter-proxy SNP interactions, 118

Cell-specific Drosophila screen of candidate effector genes 134 

To test candidate genes identified through our variant-to-gene approach for their contribution to 135 

sleep regulation, we performed cell-type-specific knockdown in Drosophila of these genes in 136 

either neurons or glia, guided by variant-to-gene mapping that predicts the relevant cellular 137 

context of action. 138 

139

This resulted in the identification of multiple genes that suppressed or increased sleep in each 143 

cell type. Notably, pan-neuronal knockdown of the ortholog to HPCA , neurocalcinin (nca ), 144

Several genes identified through our variant-to-gene mapping approach were specifically 160 

implicated in neural cells only or glial cells only, while others were common to both. To 161 

determine the cell-specific roles of these genes, we examined sleep phenotypes resulting from 162 

neural-specific knockdown of neural genes followed by glial-specific knockdown of glial genes. 163 

For genes specifically implicated through variant-to-gene mapping in neurons only, nSyb -164 

GAL4>RNAi resulted in increased sleep in 5/7 lines tested ( Fig. 2D and Figure S2 ). For genes 165 

specifically implicated as operating in glia only, repo >RNAi resulted in increased sleep in 4/8 166 

lines ( Fig. 2E and Figure S2 ). Thus, when the human cell type used for identification was 167 

matched to the Drosophila driver (e.g. neural – nSyb or glial – repo ) the resulting phenotype 168 

was excessive sleep at least 50% of the time. 169

To validate cell-type specificity, we next tested the effects of knocking down neuron-specific 171 

drivers in glia and vice versa. When the GAL4 driver was discordant to the cell type in which the 172 

effector gene was implicated, the effects were less robust. When neural genes were knocked 173 

down with repo -GAL4, 1/7 increased sleep, and 1/7 decreased sleep ( Fig. 2F and Figure S2 ). 174 

When glial genes were knocked down with nSyb -GAL4, 1/8 increased sleep ( Fig. 2G and 175 

Figure S2 ). We next tested the seven genes that were common to both neurons and glia using 176 

both drivers. For the genes orb (CPEB1 ) and nca (HPCA ), knockdown in glia yielded an 177 

opposite, but non-significant, phenotype to neuronal knockdown ( Fig. 2H and Figure S2 ), 178 

suggesting that these genes influence sleep in a cell-specific manner. Only with archease 179 

(ZBTB8OS ) was sleep significantly increased with both neuronal and glial knockdown ( Fig. 2H 180 

and Figure S2 ). These results indicate sleep regulators have unique functions in neurons and 181 

glia and support the hypothesis that cell type-specific variant-to-gene mapping enriches for 182 

functional regulation of sleep. 183

selectively affected sleep with glial knockdown. ruby encodes the beta3-adaptin subunit of the 188

UAS-RNAi lines alone ( Fig. 3A-C ). We quantified the total time spent in sleep bouts lasting 194 

more than 25 minutes, in line with what has been reported for identifying a deeper sleep stage 195

Multiple classes of glia, including ensheathing glia, cortex glia, subperineural glia, and astrocyte-205 

like glia have been shown to contribute to sleep regulation [31,32,34]. Using Drosophila glial-206 

specific GAL4-RNAi, we sought to localize the subtype of glia where ruby functions to regulate 207 

sleep. Glial specific knockdown of both RNAi lines targeting ruby in astrocyte-like glia (alrm-208 

GAL4> ruby RNAi ) increased sleep compared to controls harboring the RNAi or driver line alone 209 

(Fig. 3E-H and Figure S3E-F ). There was no difference between selective knockdown in 210 

astrocyte-like glia compared to pan-glia knockdown (repo-GAL4> ruby RNAi) ) (Fig. 3G-H ), 211 

suggesting the primary effect operates through astrocyte-like glia. The increased sleep was also 212 

associated with a reduced arousal threshold, as ruby knockdown in astrocyte-like glia 213 

responded to weaker stimuli in the Drosophila Arousal Tracking (DART) assay compared to 214 

controls ( Fig. 3I-J ), suggesting the longer sleep bout length may actually compensate for lighter 215 

sleep and a lack of restorative effects of sleep. Together, these findings provide evidence that 216 

ruby/AP3B2 functions primarily in astrocyte-like glia to regulate sleep, supporting a novel role for 217 

this gene. 218

Discussion 261 

GWAS have provided an initial resource for causal gene identification; however, a major hurdle 262 

is in correct assignment of underlying causal variants to their corresponding effector genes in 263 

the appropriate cellular context. We performed cell-specific chromatin-based variant-to-gene 264 

mapping for a pathological subtype of excessive sleepiness driven by sleep fragmentation. This 265 

approach, importantly, provides cellular context for putative effector genes implicated at GWAS 266 

loci for this sleep abnormality. 267 

268 

Our data demonstrate there is predictive capacity in variant-to-gene mapping whereby cell-269 

specific chromatin interactions implicate genes in the cellular context in which they are most 270 

likely to influence the trait. Specifically, we found that genes exert their influence on sleep 271 

differently depending on the cellular context. For example, TyrRs (YARS1 ) had opposing 272 

phenotypes when knocked down in neurons compared to glia. While some genes, such as ruby 273 

(AP3B2 ) only significantly influenced sleep in a single cell type. The cell-specific knockdown 274

approach also identified potential novel cellular roles for well-known regulators of sleep. It was 275 

particularly notable that loss of homer in glia produced a significant sleep phenotype, while it 276 

had no effect when knocked down in neurons using nSyb -GAL4. Though the variant-to-gene 277 

mapping implicated HOMER2 on chromosome 15, HOMER1 is well-known for its role in 278 

neuronal synaptic plasticity as it relates to sleep [56-58]. However, this effect is largely driven by 279 

alternative splice isoforms that are not present in Drosophila [59,60]. Neuronal knockdown of 280 

homer using elav-GAL4, which acts in early development [61], has been shown to reduce sleep 281 

duration in flies [62], suggesting that, while this gene only has a single ortholog in Drosophila , it 282 

serves unique roles in different brain cells. Similar to homer , ruby (AP3B2 ), is also known for its 283 

neuronal role [63,64]. AP3B2 plays a role in the formation of clathrin-coated synaptic vesicles 284 

involved in endocytosis [65], though we observed a primary effect with glial knockdown, 285 

suggesting it may be important for glial endocytosis like other AP-complex members [66]. 286 

AP3B2 was considered a neuron-specific AP-Complex subunit; however, single cell expression 287 

data from human [67], fish [68], and flies [69] indicate it is expressed in glia. These results 288 

demonstrate genes that were previously known for their roles in neuronal synaptic regulation, 289 

also serve important functions in glia. 290

While we elected to focus our further analysis on ruby (AP3B2), our validation screen identified 292 

numerous other regulators of sleep. These included previously established sleep and circadian 293

[4,70,71]. In addition, screening identified previously uncharacterized genes with short or long 295 

sleep phenotypes. For example, loss of the ortholog to ZBTB8OS , archease, in neurons or glia 296 

increases sleep. Loss of archease is associated with dysregulation of tRNA splicing and deficits 297 

in rapid protein synthesis [72], two cellular mechanisms that have yet to be implicated in sleep 298 

regulation. However, archease , mediates XBP1 splicing, which has been shown to control sleep 299

Functional validation screens have demonstrated that some GWAS loci harbor multiple genes 303 

that contribute to a trait outcome [17,75,76]. This is likely due to the fact that genomic 304 

organization is not random and genes with shared functions can be clustered together sharing 305 

regulatory elements [77-79]. This was apparent in our Drosophila behavioral screen as multiple 306 

effector genes were contacted through variant-to-gene interactions at both the 1p35.1 and 307 

15q25.2 loci, and ten of the fourteen positive hits were implicated at these two loci. Therefore, 308 

our high hit rate is likely due to variant-to-gene mapping uncovering coordinated gene programs 309 

involved in similar biological processes. For example, archease (ZBTB8OS ) and TyrRs 310 

(YARS1 ), which were implicated at same GWAS locus, are both involved in tRNA processing 311 

and significantly altered sleep following both neural and glial knockdown. 312 

313 

It is also possible that variants within or near a gene may not act on the nearest gene at all. 314 

Through physical variant-to-gene mapping, we identified a variant within an intron of CPEB1 315 

that contacts the promoters of multiple distal genes including AP3B2 . CPEB1 and AP3B2 316 

maintain their syntenic relationship in zebrafish, and chromatin contact data indicate they may 317 

also share a regulatory interaction in zebrafish. However, through functional phenotyping of both 318 

ap3b2 and cpeb1 zebrafish CRISPR mutants, we determined ap3b2 contributes an outsized 319 

effect to the EDS phenotype, while the effect of cpeb1 was not significant. While we cannot rule 320 

out a role for CPEB1 in human sleep regulation, our data from model organisms indicate this is 321 

not the only gene influencing sleep at the locus. In summary, our data indicate the gene 322 

contributing to the observed EDS phenotype is often a gene distal to the implicated risk variant. 323

More work is needed to resolve the underlying genetic mechanisms that contribute to sleep 325 

dysfunction. Here we found multiple genes associated with synaptic signaling and neuronal 326 

excitability alter sleep, including genes whose loss-of-function result in epileptic phenotypes. 327 

There is a known relationship between excessive sleepiness and seizure risk [80-82] suggesting 328 

at least some of the genetic liability to high sleep propensity may arise from the genetic 329 

predisposition to heightened neuronal activity, which may occur through neural or glial 330 

dysfunction [81,82]. Understanding genetic risk for excessive sleep is important for developing 331 

targeted therapies. It is, however, critical that genetic findings from GWAS are validated and 332 

interpreted correctly. While we cannot rule out that some implicated genes impact sleep, our 333 

findings implicate multiple novel genes in EDS. In many cases the gene identified was not the 334 

nearest gene implicated by the given GWAS locus. Therefore, GWAS results should be 335 

interpreted with caution when relying on nearest-gene assumptions. 336 

For the first time, we demonstrate that cell-specific variant-to-gene mapping improves in vivo 337 

cell-type inference for genes implicated at human GWAS loci for sleep traits. Together, these 338 

studies establish a generalizable discovery framework in which GWAS-informed variant-to-gene 339 

mapping, coupled with functional validation in tractable genetic models, can identify genes and 340 

pathways underlying distinct sleep phenotypes. 341

Variant-to-gene mapping 343 

Definition of cis-Regulatory Elements (cREs): We intersected ATAC-seq open chromatin 344 

regions (OCRs) of each cell type with chromatin conformation capture data determined by Hi-345 

C/Capture-C of the same cell type, and with promoters (-1,500/+500bp of TSS) defined by 346 

GENCODE v40. 347 

Genetic loci included in variant-to-genes mapping: we used the 27 significant variants that were 348 

implicated by Wang et. al. 2019 [3] to influence excessive sleepiness via sleep fragmentation. 349 

Proxies for each lead variant were queried using TopLD [83] and LDlinkR tool [84] with the 350 

GRCh38 Genome assembly, 1000 Genomes phase 3 v5 variant set, European population, and 351 

LD threshold of r2≥0.8 (results in 2206 variants). 352 

353 

We mapped variants to genes only when the variant resided within a defined cRE at one anchor 354 

of a chromatin loop and the distal anchor of that same loop intersected a gene promoter. This 355 

approach allowed us to link non-coding variants to distal target genes, prioritizing functional 356 

variant-gene pairs over linear proximity. 357

Sleep and arousal threshold measurements in Drosophila 369 

Flies were acclimated to experimental conditions for at least 24 hours prior to the start of all 370 

behavioral analysis. Measurements of sleep and arousal threshold were then measured over 371 

the course of three days starting at ZT0 using the Drosophila Locomotor Activity Monitor (DAM) 372 

System (Trikinetics, Waltham, MA, USA), as previously described [85-87]. For each individual 373 

fly, the DAM system measures activity by counting the number of infrared beam crossings over 374 

time. These activity data were then used to calculate sleep, defined as bouts of immobility of 5 375 

min or more, using the Drosophila Sleep Counting Macro [88], from which sleep traits were then 376 

extracted. Waking activity was quantified as the average number of beam crossings per waking 377 

minute, as previously described [88]. HMM was calculated from 1 min locomotor bins, as 378 

previously described [44]. P(Wake) was measured as the probability of transition from inactivity 379 

to activity, and P(Doze) as the probability of transition from activity to inactivity in the 380 

subsequent bin. These transition probabilities were used as measures of sleep depth and sleep 381 

pressure, respectively. 382 

383 

Arousal threshold was measured using the DART, as previously described [89]. In brief, 384 

individual female flies were loaded into plastic tubes (Trikinectics, Waltham, Massachusetts) 385 

and placed onto trays containing vibrating motors. Flies were recorded continuously using a 386 

USB webcam (QuickCam Pro 900, Logitech, Lausanne, Switzerland) with a resolution of 800 × 387 

600 at 5 frames/s. The vibrational stimulus, video tracking parameters, and data analysis were 388 

performed using the DART interface developed in MATLAB (The MathWorks Inc., Natick, MA). 389 

To track fly movement, raw video flies were subsampled to 1 frame/s. Fly movement, or a 390 

difference in pixilation from one frame to the next, was detected by subtracting a background 391 

image from the current frame. The background image was generated as the average of 20 392 

randomly selected frames from a given video. Fly activity was measured as movement of 393
