Abstract 41 

Sulfide:quinone oxidoreductase (SQR) is a critical enzyme that maintains sulfur 42 metabolism by oxidizing sulfide to supersulfides, currently defined as sulfur metabolites with six 43 valence electrons and no charge that are covalently catenated with other sulfur atoms and 44 excludes disulfides. While SQR is known to contribute to mitochondrial electron transport, its 45 physiological impact on systemic energy metabolism and longevity remains largely undefined. In 46 this study, we investigated the role of SQR in mitochondrial bioenergetics and aging using SQR-47 deficient Schizosaccharomyces pombe (Δhmt2 ) and a mitochondria-selective SQR-deficient 48 (Sqrdl ΔN/ΔN ) mice model. Functional analysis demonstrated that Δhmt2 grew normally in glucose 49 but not in glycerol, indicating impaired mitochondrial respiration. It showed reduced membrane 50 potential, ATP, and lifespan. Consistent with the yeast findings, Sqrdl ΔN/ΔN mice exhibited 51 accumulated levels of hydrogen sulfide and persulfides, and demonstrated impaired 52 mitochondrial energy metabolism. Furthermore, supersulfide donor supplementation selectively 53 conferred lifespan extension in wild-type yeast, but not in SQR-deficient strain, and similarly 54 improved mitochondrial function exclusively in wild-type mouse embryonic fibroblasts, with no 55 benefit observed in SQR-mutant counterparts. Together, our findings demonstrate that 56 mitochondrial SQR plays an essential role in sulfur respiration, critically supporting mitochondrial 57 function and organismal longevity across eukaryotes. 58 59

1. Introduction 69 

70 

Sulfur metabolism is a fundamental biochemical process that supports cellular signaling, 71 redox homeostasis, detoxification, structural integrity, and energy production [1–3]. It involves the 72 transformation of sulfur-containing compounds such as cystine, methionine, and hydrogen sulfide 73 (H 2S). Among these important sulfur species are supersulfides, which include hydropersulfides 74 (RSSH) and polysulfides (RS nR, n > 2, R represents hydrogen or an alkyl group including cyclized 75 forms). These species have recently emerged as key regulators of redox signaling and cellular 76 functions [4–9]. Importantly, the dysregulation of supersulfides has been implicated in a wide 77 range of diseases, including neurodegenerative and cardiovascular diseases, infectious diseases, 78 skeletal abnormalities, and cancer, highlighting the importance of tightly regulated supersulfide 79 metabolism [7, 10–15]. 80 Sulfide:quinone oxidoreductase (SQR) is a key mitochondrial enzyme that catalyses the 81 first committed step in sulfide oxidation by transferring electrons from H 2S to ubiquinone in the 82 electron transport chain (ETC) [16]. Originally identified in fission yeast as a determinant of 83 cadmium (Cd) sensitivity [17], SQR is highly conserved across eukaryotes, including worms, fruit 84 flies, mice, and humans [18, 19]. In mammalian systems, SQR has been primarily regarded as a 85 detoxification enzyme that protects cells from sulfide toxicity [20–24]. However, most previous 86 studies have relied on exogenous sulfide donors, leaving the physiological roles of SQR in 87 endogenous sulfur metabolism mitochondrial function unknown. Emerging evidence suggests 88 that SQR contributes to mitochondrial homeostasis by not only removing H 2S, but also generating 89 supersulfides intermediates that may participate in cysteine biosynthesis and redox regulation 90 [25]. Nevertheless, the precise role of SQR in mitochondrial bioenergetics remains poorly 91 understood. 92 The fission yeast Schizosaccaromyces pombe provides a unique model for studying SQR-93 dependent sulfur metabolism. Notably, S. pombe lacks key enzymes of the canonical cysteine 94

We and others have shown that reactive persulfides such as cysteine hydropersulfide are 104 primarily produced by cysteinyl-tRNA synthetase (CARS), a highly conserved enzyme now 105 recognized for its moonlighting function as a cysteine persulfide synthase [6, 28]. In eukaryotes, 106 the mitochondrial isoform CARS2 generates the majority of intracellular persulfides. We 107 previously demonstrated that CARS2-dependent persulfide production is required to maintain 108 mitochondrial membrane potential and ATP synthesis. Previous studies have reported that 109 exogenously supplied sulfide increased oxygen consumption and mitochondrial membrane 110 potential [25, 29], which implies that, under a particular condition, eukaryotes including mammals 111 may exploit sulfide-based respiration as an evolutionary legacy derived from ancient organisms 112 that lived in sulfide-rich habitats. For this paper, we hypothesized that SQR-mediated 113 hydropersulfide oxidation is coupled with CARS2-mediated persulfide generation to fuel 114 mitochondrial energy metabolism. 115 To investigate the physiological relevance of mitochondrial SQR-mediated sulfur 116 metabolism in mammals, we employed a mitochondria-targeting-deficient SQR mutant mouse 117 model. Through integrated sulfur metabolomics and bioenergetics in yeast, mouse embryonic 118 fibroblasts (MEFs), and isolated mitochondria, we provide mechanistic evidence that SQR 119 mediates electron transport and proton transfer from sulfide and hydropersulfide to the ETC. 120

2.1. Validation of the Δhmt2 S. pombe model organism. 126 

Recent studies have indicated that the mitochondrial SQR is important for maintaining 127 mitochondrial function by transferring electrons from H 2S to coenzyme Q (CoQ), thereby linking 128 sulfur metabolism to the ETC at the level of complex III [24, 25]. To investigate the role of SQR, 129 we generated SQR-deficient fission yeast ( Δhmt2 ) using the homologous recombination system 130 (Fig. 1A). To verify the toxicity of CdCl 2 towards the newly generated SQR-deficient yeast, various 131 concentrations of CdCl 2 (0 to 300 μM) were added to the culturing media of SQR-deficient and 132 wild-type (WT) yeast and the subsequent growth rates were compared (Fig. 1B). As expected, 133 the growth rate of the Δhmt2 yeast significantly decreased with the increased concentrations of 134 CdCl 2. This was consistent with previously reported results [17], validating our model yeast for 135 use in subsequent experiments. Additionally, the susceptibility of Δhmt2 yeast towards NaHS (a 136 sulfide donor) was also determined by comparing the growth rate of the Δhmt2 yeast with that of 137 the WT after being cultured in media containing 0-1,000 μM NaHS (Fig. 1C). As expected, Δhmt2 138 growth rates decreased with increasing concentrations of NaHS compared to that of the WT, 139 further validating our in vivo model. 140

2.3. SQR is important in mitochondrial energy metabolism. 152 

To examine SQR’s contribution to mitochondrial energy metabolism, we compared yeast 153 growth in either glycerol medium, where energy production during growth depends on 154 mitochondrial energy metabolism, or in a glucose medium when energy production is primarily 155 mediated by glycolysis and independent of mitochondrial respiration. Both WT and Δhmt2 yeast 156 grew similarly in glucose medium (Fig. 3A and B, left panels), but the growth of Δhmt2 was 157 strongly arrested compared with that of the WT yeast when glycerol was the sole carbon source 158 (Fig. 3A and B, right panels). This finding suggests that SQR has an important role in 159 mitochondrial energy metabolism. Moreover, we conducted JC-1 fluorescence staining with 160 Fluorescence Activated Cell Sorter (FACS) analyses and observed increased mitochondrial 161 membrane potential at the stationary phase when WT yeast were grown in glucose medium. This 162 was not observed in SQR-deficient yeast (Fig. 3C, D), further demonstrating the importance of 163 SQR in mitochondrial energy metabolism. Additionally, cellular ATP content was increased in WT 164 yeast during stationary growth, while no such change was observed in that of SQR-deficient yeast 165 (Fig. 3E). Thus, SQR-deficient yeast were shown to experience impaired mitochondrial energy 166 production. 167

2.5. Increased longevity with sulfur donors is SQR-dependent. 177 

Considering the role of SQR in oxidizing hydrogen sulfide to hydropersulfide and 178 persulfide intermediates, we also wondered whether sulfide/persulfide donors could increase the 179 lifespan of WT or SQR-deficient yeast (Fig. 4B). After treating the WT and Δhmt2 yeast with the 180 hydrogen sulfide donor NaHS or the hydropersulfide donor Na 2S2 (the model equivalent of H 2S2), 181 we observed that both donors could extend the lifespan of WT yeast by at least 3 days. On the 182 other hand, the donors did not affect the lifespan of SQR-deficient yeast (Fig. 4B). This result 183 suggests that SQR can utilize both hydrogen sulfide and hydropersulfides as substrates to 184 promote the lifespan extension of S. pombe , which could potentially thereby influence the 185 bioenergetics and maintenance of the mitochondrial membrane potential in the yeast’s 186 mitochondria. As Δhmt2 yeast were SQR-deficient, their ability to utilize sulfides and persulfides 187 was impacted, likely resulting in the lack of effect of the donors on extending the lifespan of the 188 yeast. 189 190

2.7. Mitochondrial SQR deficiency results in persulfide accumulation 200 

In yeast, SQR-mediated effects on mitochondrial metabolism and longevity were closely 201 correlated with the role of SQR in the metabolism of H 2S and CysSSH. Therefore, we decided to 202 study the overall effects of mitochondrial SQR deficiency or mislocalization of SQR to the 203 cytoplasm on sulfur metabolism and levels of supersulfides in Sqrdl ∆N/∆N immortalized (MEFs)s 204 and their isolated mitochondria (Fig. 5 and Fig. S2A). In the Sqrdl ∆N/∆N iMEFs, we found a dramatic 205 increase in levels of hydropersulfides, including cysteine hydropersulfide, glutathione 206 hydropersulfide, and hydrogen persulfide (HSSH) and H 2S. This suggests that not only H 2S but 207 also hydropersulfides, such as cysteine hydropersulfide and glutathione hydropersulfide, serve 208 as primary substrates for SQR. This is because SQR inactivation can result in persulfide 209 accumulation; the same phenotype was observed in our yeast models. 210 211

2.9. Hydrogen sulfide and hydrogen persulfide generate mitochondrial membrane potential in an 247 

SQR-dependent manner 248 

To more closely analyze the direct effects of hydrogen sulfide and hydropersulfide on 249 mitochondrial membrane potential and complex I-IV activity, we established a250 tetramethylrhodamine ethyl ester (TMRE) time-lapse imaging system of isolated mitochondria 251 from iMEFs (Fig. S4A-C). Sulfide supplementation to isolated mitochondria was found to generate 252 the membrane potential in an SQR-dependent manner, although the effective sulfide dose was 253 much lower for the isolated mitochondria than for the whole cells (Fig. 7A and Fig. S4D). The 254 addition of 3 μM NaHS retained the membrane potential response to succinate, malate, and 255 glutamate and to N,N,N',N' -tetramethyl-1,4-benzenediamine (TMPD) with ascorbate, which 256 indicates that sulfide does not suppress the activities of complex I/II and complex IV. In contrast, 257 the membrane potential response was halted by concentrations of NaHS above 30 μM, 258 suggesting that complex IV is sensitive to sulfide-mediated inhibition (data not shown). The 259 sulfide-mediated generation of membrane potential was not affected by the inhibition of 260 complexes I and II (Fig. 7B) but was stopped by complex III inhibition (Fig. 7C). 261 Considering these results, we wondered whether they could also be observed in diseased 262 human mitochondria. A 10 μM NaHS supply induced a 266 small oxygen consumption only in WT mitochondria (Fig. 7D-F), suggesting that the SQR was 267 responsible for the sulfide-derived electron transport. 3. Discussion 290 

291 This study demonstrated the essential functions of SQR in promoting mitochondrial 292 persulfide synthesis and in maintaining metabolic activities. Our results suggest that eukaryotes 293 (i.e. yeast, mammals, etc…) utilize sulfur respiration under physiological conditions. Interestingly, 294 both sulfide and hydropersulfides could be oxidized by SQR, leading to electron and proton 295 donation to the ETC via ubiquinone and maintaining the mitochondrial membrane potential. This 296 finding is a missing piece of the sulfur respiration system that we proposed in our previous study 297 [6, 30]. Hydropersulfides produced by CARS likely serve as substrates for SQR, as they can 298 donate electrons and protons to CoQ, and the resulting oxidized persulfides are expected to 299 receive electrons from the ETC and other redox systems in the mitochondria. From this, a new 300 aspect of energy metabolism through sulfur respiration has now updated the concept of 301 cysteinolysis [35]; cysteine is a source of hydropersulfides, which play a central role not only in 302 cellular redox reactions, but also in mitochondrial energy metabolism because they can serve as 303 substrates for ATP production. 304 In our previous study, we proposed that cysteine hydropersulfide synthesized by CARS2 305 served as an electron acceptor from the ETC and that the resulting hydrosulfide served as a 306 substrate for SQR, thus generating the mitochondrial membrane potential [6, 29]. Consistent with 307 this model, CARS-deficient yeast exhibited decreased production of cysteine hydropersulfide and 308 other supersulfides, accompanied by an impaired mitochondrial energy metabolism [36]. In our 309 current study with S. pombe , however, SQR deficiency in the mitochondria unexpectedly resulted 310 in a large accumulation of hydropersulfides as well as hydrogen sulfide, which strongly suggests 311 that both hydropersulfides and hydrogen sulfide are direct substrates for SQR (Fig. 2). Similar 312 results were reported from the analysis of SQR mutant mice (Figs. 5-7 and S2, 4), further 313 supporting the growing role of SQR in the metabolism of both hydropersulfides and hydrogen 314 sulfide [29]. This result allowed us to revise our model of sulfur respiration, where cysteine 315

16 hydropersulfide and derivative hydropersulfides (e.g. GSSH) generated by CARS2 are oxidized 316 by SQR and serve as electron and proton donors in the ETC. The resulting oxidized persulfides 317 (most typically, GSSSG) function as electron acceptors in the ETC. Thus, the idea that sulfur 318 respiration in eukaryotes can operate under physiological conditions to exploit the redox cycles 319 of persulfides is highly plausible. Additional detailed studies are required to obtain solid evidence 320 that persulfides can act as electron acceptors equivalent to oxygen. 321 Interestingly, our scavenger experiments further refine this model. The persistence of 322 mitochondrial membrane potential formation in the presence of an H ₂S scavenger suggests that 323 free H ₂S is not the primary bioenergetic substrate (Fig. S4G, H). Instead, these data support the 324 notion that hydropersulfides and polysulfides, likely generated downstream of H ₂S, represent the 325 functionally relevant substrates for SQR. In this context, hydropersulfides and polysulfides appear 326 to be more efficient electron donors to the ETC than H ₂S itself, thereby more effectively supporting 327 mitochondrial membrane potential formation and bioenergetics. This interpretation is consistent 328 with our metabolomic data showing accumulation of persulfide species in SQR-deficient systems 329 (Fig. 5 and S2) and highlights a central role of supersulfide species in driving sulfur respiration. 330 In this context, the recent development of chemical supersulfide boosters provides an 331 important proof-of-concept for the therapeutic targeting of sulfur respiration. For example, 2 H-332 thiopyran-2-thione sulfine has been shown to convert H 2S into hydrogen persulfide and elevate 333 intracellular supersulfide levels, leading to enhanced mitochondrial membrane potential and 334 energy metabolism [37]. These findings suggest that the pharmacological activation of SQR-335 mimic sulfur metabolic pathways may represent a promising strategy to augment mitochondrial 336 bioenergetics, particularly in conditions of mitochondrial dysfunction. 337 Of note, sulfur respiration does not require a functional complex I, and electrons and 338 protons enter from CoQ and proceed to the ETC. This suggests that the dietary supplementation 339 of cystine activates sulfur respiration and increases ATP production in patients with mitochondrial 340 diseases, particularly those suffering from complex I deficiency. 4.1. Yeast strain, medium, and plasmid. 370 

The media used for the growth were Yeast Extract-Peptone-Dextrose (YPD), Synthetic 371 Complete (SC), and Sehgal and Gibbon’s (SG). YPD contains 1% yeast extract (Difco 372 Laboratories, Detroit, MI), 2% peptone (Difco Laboratories), and 2% glucose. SC has 2% glucose, 373 0.67% yeast nitrogen base without amino acids (Difco Laboratories), and 0.2% dropout 374 supplement (Takara Bio, Shiga, Japan). SG contains 2% galactose instead of glucose in SC. The 375 appropriate amino acids and/or nucleotides were removed for auxotrophic markers. When 376 necessary, 2% agar (Nacalai Tesque, Kyoto, Japan) was added to solidify the medium. 377 The yeast Schizosaccharomyces pombe with a 972 background was used in this study. 378 The HMT2 disrupted strain ( Δhmt2 ) was constructed by using a two-step PCR method with minor 379 modifications [42]. The 5,000-bp homologous sequences were added to the kanMX6 deletion 380 cassette by using a two-step overlapping PCR with primers and pFA6-KanMX6 plasmid. The PCR 381 fragment was integrated into the genome by transformation via the LiAc/SS carrier DNA/PEG 382 method [43]. Screening for G418 resistance was performed, and the correct integration event was 383 verified via PCR with chromosomal DNA. 384

4.2. Growth inhibition by CdCl 2 and NaHS in yeast. 386 

Yeast cells were cultured in synthetic complete medium [SC, 2% glucose, 0.67% yeast 387 nitrogen base without amino acids, and 0.2% dropout supplement (Takara Bio Inc., Shiga, Japan)] 388 at a starting OD 600 of 0.01. After 10 min at 30 ºC, CdCl 2 (10-300 μM) or NaHS (50-1,000 μM) was 389 added to the culture, and the culture was allowed to continue growing at 30ºC. The OD 600 of the 390 culture was measured after 24 h. The growth rate was converted into percent growth relative to 391 the untreated controls. 392

4.4. Measurement of mitochondrial membrane potential of yeast 402 

The mitochondrial membrane potential was measured by using JC-1 (Molecular Probes, 403 Eugene, OR, USA), a cationic dye that shows potential-dependent accumulation in the 404 mitochondria [44]. Yeast cells were cultured in SC medium at 30 °C for 24 h (log phase) or 48 h 405 (stationary phase), harvested by centrifugation, and washed once with phosphate-buffered saline 406 (PBS) buffer. The harvested cells were incubated with 10 μM JC-1 in PBS for 30 min at 30°C and 407 then subjected to flow cytometry analysis. Flow cytometry was performed by using a BD Accuri 408 C6 flow cytometer (BD Bioscience, Franklin Lakes, NJ, USA), and the FL2/FL1 values of 409 individual cells were calculated to determine mitochondrial membrane potential. Negative control 410 cells with depolarized mitochondrial membranes were treated with 5 μM carbonyl cyanide m-411 chlorophenyl hydrazine (CCCP) (Sigma-Aldrich, St. Louis, MO) for 30 min at room temperature 412 before JC-1 labeling. 413

4.7. Chronological lifespan assay. 433 

Chronological lifespan was determined according to a previously described method with 434 simple modifications [36, 45]. Briefly, yeast cells were cultured in SC medium (at a starting OD 600 435 of 0.1) at 30 °C with shaking. To investigate the effects of NaHS and Na 2S2, the 438 donor compounds were added every 3 days. Cell survival rates were calculated by normalizing 439 the CFUs at each time point to the CFUs obtained at day 0. 440 441

4.11. Measurement of the membrane potential of isolated mitochondria 473 

iMEFs mitochondria were isolated by differential centrifugation. WT and Sqrdl ΔN/ΔN iMEFs 474 were homogenized in the extraction buffer (10 mM Tris-HCl, 250 mM sucrose, and 0.5 mM EGTA, 475 pH 7.4) with a Dounce homogenizer (Teflon glass) and were centrifuged at 2,000 x g at 4 ºC for 476 5 min to remove nuclei and cell debris. The supernatants were centrifuged at 12,000 x g at 4 ºC 477 for 10 min, and mitochondrial pellets were washed three times with a buffer and kept at -80 ºC 478 until use. To determine the membrane potential in isolated mitochondria under several 479 experimental conditions, TMRE staining was performed according to the method previously 480 reported [48]. The TMRE fluorescence intensity at a maximum emission of 574 nm depends on 481 the mitochondrial membrane potential. Briefly, mitochondria from WT and Sqrdl ΔN/ΔN iMEFs were 482 adsorbed on the bottom of 8-well slides (Ibidi, Martinsreid, Germany) coated with poly-D-lysine 483 (Sigma-Aldrich, St. Luis, MO, USA), after which they were stained with 10 nM TMRE (Biotium, 484 Fremont, CA, USA) and 50 nM MitoTracker Green FM (Thermo Fisher Scientific, Waltham, MA, 485 USA) at 25 ºC for 10 min. TMRE fluorescence intensity (561 nm excitation and 605 ± 15 nm 486 emission), which is positive with MitoTracker Green FM fluorescence, was measured in individual 487 mitochondria by using the region of interest (ROI) tool of the confocal laser scanning microscope 488 (Nikon C2 plus, with NIS elements version 5.01 software). ROIs, 200 each for WT and Sqrdl ΔN/ΔN 

489 mitochondria, were manually drawn around a single mitochondrion that did not overlap with others, 490 and the average non-zero pixel intensity within the ROIs was measured and plotted against time 491 as arbitrary units. Because damage to some mitochondria is technically inevitable, we selected 492 mitochondria that showed a sufficient response to TMPD/ascorbate. Specifically, of 200 ROIs, the 493 top 30 ROIs were selected for analysis of TMRE fluorescence intensity in response to 494 TMPD/ascorbate, and their means and s.d. values were calculated. 495
