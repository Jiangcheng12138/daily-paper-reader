Abstract 20 

Integral membrane proteins (IMPs) are central regulators of cellular signaling and represent a 21 

major class of therapeutic targets. GPR155 (also known as LYCHOS), an evolutionarily conserved 22 

protein containing both transporter -like and GPCR -like domains, has recently emerged as a 23 

lysosomal nutrient sensor implicated in mTORC1 signaling. Despite its enriched expression in 24 

brain regions associated with reward processing, the in vivo neuronal and behavioral functions of 25 

GPR155 remain undefined. Here, we leverage the genetic tractability of Drosophila melanogaster 26 

to characterize the role of the GPR155 ortholog, anchor , in neural circuit function and behavior. 27 

Here , we demonstrate that pan -neuronal downregulation of anchor leads to significant alterations 28 

in multiple behavior s, including reduced feeding, disrupted light -dependent rhythmicity, decreased 29 

sleep, increased waking locomotor activity, and diminished sedation sensitivity to ethanol. We also 30 

selectively manipulated anchor expression in the neuroendocrine insulin -producing cells (IPCs ),31 

which phenocopied impaired rhythmicity and decreased ethanol sedation sensitivity observed in 32 

pan -neuronal manipulations , indicating that anchor function within IPCs is sufficient to modulate 33 

discrete behavioral outputs. Our results suggest that anchor regulates behavior in a sexually 34 

dimorphic manner as changes in ethanol sedation sensitivity were more penetrant in females, 35 

whereas altered feeding and ethanol preference was observed only in males . These findings 36 

establish a previously unrecognized role for anchor in the regulation of neuroendocrine signaling 37 

and behavior. Given the conservation of mTORC1 signaling and neuropeptidergic systems across 38 

species, this work provides mechanistic insight into how multifunctional IMPs integrate metabolic 39 

and environmental cues to influence complex behaviors, with potential implications for 40 

understanding the molecular basis of feeding, sleep regulation, and substance use disorders. 41

Introduction 43 

Nearly 25% of human genes encode i ntegral membrane proteins (IMP)s 1, proteins 44 

permanently embedded in cellular membrane s, including G -Protein Coupled Receptors (GPCRs) ,45 

ion channels, transporters, and cell adhesion molecules . This class of proteins play s important roles 46 

in cellular communication and integration of environmental information, which makes them 47 

immensely important drug targets . In fact, they are acted on by >50% of approved drugs by the 48 

U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) 2. Furthermore, different IMPs often interact, engaging 49 

in c ross -oligomerization to adjust signaling and cellular outcomes. However, a unique one ,50 

GPR155, combines multiple types of IMPs into one. GPR155 is comprised of a physically -linked 51 

transporter -like region and a GPCR -like region 3–5.52 

Recently, GPR155 has garnered attention not only for its structure, but also for its 53 

involvement in a major cell growth pathway , mechanistic Target of Rapamycin Complex 1 54 

(mTORC1) signaling 6,7 . Specifically, in vitro findings indicate that GPR155 localizes to lysosome s55 

where it integrates nutrient information in order to modulate mTORC1 signaling, leading 56 

researchers to give it an additional name, LYsosomal CHOlesterol Sensing protein (LYCHOS )7.57 

While mTORC1 is ubiquitously expressed, GPR155 has a specific expression pattern , especially 58 

in the brain . In particular , in situ hybridization experiments have shown high est expression of 59 

GPR155 in the pyramidal cell layer of the hippocampus and in the dorsal striatum 8,9 . Given the 60 

well -studied association of these brain regions with reward learning as well as mTORC1’s role in 61 

encoding rewarding information, these findings suggest that GPR155 could play a role in 62 

numerous behavioral outputs, particularly reward -related behavior s.63

GPR155 is well -conserved among vertebrates and invertebrates. Previous studies in 64 

Drosophila melanogaster have identified the anchor gene as the fly ortholog of GPR155 10 . anchor 65 

regulates wing development and gut homeostasis by interacting with Bone Morphogenic Pathway 66 

(BMP )10 and c-Jun N -terminal kinase (JNK )11 signaling, respectively. However, the role of anchor 67 

in behavior and in the nervous system remains unknown , although its expression in neurons has 68 

been confirmed by RNAseq 12,13 . Here, we further characterize a specific neuronal population 69 

expressing anchor , the insulin -producing cells (IPCs ).70 

The IPCs are a cluster of 14 neurosecretory cells, analogous to mammalian pancreatic β-71 

cells, and they produce Drosophila Insulin -Like Peptides (DILPs) 2, 3, and 5 14 . They also express 72 

drosulfakinins (DSKs), which are cholecystokinin -like peptides involve d in satiation 15 . IPCs are 73 

located in the pars intercerebralis (PI), the neuroendocrine center of the fly brain and functional 74 

homolog of the mammalian hypothalamus. From there, IPC axons terminate in several regions, 75 

including the corpora cardiaca (CC), a gland essential for glucose regulation. The CC is 76 

functionally similar to the pituitary gland in vertebrates, and it contains endocrine cells similar to 77 

vertebrate pancreatic α-cells 16,17 . IPCs additionally project into the fly crop (a stomach -like organ ), 78 

the foregut , the anterior midgut , and to the anterior aorta , from which DILPs enter the hemolymph 79 

(the fly circulatory fluid )18 –20 . Besides ethanol sensitivity 21 , Drosophila IPCs have been described 80 

to regulate numerous aspects of behavior, ranging from appetite and feeding to sleep and circadian 81 

rhythm 22,23 . Thus, we sought to investigate whether manipulation of anchor would affect behaviors 82 

associated with IPC activity .83

Phototaxis assay 195 

Light perception was assessed through a phototaxis assay in which flies cross from a dark 196 

area towards a light source . The apparatus consisted of a tube with a dark side and a light side 197 

separated by a door. The dark side was wrapped in aluminum foil, and flies were habituated there 198 

for at least 30 minutes prior to the start of the experiment. The light side was not wrapped in foil ,199 

and it was next to a lamp, providing a light cue . Dim red light was used for short periods of time 200 

for experimenter visibility. All other light sources in the experiment room were turned off or 201 

removed. At the start of the experiment, the door separating the dark side from the light side was 202 

opened, and flies were allowed for 5 seconds to cross into the light side. After 5 seconds, the door 203 

was closed, and the number of flies on the light side were counted. 5 flies were tested in each run. 204

Cellular characterization of GPR155/ anchor expression and function 400 

Our results provide experimental evidence that anchor is expressed in all of the IPCs in the 401 

brain. IPCs are a cluster of 14 neurosecretory cells, analogous to mammalian pancreatic β-cells, 402 

based on similar expression pattern of genes , and t hey are expressed in the PI of the fly brain, 403 

which is analogous to the mammalian hypothalamus. IPCs produce Drosophila Insulin -Like 404 

Peptides (DILPs) 2, 3, and 5 14 in addition to expressing drosulfakinins (DSKs), which are 405 

cholecystokinin -like peptides 15 . In mammals, insulin is classically thought to be produced in β-406 

cells of the pancreas, but mounting evidence reveals that insulin is also produced in several 407 

mammalian brain regions, including the hippocampus, cortex, and hypothalamus 43 . While GPR155 408 

expression in pancreatic β-cells has not been reported, in situ experiments show that it is expressed 409 

in these brain regions . Mechanistically , GPR155 and anchor could have a similar behavioral role 410 

through action on insulin production via mTORC1 signaling (discussed below ), though whether 411 

GPR155 has a similar behavioral rol e, whether anchor and GPR155 modulate mTORC1 signaling 412 

in vivo , and whether they act upstream of insulin remain s to be studied. 413 

Despite anatomical support for behavioral conservation of GPR155 and anchor , there are 414 

also differences in the expression patterns of anchor and GPR155. 8,9 For instance, GPR155 is 415 

highly expressed in the mammalian striatum. GPR155 is, in fact, a marker for a striatal subregion, 416

Molecular mechanisms underlying GPR155/ anchor -dependent regulation of behavior 441 

Though not well studied in Drosophila , mTORC1 regulates insulin production in mammals 442 

through a negative feedback loop .47 In vitro mTORC1 signaling is reduced by GPR155 knockout 443 

because GPR155 cannot sequester the mTORC1 inhibitor, GATOR1 (guanosine triphosphatase -444 

activating protein) via the lysosomal cholesterol signaling (LYCHOS) effector domain (LED) 445 

identified on GPR155 7. In Drosophila , c hanges in mT OR signaling in IPCs are linked to altered 446 

production of insulin , which is linked to changes in feeding, ethanol sensitivity, and circadian 447 

rhythm. Therefore, a possible mechanism underlying the phenotypes observed in this study 448 

involves positioning anchor upstream of mTOR, which would in turn regulate insulin signaling. 449 

Future studies will assess whether anchor manipulations lead to changes in mTOR - and insulin -450 

dependent signaling in a similar way as described in mammals .451 

Our results linking anchor function and ethanol -dependent behaviors in flies are consistent 452 

with mammalian studies showing that GPR155 is downregulated in the amygdala of rats that 453 

exhibit high ethanol preference 48 as well as a established role of mTORC1 in addiction. Through 454 

its role in synaptic growth, striatal mTORC1 is thought to encode reinforcing properties of 455 

substances such as ethanol. In fact even during first -time exposure, ethanol triggers mTORC1 -456 

dependent plasticity in the striatum 49 . These findings underscore the need to better understand the 457 

link between GPR155 / anchor , mTORC1 signaling , and plasticity. 458
