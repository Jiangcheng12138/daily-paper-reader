In cortical circuits, inhibitory interneurons play a dual role: they regulate overall activity levels to prevent runaway 8

excitation, and contribute to diverse computations. While unstructured inhibitory synaptic connections achieve 9

the first role by homeostatically regulating firing rates, computational tasks often require structured excitatory-10 

inhibitory (E/I) connectivity. Here, we consider a broad class of pairwise inhibitory spike-timing dependent 11 

plasticity (iSTDP) rules, demonstrating how inhibitory connections can self-organize to both stabilize excitation 12 

and generate functionally relevant connectivity motifs—a process we call “structured stabilization”. We show that 13 

in both small E/I circuits and large spiking recurrent neural networks the choice of iSTDP rule can lead to either 14 

mutually connected E/I pairs, or to lateral inhibition, where an inhibitory neuron connects to an excitatory neuron 15 

that does not directly connect back to it. In a one-dimensional ring network with two inhibitory subpopulations 16 

following these distinct iSTDP rules, the effective connectivity within the excitatory units self-organizes into a 17 

Mexican-hat-like profile, with excitatory influence in the center and inhibitory influence away from the center. 18 

This leads to emergent network responses such as contextual modulation effects as in the visual cortex and 19 

spatially modular activity characteristic of developmental spontaneous activity. Our theoretical work introduces a 20 

family of rules that retains the broad applicability and simplicity of spike-timing-based plasticity, while stabilizing 21 

activity and promoting specific connectivity motifs which support emergent network computations. 22 

Significance Statement 

Neural circuits are faced with the dual challenge of keeping activity stable while modifying synaptic strengths during development and learning. A prominent mechanism to keep excitation in check is inhibitory synaptic plasticity. In recurrent circuit models, we show that plasticity rules based on spike timing at synapses from inhibitory to excitatory neurons can stabilize activity while reinforcing specific connectivity motifs. The shape of these rules dictates the resulting motifs, and determines whether inhibitory neurons form strong reciprocal connections with their excitatory partners or lateral connections onto excitatory neurons that do not connect back. In recurrent networks with local excitatory connectivity, these motifs generate surround suppression as in the visual cortex, and long-range spatial correlations characteristic of developmental spontaneous activity.

# 1 Introduction 24 

In cortical circuits, GABA-ergic interneurons constitute approximately 10% to 20% of the total neural population, 25 

and can be further divided into subtypes that exhibit distinct characteristics in connectivity, plasticity and tuning 26 

properties (Jiang et al. 2015; Tremblay et al. 2016). Fully understanding how these diverse interneuron subtypes 27 

influence neural circuit function remains a significant challenge. A key open question is how activity-dependent 28 

plasticity of inhibitory synapses in recurrent circuits sculpts this diversity into specific connectivity motifs while 29 

maintaining stable network activity. 30 

The dynamic balance between excitation and inhibition is crucial for generating the irregular, Poisson-like firing 31 

patterns commonly observed in vivo in cortical circuits (Van Vreeswijk and Sompolinsky 1998; Brunel 2000). This 32 

balance enhances the circuit’s sensitivity to input, facilitating rapid information processing (Tsodyks and Sejnowski 33 

1995). Furthermore, it supports essential computations, such as receptive field emergence (Wallenstein and 34 

Hasselmo 1997; Froemke et al. 2007; Dorrn et al. 2010; Kleberg et al. 2014; D’amour and Froemke 2015; Rubin 35 

et al. 2017), neural assembly formation (Sadeh and Clopath 2021), and the storage of precise temporal firing 36 

sequences (Maes et al. 2021; Riquelme et al. 2023; Gong and Brunel 2024; Shao et al. 2024). 37

Excitatory-inhibitory (E/I) balance can be achieved without any specific inhibitory connectivity structures. Often 38 

circuit models consider inhibition simply as a “blanket” that regulates excitation, and incorporate it into a global 39 

normalization term or a correction to the synaptic weights between excitatory neurons (Jin 2002; Vogels et al. 40 

2011; Ravid Tannenbaum and Burak 2016; Montangie et al. 2020). While this simplified approach captures 41 

fundamental properties of neural dynamics, it overlooks the possible advantages of more specific E/I connectivity 42 

patterns. Incorporating structured inhibitory connections can further refine computations and circuit properties 43 

such as E/I co-tuning (Clopath et al. 2016; Mackwood et al. 2021; Miehl and Gjorgjieva 2022; Eckmann et al. 44 

2024), memory storage (Mongillo et al. 2018; Bergoin et al. 2023; Miehl et al. 2023; Agnes and Vogels 2024; 45 

Meissner-Bernard, Jenkins, et al. 2025; Meissner-Bernard, Zenke, et al. 2025), predictive coding (Schulz et al. 46 

2021), and other aspects of neural dynamics (Mongillo et al. 2018; Dahmen et al. 2020; Sadeh and Clopath 2020; 47 

Hafizi et al. 2022). 48 

Experimental findings demonstrate that inhibitory neurons form non-random connectivity patterns that cannot 49 

be explained solely by cell distance or neuron morphology. Additional factors, including developmental factors 50 

such as spontaneous activity, interneuron subtype, synaptic dynamics and targeting preference significantly 51 

shape this connectivity structure (Peng et al. 2021; Udvary et al. 2022; Bollmann et al. 2023; Reimann et al. 52 

2023; Wu et al. 2023; Kuan et al. 2024; Lakhera et al. 2024; Znamenskiy et al. 2024; Harth et al. 2025). In 53 

the neonatal mouse cortex, even before the onset of external sensory inputs, inhibitory hub neurons regulate 54 

circuit development (Bollmann et al. 2023). Moreover, theoretical studies suggest that spatial organization with a 55 

widespread Mexican-hat-like effective connectivity, consisting of local excitation and longer-range inhibition, can 56 

give rise to modular activity patterns with long-range spatial correlations propagating throughout the neonatal 57 

ferret cortex (Smith et al. 2018). Such correlated activity is thought to serve as a scaffold for the emergence of 58 

specific stimulus feature tuning in the early stages of development (Mulholland et al. 2021; Lakhera et al. 2024; 59 

Trägenap et al. 2025). 60 

Despite these advances, the mechanisms and principles that enable the formation and maintenance of inhibitory 61 

connectivity patterns, especially in the context of excitatory connectivity, remain elusive. Genetic mechanisms 62 

play a fundamental role, particularly in early developmental stages (Duan et al. 2020; Hanganu-Opatz et al. 63 

2021; Wu et al. 2023). Equally important is synaptic plasticity driven by neural activity, which refines inhibitory 64 

connectivity through a dynamic, activity-dependent process (Sreenivasan et al. 2022). This mechanism enables a 65 

closed-loop regulation of neural dynamics, allowing for precise tuning and adaptability in the face of change. 66 

While the majority of experimental and theoretical studies investigate excitatory plasticity (e.g. Kempter et al. 67 

1999; Song et al. 2000; Morrison et al. 2007; Shulz and Feldman 2013, far fewer examine inhibitory plasticity. 68 

Although some theoretical work has considered the impact of different forms of iSTDP in feedforward circuit 69 

architectures with a single postsynaptic neuron (Kleberg et al. 2014; Luz and Shamir 2014), there is still no general 70 

understanding of how pairwise iSTDP might influence the formation of specific connectivity motifs at the level of 71 

recurrently connected circuits. Theoretical work has proposed an iSTDP rule that guarantees the homeostatic 72 

regulation of neuronal rates (Vogels et al. 2011), becoming the “canonical” choice for plastic circuits. However, this 73 

rule tends to over-regulate rates in a homeostatic manner, producing narrow rate distributions in the excitatory 74 

population, which contradicts experimental findings Buzsaki and Mizuseki 2014. In other work, inhibitory rules 75 

are specifically fit to experimental measurements (Lagzi et al. 2021; Bergoin et al. 2025), derived normatively 76 

from computation objectives (Mackwood et al. 2021), or are found through numerical optimization on a large 77 

parameter space (Confavreux et al. 2024). Here we ask how different spike timing-based rules at inhibitory 78 

synapses shape which excitatory and inhibitory neurons become strongly connected in recurrent circuits, and 79 

how the resulting excitatory-inhibitory motifs stabilize network firing rate, while supporting surround modulation 80 

and the generation of spatially correlated spontaneous activity. 81 

To address these questions, rather than studying single rules, here we propose an integrated approach by analyzing 82 

a broad family of pairwise iSTDP rules and study their impact in small analytically tractable E/I circuits as well as 83 

in large-scale recurrent neuronal networks (RNNs) of spiking neurons. We identify mechanisms that stabilize 84 

excitatory activity while shaping circuit motifs, and show how, depending on the profile of the rule considered, 85 

inhibitory connectivity adjusts to the existing excitatory connectivity in distinct ways. 86 

We first consider a recurrently connected two-neuron circuit of one excitatory and one inhibitory neuron, and 87 

derive how the choice of the iSTDP rule determines which features of neural activity drive the plasticity of inhibitory 88 

connections. At one extreme, rules dominated by firing rates are weakly influenced by existing connectivity 89 

structure in the two-neuron circuit, aiming primarily at the homeostatic regulation of firing rates, and thus 90

2.1 Inhibitory plasticity shapes specific E/I circuit motifs 106 

We initially considered a general iSTDP rule that depends exclusively on local spike times applied to a synapse 107 

from an inhibitory presynaptic to an excitatory postsynaptic neuron (inh-to-exc) (Kempter et al. 1999; Luz and 108 

Shamir 2014; Akil et al. 2021). This rule contains two fixed components triggered by each pre- and post-synaptic 109

stochastic process, the mean-field equation for the evolution of the average synaptic weight can be decomposed 112

patterns, we first developed an analytical framework, in which rather than choosing a single rule, we defined a 119 

family of iSTDP rules. Using these rules, we aimed to derive the closed-form solution for a two-neuron recurrent 120 

E/I circuit, and link the shape of iSTDP to the pattern of learned inhibitory connections. 121 

Several terms in Eq. 1 depend solely on pre- and post-synaptic firing rates. Furthermore, in the general category 122 

of rules where 𝐵 is high, the contribution of the 𝐵 𝑟 pre 𝑟 post term can easily be an order of magnitude above 𝑄 ,123 

even in the presence of strong pre-post correlations (see Methods, Section 4.3). Therefore, when 𝑄 ≪ 𝐵 𝑟 pre 𝑟 post ,124 

the plasticity dynamics of Eq. 1 are strongly dominated by the firing rates. We call this plasticity regime “rate-125 

dominated”. A prominent example of this is the previously established and well-studied rate-homeostatic iSTDP 126 

rule (Vogels et al. 2011) With 𝛼 post = 0 and kernel integral 𝐵 > 0 , this rule drives the inhibitory weight to adapt 127 

continuously so that the postsynaptic rate converges to the target value ̃ 𝑟 post , corresponding to a stable fixed 128 

point of Eq. 1, namely: ̃ 𝑟 post ≈ −𝛼 pre /𝐵 .129 

While a large value of 𝐵 simplifies the analysis by making covariance-based terms negligible, it only represents 130 

a subset of possible plasticity rules. We therefore explored a broader range, including those for which the 131 

iSTDP kernel has zero or near-zero net area ( 𝐵 ≈ 0 ). These rules amplify the contribution of the second-order 132 

correlations between pre- and postsynaptic neuron in the 𝑄 term. We refer to them as “covariance-dominated”. 133 

In choosing specific pairwise interaction kernels, we considered recent experimental findings that reported a 134 

symmetric iSTDP rule for parvalbumin-expressing (PV) interneurons, as well as an antisymmetric iSTDP rule for 135 

somatostatin-expressing (SST) interneurons, both projecting onto excitatory pyramidal neurons in the orbitofrontal 136 

cortex (Lagzi et al. 2021). Consequently, we focused on symmetric kernels with strong short-range potentiation 137 

and weak long-range depression (Fig. 1C), or antisymmetric kernels in which pre-post and post-pre interactions 138 

balance each other with opposite signs (Fig. 1D). 139 

Analyzing the full dynamics of Eq. 1 in a recurrently connected circuit is challenging because the covariance 140 

𝐶(𝜏 ) generally depends on the synaptic weights themselves. We therefore considered a minimal two-neuron 141 

E/I recurrent circuit (Fig. 1A) with fixed exc-to-inh connection (𝑤 exc ) but plastic inh-to-exc weight (𝑤 inh ). We 142 

modeled the neurons as time-varying Poisson processes, where each incoming spike changes the instantaneous 143 

spiking probability through an excitatory or inhibitory post-synaptic potential (EPSP/IPSP) kernel (internal traces 144 

shown in Fig. S1, see also Methods Section 4.4). The inhibitory neuron’s firing rate is kept constant by an external 145 

homeostatic mechanism. The excitatory neuron receives an excitatory fixed external input in addition to the 146 

inhibitory input. Using this simplified model, we derived analytically a closed-form expression for the fixed-point 147 

of the iSTDP dynamics (Methods, Eq. 11; Supporting Information). The condition of fixed 𝑟 inh is not required 148 

for a stable solution. Allowing 𝑟 inh to vary still leads to an analytic expression for the fixed point of the iSTDP 149 

dynamics, but the resulting closed-form involves the root of a quadratic equation and is therefore less intuitive 150 

(see Supporting Information, S1.3, Fig. S3). 151

In contrast, the covariance-dominated rules are sensitive to both the circuit’s connectivity structure and the 162 

shape of iSTDP kernel. With the symmetric kernel, the final inhibitory weight becomes significantly stronger in 163 

the M configuration compared to the U configuration (Fig. 1F, compare brown and light orange). Conversely, 164 

with the antisymmetric kernel, the inhibitory weight is lower in the M configuration (Fig. 1G). Such specificity 165 

enables these rules not only to regulate excitatory rates, but also to shape specific E/I connectivity motifs—an 166 

effect we termed “structured stabilization”. This property emerges from the well-known interaction between 167 

the iSTDP kernel and second-order spiking statistics. While this has been previously studied for excitatory 168 

plasticity (Kempter et al. 1999; Song et al. 2000; Babadi and Abbott 2013; Shulz and Feldman 2013), here we 169 

extend it to inhibitory plasticity in a minimally recurrent two-neuron E/I circuit and hence consider how recurrent 170 

inhibition regulates excitation. Specifically, the strong excitatory-to-inhibitory connection in the M configuration 171 

generates positively-correlated post-pre events. For the symmetric iSTDP, these events occur within the positive 172 

region of the kernel, leading to synaptic potentiation. Conversely, with the antisymmetric iSTDP rule, the same 173 

positively-correlated events interact with the negative region of the kernel, resulting in synaptic depression. 174 

Additionally, the inhibitory-to-excitatory weight generates a negative covariance density, localized instead in 175 

the pre-post region of the curve. As both rules are positive here, this introduces a negative feedback in both 176 

cases, limiting the value that 𝑤 inh can reach before plasticity switches into depression. Finally, adjusting the fixed 177 

components of the iSTDP rule, 𝛼 pre and 𝛼 post , is also crucial for reaching the desired excitatory firing rate (set to 178 

25 Hz) in the preferred circuit configuration. 179 

Hence, our theoretical framework provides a link between different iSTDP rules and inhibitory connectivity in a 180 

reduced two-neuron E/I recurrent circuit motif. 181 

2.2 The shape of inhibitory STDP affects inhibitory connectivity 182 

Having established that different covariance-dominated rules can achieve structured stabilization, namely stabilize 183 

excitatory firing rates and shape specific E/I connectivity motifs, we next investigated how the specific form 184 

of the iSTDP kernel shapes the learned inhibitory connectivity. We considered four kernel shapes: symmetric, 185 

antisymmetric, and their “inverted” counterparts, obtained by reversing the sign of the kernel (Fig. 2A). We 186 

analyzed the behavior of these kernels in the reduced two-neuron E/I circuit. To explore the parameter space, 187 

we varied the rate-dependent parameters 𝐵 and 𝛼 pre , while keeping 𝛼 post = 0 . With this parametrization, the 188 

covariance-dominated regime corresponds to values of 𝐵 and 𝛼 pre close to zero. 189 

Our analysis found that both symmetric and antisymmetric kernels yield stable fixed-points in the covariance-190 

dominated regime (Fig. 2A,B, top row, and Supporting Information Fig. S2C,D). These kernels, which we term 191 

“self-stabilizing” exhibit robustness to parameter fluctuations and reliably converge to a stable inhibitory weight. 192 

In contrast, the inverted antisymmetric and the inverted symmetric rule are unstable in the covariance-dominated 193 

regime, with inhibitory weights either decaying to zero or saturating depending on the initial conditions. We 194 

categorize these as “saturating” kernels (Fig. 2A, bottom panels, and Fig. S2E,F in Supporting Information). 195 

Saturating kernels can still reach stability with strong rate-dependent terms, which reduce the overall influence of 196 

the kernel shape on the final weight. 197 

This difference in stability arises from how the kernels interact with changes in pre- and postsynaptic correlations. 198 

Consider a synapse operating under a covariance-dominated plasticity rule at its fixed point. At equilibrium, 199 

potentiation and depression events balance exactly. A perturbation that strengthens the inhibitory weight leads 200 

to a reduction in pre-post firing events due to the increased inhibition. For self-stabilizing kernels, which have a 201 

positive right branch (corresponding to pre-post events), this reduction in correlation reduces the occurrence 202 

of synaptic potentiation events, shifting the balance in favor of synaptic depression. The synapse therefore 203 

depresses, eventually compensating the initial perturbation from equilibrium. Conversely, saturating kernels show 204

In summary, our analysis of the reduced E/I circuit reveals a spectrum of iSTDP rules with distinct outcomes on 223 

circuit connectivity and dynamics. On the one hand, covariance-dominated rules with self-stabilizing kernels 224 

enable structured stabilization (stable activity and specific connectivity motifs). As a consequence, they selectively 225 

reinforce connectivity motifs based on pre- and post-synaptic correlations, and allow a broader range of excitatory 226 

firing rates. On the other hand, rate-dominated rules prioritize homeostatic control of excitatory activity over 227 

the formation of specific connectivity motifs. Thus, these rules ensure robust dynamical stability, potentially at 228 

the cost of computational flexibility. These results demonstrate a trade-off between the emergence of specific 229 

excitatory-inhibitory motifs and the tight regulation of excitatory rates in the class of iSTDP rules we considered. 230 

2.3 Structured stabilization in randomly connected RNNs 231 

How do our results on structured stabilization from two-neuron E/I circuits translate to large recurrent networks? 232 

We simulated a randomly connected RNN of conductance-based leaky integrate-and-fire (LIF) neurons, with 900 233 

excitatory and 100 inhibitory units. We modeled the excitatory connections (exc-to-exc and exc-to-inh) as fixed 234 

and sparse, while the inh-to-exc connections were modeled as dense and initially small, but subject to iSTDP 235 

(Table 2). In this case the firing rate of each inhibitory neuron was not fixed as in the two-neuron E/I circuit, but 236 

was determined by the total amount of input into each neuron from its presynaptic partners, without any external 237 

constraint. 238 

We first implemented a covariance-dominated plasticity rule with a symmetric kernel (Fig. 3A). Initially, the network 239 

produces a pathological high-firing regime, due to the low inhibitory weights. However, inhibitory plasticity quickly 240 

stabilizes the dynamics, generating an asynchronous irregular firing regime characteristic of balanced networks 241 

(Fig. 3B). To analyze the resulting inhibitory connectivity, we categorized inh-to-exc synaptic weights as either 242 

“mutual” or “unidirectional”. We called an inh-to-exc weight mutual when a reciprocal exc-to-inh connection 243 

exists between the same two neurons, reflecting a bidirectional link. Conversely, a weight is unidirectional in the 244 

absence of such excitatory reciprocal connection. Following the onset of plasticity, we observed that mutual 245 

inhibitory weights increase much more than unidirectional ones, up to the saturation threshold (Fig. 3C). This 246 

selectivity can be directly visualized through the matrix of synaptic weights after learning: the pattern of learned 247 

inh-to-exc weights mirrors the fixed exc-to-inh weights (Fig. 3E,F; see Supporting Information for full weight 248 

matrix). 249

Next, we investigated the impact of an asymmetric iSTDP rule within the same random RNN architecture and 250 

neural dynamics. As with the symmetric rule, this asymmetric iSTDP stabilizes the network dynamics (Fig. 3G). 251 

However, it promotes a markedly different connectivity pattern: purely lateral inhibition. In this configuration, 252 

inhibitory neurons strengthen their connections onto excitatory neurons lacking reciprocal connections back 253 

to them, thus forming exclusively unidirectional connections (Fig. 3H). This yields an inhibitory-to-excitatory 254 

connectivity pattern almost perfectly opposite to the previous configuration, so that existing exc-to-inh synapses 255 

carve gaps in the inh-to-exc connections (Fig. 3J,K). Achieving robust lateral inhibition with the asymmetric iSTDP 256 

rule requires the introduction of a small rate-dependent component ( 𝐵 = 0.3 ). This deviation from a purely 257 

covariance-dominated regime introduces characteristics of rate-homeostatic plasticity, such as rapid convergence 258 

to a narrow distribution of excitatory firing rates (Fig. 3G). This requirement is perhaps unsurprising given the 259 

challenges of parameter tuning in large RNNs, where closed-form solutions are unavailable (see Discussion for 260 

further considerations). Both the symmetric and the asymmetric rules converged to stable weight distributions, 261 

except for a very slow tendency for some mutual weights to reach either the upper bound (symmetric) or zero 262 

(asymmetric). These results are also robust to large variations in external input, and are compatible with network 263 

models where asynchronous irregular firing is generated intrinsically, without any external source of random noise 264 

(SI, Section S2 and Fig. S5; see also Mastrogiuseppe and Ostojic 2017). 265 

In sum, our RNN simulations generalize the analytical predictions from the reduced E/I circuit model to larger 266 

networks of recurrently connected integrate-and-fire units. Symmetric iSTDP rules reinforce mutual inhibition, 267 

mirroring pre-existing excitatory connectivity, whereas asymmetric kernels favor lateral inhibition. 268

2.4 Response properties of a one-dimensional ring network 269 

Having shown that different forms of iSTDP shape E/I connectivity in large-scale recurrent networks based on 270 

local correlations arising from direct connections, we next explored the functional consequences of structured 271 

stabilization in a recurrent network with a one-dimensional ring architecture. Ring networks are well-suited to 272 

model continuous attractor dynamics, supporting bumps of persistent activity (Amari 1977; Ben-Yishai et al. 273 

1995; Khona and Fiete 2022). This makes them relevant for studying neural computations such as head-direction 274 

encoding ( petruccoNeuralDynamicsArchitecture2023 kimGenerationStableHeading2019 ; Xie et al. 2002; Kim 275 

et al. 2017; Kim et al. 2019) and orientation selectivity in the primary visual cortex (Goldberg et al. 2004). 276 

We built a ring network of 800 excitatory and 200 inhibitory neurons arranged on a circle, with exc-to-exc and 277 

exc-to-inh weights decaying exponentially with the angular distance between neurons, following a rescaled Von 278 

Mises (Fig. 4A,B). We further divided the inhibitory population into two subclasses, governed by distinct iSTDP 279 

rules, but sharing identical neural dynamics. Inspired by experimental results (Lagzi et al. 2021), one population 280 

of “PV” interneurons followed a symmetric kernel, and another population of “SST” interneurons followed an 281 

antisymmetric one, both rules operating in the covariance-dominated regime. Initially, we set both PV-to-exc 282 

and SST-to-exc synapses as all-to-all, weak, and unbiased (see Fig. 4A for schematics, and Methods Table 3 for 283 

numerical parameters). 284 

As the network evolves under the influence of the two plasticity rules, the inhibitory populations develop 285 

specialized roles. PV interneurons, governed by symmetric iSTDP, form strong mutual connections with excitatory 286 

neurons, mirroring the distance-dependent excitatory connectivity (Fig. 4B and Fig. 4C, red). Conversely, SST 287 

interneurons, driven by antisymmetric iSTDP, establish lateral inhibition across the ring (Fig. 4B and Fig. 4C, 288 

orange). This structured stabilization, with both localized and broader components, raises the possibility of 289 

nontrivial effective interactions between excitatory neurons, potentially mediated by the different roles of the PV 290 

and SST interneurons. 291 

To estimate the effective interaction profile between excitatory neurons, we used linear response theory, equiva-292 

lent to a Taylor expansion of the network dynamics around its operating point (Sadeh and Clopath 2020, see 293 

Methods for details). We found an effective connectivity profile resembling a Mexican-hat, with short-range 294 

excitation directly inherited from exc-to-exc connectivity and a broader inhibitory surround resulting from the 295 

combined action of PV and SST interneurons (Fig. 4D). This effective connectivity profile motivated us to explore 296 

the resulting network dynamics and its response to external stimuli. We examined the emergent spontaneous 297

After identifying aspects of emergent cortical dynamics in our network consistent with early developmental 302

We also investigated whether our model could also reproduce contextual facilitating effects, for example when a 325 

stimulus in the preferred orientation is surrounded by an orthogonal stimulus (Sillito and Jones 1996). These 326 

effects are mediated by a third subclass of interneurons, the vasoactive intestinal peptide (VIP)-expressing 327 

cells, that inhibit the SST population and thereby disinhibit excitatory neurons (Keller et al. 2020). We thus 328

restricted to the neuron’s receptive field; (2) a broader iso-oriented stimulus; (3) an iso stimulus in the receptive 332

These results demonstrate how structured stabilization shaped by the interplay of symmetric and antisymmetric 336 

iSTDP rules can give rise to network connectivity which generates activity consistent with both suppressive and 337 

facilitating contextual effects in the primary visual cortex, and to long-range correlation patterns consistent with 338 

spontaneous activity in the developing sensory cortex. 339 

# 3 Discussion 340 

In this work, we have shown that a family of inhibitory STDP rules can simultaneously stabilize excitation at the 341 

circuit level, and drive the formation of specific connectivity patterns among excitatory and inhibitory neurons in 342 

the form of E/I motifs. In the absence of correlated external inputs, or training signals, circuits self-organize purely 343 

through their spontaneous internal dynamics. We found that the shape of the iSTDP rule selectively reinforces 344 

different E/I motifs—blanket inhibition, reciprocal E/I connectivity and, lateral E/I connectivity—based on the 345 

correlations present in the network. 346 

Prior computational studies addressing the self-organized formation of motifs in recurrent networks through 347 

STDP mostly focused on exc-to-exc plasticity (Ocker et al. 2015; Ravid Tannenbaum and Burak 2016; Triplett 348 

et al. 2018; Montangie et al. 2020; Manz and Memmesheimer 2023). Previous work on iSTDP has been largely 349

restricted to feedforward circuits and rules dominated by either firing rates or covariance (Kleberg et al. 2014; 350 

Luz and Shamir 2014; Akil et al. 2021; Vignoud and Robert 2022). In contrast, our approach combines both: 351 

recurrence and iSTDP. This combination makes it possible to characterize how different timing-based rules at 352 

inhibitory synapses shape excitatory-inhibitory connectivity in recurrent circuits, and how the resulting E/I motifs 353 

influence circuit dynamics, including rate stabilization, surround modulation, and the structure of spontaneous 354 

activity. 355 

We implemented a generalized iSTDP framework where individual contributions from spike pairs additively 356 

contribute to synaptic change, as in many models of excitatory synaptic plasticity. While many other factors may 357 

also contribute to inhibitory plasticity, including multiplicative effects (Morrison et al. 2007; Kunkel et al. 2011), 358 

synaptic location (Agnes and Vogels 2024) or plasticity on behavioral timescales (Gonzalez et al. 2023), we found 359 

that additive iSTDP can still generate many interesting insights about E/I connectivity in recurrent networks. Our 360 

iSTDP framework combines both rate-dependent and covariance-dependent components in inducing inhibitory 361 

plasticity. The rate-dependent terms guarantee stable neural activity, whereas the covariance-dependent terms 362 

support the emergence of specific E/I motifs, motivated by recent experimental evidence (Znamenskiy et al. 363 

2024). Consequently, our framework provides deeper insight into both the regulatory and structural properties 364 

of generalized iSTDP rules, expanding our understanding of plasticity in closed-loop recurrent circuits. 365 

One strength of our framework is that it clarifies how the iSTDP kernel interacts with other plasticity parameters. 366 

For example, in rate-dominated regime the weight evolution mainly depends on the net area of the STDP kernel 367 

and the rate-dependent terms, and not on the precise kernel shape. In the covariance-dominated regime, the 368 

weight evolution depends on specific spike covariance between pre- and postsynaptic spiking and the iSTDP 369 

kernel shape. The covariance-dominated regime, however, can pose greater challenges for parameter tuning. A 370 

promising way forward is to employ numerical methods, for instance using simulation-based inference and deep 371 

learning, which have already been applied to optimize both excitatory and inhibitory STDP rules in recurrent 372 

circuits (Confavreux et al. 2024). Our analytical approach can guide and complement these efforts, helping clarify 373 

parameter degeneracies and simplifying the parameter search process. 374 

Here we derived analytic solutions for the two-neuron Poisson model, and relied on numerical simulations for 375 

larger networks.By contrast, a recent study showed that iSTDP can stabilize recurrent LIF networks and converge 376 

to stable solutions through a mean-field approach (Akil et al. 2021). To achieve analytical tractability, that work 377 

assumed a regime of weak interactions, where internally generated correlations become negligible as the size 378 

of the neural population grows, and plasticity was driven exclusively by externally-induced correlations. In our 379 

approach, finite network size, strong interactions, and a dominance of correlation-dependent terms together 380 

drive self-organization. It remains an open question whether these two analytic frameworks, based on different 381 

assumptions, can be integrated. 382 

While our analytical approach primarily examined how small E/I circuits self-organize under different iSTDP 383 

rules, neural circuits can also form larger structures, including feedforward firing sequences and assemblies. 384 

Assemblies are groups of strongly connected neurons, often regarded as the fundamental building blocks of neural 385 

computations (Buzsáki 2010; Papadimitriou et al. 2020; Miehl et al. 2023). Previous work has examined how 386 

various iSTDP rules enable the learning, maintenance and self-repair of neural assemblies (Ocker and Doiron 2019; 387 

Lagzi et al. 2021; Bergoin et al. 2023; Bergoin et al. 2025). In particular, recent findings suggest that assemblies 388 

are formed and best preserved when inhibitory neurons are divided into subclasses that combine symmetric 389 

and antisymmetric iSTDP (Lagzi et al. 2021). Here, the first rule can maintain network stability while the second 390 

prevents assemblies from merging through lateral inhibition, as in our work. Related work has also found that 391 

a combination of positive symmetric (Hebbian) and negative symmetric (anti-Hebbian) covariance-based rules 392 

can form and maintain stable assemblies governed by similar principles (Bergoin et al. 2025). These results on 393 

assemblies agree with ours in underscoring the intuition that different rules serve different purposes: some 394 

promote E/I balance and internal network stability, while others introduce competition through lateral inhibition. 395 

In this work we further combined multiple iSTDP rules in a network with a ring topology (Ben-Yishai et al. 1995) to 396 

demonstrate how their interaction stabilizes activity while also supporting specific network computations, such as 397 

contextual input modulation as observed in the visual cortex(DeAngelis et al. 1994; Cavanaugh et al. 2002), and 398 

long-range spatial correlations reminiscent of modular spontaneous activity observed in the developing cortex 399 

(Smith et al. 2018; Powell et al. 2024). The parametrization of iSTDP rules allowed us to explore the entire space 400 

of rules and link them to the emergence of specific E/I connectivity motifs beyond the choice of pre-set, fixed 401 

plasticity rules in existing work (Lagzi et al. 2021; Bergoin et al. 2023; Lagzi and Fairhall 2024; Bergoin et al. 2025). 402

and produce specific connectivity motifs in a self-organized manner in recurrent circuits, with implications for 411 

understanding neural development and learning under structured stimuli. 412

4.3 Low impact of correlations in rate-dominated rules 457 

To better understand the role of the net area 𝐵 of the iSTDP kernel in the weight dynamics (Eq. 4), and how it 458 

determines whether a rule is rate-dominated or covariance-dominated, we will consider here a simplified scenario, 459 

where 𝐿(𝜏 ) prescribes a +1 increase in weight for coincident pre-post spikes and no increase otherwise. This 460 

choice yields 𝐵 = 𝐴 + = 1 . Consider now a pre and a post-synaptic neuron that fire with Poisson statistics at rates 461 

𝑟 pre = 𝑟 post = 𝑟 = 10 Hz . The term 𝐵 𝑟 pre 𝑟 post in Eq. 4 takes a value of 100 s−2 regardless of the correlation between 462 

the two neurons. But how does it compare to the covariance term 𝑄 in the presence of strong correlations 463 

between pre and post-synaptic activity? We can consider a regime of (unnaturally) high correlations, such that 464 

half of the spikes of the two neurons are synchronous (Pearson correlation 0.5 ). The term 𝑄 , corresponding to 465 

the spike-count covariance, is 5 s−2 . So even for very strong correlations 𝑄 is 20 times smaller than 𝐵 𝑟 pre 𝑟 post .466 

This illustrates that in regimes with strong 𝐵 , the weight dynamics in Eq. 4 are primarily governed by first-order 467 

statistics, with — at best — second-order contributions from correlations in spiking activity. 468

Figure S1: Schematics of the reduced circuit model and of interactions between covariance density and iSTDP kernel. A. Representation of the model used for Fig. 1 in main text. Two time-varying coupled Poisson units, one excitatory and one inhibitory, are connected by a non-plastic excitatory weight 𝑤 exc and a plastic inhibitory weight 

𝑤 inh . The instantaneous rates, here shown as traces, are computed as the convolution between each incoming spike and an exponential synaptic kernel that either increases (exc) or decreases (inh) the instantaneous firing probability. The input to the excitatory neuron ℎexc is fixed, whereas the inhibitory input changes dynamically, keeping the inhibitory neuron at a fixed rate 𝑟 inh . Only the inhibitory synapse (dashed line) is plastic. B. Unidirectional configuration. Here 𝑤 exc = 0 and the covariance density between the pre (inh) and post (exc) neuron can only be negative in the pre-post ( Δ𝑡 > 0 ) section of the curve, due to the inhibitory interaction given by 𝑤 inh . C. Mutual configuration, 𝑤 exc = 0.5 . Here the covariance density is positive in the post-pre ( Δ𝑡 < 0 ) section of the curve due to 𝑤 exc , and negative for ( Δ𝑡 > 0 ) for the effect of 𝑤 inh . D. Effect of a symmetric, covariance-dominated iSTDP kernel on a circuit in the M configuration: the positive left branch of the covariance density interacts with the kernel, resulting in a positive 𝑄 factor in the weight update. (Eq. 1). E. Effect of an antisymmetric iSTDP kernel. Here the positive covariance density interacts with the negative branch of the kernel, resulting into synaptic depression.
