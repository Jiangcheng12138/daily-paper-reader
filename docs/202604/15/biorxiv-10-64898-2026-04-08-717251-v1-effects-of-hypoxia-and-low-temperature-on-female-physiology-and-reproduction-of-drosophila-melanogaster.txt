Abstract 20 

Because hypoxia and low temperature independently alter metabolism and reproductive investment, their 21 

interaction provides a tractable framework for testing whether combined stressors produce non-additive 22 

physiological and reproductive effects. Here, we investigated the single and combined effects of hypoxia 23 

and low temperature in Drosophila melanogaster across multiple genetic backgrounds. We quantified 24 

metabolic rate, thermal tolerance, body mass, fertility, oogenesis progression, and oocyte apoptosis to 25 

assess organismal responses to environmental stress. Hypoxia generally increased respiratory quotient and 26 

body mass, but its effects on thermal tolerance and fertility were highly genotype dependent. Across 27 

traits, combined stressors frequently produced responses that differed from those observed under single 28 

stressors, including reduced fertility, altered oogenesis, and changes in oocyte cell death. Importantly, 29 

these effects were not uniform: some genotypes exhibited increased oocyte production or reduced cell 30 

death under combined stress, highlighting pronounced genotype-dependent differences in stress 31 

sensitivity and reproductive allocation. Together, our results demonstrate that the interaction between 32 

hypoxia and temperature can modulate metabolic and reproductive responses in ways that are not 33 

predictable from single-stressor responses alone. These findings highlight the importance of incorporating 34 

genetic background and interacting environmental stressors when evaluating organismal tolerance and 35 

adaptive potential under ongoing environmental change. 36 

Keywords: Drosophila , Hypoxia, Low temperature, Fertility, Thermal Tolerance, Oogenesis. 37 

1. Introduction 38 

Understanding how multiple stressors affect living organisms is critical to predicting their resilience and 39 

plastic responses to changing environments (Gunderson et al., 2017; Helmuth et al., 2014). Most studies 40 

focus on single stressors, yet organisms in nature are typically exposed to multiple, interacting stressors that 41 

can enhance, mitigate, or neutralize one another’s effects (Crain et al., 2008; Gunderson & Stillman, 2015; 42 

Piggott et al., 2012). These interactions challenge plasticity capacities by combining physiological and 43

simultaneously, demanding a better understanding of their combined impacts on behavior, physiology, and 45 

survival (Boyd et al., 2018; Folguera et al., 2011). 46 

Reproductive functions are particularly vulnerable to environmental stress, as energy-intensive processes 47 

such as oogenesis and egg-laying are directly affected by metabolic and physiological changes (Folguera 48 

et al., 2011; Rivera-Rincón et al., 2024; Zizzari & Ellers, 2014). In females, these stress-induced demands 49 

can compromise reproductive output and fitness, influencing population dynamics (Lord et al., 2021; 50 

Meiselman et al., 2018; Teder & Kaasik, 2023). Given the ecological importance of reproduction, 51 

understanding how combined stressors impact female fitness provides insights into broader ecological 52 

consequences. Studying the impact of combined stressors is especially biologically relevant for natural 53 

populations, where multiple stressors often co-occur and present unique challenges to reproduction (Boyd 54 

et al., 2018; Gunderson et al., 2017). 55 

The model organism Drosophila melanogaster offers valuable insights into stress responses, as it is well-56 

studied under single stressors, such as hypoxia and extreme temperatures (T. Gorr et al., 2006; T. A. Gorr 57 

et al., 2004; Hoffmann et al., 1997). Drosophila larvae are naturally adapted to hypoxic conditions in 58 

fermenting fruits and thus, exhibit physiological resilience that highlights the role of genetic background 59 

in coping with environmental stress (Lee et al., 2019; Valko et al., 2022). However, the interaction 60 

between stressors such as hypoxia and cold remains underexplored, as does the extent to which genetic 61 

variability influences these responses. In Drosophila , different genotypes exhibit varied responses to 62 

individual stressors such as hypoxia or cold, with some showing heightened resilience and others 63 

demonstrating significant declines in performance (Lazzaro et al., 2008; Mackay et al., 2012; Singh, 64 

2019; Weber et al., 2012). Certain genotypes may exhibit synergistic effects, where combined stressors 65 

amplify physiological or reproductive declines, while others demonstrate tolerance or compensatory 66 

responses. Incorporating genetic background into studies of environmental stress can improve predictions 67

This study aimed to describe the effects of hypoxia and low temperature individually and their interaction 70 

on both the physiological and reproductive traits of Drosophila . Key metrics, including Respiratory 71 

Quotient, Critical Thermal Maximum, and body mass, were assessed alongside reproductive success 72 

metrics like fertility and oogenesis progression. By examining genetic variability’s role in responses to 73 

combined stressors, this research aims to better understand how genetic variation shapes physiological and 74 

reproductive plasticity under interacting environmental stressors. These findings are critical for 75 

understanding resilience and plasticity in natural populations, with implications for understanding how 76 

genotype-by-environment interactions shape responses to global climate change. 77 

2. Methods 78 

2.1. Genetic Background 79 

The Drosophila Genome Reference Panel (DGRP) (Huang et al., 2014) consists of over 200 fully 80 

sequenced lines derived from a natural population in North Carolina (Mackay et al., 2012). These lines 81 

exhibit extensive phenotypic and genotypic diversity across a wide range of traits. From this panel, we 82 

focused on two previous experimental manipulations related to our perturbations. While no study 83 

manipulated oxygen concentration directly, a previous study treated the flies with a drug known as 84 

paraquat through their diet, to induce oxidative stress and measured survival across all 200 lines (Weber 85 

et al., 2012). These two stressors are known to have overlapped genetic and molecular pathways, with 86 

hypoxia generating ROS and subsequent oxidative damage as a consequence of low oxygen (Zhao and 87 

Haddad 2012). A second study measured chill coma recovery time following 3 hours under ice (Mackay 88 

et al., 2012). Based on the results from these two studies, we selected a subset of five DGRP lines that 89 

had a range of paraquat survival and chill coma recovery times representing the distribution of values 90 

across each experiment. Specifically, we selected lines DGRP-42 (BDSC_25193), DGRP-57 91

2.2. Treatments 95 

To select a non-lethal stressful low temperature, we conducted pilot experiments and compared fertility for 96 

a different DGRP-217 (BDSC_28154) line at 14℃, 16℃, and 18℃. The temperature that resulted in the 97 

highest mean per female across these temperatures (18℃) was selected for subsequent experiments as the 98 

low temperature (see Supplemental figure S2).Because this pilot was conducted on DGRP-217, which was 99 

not included in the main experiment, the optimal low temperature may differ for the focal lines given the 100 

genotype-dependent responses observed throughout this study. Additionally, 25℃ was used as a control 101 

temperature since it is the standard temperature used in Drosophila melanogaster rearing in laboratory 102 

environments. Oxygen availability was selected based on previous studies on survivorship at different 103 

oxygen concentrations (Zhou et al., 2007). Specifically, we selected 8% oxygen throughout development, 104 

which was where the egg-to-adult survivorship was comparable with the temperature treatments (survival 105 

reduction of 20%). We selected oxygen levels of 21% O 2 and 8% O 2 as normoxia and hypoxia, respectively. 106 

Custom-made chambers (BioSpherix) were used to control oxygen levels and were placed into incubators 107 

to control temperature. Certified tanks from AirGas, with either 21% or 8% oxygen, were used to flush the 108 

chambers for twenty minutes, every 12 hours, and every time the chambers were opened. Each chamber 109 

had a small USB powered fan attached, to help with the air circulation within the chamber. 110 

2.3. Fly husbandry 111 

All lines were maintained at ± 25ºC with a photoperiod of 12:12 (light:dark) in an incubator (Percival 112 

DR41-VL). Flies were reared on standard cornmeal-sugar-yeast-agar media (Bloomington Drosophila 113 

Stock Center recipe) in polypropylene enclosures. Stocks were routinely authentication via a RFLP-based 114 

protocol using custom markers for these stocks (Shiran et al., 2026). 115

2.4. Genetic Crosses 116 

A total of 200 genetic crosses were set up, 20 per genetic background per treatment. An average of 10 117 

mutant females and 5 DGRP males age-matched (2 days old, post eclosion from pupal case) were used for 118 

the crosses. These genetic crosses were allocated to one of the four different treatment combinations: 119 

control (Normoxia + Control temperature), hypoxia (Hypoxia + Control temperature), low temperature 120 

(Normoxia + Low temperature), or combined (Hypoxia + Low temperature). The F 1 generation was then 121 

measured across six parameters: Respiratory Quotient (RQ), Critical Thermal Maximum (CT max ), body 122 

mass, fertility, stages of oogenesis, and cell death (Fig. 1). 123 

2.5. Physiology 124 

Respiratory Quotient (RQ). The respiratory quotient (RQ) was calculated as the ratio of CO₂ produced to O₂ 125 

consumed. In this study, both CO₂ and O₂ were measured in individual flies at 10 days post-eclosion. This 126 

age was selected because, as previous research shows, metabolic rates remain relatively stable in 127 

Drosophila melanogaster at this stage (Arking et al., 1988; Hulbert et al., 2004; Mockett et al., 2001; 128 

Promislow & Haselkorn, 2002; Van Voorhies et al., 2003, 2004). RQ was determined using closed-system 129 

respirometry following the method outlined by DeVries and Appel (2013). Briefly, flies were weighed both 130 

before and after incubation. Each fly was placed in an individual 1 mL syringe serving as a respirometry 131 

chamber, flushed with dry, CO₂-free air, sealed, and incubated in the dark at the treatment temperature for 4 132 

hours (and no longer than 5 hours, to avoid starvation stress). 133

After incubation, a 0.25 mL air sample from each chamber was injected into a respirometry system for 134 

analysis, and data was collected with ExpeData software (Sable Systems, North Las Vegas, NV, USA). 135 

Calculations involved converting the recorded data into units of mL/min, integrating the CO₂ and O₂ peaks, 136 

and standardizing by body mass to determine total CO₂ production or O₂ consumption per chamber. RQ 137 

was then calculated as the ratio of CO₂ produced to O₂ consumed. To ensure accuracy, metabolic rates were 138 

measured for at least 10 female flies per treatment per genetic background. Any RQ values exceeding 1.5, 139 

which was associated with flies that appeared dead during measurement, were excluded from analysis (n = 140 

20). 141 

Critical thermal maximum ( CT max ). Ten-day-old flies were tested in a custom-designed, microprocessor-142 

controlled incubator that maintained a constant heating or cooling rate (Hu & Appel, 2004). Each fly was 143 

individually weighed and placed in a small chamber inside the incubator, along with an additional open 144 

chamber containing water to minimize desiccation. The temperature was gradually increased at a rate of 145 

0.1°C per minute, and flies were monitored for knockdown. CT max was defined as the highest temperature 146 

at which a fly was knocked down, unable to right itself, but still able to recover afterward, following the 147 

methods of Sponsler and Appel (1991). CT max measurements were taken from at least 15–20 females per 148 

treatment, and genetic background. 149 

Body mass was measured in freshly eclosed females (age 0 days post-eclosion) to capture whole-organism 150 

mass immediately following development under each treatment. Females were anesthetized with CO₂, 151 

placed individually in pre-tared weighing boats, and weighed once each. Body mass was recorded to the 152 

nearest 0.00001g. The tare function in the balance was used before and after each individual measurement. 153 

All weights were taken using a Sartorius 1712 MP8 balance. A minimum of 10 females per treatment and 154 

genetic background were used for these measurements. This metric was included as an additional 155 

physiological measure to assess treatment associated differences in whole-organism mass allocation 156 

following development under hypoxia and temperature treatments. 157

2.6. Reproduction 158 

F1 fertility. For 7 days, all flies were cleared from each cross vial, and the number of adult females and 159 

males was recorded. F 1 fertility was calculated by dividing the total adult offspring per vial by the number 160 

of females used in the cross. 161 

F2 Fertility. Ten virgin females from the F 1 crosses were mated with 5 males from the same DGRP line 162 

used on the F 1 cross (Fig. 1). These F 2 crosses were placed into an incubator and reared at 25°C. The 163 

crosses were transferred into new vials every 2 days for 8 days (A, B, C, D), and the number of adults was 164 

recorded daily for 7 days for each vial. F 2 fertility was calculated by dividing the total adult offspring across 165 

the 8 days by the number of females used in the cross. 166 

Oogenesis Progression and Apoptosis. Using previous studies by Rivera-Rincón et al. (2024), three time 167 

points (age in days post-eclosion of pupal case) were selected to compare the differences in oogenesis and 168 

apoptosis due to changes in temperature and oxygen availability: early (age 0), mid (age 2), and late (age 169 

5). The early time point consisted of only oocytes at the germarium stage. Mid-time point was defined as 170 

consisting of oocytes in all stages, with its majority at developmental stages 1-7. The late time point was 171 

defined as consisting of oocytes in all stages with its majority at the developmental stages 12-14 (Fig. 2). 172 

Dissections of the adult flies were performed in the morning for consistency across time points and 173 

samples, ensuring that no more than 20 minutes passed between the first dissection and its fixation to avoid 174

2.7. Image Analysis. 179 

Using the oocyte developmental stages described by Jia et al. (2016), oocytes were pooled into five 180 

developmental time points: Germarium, stages 1 – 7, stages 8 – 10, stage 11, and stages 12-14 (Fig. 2). 181 

Through the Jupyter platform (Kluyver et al., 2016), the Scikit-image (v0.22.0) (Van der Walt et al., 2014) 182 

and the Napari (Chiu et al., 2022) packages were used to standardize each DAPI image in terms of size and 183 

brightness and transform it to black and white for better nuclei identification. Annotation of the images was 184 

performed using a trained AI model through the APEER platform (Dang et al., 2021), which was then used 185 

to identify, characterize, and count oocytes from the image data. At least 50 objects from each class 186 

(developmental stages and background) were individually annotated in a randomly selected subset of 187 

images for AI model training. The model was updated with new and corrected labels on each iteration until 188 

the detection accuracy exceeded 95%. The AI model was then used to create an instance segmentation for 189 

each image, to detect and identify individual objects (characterize), and to segment them as separate 190 

instances (quantify). The segmentation was inspected, and images with poor characterization due to blurry 191 

backgrounds or overlapping objects that impeded accurate characterization were removed (n=3). The 192 

number of oocytes per stage was standardized with the number of ovarioles present per ovary. Using the 193 

extension “Fiji” of the software, ImageJ (Schindelin et al., 2012), each image was split into three channels, 194 

and using the third (green) channel only, TUNEL brightness was used to determine the presence or absence 195 

of DNA-fragmentation per oocyte. 196

aiming to focus on the contrasting response profiles among a defined subset of DGRP lines selected a199

linear mixed-effects models fitted with the ‘lmer’ function from the “lme4” package (v.1.1-34, Bates et al. 201 

(2014)). Oxygen availability, temperature, and genetic background were included as fixed effects along 202 

with their interactions. Vial was included as a random effect to account for non-independence among 203 

individuals reared together ( e.g., Physiology (1|vial) + Oxygen*Temperature* Genetic Background ). For F 2204 

fertility, mixed-effects models were also used, with vial number included as a random effect and day as a 205 

blocking factor to account for temporal variation in offspring counts ( e.g., Fertility (1|vial) + (1|Day) + 206 

Oxygen*Temperature* Genetic Background ). 207 

The number of oocytes was analyzed using a zero-inflated negative binomial mixed-effects model, which is 208 

appropriate for overdispersed count data with excess zeros. The model was implemented using the R 209 

package ‘glmmTMB’ v1.1.9 (Brooks et al. 2017; McGillycuddy et al. 2025). The model included fixed 210 

effects for Stage, Timepoint, Temperature, Oxygen, and Stock, as well as their relevant interaction terms. 211 

Ovary identity was included as a random intercept, to account for non-independence among observations 212 

from the same ovary, and the zero-inflation component was modeled as a function of Stage to allow the 213 

probability of structural zeros to vary across developmental stages. Oocyte cell death (TUNEL assay) was 214 

analyzed using a binomial generalized linear mixed-effects model with a logit link function. The response 215 

was modeled as the number of TUNEL-positive and TUNEL-negative oocytes. Fixed effects included stage 216

3. Results 230 

A total of 3,487 females were used to measure F 1 fertility. From the 29,668 flies collected across 366 231 

replicates and treatments combinations, 1,680 females were used to measure respiratory quotients (RQ), 232 

critical thermal maximum ( CT max ), body mass, and F 2 fertility. In addition, 453 individual ovaries were 233 

dissected across treatments, time points, and genetic backgrounds (GB’s) to measure oogenesis and oocyte 234 

cell death (see Supplemental Table S1). For clarity, we refer to the four experimental conditions as control, 235 

hypoxia only, low temperature only, and combined treatment. Because oxygen availability and temperature 236 

were analyzed as crossed factors, responses under the combined treatment correspond to interaction effects 237 

between these factors. 238

4. Discussion 396 

Understanding how organisms respond to environmental stressors is critical for predicting their tolerance 397 

and adaptability in changing ecosystems. While many studies investigate the effects of individual 398 

stressors, natural environments often present organisms with multiple simultaneous challenges that 399 

interact in complex ways. These interactions can lead to synergistic, antagonistic, or additive effects, 400 

which may significantly alter physiological and reproductive outcomes. In this study, we explored the 401 

combined effects of hypoxia and low temperature on physiological traits, fertility, and reproduction in 402

Drosophila melanogaster across five different genetic backgrounds. By examining responses to both 403 

single and combined stressors, we aimed to better understand how genetic variation shapes reproductive 404 

and physiological responses and identify the unique challenges posed by multiple environmental 405 

pressures. 406 

4.1 Physiological and reproductive stress response is influenced by genetic background 407 

The results confirm a genetic influence on the variation in metabolic rate, thermal tolerance, and body 408 

mass under hypoxia and low temperature. Across most genetic backgrounds, hypoxia generally led to 409 

increased respiratory quotient (RQ) values, indicating a shift in metabolic pathways likely toward 410 

carbohydrate metabolism. This aligns with findings from Van Voorhies (2009), who reported increasing 411 

RQ values in Drosophila under hypoxic conditions. However, this study also noted exceptions in certain 412 

genotypes, such as the ones observed in this study for DGRP-57 and DGRP-491, which exhibited 413 

minimal changes. This suggests that genetic diversity among individuals may lead to differential 414 

responses, a phenomenon also observed in other ectothermic species (Hoffmann et al., 2013) where some 415 

genotypes also demonstrated metabolic tolerance under stress. 416 

The thermal tolerance, measured by CT max , was significantly influenced by genetic background, with 417 

interactions between temperature and oxygen driving some of the observed changes in this trait. Most 418 

genotypes exhibited increased CT max under hypoxia, particularly at control temperature, suggesting a 419 

potential stress-buffering effect of low oxygen on heat tolerance. This observation follows the patterns 420 

described previously for Drosophila melanogaster (C Teague et al., 2017), where a positive correlation 421 

between hypoxia and thermal tolerance was described. Which could indicate that in certain genetic 422 

backgrounds, hypoxia may confer an advantage in extreme heat scenarios, highlighting the need for 423 

further research into species-specific responses to simultaneous thermal and oxygen stress. 424 

Body mass at eclosion was strongly influenced by genetic background and environmental conditions, 425 

with hypoxia often associated with increased mass relative to normoxia. This pattern contrasts with the 426

well-established negative relationship between oxygen availability and structural body size reported in 427 

Drosophila and other insects (Czarnoleski et al., 2023; Heinrich et al., 2011; Noguchi et al., 2022). 428 

Importantly, most previous studies have quantified body size using morphological traits such as wing area 429 

or pupal case dimensions, whereas the present study measured whole-body mass immediately following 430 

eclosion. 431 

Body mass at this developmental stage may reflect a short-term physiological state rather than fixed 432 

morphological size, as it can be influenced by hydration status, gut contents, and allocation of internal 433 

reserves. Newly eclosed flies also undergo rapid physiological adjustments, including meconium 434 

excretion, which can introduce additional variability in mass measurements. Consequently, the observed 435 

increases in mass under hypoxia should not be interpreted as evidence that low oxygen promotes 436 

increased structural body size. 437 

These results suggest that hypoxia and temperature may alter patterns of mass allocation or physiological 438 

condition depending on the genetic background (C. Teague et al., 2017). Differences among genetic 439 

backgrounds indicate that some genotypes may respond to hypoxic development by retaining greater 440 

internal reserves or water content, whereas others show reduced mass under the same conditions. Future 441 

studies combining morphological size measurements with mass and compositional analyses would help to 442 

better understand these genetic differences. In particular, measuring dry mass alongside wet mass would 443 

help disentangle structural size changes from differences in water content or internal reserves, providing a 444 

clearer physiological interpretation of the body mass responses observed here. 445

Hypoxia negatively impacted F 1 fertility across all genetic backgrounds, consistent with existing evidence 447 

that low oxygen disrupts metabolic and physiological functions critical for survival (Callier et al., 2015; 448 

Lee et al., 2019; Lighton, 2007; Peck & Maddrell, 2005; Polan et al., 2020; Rand et al., 2018; Romero et 449 

al., 2007; Van Voorhies, 2009; Zhou et al., 2007). In contrast, low temperature alone did not exhibit a 450

Comparing F 1 and F 2 fertility across genotypes, we observed notable differences in reproductive outcome. 455

The effects of hypoxia and low temperature on oogenesis were highly variable across genetic 472 

backgrounds, time point, and developmental stages. At the early time point (0 days post-eclosion), 473 

hypoxia had an effect on the oocyte number across genetic backgrounds, indicating that initial 474

5. Conclusions 573 

These findings underscore the significant role of genetic background in shaping stress responses, 574 

particularly when multiple stressors are involved. The observed changes in metabolic pathways, such as 575 

higher respiratory quotient (RQ) values under hypoxia, suggest that certain genotypes may adaptively adjust 576 

to low oxygen levels, while others, like DGRP-491, show limited metabolic changes, indicating varying 577 

levels of tolerance to environmental stress. Similarly, thermal tolerance was found to be influenced by 578 

genetic background and the interplay between oxygen levels and temperature. Some genotypes exhibited 579 

increased tolerance to heat under hypoxia, suggesting that low oxygen may help buffer stress in extreme 580 

heat conditions. 581 

Fertility under combined hypoxia and low temperature in the F 1 generation revealed that while low 582 

temperature alone was not significantly impacted, the combination of both stressors magnified the effects, 583 

particularly in genotypes like DGRP-491, which showed consistent susceptibility to stress. Fertility in the 584 

F2 generation further emphasized the importance of genetic background, with some genotypes maintaining 585 

high reproductive output under stress, while others experienced declines, particularly when exposed to both 586 

hypoxia and low temperature. Oogenesis progression showed varied responses across genotypes, with 587 

hypoxia affecting oocyte counts in a genotype- and time point manner. Early developmental stages saw 588 

reduced oocyte counts for most genotypes, but certain lines, such as DGRP-508, demonstrated adaptive 589 

responses. This variation highlights the need to consider genetic diversity when assessing reproductive 590 

tolerance under environmental stress. Additionally, the TUNEL assay results provided insights into the 591 

reproductive costs of stress, showing that hypoxia influenced apoptosis in a genotype-specific manner, 592 

prioritizing survival over reproduction in stress-sensitive lines. Overall, this study enhances our 593 

understanding of how environmental changes involving multiple stressors interact with genetic factors to 594
