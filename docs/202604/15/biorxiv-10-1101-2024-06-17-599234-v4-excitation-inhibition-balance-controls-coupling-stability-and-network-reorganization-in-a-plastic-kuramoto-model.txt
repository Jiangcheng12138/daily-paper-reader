Abstract 13 

Sleep and rest, characterized by synchronized neuronal activity that emerges under large 14 shifts in the excitation–inhibition balance, are crucial for synaptic reorganization in the 15 brain. However, the network dynamics that permit rewiring without erasing stable 16 connections remain unclear. To address this question, we extended a Kuramoto framework 17 with excitation–inhibition balance by adding Hebbian and homeostatic plasticity. The 18 resulting model showed that networks with robust inhibition consistently exhibited 19 desynchronized dynamics and stable couplings. In contrast, networks with weaker 20 inhibition exhibited a bistable regime, in which couplings of intermediate strengths 21 fluctuated while strong couplings remained stable. These findings suggest that the 22 dynamic interplay between network activity and plasticity selectively stabilizes stronger 23 connections while permitting flexible reorganization of weaker ones, providing a potential 24 mechanism for network reorganization during sleep and rest. 25

Introduction 26 

Empirical evidence suggests that brain activity during sleep and rest promotes memory 27 consolidation, knowledge abstraction, and creative insight 1,2 through the reorganization of 28 neural circuits 3,4 . Such reorganization should rely on mechanisms distinct from those 29 involved in learning during task performance. Indeed, synaptic plasticity during sleep and 30 rest improves task performance 5,6 .31 

Neuronal synchronization occurs in two distinct states. Collective neuronal firing is 32 desynchronized during active wakefulness, including task engagement, movement, and 33 attentional states, as well as during rapid eye movement (REM) sleep, but more 34 synchronized during quiet wakefulness and non-REM (NREM) sleep. Throughout the 35 paper, we refer to these regimes descriptively as wake-like/desynchronized and sleep/rest-36 like/synchronized or bistable states, depending on the model dynamics being discussed. 37 The transition between these states is associated with shifts in the excitation–inhibition 38 (EI) balance. Firing rates of inhibitory neurons and synaptic conductance of inhibitory 39 synapses are significantly elevated during wake-like states, while those of excitatory 40 synapses show comparatively smaller changes 7–10 . These observations raise the possibility 41 that, during sleep/rest-like states, EI-balance-dependent changes in collective neuronal 42 synchronization interact with synaptic plasticity to reshape network structure. However, 43 detailed mechanisms by which neuronal synchronization in sleep/rest-like states reshapes 44 network structure through plasticity remain unclear. 45 

Collective synchronization of oscillators, including neurons, has been studied using 46 coupled-oscillator models. Among these, the Kuramoto model is one of the simplest 47 frameworks 11,12 , and numerous variants have been proposed. One variant, the EI-48 Kuramoto model, incorporates the EI balance by dividing oscillators into excitatory and 49

We have 50 previously shown that tuning these four strengths toggles the network between 51 synchronized, desynchronized, and bistable states, potentially reflecting a neuronal 52 synchronization mechanism 14 . That study, however, kept all coupling weights—defined in 53 this paper as pair-specific interaction strengths—fixed and identical, and therefore could 54 not address how EI-balance-dependent dynamical states shape synaptic coupling through 55 plasticity over time. 56 

In this study, we focused on Hebbian and homeostatic plasticity. Hebbian plasticity, based 57 on the principle “fire together, wire together,” strengthens synapses between neurons that 58 fire in close temporal proximity 15 . In contrast, homeostatic plasticity regulates neuronal 59 excitability and synaptic strength to maintain network stability, thereby preventing 60 excessive excitation or quiescence 16 . The interplay between these plasticity mechanisms 61 enables the emergence of diverse neuronal functions and firing patterns 17–20 . Previous 62 studies have incorporated plasticity into Kuramoto models, enabling coupling weights to 63 adapt based on the oscillator activity to examine their impact on system synchronization 21– 64 31 . However, these studies focused on promoting synchronization under a single dynamic 65 state. How distinct collective dynamics, controlled by the EI balance, differentially shape 66 synaptic plasticity remains unexplored. 67 

To address these gaps, we developed a plastic EI (pEI)-Kuramoto model in which 68 excitatory–excitatory couplings undergo Hebbian potentiation and homeostatic regulation. 69 We found that strong inhibition produced a desynchronized regime with relatively stable 70 couplings, whereas weaker inhibition produced a bistable regime in which intermediate-71 strength couplings fluctuated while strong couplings remained stable. Results 76 

Establishment of the pEI-Kuramoto model 77 

The pEI-Kuramoto model comprised two oscillator groups: excitatory units, referred to as 78 excUnits, and inhibitory units, referred to as inhUnits. These specific terms clarify that the 79 units represented excitatory and inhibitory factors in this model, rather than actual neurons 80 or synapses. Four interaction strengths were assigned between the groups, with only 81 excitatory-excitatory couplings ( Jee ) being distributed and plastic. The other interaction 82 strengths, K ii (repulsive strength between inhUnits), K ei (attractive strength from excUnits 83 to inhUnits), and K ie (repulsive strength from inhUnits to excUnits) were constant across 84 time and across unit pairs (Figure 1A; see Materials and Methods). The interactions are 85 modeled as zero-phase-lag sine functions. The interactions from excUnits are purely 86 attractive, while those from inhUnits are purely repulsive. Accordingly, the terms 87 "excitatory" and "inhibitory" are used synonymously with "attractive" and "repulsive," 88 respectively. 89 

First, we investigated how the distribution of pair-specific excitatory-excitatory coupling 90 weights Jee affected the dynamics of the pEI-Kuramoto model in the absence of plasticity 91 (Figure 1B and C). In our previous study, we demonstrated that the Kuramoto model with 92 EI balance (EI-Kuramoto model) exhibits three distinct dynamic states—synchronized, 93 bistable, and desynchronized—depending on the balance between the constant values of 94 the interaction strengths 14 . In this study, Jee followed a Gaussian distribution with a mean 95 of μJee and a standard deviation of σJee , where σJee was set to 0, 1/10, and 1/2 of μJee 96 (Figure 1B). Next, we incorporated plasticity into Jee by applying Hebbian potentiation and homeostatic 102 regularization plasticity rules (see Materials and Methods). Among the various methods 103 available for implementing Hebbian plasticity in the Kuramoto model, we initially 104 selected the simplest method: the cosine function (Figure S1A; see Materials and 105 Methods) 21–23,26,30,31 . In this process, when a pair of excUnits exhibits closer phases, their 106 

Jee coupling weight is potentiated. Simultaneously, the Jee distribution was rescaled to 107 maintain the initial mean and standard deviation, thereby preventing overpotentiation 108 through homeostatic regularization plasticity (see Materials and Methods). The system 109 dynamics were evaluated using the parameter set ( μJee = 1, σJee = 0.1, K ei = 3, K ie = 3, Kii =110 3), at which the system exhibited a desynchronized state in the absence of Jee plasticity. 111 Under the cosine rule, unit pairs with smaller phase differences acquire a larger Jee weight. 112 This rule causes the J ee distribution, which was initially Gaussian, to become bimodal 113 (Figure S1B). Plasticity also influences the distribution of oscillator units, resulting in 114 bimodal distributions of both phase and phase difference (Figure S1C), consistent with 115 previous studies that incorporated the Hebbian rule into the Kuramoto model 21,26 . This 116 bimodal distribution of the coupling weight deviates from the long-tailed skewed 117 distributions typically observed in biological synaptic weights 10,32,33 .118

Coupling fluctuations increase in the bistable state 134 

We next investigated how distinct dynamic states associated with different EI balances 135 affect coupling weights. To this end, we altered the EI balance of the pEI-Kuramoto 136 model by manipulating the interaction strength from inhUnits. We unified K ie and Kii into 137 a single parameter, K i, to simplify the simulation, following the approach used in our 138 previous study 14 . We then examined how the Jee fluctuations were associated with the K i139 values, quantified as the standard deviation of Jee over time (S[ Jee ]t ; see Materials and 140 Methods) (Figure 2A and B). We determined the system’s dynamic state by analyzing the 141 time course of Rexc (Figure 2C and D), as previously described 14 .142

Factors determining coupling weight properties 220 

To identify the parameters that determine the individual Jee coupling properties (E[ Jee ]t221 and S[ Jee ]t ) shown in Figure 3, we explored the candidate parameters (natural frequency 222 difference Δω , initial phase difference Δθ , and initial Jee values of each excUnit pair) that 223 may contribute to Jee fluctuations (Figure 4). We colored the dots of E[ Jee ]t vs. S[ Jee ]t224 distribution (Figure 3C) according to each parameter value (Figure 4A–C). For statistical 225 comparison, couplings were roughly categorized based on whether E[ Jee ]t exceeded 0.018 226 (high-E[ Jee ]t group) or not (low-E[ Jee ]t group), because the correlation between E[ Jee ]t and 227 S[ Jee ]t reversed around this point (Figure 4D–F). 228 

Regarding differences in the natural frequency Δω , most pairs in the high (≥0.018) E[ Jee ]t229 group had relatively small Δω , whereas pairs with larger Δω tended to be in the low 230 (<0.018) E[ Jee ]t group (Figure 4A and 4D). In the low-E[ Jee ]t group, S[ Jee ]t seemingly 231 increased with Δω (Figure 4A). The initial phase differences Δθ between the pairs were 232 also significantly different between the high- and low-E [ Jee ]t groups (Figure 4B and E). In 233 the high-E[ Jee ]t group, most pairs exhibited small initial Δθ , whereas in the low-E[ Jee ]t234 group, the initial Δθ was uniformly distributed. Additionally, we examined the effect of 235 the initial Jee coupling weight, which showed no noticeable bias (Figure 4C) and followed 236 a normal distribution in both the high and low-E[ J ee ]t groups (Figure 4F). Considering 237 these results, pairs with small differences in natural frequency and initial phase positions 238 maintained more stable and larger Jee weights (Figure 4G and H). Conversely, the initial 239 

Jee weight was not an important factor in determining the stability and strength of the 240 couplings. 241

Cyclic modulation of inhibition 295 

Finally, we varied K i to mimic the sleep – wake cycle 39,40 . Jee fluctuated (Figure 5A, Video 296 S5) in response to K i modulation (Figure 5B, top panel). Rexc alternated between the 297 desynchronized and bistable states (Figure 5B, middle), with corresponding changes in 298 E{S[ Jee ]t }n (Figure 5B, bottom). When K i was high, the system was in a desynchronized 299 state, and E{S[ Jee ]t } n was small. Conversely, when K i was low, the system exhibited a 300 bistable state, and E{S[ Jee ]t } n increased. These dynamics can be considered as modeling 301 how the circuit structure is stabilized during the wake-like state and destabilized during 302 the sleep/rest-like state, thereby promoting circuit reorganization. 303

The main conceptual advance of this study is to show that EI balance can determine not 305 only the synchronization state of a network but also whether plastic couplings are 306 stabilized or rendered labile. In the model, strong inhibition favored a desynchronized 307 regime with relatively stable couplings, whereas weaker inhibition produced a bistable 308 regime that selectively destabilized intermediate couplings while preserving the strongest 309

Although the sleep/rest-like state is often associated with synchrony, it is not uniformly 312 synchronized. Near the transition between synchronized and desynchronized states, the 313 dynamics tend to become irregular or weakly stable, as captured by two-population 314 coupled-oscillator models 41,42 . Sleep onset can be formalized as a noisy saddle-node 315 bifurcation 43 , and macroscopic brain-state transitions are often argued to occur near the 316 bifurcation point 44,45 . Beyond the transitions between wakefulness and sleep, even within 317 sleep, the microarchitecture exhibits intrinsic instability, such as the NREM-REM sleep 318 cycle 3,4,39,40 , the cyclic alternating pattern in NREM sleep 46 , UP–DOWN state 319 alternations 47,48 , and microarousals 49 , which may reflect switching among synchronized 320 and desynchronized states. These fluctuation-driven transitions align with the bistable 321 regime of our model under reduced inhibition. 322 

Preserving robust couplings while randomizing weaker ones simplifies the network, 323 rendering it sparse. The plasticity parameters used here, which yield a long-tailed 324 coupling-weight distribution (Figure 1E and F) similar to that observed in the brain 10,32,33 325 and mirror the EI balance during state transitions and in sleep/rest-like states 7–10 , appear to 326 facilitate network sparsification. The selective preservation of strong synaptic connections 327 is also frequently observed in the brain 35–37 and is understood through molecular 328 mechanisms, including the dynamics of NMDA receptors 5,50 and actin filaments 51 . Our 329 results introduce a complementary, system-level mechanism for sustaining strong 330 coupling, viewed through the dynamics of collective oscillators. Additionally, our results 331 may be mechanistically linked to the systems-level observation that only a subset of 332 neuronal populations remain stable while others drift 52–54 . While the Kuramoto model provides conceptual insight, it is substantially simpler than 388 biological neural circuits. The model reduces neuronal interactions to attractive and 389 repulsive forces, neglecting pulse-based communication, conduction delays, and complex 390 network topology 62 . Additionally, our model simplifies the complexity of 391 neuroplasticity—excluding neurotransmitters 63 , inhibitory plasticity 64,65 , and 392 adaptation 66 —and does not distinguish between specific sleep stages (e.g., NREM vs. 393 REM), which are thought to play distinct roles in memory processing and circuit 394 reorganization 3,4 . Addressing these limitations is essential for a comprehensive 395 understanding of neural dynamics. 396 

Future work can extend the framework by introducing explicit evaluation and stabilization 397 signals that select among reorganized circuits 57,67 , potentially linking fluctuation-driven 398 reorganization to selective retention during sleep and rest. Another important direction is 399 to examine how synchrony transitions influence interregional plasticity between 400 functionally distinct networks, such as hippocampus and cortex 68 , thereby linking circuit-401 level reorganization to systems-level consolidation. 402
