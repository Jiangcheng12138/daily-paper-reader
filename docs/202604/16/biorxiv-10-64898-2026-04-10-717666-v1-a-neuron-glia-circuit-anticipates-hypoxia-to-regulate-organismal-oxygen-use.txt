Abstract 22 

Organisms must regulate metabolic resources such as oxygen (O 2) and nutrients despite 23 

environmental variability and the energetic costs of their own actions 1–3 . Such regulation can occur 24 

reactively, through homeostatic corrections of recent imbalances, or predictively, through 25 

allostatic adjustments that anticipate future demand 4,5 . Predictive regulation is particularly 26 

important because metabolic resources often continue to be consumed for seconds to minutes after 27 

motor actions cease as tissues repay incurred costs, making it advantageous to prevent depletion 28 

before it occurs 6. However, the cellular and circuit mechanisms for allostatic control remain largely 29 

unknown 5,7,8 . Using whole-brain neuronal and astroglial imaging and O 2 measurements in 30 

behaving zebrafish, we identified a noradrenergic–astroglial circuit that detects, anticipates, and 31 

prevents internal O 2 depletion. We found that swimming exacerbated internal hypoxia with a 32 

multi-second delay, but behavioral adaptations occurred before such self-generated hypoxia 33 

manifested, suggesting predictive control, confirmed using computational modeling. 34 

Noradrenergic neurons in the nucleus of the solitary tract directly detected brain hypoxia and 35 

received efference copies of swimming actions; these inputs summed at the level of membrane 36 

voltage to increase spiking and norepinephrine release when actions and resource scarcity co-37 

occurred. Astroglia integrated noradrenergic input into prolonged Ca 2+ elevation that tracked the 38 

O2 cost of recent actions and thereby predicted O 2 debt relative to O 2 availability, rising ~8 s before 39 

O2 fell. This astroglial prediction reorganized brain-wide activity to suppress locomotion and 40 

promote respiration, preempting O 2 depletion. Silencing noradrenergic neurons or astroglial 41 

signaling abolished these hypoxia coping behaviors, whereas selective activation evoked them. 42 

This neuronal–astroglial mechanism constitutes a predictive control system that integrates 43 

physiological state with behavioral intent to avert metabolic crisis, revealing a cellular substrate 44 

for proactive energy management. 45

Main 46 

Animals must regulate their physiology and behavior in response to changing environmental and 47 

internal resources 9–14 . Both aquatic and terrestrial animals consume nutrients and O 2 as they run, 48 

swim, or fly 15–18 , while also experiencing varying resource availability caused by environmental 19–  

> 49
> 21

and bodily factors 22 . Survival under stressors like hypoxic conditions, such as fish in stagnant 50 

water 23,24 , thus requires brain mechanisms to estimate the physiological risk of performing 51 

vigorous physical actions when resources are limited, and regulate bodily O 2 levels for future use 52 

(Fig. 1a). Motor actions rapidly consume ATP, but consume O 2 gradually with a multi-second 53 

lag 6,25 , requiring that effective behavioral regulation be based on both current reserves and 54 

anticipated future O 2 depletion. Thus, although animals might in principle just react to current 55 

internal O 2 levels, an alternative and more effective strategy would be to also make a prediction of 56 

how internal O 2 levels will vary in the future as a result of recent actions and preemptively adjust 57 

behavior to minimize anticipated hypoxic stress 3,8,26 . However, little is known about behavioral 58 

control strategies that predict the physiological risk of actions and their cellular and circuit-level 59 

mechanisms. 60 

> 61

Zebrafish larvae adapt behavior to varying oxygen levels 62 

To examine the effects of hypoxia on the brain and behavior, we established a preparation in larval 63 

zebrafish that included whole-brain calcium imaging of neurons and astroglia, while monitoring 64 

fictive swimming using electrophysiology, and measuring O 2 levels in both the water bath and in 65 

the brain using Clark-type O 2 microelectrodes with sub-second response times 27,28 (Fig. 1b; see 66 

Methods). In addition, using separate recording electrodes placed on the skin above the head, we 67 

also captured respiration-relevant electrical signals, which were synchronized with bodily 68 

movements associated with respiration 29 , including movements of the operculum, jaw, and 69 

pectoral fins (Extended Data Fig. 1a-c). Perfusion of a hypoxic solution (4 mg/l O 2 compared to 8 70 

mg/l O 2 for normoxia) resulted in a drop of O 2 levels by ~50% in the bath and ~80% inside the 71 

brain by ~5 minutes (Fig. 1c), a difference that is likely due to consumption of O 2 by the animal. 72 

> 73

hypoxia resulted in an increase of respiration rate, a decrease of swim frequency with an increased 75 

occurrence of long pauses (5 seconds or more of no swimming 32 ), more vigorous swim bouts, and 76

Reactive versus predictive control reflected by fish behaviors 82 

In addition to the effects of environmental O 2 levels, swimming caused a ~2% per 10 seconds 83 

decrease in brain pO 2 (Fig. 1g and Extended Data Fig. 1i,j). This decrease lagged multiple seconds 84 

behind locomotion, continuing for several seconds after swimming ceased, and recovered on a 85 

timescale of ~10 seconds (Fig. 1g,h and Extended Data Fig. 1i-k). The effect was observed both 86 

during fictive swimming in paralyzed fish and in restrained, non-paralyzed fish (Extended Data 87 

Fig. 1i), consistent with O 2 consumption arising from neuronal activity in the brain and spinal 88 

cord 33–35 . The decrease was larger in non-paralyzed fish, presumably due to muscle contractions. 89 

> 90

To mitigate the effects of hypoxia, one possible behavioral strategy is to stop swimming when 91 

brain pO 2 drops below a threshold, a form of reactive control (Fig. 1i). However, because pO 292 

continues to decline for several seconds after swimming ends—as it is consumed by oxidative 93 

phosphorylation to regenerate metabolic resources like ATP in muscle and nerve cells 6,36,37 , and 94 

as O 2 equilibrates diffusively throughout tissues 38 (Fig. 1h and Extended Data Fig. 1j)—fish might 95 

also benefit from proactively adjusting behavior in anticipation of such delayed O 2 depletion. This 96 

would use an alternative predictive control strategy, in which recent swimming history is used to 97 

estimate forthcoming O 2 decline and preemptively pause locomotion (Fig. 1j and Extended Data 98 

Fig. 1l,m). These two strategies—reactive and predictive—provide complementary hypotheses for 99 

how behavioral control of O 2 is organized. 100 

> 101

To test which strategy best describes fish behavior, we fitted swim data with two models (Fig. 102 

1i,j): (1) a reactive controller in which swim actions depend only on current pO 2, and (2) a 103 

predictive controller in which fish integrate both recent pO 2 and past swim actions to estimate 104 

future pO 2 and select subsequent actions. The predictive model outperformed the reactive model 105 

with a high level of variance explained (>40%), and explained fourfold more variance in behavior 106 

(Fig. 1k,l; see Methods). The fitted kernels revealed two key components: (1) a direct dependence 107

Together, these analyses defined the computational requirements of a predictive controller: a 115 

representation of current brain pO 2, an integrator of recent motor output that predicts future pO 2116 

decreases, a multiplicative interaction between these signals to represent expenditure relative to 117 

availability, and downstream control of swimming and respiration to preemptively compensate for 118 

O2 debt before it manifests. Guided by this theoretical framework, we next examined the neural 119 

substrates implementing predictive O 2 regulation. 120

Neuronal and astroglial representations of brain pO 2122 

In order to identify the control mechanism through which prediction of pO 2 regulated behavior, 123 

we performed whole-brain light-sheet calcium imaging of neurons and astroglia in response to 124 

changes in pO 2 (Fig. 1m; Tg(elavl3:H2B-jGCaMP7f) fish for neurons and Tg(gfap:GCaMP6f) fish 125 

for astroglia). Based on the predictive model, we hypothesized that adaptations to hypoxia are 126 

driven by brain activity that accounts for two factors on different timescales: a slow factor on the 127 

timescale of tens of seconds reflecting changes in pO 2 that tend to arise from ambient 128 

environmental O 2 level fluctuations, and a fast factor on the timescale of seconds reflecting O 2-129 

consuming swim behaviors. We therefore decomposed brain-wide neuronal and astroglial calcium 130 

signals into a slow component (F slow ) which mainly reflected pO 2-related baseline calcium 131 

dynamics, and a fast component (ΔF/F) which captured behavior-related calcium transients (Fig. 132 

1n and Extended Data Fig. 2a; r(swim) represents rank correlation between swim vigor and ΔF/F; 133 

see Methods). 134

Brainstem noradrenergic neurons directly detect intracranial pO 2179 

To examine whether and how hypoxia activates noradrenergic populations in the NTS (i.e. NE-180 

MO), we imaged Tg(th1:Gal4);Tg(UAS:GCaMP6f) fish, which express GCaMP6f in all 181 

noradrenergic neurons in the brainstem including NE-MO, the locus coeruleus (LC) and area 182 

postrema (AP), and sympathetic ganglia (SG, in the peripheral nervous system) (Fig. 2a). Most of 183 

these noradrenergic neurons showed hypoxia-related signals, but to different extents (Fig. 2b-d). 184 

NE-MO and SG neurons showed large F slow increases during hypoxia, LC showed moderate 185 

increases, and AP showed no change (Fig. 2c,d). Moreover, hypoxia enhanced the swim-related 186 

fast dynamics (∆F/F) in NE-MO and LC, but SG and AP neurons showed little or no change (Fig. 187 

2c,e). Conversely, treatment with hyperoxic water (100% increase in bath O 2 relative to normoxia) 188 

led to a decrease of F slow in NE-MO, SG and LC, but not in AP (Fig. 2f). Strikingly, we found that 189 

Fslow in these areas correlated negatively with brain pO 2, with strongest correlations in NE-MO 190 

and SG, less in LC, and none in AP (Fig. 2g,h). These results imply a link between brain pO 2 and 191 

the activity of noradrenergic neurons in the brain, particularly NE-MO. 192

NE-MO neurons integrate brain pO 2 and motor efference copies, consistent with predictive 235 

signaling for anticipated hypoxic stress 236 

Hypoxia elevated the membrane excitability of NE-MO neurons and also increased fast calcium 237 

dynamics, which was coupled with swimming (Fig. 2c). Therefore, we hypothesized that brain 238 

pO 2 would impact NE-MO spiking in response to motor signals. To test this, we performed 239 

simultaneous electrophysiological recordings of NE-MO and fictive swimming, and found a co-240 

occurrence of excitatory postsynaptic potentials (EPSPs) in NE-MO and individual swim bouts 241 

(Fig. 3a), with swim bouts preceding the EPSPs by ~58 ms (Extended Data Fig. 5a), indicating 242 

that NE-MO cells receive excitatory motor efference copies. Consistent with increased fast 243 

calcium activity in hypoxia (Fig. 2c,e and Extended Data Fig. 5b-d), hypoxia led to an elevation 244 

of swim-coupled phasic spiking in NE-MO neurons (Fig. 3b–d). Therefore, the hypoxia-induced 245 

Vm rise appeared to make NE-MO cells more responsive to synaptic inputs, including motor 246 

efference copies (Fig. 3e and Extended Data Fig. 5h-k). We further tested this possibility by 247 

injecting current into NE-MO neurons to elevate their V m, which also led to an increase of swim-248 

coupled spike rates (Extended Data Fig. 5e-g). Therefore, NE-MO can integrate current pO 2 with 249 

motor efference copies, consistent with the predictive model combining both pO 2 and swim history 250 

(Fig. 1j-l and Extended Data Fig. 1n,o). Through this approximately multiplicative interaction of 251 

hypoxia levels (~ 1/pO 2) and motor signals via the membrane potential-to-spiking transformation 252 

(Fig. 3d,e), NE-MO spiking may represent predicted hypoxic stress, defined as the decrease in O 2253 

levels relative to prior O 2 availability or −∆pO 2/pO 2, and thereby encode the urgency to preserve 254 

O2 under limited supply. Moreover, such a multiplicative interaction between hypoxia levels and 255 

swimming at NE-MO matched the prediction of the behavioral model (Fig. 1k). Remarkably, 256 

during normoxia, the slope of the approximately-linear relationship between swim vigor and NE-257 

MO spiking was ~0.41 of that during hypoxia (Fig. 3d), closely matching the corresponding ratio 258 

of swim-history weights in the behavioral model (~0.44; Fig. 1k, inset). This concordance suggests 259 

that NE-MO activity implements the swim-history computation posited by the predictive model to 260 

transform current O 2 availability and behavior into anticipated hypoxic stress. 261

Astroglial calcium is predictive of future hypoxic stress 263 

The NE-MO representation of predicted hypoxic stress is transient (Fig. 3f, top), and thus 264 

insufficient to explain the prolonged integration of swim history in the predictive model (Fig. 1k). 265 

NE-MO spiking also does not, by itself, account for the intermittent, repeated ~18 second-long 266 

periods of locomotion suppression in hypoxia (Extended Data Fig. 1h), whose function may be to 267 

allow for sufficient time for pO 2 recovery, which is also slow (Fig. 1h). We therefore tested 268 

whether astroglia, as a slow integrator of NE-MO activity 32 , could better predict the time course 269 

of future hypoxic stress from current swims. We examined swim-induced pO 2 changes together 270 

with astroglial activity during sets of swims with similar vigor in normoxia and hypoxia (swim-271 

matched trials) and found that astroglial calcium changes were similar in shape to the inverse of 272 

swim-induced O 2 changes and, importantly, were shifted ~8 seconds earlier in time (Fig. 3f, 273 

bottom). Furthermore, swim events with similar vigor evoked larger astroglial calcium activity 274 

under hypoxia as compared to normoxia (Fig. 3g), consistent with the larger noradrenergic drive 275 

during hypoxia (Fig. 3c). Swim-induced pO 2 decrease (−∆pO 2) was similar in the two conditions 276 

(Fig. 3h, left), but hypoxic stress (−∆pO 2/pO 2) was larger during hypoxia due to the lower baseline 277 

level of pO 2, mimicking astroglial scaling between the conditions (Fig. 3f and Fig. 3h, right). 278 

Moreover, hypoxic stress had a similar time course (Fig. 3i) and magnitude to astroglial dynamics 279 

(Fig. 3j). Similar to NE-MO (Fig. 3d), swim-triggered astroglial activation exhibited a hypoxia-280 

dependent gain increase so that astroglial calcium signals encode a combination of swim vigor and 281 

depth of hypoxia, captured by the functional form F(z)~[1/pO 2]×[integrated swim vigor] (Fig. 3j), 282 

suggesting both of them transform current O 2 availability and behavior into anticipated hypoxic 283 

stress. Using LOWESS regression to compare astroglial and pO 2 dynamics, we confirmed that, 284 

remarkably, astroglial activity accurately forecasted hypoxic stress for individual swims ~8 285 

seconds in advance with high explained variances (0.51 in normoxia, 0.63 in hypoxia) (Fig. 3k,l). 286 

Moreover, optogenetic activation of astroglia in the hindbrain suppressed swimming and increased 287 

respiration (Extended Data Fig. 5l,m). These results show that astroglial calcium signaling is 288 

predictive of future hypoxic stress, i.e. predictive of pO 2 decreases relative to pO 2 availability. By 289 

encoding hypoxic stress several seconds in advance, and by its function of suppressing swim and 290 

promoting respiration, astroglial calcium signaling provides a time window to enable animals to 291 

preemptively adjust their behavior to minimize future internal hypoxia (Supplementary Video 1). 292

Control theory accounts for neuron-glia dynamics 294 

Based on NE-MO and astroglial dynamics predicting hypoxic stress (Fig. 3a-l), we extended the 295 

simple behavioral model (Fig. 1j) into a circuit implementation (Fig. 3m,n). This implementation 296 

was derived from a control model that optimized for a balance between brain pO 2 and locomotion 297 

(Methods). This model recapitulated (1) the fish’s behavioral patterns and pO 2 fluctuations during 298 

normoxia and hypoxia, (2) a multiplication of pO 2 and swimming that resembles NE-MO activity, 299 

(3) an integration that resembles astroglial activity, and (4) occasional high vigor swims (reflected 300 

as an increase of swim bout duration both in simulation and real data) (Extended Data Fig. 1n,o). 301 

In simulation, this model recapitulated the change of swim patterns and noradrenergic-astroglial 302 

dynamics during normoxia and hypoxia (Fig. 3o,p). In summary, the optimal predictive model and 303 

its circuit implementation can account for the observed NE-MO-astroglial dynamics that underlies 304 

behavioral adaptation to hypoxia. 305 

> 306

A NE-MO-astroglial circuit mediates the hypoxia-induced brain and behavioral state 307 

Since NE-MO shows the largest increases in both F slow and ∆F/F in response to hypoxia, and can 308 

be directly activated by hypoxia, we asked whether the effects of hypoxia on brain state and 309 

behavior are dependent on NE-MO. We first ablated NE-MO using a two-photon laser in 310 

Tg(th1:Gal4);Tg(UAS:NTR-mCherry) fish, and found that the effects of hypoxia on neuronal and 311 

astroglial fast dynamics were almost completely abolished after NE-MO ablation (Fig. 4a-c and 312 

Extended Data Fig. 6a-g), while brain dynamics during normoxia were less affected. This result is 313 

consistent with a brain-wide modulatory role for NE. Specifically, hypoxia-induced functional 314 

relations among the neural clusters were suppressed by NE-MO ablation (Fig. 4d-f). For example, 315 

the normoxia and hypoxia ∆F/F manifolds of the midbrain- versus DRN-cluster and midbrain-316 

versus forebrain-cluster were separated before NE-MO ablation, but became indistinguishable 317 

after ablation (Fig. 4e,f; see Methods). In summary, although neuronal slow dynamics retained 318 

some O 2 dependence after the ablation (Extended Data Fig. 6b,f), potentially due to direct effects 319 

on cellular metabolism 39,51 , NE-MO mediated the hypoxia-induced modulations of brain-wide 320 

∆F/F dynamics. 321

Discussion 359 

To survive in variable environments, animals continuously regulate their physiology and behavior 360 

through reactive mechanisms (i.e. homeostasis) to correct for deviations in response to 361 

environmental or internal changes 7,56 . Examples of reactive regulation include sweating to 362 

maintain body temperature in hot weather, and drinking water after dehydration 57 . In addition to 363 

reactive homeostasis, animals also use anticipatory control (i.e. allostasis) to preempt 364 

physiological challenges 58 . For example, insulin is released upon tasting food, before nutrients 365 

enter the digestive system 59 , and ingestion of water causes rapid suppression of vasopressin release 366 

prior to the resulting change of blood osmolarity, thus preventing excessive renal water retention 60 .367 

However, aside from a small number of well-studied cases, neural mechanisms for many forms of 368 

physiological regulation, especially predictive control, are poorly understood 3,58 . Here, we 369 

discovered that both behavior and brain dynamics of O 2 regulation align with a predictive control 370 

strategy, through which future O 2 consumption is estimated from swim history and used to 371 

proactively suppress swimming during resource scarcity. Our findings establish a mechanism of 372 

predictive O 2 regulation, supported by convergent evidence across behavior, circuits, cells, and 373 

theory. 374 

> 375

Although allostasis is often defined as predictive regulation 4,5 , it also has been framed as regulation 376 

via context-dependent set points 61 . Consistent with the predictive view, astroglial calcium is 377 

predictive of future hypoxia. Moreover, astroglial calcium integrates recent motor output scaled 378 

by recently experienced hypoxia to suppress future swimming, and this scaling is analogous to a 379 

context-dependent shift in set point for O 2 preservation. Thus, our findings bridge these 380 

complementary views of allostatic control at the mechanistic level. 381 

> 382

As animals encounter changing environmental conditions and physiological states, their behavior 383 

and physiology must adapt 9,62 . Despite a limited capacity for anaerobic metabolism, hypoxia is a 384 

threat to all cells of an animal and requires extensive behavioral adaptations and a global 385 

reconfiguration of brain dynamics. Neuromodulatory systems are well-suited to orchestrate such 386 

changes because they project across large swaths of the brain and can influence global brain 387 

dynamics 63,64 , sometimes through astrocytes 43,65–70 , which aligns with our discovery that a 388 

noradrenergic-astroglial system encodes and integrates hypoxic stress over ethologically-relevant 389

Relative to peripheral chemoreceptors, such as the mammalian carotid bodies, central neurons for 406

the neuron-glia coupling within breathing circuits that produce rhythmic activity 82,83 ; these circuits 411 

have been mapped out at the regional scale in mammals and include the pre-Bötzinger 412

increases immediately upon the onset of exercise—before any measurable changes in arterial O 2,414

exemplifies a predictive regulation of O 2 demands similar to the strategy identified in our study. 416 

Additionally, land-roaming mammals exist in conditions that are less susceptible to large 417 

fluctuations in environmental O 2 levels compared to fish, although burrowing mammals and those 418
