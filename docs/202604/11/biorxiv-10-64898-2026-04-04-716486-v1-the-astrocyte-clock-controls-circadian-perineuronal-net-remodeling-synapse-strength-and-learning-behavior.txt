Abstract 14 The circadian clock controls a vast array of cellular and organismal functions, from the 15 molecular scale to behavior. While each cell is regimented by a cell-autonomous clock, few 16 studies in the brain have dissected the circuit and behavioral contributions of cell-specific 17 clocks. Relatedly, astrocytes are now known to play key roles in regulating synaptic function, 18 circuit activity and behavior, but whether these functions are guided by astrocyte-autonomous 19 clocks is unknown. Here, we report that post-natal deletion of the critical circadian clock gene 20 

Bmal1 in astrocytes, which abrogates core clock function in a cell type specific manner, induced 21 expression of genes related to extracellular matrix (ECM) production, maintenance, and 22 remodeling. Circadian variations have been shown in a specific ECM structure, perineuronal 23 nets (PNNs), which are implicated in synaptic function and plasticity. In astrocyte-specific Bmal1 24 knockouts, hippocampal PNN abundance was decreased, and the circadian rhythm of these 25 structures was also abolished. In line with evidence implicating PNNs, and the ECM in general, 26 in synaptic function and plasticity, we found that astrocyte-specific Bmal1 KO mice had 27 increased synaptic strength but blunted long term potentiation (LTP), as well as impaired 28 learning and memory performance in a novel object recognition task. Taken together, these 29 findings suggest that the astrocyte circadian clock regulates circadian rhythms in perineuronal 30 net abundance as well as synaptic plasticity and behavioral learning and memory. 31

Introduction 33 Circadian rhythms are endogenous biological rhythms entrained by zeitgebers (environmental 34 cues, including light), and have been shown to be important for myriad biological processes. In 35 addition to their best-known function as regulators of the sleep-wake cycle, circadian rhythms 36 have been shown to have effects on metabolism, endocrine processes, cellular stress response, 37 and many other core biological functions. The circadian clock has also been implicated in the 38 control of neuronal function in the setting of learning and memory 1,2,3 . Multiple studies have 39 shown a relationship between circadian rhythms and formation and consolidation of associative 40 learning and declarative memories (for review see 4 ). Consistently, hippocampal synaptic 41 plasticity varies over the circadian cycle, strengthening during the active period of mice, and can 42 be disrupted by genetic deletion of circadian clock genes in neurons 1,5,6,7 . However, cellular-43 molecular mechanisms of how circadian rhythms impact learning and memory remain a gap in 44 our knowledge. 45 Anatomically, the central circadian clock is located in the Suprachiasmatic Nucleus (SCN) of the 46 hypothalamus. While the SCN synchronizes circadian function to the light-dark cycle, 47 remarkably, multiple brain cell types outside of the SCN have been shown to have their own 48 endogenous circadian rhythms. In particular, glial cells such as astrocytes show daily 49 rhythmicity in many brain areas 1,7,8 . Like in neurons, these cell-autonomous circadian rhythms 50 are governed by the core circadian clock, which is dependent on the function of BMAL1 (Brain 51 and muscle arnt-like, or Arntl), the key transcription factor of the circadian clock 9,10 . BMAL1 52 binds with either CLOCK or NPAS2 to form a nuclear heterodimer which drives circadian 53 transcription of many genes, including Per (Per1 and Per2 ), Cry (Cry1 and Cry2 ), and REV-ERB 54 (Nr1d1 and Nr1d2 ). These negative-limb clock proteins then provide negative feedback 55 regulation of BMAL1/CLOCK on a 24-hour cycle, forming the transcriptional basis of the 56 circadian rhythm. While the deletion of Bmal1 has been used to probe the 59 effects of cell-type-specific clock disruption, little is known at this time about the roles of the 60 astrocyte-specific clock in brain functions or disorders. 61 Astrocytes have emerged as powerful regulators of synaptic function and plasticity, network 62 activity, and behavior 22 . Astrocytes exhibit robust circadian rhythms 23 , especially in the SCN, 63 where they regulate circadian behaviors 24,25,26,27 . In the hippocampus, circadian rhythms in 64 astrocyte morphology and function are associated with circadian rhythms of synaptic plasticity 65 and learning and memory 7 . The ECM is the noncellular scaffolding component present within 73 all tissues and organs, including the brain. It provides crucial chemical and mechanical input to 74 cell behavior. Accordingly, astrocyte Bmal1 knockout mice showed 97 reduction in the number of hippocampal PNNs, as well as synaptic electrophysiological 98 changes, including blunting of hippocampal LTP, and decreased performance in a hippocampal-99 dependent learning and memory task. Taken together, these findings suggest that the circadian 100 clock in astrocytes regulates the extracellular matrix and perineuronal nets, and plays a key role 101 in controlling the molecular and cellular processes underlying behavioral learning. 102

Astrocyte Bmal1 regulates ECM-related gene expression in a circadian-dependent 203 

manner 204 To investigate the impact of BMAL1 in astrocytes, we employed a line of transgenic mice with 205 inducible Cre-dependent selective deletion of Bmal1 in astrocytes ( Aldh1l1 -Cre ERT2 ;Bmal1 f/f ), 206 which we have previously characterized 28,29,47 . This mouse line presents the advantage of being 207 able to isolate the impact of astrocyte circadian rhythm on downstream processes without the 208 confounding factors presented by deletion of circadian regulation in other cell types. We 209 analyzed existing bulk RNA sequencing data from cortical tissue from these animals 47 ,210 employing a permissive cutoff of genes upregulated in Cre + (astrocyte Bmal1 knockout 211 (Bmal1 KO )) mice with an uncorrected P<0.05 (Table S1). We then interrogated this list for gene 212 ontology using the DAVID database 48 (Fig. 1A). Our analysis revealed several enriched 213 pathways, including a cluster of genes involved in extracellular matrix (ECM) production and 214 remodeling (Fig. 1B). We focused on these pathways due to previous studies implicating ECM 215 in regulation of synaptic plasticity 53,54 . The gene with the most prominent degree of change was 216 

Mmp14 , a matrix metalloproteinase that regulates ECM degradation 55 , which was upregulated 217 by 2.4-fold in the transcriptome of Bmal1 KO mice at ZT 6 relative to their Cre-negative littermate 218 controls. Discussion 302 In this manuscript, we show that the master circadian regulator Bmal1 in astrocytes impacts 303 neuronal gene expression, anatomy, circuitry, and behavior related to learning and memory. 304 Astrocytic Bmal1 deletion changes the expression of multiple genes involved in extracellular 305 matrix production, maintenance, and turnover, many of which are regulated in a circadian 306 manner. At the protein level, we found that astrocytic Bmal1 deletion reduces the expression of 307 perineuronal nets in the hippocampus. At the level of neuronal circuitry, we show that Bmal1 308 astrocytic deletion results in enhanced synaptic strength and a decrease in synaptic potentiation 309 in a circuit key for learning and memory, consistent with PNN removal lifting a brake on 310 plasticity, and in line with prior findings 61 . Consistently, at the level of behavior, we find that 311 astrocytic Bmal1 deletion results in decreased performance in hippocampal learning and 312 memory. Taken together, these results show that the core clock gene BMAL1 in astrocytes 313 controls a cascade of downstream effects on neuroanatomical structures and circuitry involved 314 in learning and memory, as well as on behavioral tests of such memory activity. While neuronal 315 circadian rhythms are known to regulate daily variation in hippocampal synaptic plasticity and 316 learning and memory, our results provide evidence that the astrocyte circadian clock contributes 317 to these processes, potentially through regulation of PNNs. 318 Previous work from our group and others has demonstrated key functions on BMAL1, and the 319 circadian clock in general, in the regulation of astrocyte gene expression and reactivity state. 320 Tissue-specific deletion of Bmal1 can be used as a strategy to probe the effects of cell-type-321 specific clock disruption, though it should be noted that BMAL1 exerts some non-circadian 322 effects 21 which could result in off-target effects in this model. Astrocytes show robust circadian 323 rhythms in clock gene expression in culture, and the astrocyte clock has been implicated in 324 regulation of specific functions such as ATP and glutamate release and reuptake 30 .
