# Abstract 13 

The locus coeruleus (LC) -noradrenaline (NA) system is a central component of the brain ’s response to 14 

arousal and stress . Y et how LC activity contributes to the cellular response profiles observed during natural 15 

arousal remains unc lear . Here, we directly compared natural arousal with selective LC activation in mouse 16 

CA1 , using physiologically titrated optogenetics, fiber photometry of NA and calcium signals, chronic two -17 

photon imaging, and behavioral monitoring.  While natural arousal robustly activate d astrocytes, pyramidal 18 

cells, and interneurons, direct LC stimulation reveal ed a striking divergence in cellular response polarity 19 

and sensitivity. At levels of LC activation that correspond to arousal and moderate stress , we observe d20 

strong and  reliable  calcium responses in astrocytes, whereas pyramidal neurons and interneurons remain ed 21 

largely unaffected. Only at high -intensity LC stimulation d id neurons exhibit a response, characterized by 22 

broad population -level inhibition of both pyramidal cells and interneurons, alongside the transient activation 23 

of a n interneuron subpopulation that occupied distinct laminar positions in CA1 . Thus, LC -driven NA release 24 

produces cell -specific effects in hippocampal CA1 that are distinct from and , at the population level for 25 

neurons even opposite to , cellular dynamics during natural arousal . Together, our results reveal a 26 

divergence in how astrocytes and neurons respond to LC -driven NA release and  suggest that noradrenergic 27 

effects in the hippocampus at moderate levels of arousal and stress are predominantly mediated by 28 

astrocytes. 29 

# Introduction 30 

The arousal system in the brain is fundamental for our ability to flexibly recognize and respond to changes in 31 

the environment during rest, exploration, and stress. Readouts of arousal such as pupil dilations or unspecific 32 

motor activity have recently been shown to explain a large fraction of neuronal activity across the brain 33 

(McCormick et al., 2020; Musall et al., 2019; Stringer et al., 2019) . These global signatures suggest that 34 

neuromodulatory systems play a key role in shaping arousal -related cellular activity. However, the specific 35 

contributions of individual neuromodulatory subsystems to these signals remain incompletely understood. 36 

One of the central candidates is the locus coeruleus (LC), a small nucleus located in the pons and the principal 37 

source of noradrenaline (NA) in the brain. Through its highly divergent projections, LC broadcasts signals 38 

throughout virtually all regions of the central nervous system  (Adér et al., 1980; Chandler et al., 2014) . LC 39 

activity is tightly linked to changes in arousal, cognitive adaptation, and stress  (Bouret and Sara, 2005; Devilbiss 40 

and Waterhouse, 2011; Vazey et al., 2018) , and correlates closely with pupil dilations in both rodents and 41 

primates  (Joshi et al., 2016; Privitera et al., 2020; Reimer et al., 2016) . Despite this central role, it remains 42 

unclear how LC -mediated NA release acts at the cellular level in downstream regions and to what extent these 43 

direct effects contribute to the circuit dynamics responses observed during natural arousal. 44

## 2

The hippocampus (HC) is a stress -sensitive brain region that receives noradrenergic input exclusively from the 45 

LC. Its well -characterized circuitry and dense noradrenergic innervation make it an ideal system to study the 46 

cellular actions of LC -mediated NA -release (LC -NA). NA release strongly influences hippocampal function 47 

particularly during states of arousal or stress  (Berridge and Waterhouse, 2003; Clewett et al., 2018; Giustino et 48 

al., 2020; Grueschow et al., 2020; Murchison et al., 2004; Roozendaal and McGaugh, 2011) . Hippocampal 49 

activity is also strongly modulated during arousal and movement: pyramidal cells increase firing rates during 50 

locomotion in a speed -dependent manner  (Czurkó et al., 1999; Fuhrmann et al., 2015; McNaughton et al., 51 

1983) , interneurons are robustly activated or, for a subset, inhibited during arousal  (Arriaga and Han, 2017; 52 

Geiller et al., 2020; Lapray et al., 2012; Yang et al., 2025) , and astrocytes exhibit slow centripetal activations 53 

upon movement and arousal  (Rupprecht et al., 2024) . These diverse, cell type -specific responses highlight that 54 

arousal profoundly shapes hippocampal dynamics. Yet it is not known to what degree these effects are driven 55 

directly by LC -NA release versus other arousal -related pathways. 56 

Early work by Segal and Bloom in the 1970s showed that NA can strongly influence hippocampal activity, 57 

reporting NA -mediated inhibition in the hippocampus using electrical stimulation of the LC and iontophoretic 58 

application of NA in awake rats  (Segal and Bloom, 1976, 1974a, 1974b) . However, these pioneering 59 

experiments lacked the cell -type specificity required to disentangle the effects on the local circuit. More recent 60 

work, primarily in brain slices, has characterized the responses to NA of specific hippocampal cell types, 61 

describing an NA -mediated increase of excitability in pyramidal cells  (Bacon et al., 2020; Church et al., 2019; 62 

Madison and Nicoll, 1986) , an activation of interneurons  (Bergles et al., 1996; Hillman et al., 2009; Madison 63 

and Nicoll, 1988) , and a very prominent activation of astrocytes  (Gao et al., 2016; Lefton et al., 2025) . Yet most 64 

of these studies relied on ex vivo slices or exogenous NA application. Therefore, these findings demonstrate 65 

the strong influence of LC -NA on hippocampal circuits, but they do not reveal how physiological LC -NA release 66 

modulates identified cell types in vivo , nor to which extent these effects mimic arousal -driven activity in awake 67 

animals. 68 

To clarify how LC -NA shapes hippocampal activity and to determine its contribution to the arousal response, 69 

we systematically investigated the effects of both natural arousal and selective LC -NA stimulation on 70 

hippocampal cell types in awake mice. We first assessed physiologically relevant NA levels during acute stress 71 

and arousal, and titrated optogenetic LC stimulation accordingly. Using a combination of LC stimulation, fiber 72 

photometry, chronic two -photon imaging in CA1, and behavioral monitoring, we then compared the response 73 

of pyramidal cells, interneurons, and astrocytes to natural arousal and selective LC -NA stimulation. This 74 

approach provides a comprehensive, cell type -specific analysis of LC -NA effects in vivo and enables a direct 75 

evaluation of the contribution of LC -NA to hippocampal arousal responses. 76 

# Results 77 

Titration of LC stimulation to physiological NA levels 78 

LC activity is known to scale with wakefulness and arousal, and reaches its highest firing rates during stress 79 

(Akaike, 1982; Aston -Jones and Bloom, 1981; Carter et al., 2010) . To relate LC activity to physiologically 80 

relevant levels of NA release, we measured NA dynamics in hippocampal CA1 during both acute stress and 81 

LC stimulation using fiber photometry. We used virally induced expression of the optogenetic actuator 82 

ChrimsonR in the LC of DBH -iCre animals and of NA sensors (GRAB -NE2m in Figure 1; GRAB -NE1m and 83 

nLightG in Figure S1 -4) in neurons of the HC (Figure 1A -C). We subjected animals to a short 10 -s tail lift and a 84 

3-s mild foot shock (Figure 1D). As previously reported  (Feng et al., 2024, 2019) , the NA level increased during 85 

the tail lift and gradually decayed afterwards (Figure 1E). The foot shock triggered NA signals with comparable 86 

peak amplitudes that, however, remained elevated throughout the time recorded (Figure 1F), in line with 87 

published work  (Li et al., 2023) . As additional control for potential movement artifacts, we repeated all 88 

experiments with expression of GFP instead of NA sensors but found no signal change in response to either 89 

stressor in these animals (Figure S1 -1). To estimate the maximal range of NA release, animals were subjected 90 

to the cold forced swim paradigm, a stressor known to potently activate NA release  (Ebner and Singewald, 91 

2007; Privitera et al., 2024) . This paradigm, compared to the other stressors, induced substantially higher peak 92

LC stimulation and natural stress elicit similar responses in astrocytes but distinct responses in both 122 

pyramidal cells and interneurons 123 

To assess cellular responses in the HC, we next recorded from astrocytes, a cell type known for their sensitivity 124 

to stress and their abundant expression of adrenergic receptors  (Hertz et al., 2010) . We first used fiber 125 

photometry to record astrocytic Ca 2+ responses (GFAP -GCaMP6s). When animals were subjected to stressors, 126 

we found a surge in astrocytic Ca 2+ during the acute stress exposure that went back to baseline on a timescale 127 

of seconds (Figure 1J,K). Similarly, both LC stimulation paradigms (5 Hz and 20 Hz) triggered elevations in 128 

astrocytic Ca 2+ (Figure 1L,M). Peak astrocyte Ca 2+ signals (Figure 1N) followed a similar trend as peak NA 129 

levels (Figure 1I), albeit with a seemingly smaller dynamic range. This similarity of response profiles suggests 130 

a close relationship between stress, NA and astrocytic activation. 131 

Next, we investigated neuronal responses to LC stimulation and natural stress. To this end, we expressed Ca 2+ 132 

sensors in hippocampal pyramidal cells (CamKIIα -GCaMP8m) or GABAergic inhibitory neurons (mDlx -HBB -133 

GCaMP8m) and used fiber photometry to record neuronal bulk responses during the same conditions as before. 134 

Both neuronal cell types displayed elevated Ca 2+ signals during tail lift, predominantly at onset (Figure 1O,T), 135 

and during foot shock (Figure 1P,U). LC stimulation, in contrast, evoked a long -lasting decrease in Ca 2+ that 136 

scaled with the intensity of the stimulation (Figure 1Q,R,V,W). Therefore, unlike NA levels and signals recorded 137 

from astrocytes, bulk Ca 2+ signals from pyramidal cells and interneurons showed opposite responses to natural 138 

stress and optogenetically induced NA release (Figure 1S,X). Together, these fiber photometry results 139 

demonstrate a direct inhibitory effect of NA released from LC on neuronal activity, which, however, appears to 140 

be outweighed by other factors driving activity during natural stress. The increased neuronal activity during 141 

stress is therefore unlikely to be directly driven by release of NA upon LC activity. These analyses are, however, 142 

limited by the fact that fiber photometry reflects population -averaged signals and cannot resolve cell -specific 143 

contributions to neuronal or astrocytic activity, prompting us to examine single -cell activity patterns using two -144 

photon calcium imaging. 145

To compare the effects of LC stimulation and natural arousal, we first needed a reliable proxy for endogenous 187 

arousal. Earlier work has shown that both locomotion and pupil diameter can be used as proxies for arousal 188 

state  (McGinley et al., 2015; Shimaoka et al., 2018; Stringer et al., 2019) . In our head -fixed experiments, mice 189 

exhibited spontaneous behaviors that included episodes of both low arousal (resting) and high arousal 190 

(accompanied by running on the treadmill), monitored by camera recordings of body movements and pupil 191 

diameter. We and others have previously shown for head -fixed mice that face or body movement, more so than 192 

locomotion alone, is highly predictive of astrocytic activity and arousal  (Collins et al., 2023; Rupprecht et al., 193 

2024) , which we confirm here by predicting pupil diameter with body movement (Figure S2 -2). In addition, pupil 194 

recordings, more so than recordings of body movement, were prone to noise due to pupil dilations upon 195 

optogenetic LC stimulation (Figure S2 -3A) or eye movements. Interestingly, LC stimulation not only resulted 196 

in pupil dilations but also, for stronger LC stimulation, sometimes resulted in reduced bod y movement (Figure 197 

S2 -3B), consistent with previous reports of behavioral arrest during strong and prolonged LC stimulation (Carter 198 

et al., 2010). We therefore decided to use body movement as our main proxy of endogenous arousal. Together, 199 

our experimental paradigm enabled us to monitor arousal while recording cellular activity (see also 200 

Supplementary Movies 1, 2 and 3). 201 

We find that astrocytic calcium signals were largely synchronized across the field of view (FOV) during body 202 

movement and upon optogenetic LC stimulation (Figure 2D,E; Figure S2 -4A,B), consistent with previous 203 

findings in cortex and hippocampus  (Ding et al., 2013; Paukert et al., 2014; Rupprecht et al., 2024) . Astrocytic 204 

responses to body movement as well as responses to optogenetic LC stimulation during wakefulness and 205 

anesthesia exhibited a strictly positive modulation when averaged across cells within a session (Figure 2F,G; 206 

Figure S2 -5), but also on the level of individual astrocytes (Figure S2 -5A,B). These findings are consistent with 207 

our bulk fiber photometry recordings (Figure 1, Figure S2 -6) and reflect the well -described property of astrocytic 208 

populations as a slow and synchronized tracker of past arousal or LC activity  (Fedotova et al., 2023; Rupprecht 209 

et al., 2024) .210

Astrocytes exhibit diverse but stable cell -specific sensitivity to LC stimulation 231 

It is well -established that astrocytes can be activated by NA released from LC axons (Ding et al., 2013; Paukert 232 

et al., 2014). In agreement with this, we observe that virtually all astrocytes were activated by LC stimulation, 233 

albeit to varying degrees (Figure 3A). We wondered whether this degree of activation is a property that is 234 

specific to each astrocyte or whether it depends on other factors (e.g., the current state of the cell). We found 235 

that the current state of each cell (pre -stimulus ΔF/F level) explained only a small fraction of the variance of 236 

responses (Figure 3B; correlation r = 0.17). In contrast, the identity of a cell explained a much larger fraction of 237 

the variance, both within sessions (Figure 3C,D, r = 0.56, pooled across 4 mice) as well as across days (Figure 238 

3E, r = 0.61 ± 0.12, mean ± S.D. across 4 mice). This provides direct evidence for a consistent and cell -specific 239 

responsiveness to LC stimulation. In parallel, we observed that the modulation of the activity patterns of 240 

individual astrocytes by body movement (as a proxy for arousal) was also conserved across days for individual 241 

astrocytes (Figure 3F, r = 0.56 ± 0.29). These results complement the recently described morphological and 242 

transcriptomic diversity of astrocytic cell types  (Batiuk et al., 2020; Endo et al., 2022; Viana et al., 2023)  from a 243 

functional perspective, based on each cell's responsiveness to natural arousal and NA release. 244 

To investigate astrocytic response types in more depth, we closely inspected their response patterns to LC 245 

stimulations of varying intensity. We noticed that some astrocytes already responded strongly to stimulation of 246 

intermediate strength (5 Hz), while others were only activated upon strong LC stimulation (20 Hz) (Figure 3G). 247 

In a subset of experiments, we covered a large range of stimulus strengths (ranging from 1 Hz to 40 Hz), 248 

enabling us to visualize a sigmoidal activation curve for individual astrocytes as a function of stimulation 249 

strength. We found that the inflection points of these activation functions varied across cells (Figure 3H), 250 

reflecting a lower or higher sensitivity of individual cells. To quantify astrocytic sensitivity, we computed the ratio 251 

of the astrocytic response to an intermediate stimulation (R 5Hz ), normalized by its response to a strong 252 

stimulation (R 20Hz ) that we consider as an approximation of a saturating stimulus: S 5/20 = R 5Hz /R 20Hz (Figure 3I, 253 

top). Interestingly, this cell -specific sensitivity was a stable property across days for individual astrocytes (Figure 254 

3I, bottom). These results show that the diversity of astrocytes within hippocampal CA1 spans a large range of 255 

response sensitivities for LC stimulation, and that these cell -specific functional properties are stable and 256 

consistent within and across days. 257 

Astrocytic activity during natural arousal diverges from LC -evoked responses 258 

With astrocytes activated by both natural arousal and LC stimulation (Figure 1J -N), one might assume that cells 259 

which are more strongly activated by natural arousal and body movement would also be more strongly activated 260 

by LC stimulation. However, we did not find evidence for such a relationship (Figure 3J), indicating that the 261 

axes of natural arousal and LC stimulation align only weakly. Using sensitivity instead of LC response 262 

magnitude, we found a positive albeit relatively weak correlation with movement modulation (Figure S3 -1), 263 

indicating at least a partial alignment of astrocytic movement modulation with the sensitivity to LC stimulation. 264

we compared the similarity of astrocytic population patterns across different contexts: episodes of natural 266 

arousal with increased body movement, or LC stimulation of varying intensities (5 Hz or ≥20 Hz). First, to 267 

characterize population activity patterns, we sorted recordings according to the cellular response strength to 268 

LC stimulation. Interestingly, this sorting did not reveal any obvious alignment of responses to natural arousal 269 

during movement (Figure 3K). To quantify this observation, we systematically identified global astrocytic 270

the similarity between patterns during the same (e.g., movement vs. movement) or different conditions (e.g., 272

Pyramidal cells exhibit non -specific, uniform inhibition in response to LC stimulation 306 

We found that pyramidal cells were inhibited upon LC stimulation (Figure 1Q -S; Figure 2K), but this effect 307 

emerged only after averaging across neurons. We therefore wondered whether individual pyramidal cells might 308 

display heterogeneous response profiles, potentially reflecting differences in cell identity or pre -stimulation 309 

activity state. At the single -cell level, pyramidal cell activity initially appeared largely unsorted (Figure 2I), but 310 

visible patterns became apparent when cells were reordered by their LC -evoked activity change (Figure 4A) or 311 

by the timing of their activity peaks (Figure 4B). The stimulus -aligned representation (Figure 4A) suggests that 312 

LC stimulation results in both inhibited neurons, consistent with fiber photometry results (Figure 1Q -S), as well 313 

as a set of activated neurons. However, it is known that pyramidal cells in hippocampal CA1 are sequentially 314 

active even in the absence of external stimulation, a phenomenon described as "time cells"  (Eichenbaum, 315 

2014) . Sorting by sequential time bins indeed revealed this intrinsic feature of pyramidal cell activity (Figure 316 

4B), and also highlights that fewer neurons are preferentially active in the sequential time bins following LC 317 

stimulation (Figure 4C,D). These results indicate that the observed apparent activation of pyramidal cells is a 318 

residual signature of ongoing sequential dynamics, rather than a direct effect of LC stimulation. 319 

Next, we examined how LC stimulation influences the ongoing sequential dynamics of hippocampal pyramidal 320 

cells. One possibility is that LC activity amplifies existing neuronal patterns by increasing the activity of already -321 

active neurons while further suppressing less -active ones, consistent with ideas of an LC -mediated increase of 322 

neuronal signal -to -noise  (Berridge and Waterhouse, 2003; Bouret and Sara, 2002) . Using our single -cell two -323 

photon imaging dataset, we quantified the change of neuronal activity upon LC stimulation as a function of pre -324 

stimulus activity (Figure 4D). Interestingly, we find that the net inhibitory effect of strong LC stimulation ( ≥20 Hz) 325 

was uniform across all levels of pre -stimulus activity (Figure 4D; Figure S4 -1A,B). The same pattern was 326 

observed with moderate stimulation (5 Hz) (Figure S4 -1C). These analyses indicate that LC stimulation does 327 

not selectively modulate neurons according to their current activity state. Instead, it appears to exert a relatively 328 

uniform influence on pyramidal cells, irrespective of their ongoing activity. 329 

Next, we asked whether cell identity determines the strength or direction of a neuron's response to LC 330 

stimulation. We found that neurons that were inhibited (or seemingly activated) by LC stimulation did not show 331 

consistent response characteristics across repeated LC stimulations within the same imaging session (Figure 332 

4E,F). Consistent with this observation, patterns of activation or inactivation by LC stimulation were not stable 333 

across days when neurons were tracked across sessions (Figure 4G,H). These results further support the idea 334 

that pyramidal cells do not exhibit consistent cell -specific responses to LC stimulation. Instead, activation of LC 335 

appears to produce a broad, non -specific inhibition that is distributed across the population without targeting 336 

particular pyramidal cell subpopulations. 337 

To demonstrate that activity patterns of pyramidal cells were indeed meaningful despite the absence of specific 338 

responses to LC, we confirmed that the positive or negative modulation by body movement observed earlier 339 

(Figure S2 -4) was maintained also across days (Figure 4I). This stability suggests that specific pyramidal cell 340 

populations consistently respond to natural arousal (see also the example neurons in Figure 4G). However, 341 

neurons that were positively modulated by movement were not more likely to be positively modulated by LC 342 

stimulation (Figure 4J,K; response to LC stimulation, median ± S.D.: -0.5 ± 1.9 Hz for positively modulated and 343 

a significantly higher -0.3 ± 1.1 Hz for negatively modulated neurons; p < 10 -10 , Wilcoxon rank sum test). This 344 

analysis indicates that the cell -specific activity patterns of pyramidal cells in the hippocampus during arousal 345 

and movement are not mediated by LC activity. 346

Interneurons exhibit stable responses to LC stimulation and arousal 375

For interneurons, the cell -averaged responses exhibited two main temporal response components to LC 377 

stimulation that were particularly apparent after high -intensity stimulation ( ≥20 Hz): a transient activation, 378 

followed by a prolonged inactivation (Figure 2O). To characterize these responses at the single -cell level, we 379 

visualized interneurons activity evoked by strong stimulation (≥20 Hz), pooled across all sessions. When we 380 

sorted responses by the change in neuronal activity during the 10 -s stimulation window (Figure 5A, 3970 381 

responses from 1168 neurons, 10 sessions, 3 mice), we observed a continuum of response types, ranging from 382 

strongly activated neurons (Figure 5A, right/top) to spontaneously active interneurons that were inhibited by LC 383 

stimulation (Figure 5A, right/bottom). Similar effects for these and other analyses were observed in an additional 384 

animal which was analyzed separately due to prominent movement evoked by LC stimulation (Figure S5 -1; 385 

2378 responses, 561 neurons, 4 sessions); all further analyses, unless otherwise stated, were performed on 386 

animals without this movement confound. 387 

To assess the stability of interneuron response types, we systematically compared responses across LC 388 

stimulations. We observed that interneurons responded consistently across repeated stimulations within the 389 

same session ( ≥20 -Hz stimulations; Pearson's correlation coefficient, r = 0.32, p < 10 -10 ; Figure 5B). In line with 390 

that, the sorting of neurons from activated to inhibited was preserved across stimulations within a session 391 

(Figure 5C). Although responses to moderate LC stimulations (5 Hz) were weaker than those to strong LC 392 

stimulations ( ≥20 Hz) (Figure 2N), the response profiles across neurons were similar (correlation r = 0.31; Figure 393 

S5 -2), suggesting that effects seen for strong LC stimulations are likely to generalize also to regimes of weaker 394 

LC activation, for which our experimental data are noisier due to smaller response amplitudes. 395 

Next, we investigated whether single -cell responses to LC stimulation could be predicted by pre -stimulation 396 

activity. We observed that interneurons with lower pre -stimulation activity tended to be activated by LC 397 

stimulation, whereas those with higher activity were typically inhibited (Figure 5D; Figure S5 -3). This effect 398 

disappeared when random episodes of spontaneous activity without stimulation were analyzed (Figure 5D). 399 

This dependence of LC stimulation responses on pre -stimulation levels for interneurons could be driven by 400 

either the transient state of a cell prior to stimulation, or by inherent cell identity. To distinguish between these 401 

possibilities, we permuted trial identity for pre -stimulation levels. The effect persisted despite the permutation 402 

control (Figure S5 -3B). These analyses indicate that, while response magnitude can be explained by pre -403 

stimulus activity, it is equally well and more parsimoniously explained by cellular identity. 404 

Tracking cells across sessions, we found that LC -evoked response patterns of interneurons remained relatively 405 

stable across days for all animals (Figure 5E, correlation r = 0.53). In addition, we quantified the modulation of 406 

individual cell responses by body movement and observed an even higher across -session stability (Figure 5F, 407 

r = 0.81). Together, these results demonstrate that individual interneurons respond in a consistent and cell -408 

specific manner to both natural arousal and optogenetic LC stimulation. 409 

Dissociation between natural arousal and LC modulation in interneurons 410 

We next asked whether interneurons modulated in a specific manner by body movement exhibited similar 411 

responses to optogenetic activation of the LC. If the LC were a significant driver of arousal -related modulation 412 

of hippocampal interneurons, movement -related and LC -evoked responses should align for at least a subset of 413 

interneurons. However, such an alignment was not visible in interneurons (Figure 5G). 414 

To analyze this relationship more systematically, we first considered the potential confounding effect of 415 

modulation by concomitant movement during episodes of LC stimulation. We modeled each interneuron's 416 

activity as a function of past body movement using dilated linear regression and subtracted the neuronal activity 417 

predicted from movement alone (Methods; Figure S5 -4). All subsequent analyses of responses to LC 418 

stimulation use the resulting activity traces where the effect of movement is regressed out. 419

Laminar organization of LC -responsive interneurons 465 

Our interneuron imaging experiments spanned the deep portion of CA1, encompassing stratum oriens , the 466 

stratum pyramidale , and the deep part of stratum radiatum . Within this part of CA1, we found that the functionally 467 

defined interneuron groups showed a non -random laminar distribution. Interneuron activity patterns were more 468 

similar within laminae and more distinct across the layers of deep CA1 (Figure S2 -4), motivating us to 469 

investigate the logic of how functionally defined interneuron types (Figure 5H) are distributed along the laminar 470 

axis of deep CA1. 471 

To enable the mapping onto this axis, we acquired in vivo 3D two -photon imaging stacks encompassing the 472 

imaged planes, and measured each interneuron's laminar distance from the center of the pyramidal cell layer 473 

(Methods; Figure 5I). We found that interneurons activated by LC stimulation (Group 1) were strongly enriched 474 

in the superficial part of stratum oriens , close to the pyramidal cell layer (median laminar distance [IQR]: 6.4 μm 475 

[0.0‒45.5 μm]). In contrast, interneurons inhibited by LC stimulation and negatively modulated by body 476 

movement (Group 3) tended to be located in deeper layers of stratum oriens, closer to the corpus callosum 477 

(median [IQR]: 89.8 μm [65.4‒112.6 μm]). Finally, group 2 interneurons showed no clear laminar preference 478 

(median [IQR]: 52.3 μm [22.2‒95.0 μm]). 479 

The distinction between Group 1 and Group 2 interneurons may also be described by a continuum rather than 480 

discrete response types to LC stimulation. We therefore quantified the response magnitude to LC stimulation 481 

as a function of laminar distance across neurons from groups 1 and 2 (Figure 5J; Figure S5 -1C for the outlier 482 

mouse). This analysis revealed a continuous decrease of activation by LC stimulation as a function of laminar 483 

distance from the pyramidal cell layer ( r = -0.41, p < 10 -10 , n = 383 cells). In contrast, movement modulation 484 

exhibited only weak, if any, laminar dependence for the same set of cells ( r = -0.13, p = 0.01; Figure S5 -6). This 485 

finding provides further evidence for a dissociation between LC -evoked and arousal -related interneuron activity, 486 

based on the distinct laminar location of their somata .487 

Together, our results show that hippocampal interneurons in deep CA1 exhibit stable and consistent responses 488 

to both natural arousal and optogenetic LC stimulation. Additionally, we further find that functionally distinct LC -489 

response types are spatially segregated by a logic that follows the laminar organization of deep CA1 .490

# Discussion 528 

In this study, we establish the response profiles of the main cell types in hippocampal CA1 to LC stimulation 529 

and how these differ from responses to natural arousal. Using o ptogenetic LC stimulation in combination with 530 

fiber photometry and two -photon imaging , we observe selectively induc ed NA release at levels comparable to 531 

arousal and stress, while avoiding direct engagement of other arousal -associated circuits. Our results show 532 

that astrocytes are highly sensitive mediators of LC activity in the hippocampus, whereas pyramida l cells and 533 

interneurons respond only at higher levels of LC activity. In addition , our results reveal a striking dissociation at534 

the cellular level: all examined cell classes - pyramidal cells, interneurons, and astrocytes - exhibit markedly 535 

different response profiles to natural arousal and LC stimulation (Table 1 ; Figure S6 -1). Finally , we identif y536 

functionally distinct interneuron subpopulations that exhibit diverse yet consistent responses to LC stimulation 537 

and arousal, and are distributed along the laminar axis of deep CA1. Together, our findings reveal the cell -type -538 

specific organization of NA-induced signaling in CA1 and highlight a predominant role for astrocytes in 539 

mediating LC -driven modulation in awake mice .540 

Astrocytes as diverse and highly sensitive responders to noradrenaline 541 

In our in vivo experiments, astrocytes consistently responded with robust calcium elevations to both natural 542 

stressors and optogenetic LC stimulation, closely matching NA dynamics (Figures 1). This aligns with the well -543 

established role of astrocytes as slow, synchronized trackers of arousal  (Dombeck et al., 2007; Nimmerjahn et 544 

al., 2009; Rupprecht et al., 2024) . Importantly, t he calcium elevations evoked in astrocytes were evident even 545 

at moderate LC stimulation intensities , at which pyramidal cells and interneurons showed no detectable direct 546 

responses (Figures 6). This high responsiveness most likely reflects the high sensitivity of astrocytes to NA via 547 

ɑ1-adrenergic receptors  (Wahis and Holt, 2021) .548 

Notably, sensitivity to LC stimulation varied within the astrocytic population (Figure 3) . At the same time, 549 

individual astrocytes exhibited stable response amplitudes and sensitivity , suggesting a functional diversity 550 

within astrocytic populations. Whether this diversity reflects a continuum of NA sensitivities, possibly driven by 551 

differences in α 1-receptor expression, the vicinity of noradrenergic fibers to different astrocytes, or distinct 552 

astrocyte subtypes , remains unclear. Future work will be needed to relate these functional differences to 553 

recently described anatomical and transcriptional subtypes of astrocytes  (Endo et al., 2022; Hennes et al., 554 

2025; Viana et al., 2023) .555 

Together, the high NA sensitivity of astrocytes compared to neurons and their diverse response profile makes 556 

astrocytes an ideal candidate to act as the primary mediat or of the effects of LC in the hippocampal circuit 557 

during arousal and moderate levels of stress. 558

Sara, 2002) . These ideas were inspired by brain slice work demonstrating β -adrenergic increase of excitability 562 

of hippocampal pyramidal cells by bath -applied NA  (Church et al., 2019; Madison and Nicoll, 1982; Sah and 563

2020) . However, early in vivo studies using electrical LC stimulation reported overall neuronal suppression 565 

(Segal and Bloom, 1976) . In slices, inhibition of pyramidal cells was shown to require high exogenous NA levels, 566 

likely involving activation of presynaptic ɑ2-adrenergic receptors  (Bacon et al., 2020) , or indirect effects of NA 567 

on presynaptic activity mediated by astrocytes  (Lefton et al., 2025) . In our in vivo experiments, LC stimulation 568

Interneurons as laminarly heterogeneous but reliable responders to LC 587 

Interneurons responded to LC stimulation with substantial heterogeneity across the population, yet individual 588 

neurons showed stable response profiles across days. Most interneurons displayed a slow inhibition resembling 589 

that of pyramidal cells, but a subset of interneurons exhibited, in addition, a transient activation (Figure 5A). 590 

Based on their responses to LC stimulation and their modulation by body movement, we identified three 591 

functional interneuron groups. Group 1 was activated by LC stimulation and positively modulated by movement. 592 

Group 2 was inhibited by LC stimulation but still positively modulated by movement. Group 3 was inhibited by 593 

LC stimulation and negatively modulated by movement (active during rest). 594 

Group 3 interneurons match prior descriptions of deep -laminar neurons with negative movement modulation 595 

(Geiller et al., 2020) . While this modulation has been loosely explained by laminar position, an association with 596 

specific molecular subtypes remains unclear, as small fractions across all major interneuron subtypes (SST, 597 

PV, and VIP) were found to be negatively modulated by movement  (Arriaga and Han, 2017; Geiller et al., 2020; 598 

Turi et al., 2019) . Notably, LC -induced inhibition in our dataset was an even stronger predictor of deep laminar 599 

position than negative movement modulation alone (Figure 5I,J). These results suggest that laminar location 600 

may be a key determinant of both arousal modulation and NA responsiveness that is complementary to, and in 601 

some cases as informative as, the molecular identity of interneurons in hippocampus. 602

Group 1 interneurons, located near the pyramidal cell layer, are particularly intriguing due to their reliable 603 

activation by both LC stimulation and movement. However, while these interneurons seem to respond directly 604 

to LC -evoked NA, the receptor mediating this effect remains unclear. Slice studies have shown interneuron 605 

activation by NA via ɑ1A -receptors  (Bergles et al., 1996; Hillman et al., 2009; Madison and Nicoll, 1988) . In our 606 

study, we found that astrocytes responded robustly to NA through α 1-receptors even at low stimulation 607 

intensities (Figures 1 and 3), whereas interneuron activation in vivo emerged only at substantially higher 608 

intensity (Figures 1, 5, S5 -3C). Further complicating the comparison, the activation of inhibitory cells in slices 609 

was shown to occur in SST -expressing cells  (Hillman et al., 2009, 2005) , which, based on newer transcriptomic 610 

profiling, express relatively low levels of ɑ1-receptors  (Yao et al., 2021; Smith and Von Zastrow, 2022) . This 611 

mismatch raises the question whether the mechanism underlying interneuron activation observed in slices is 612 

related at all to the activation that we observed in vivo . Higher expression levels of α 1-receptors in CA1 were 613 

found in a subset of Lamp5 interneurons and, to a lesser extent, in VIP interneurons  (Smith and Von Zastrow, 614 

2022; Yao et al., 2021) . Lamp5 interneurons comprise Ivy cells and neurogliaform cells. While neurogliaform 615 

cells are located in stratum moleculare and hence out of reach for our in vivo imaging experiments, Ivy cell 616 

somata are located near the pyramidal cell layer  (Tzilivaki et al., 2023) , matching the laminar distribution of 617 

Group 1 interneurons (Figure 5I). VIP subtypes also remain interesting candidates, given their presumed roles 618 

in pyramidal cell plasticity during neuromodulation  (Turi et al., 2019; Neubrandt et al., 2025)  and the role of 619 

hippocampal NA in responding to novelty  (Heer and Sheffield, 2023; Kaufman et al., 2020; McKenzie et al., 620 

2025) . However, the functional heterogeneity within VIP populations  (Dellal et al., 2025)  and the challenges of 621 

selectively targeting specific interneuron subtypes in vivo render a definitive identification difficult. 622 

These inconsistencies between slice physiology, receptor expression profiles, and in vivo responsiveness 623 

highlight the need for further experiments combining functional imaging and more precise transcriptomic 624 

classification to accurately map NA -responsive interneurons to molecularly defined subtypes. At present, our 625 

functional grouping and its association with laminar organization in deep CA1 provides a starting point for 626 

understanding how hippocampal interneurons respond to endogenous NA release. 627 

Mechanisms and physiological relevance of broad neuronal inhibition 628 

The broad inhibition of both pyramidal cells and most interneurons in response to LC stimulation is an 629 

observation that is both striking and puzzling. Given that interneurons regulate pyramidal cell excitability, 630 

one might expect that silencing interneurons would lead to disinhibition of pyramidal cells; instead, we 631 

find that both populations are suppressed. Several explanations could account for this observation. One 632 

possibility is that the LC acts indirectly through other brain regions that produce an effective silencing of 633 

CA1 independent of local NA release. Another is that the subset of interneurons that was transiently 634 

activated by LC stimulation (group 1 in Figure 5) is sufficient to induce a sustained suppression of local 635 

activity of both pyramidal cells and interneurons. Alternatively, inhibition may be mediated by an 636 

alternative local mechanism not dependent on interneurons. Interestingly, the slow dynamics of the 637 

inhibitory response seems to mirror the similarly slow time course of NA release or astrocytic activity 638 

(Figures 1 and 2). This similarity puts forward the hypothesis that the observed neuronal inhibition could 639 

be mediated, at least partially, either directly by the local action of NA, or indirectly via astrocytes. 640 

Astrocytes express a diverse range of adrenergic receptors, particularly α 1-adrenergic receptors, which 641 

are known to promote intracellular calcium signaling. Experiments in ex vivo slice preparations and in 642 

zebrafish show that astrocytic activation can indeed exert inhibitory effects on neuronal activity, 643 

potentially through the release of gliotransmitters. ATP, for example, is released by astrocytes and 644 

metabolized into adenosine, which acts as an inhibitor of neuronal activity via A1 receptors  (Chen et al., 645 

2025; Lefton et al., 2025) . It remains to be seen whether this signaling pathway can explain the inhibition 646 

observed in our experiments. 647 

Irrespective of the exact mechanism, the broad inactivation of both pyramidal cells and interneurons 648 

raises the question about its physiological relevance. This wide -spread inhibition is reflected on the 649 

behavioral level by the finding that high frequency stimulation of LC can induce behavioral arrest  (Carter 650 

et al., 2010) , in agreement with our own observations (Figure S2 -3B). In this light, our results may reflect 651 

a mechanism by which LC activation under highly stressful conditions selectively silences hippocampal 652 

circuits that are primarily important for cognitive processes. This interpretation aligns with recent ideas 653

Indeed, LC -evoked NA release has been shown to increase activity of other brain regions such as the 655 

lateral habenula  (Xin et al., 2025)  or periaqueductal gray  (Petersen et al., 2025) , which are closely 656

another neuromodulator, serotonin, has been recently described in the visual cortex  (Azimi et al., 2020) ,658

by multiple neuromodulators. Probing these ideas will require stimulations of neuromodulatory areas 660 

combined with recordings in other brain regions using approaches capable of disentangling population 661 

averages from single -neuron activity. 662 

To understand the physiological meaning of the observed effects of LC stimulation, it is essential to relate 663 

LC stimulation intensities to NA levels during natural behavior. We used behavioral stress paradigms to 664 

calibrate the optogenetic stimulation of the LC. Based on this calibration, LC stimulation of moderate 665 

intensity (5 Hz) corresponded to an already significant acute stressor, whereas stronger stimulation (20 –666 

40 Hz) induced NA levels comparable to those triggered by very high stress (Figure 1). In our 667 

experiments, intermediate stimulation strength evoked only modest neuronal responses, prohibiting 668 

detailed analyses on the single -cell level due to low signal -to -noise. Consequently, we focused our single -669 

cell analyses on responses to strong LC stimulation (Figure 3 to 5). However, we noticed that activity 670 

patterns in response to LC stimulation were qualitatively similar for strong and intermediate stimulation 671 

strength (Figure S4 -1; Figure S5 -3), suggesting that the observed effects likely occur at lower levels of 672 

NA as well, albeit with reduced magnitudes. 673 

Disentangling the effects of natural arousal and LC activity 674 

The LC is the principal source of NA in the brain and a central regulator of arousal. While our optogenetic 675 

paradigm successfully reproduced natural NA release dynamics, astrocytic and especially neuronal 676 

responses differed significantly between the two conditions. To compare these states, we identified 677 

arousal episodes using body movement during head -fixation as a well -established behavioral proxy 678 

(McGinley et al., 2015; Stringer et al., 2019; Collins et al., 2023) . Although it would be interesting to 679 

investigate more intense stressful conditions (cf. Figure 1) with single -cell resolution, such behaviors are 680 

not as easily compatible with head -fixed behaviors and high -quality two -photon imaging. 681 

In our head -fixed experiments, individual astrocytes were reliably activated under both conditions (Figure 682 

1; Figure 3). However, astrocytes strongly activated during arousal were not likely to be equally sensitive 683 

to LC stimulation, resulting in distinct population activity patterns (Figure 3J -L). This difference, although 684 

apparently minor, indicates that LC -evoked NA release does only partially replicate the natural effect of 685 

NA release during arousal and points out a gap of understanding that future work should fill. For example, 686 

it would be important to assess astrocytic responses to different neuromodulators (including NA, 687 

acetylcholine, serotonin) and neurotransmitters (GABA, glutamate), and their expression profiles of the 688 

respective receptors. 689 

For pyramidal cells and interneurons, the misalignment between the two conditions was even more 690 

pronounced. On average, neurons were activated during arousal but inhibited during LC stimulation 691 

(Figures 1 and 2). Pyramidal cells exhibited robust positive or negative modulation by body movement, 692 

but no such consistent modulation by LC stimulation (Figure 4). Among interneurons, a subset displayed 693 

matching response polarity across conditions (Groups 1 and 3 in Figure 5H), but response magnitudes 694 

to arousal and LC stimulation were not correlated within these groups. In addition, further supporting the 695 

dissociation from an anatomical perspective, responsiveness to LC stimulation was organized along the 696 

laminar axis in deep CA1 (Figure 5J), while modulation by arousal was not. 697 

Together, these findings highlight the low similarity between neural activity during LC stimulation and 698 

natural arousal. This difference may be partially explained by technical factors such as non -natural 699 

activation patterns of LC neurons by optogenetics, but is also very likely to reflect that other 700 

neuromodulators or neurotransmitters are released during arousal, resulting in a cellular response profile 701 

that is not aligned with LC -evoked NA release alone. Therefore, our results suggest that LC activity and 702 

NA release, while central players for arousal, are not the primary driver of the neuronal dynamics in the 703

For instance, acetylcholine (ACh), released from the basal forebrain and medial septal projections, is 707

Quantification of cellular activity modulation by movement/arousal 959 

To quantify the modulation of astrocyte, pyramidal cell or interneuron activity by natural arousal for two -960 

photon imaging data, we used body movement as a proxy for natural arousal. We computed the 961 

modulation as the correlation of each cell's activity with a time -shifted body movement for each recording 962 

segment (typically 2.5 min) within a session (typically 25 min). The time -shift (delay) was chosen to 963 

maximize the correlation of body movement with cellular activity across the entire cellular population. 964 

Delays were +3.85 s for astrocytes, +0.71 s for interneurons, and -0.28 s (almost instantaneous 965 

correlation) for pyramidal cells, in alignment with the correlation functions shown in Figure 2. To compute 966 

the same modulation index, but based on pupil diameter instead of body movement recordings, delays 967 

were +1.55 s for astrocytes, -0.64 s for interneurons, and -1.35 s for pyramidal cells. Recording 968 

segments within 60 s upon LC stimulation or shorter than 45 s were excluded from the quantification of 969 

movement modulation to prevent a confounding influence of responses to LC stimulation. The 970 

modulation indices were averaged across segments within a session by weighing according to the 971 

duration of each segment. 972

Quantification of cellular activity modulation by LC stimulation 986 

To quantify the modulation of astrocyte, pyramidal cell or interneuron activity by LC stimulation for two -987 

photon imaging data, we computed the average ΔF/F in response to LC stimulation in a window following 988 

strong (≥20 Hz) LC stimulation, with the delay and duration of the window adapted to the typical 989 

responses observed for the respective cell type. The response window was chosen as the 20 s 990 

immediately after start of LC stimulation for astrocytes (to capture the slow evoked activity patterns for 991 

astrocytes), the 10 -s window immediately after start of LC stimulation for interneurons (to capture both 992 

the slow inhibition and the fast activation in different interneuron subpopulations), and the 10 -s window 993 

delayed by 10 s after start of LC stimulation for pyramidal cells (to capture the slow inhibition across 994 

pyramidal cells). 995 

Modeling of cellular activity by movement using dilated linear regression 996 

To control for the confounding effect of movement on cellular activity, we subtracted a cross -validated 997 

linear model of cellular activity from the measured cellular activity. To model cellular activity as a function 998 

of body movement, we used a dilated variant of linear temporal regression, as previously described 999 

(Rupprecht et al., 2024) . We averaged timepoints of the movement variable around the to -be -regressed 1000 

timepoint into bins that exponentially increased their width with the temporal distance from the current 1001 

timepoint, resulting in an effective time window of ±8.5 s (±64 timepoints sampled at 7.5 Hz for 1002 

interneurons). The vector of the dilated regressor was used to linearly regress the observed activity 1003 

separately for each interneuron with the glmfit() function in MATLAB. Models were fit to 80% of the data 1004 

and applied to the remaining 20% to exclude overfitting. The activity modeled as a function movement 1005 

was subtracted from the originally recorded activity to obtain cellular activity with tentative 1006 

decontamination of the effect of movement effects. This procedure enables the improved isolation of 1007 

responses specifically to LC stimulation. 1008 

Identification of reliably responding interneurons 1009 

To identify interneurons that exhibited reliable responses towards LC stimulation and to discard 1010 

instances of spurious neuronal activation, we computed a simple reliability metric. First, we retrieved for 1011 

each neuron all ΔF/F responses to strong LC stimulation (≥20 Hz) within a session and extracted a time 1012 

window of 100 imaging data points before to 200 imaging points after start of LC stimulation (t = -13.3 s 1013 

to 26.7 s). Then, we computed the Pearson's correlation between these peri -stimulus time traces across 1014 

the repetitions of the stimulus for the same neuron within a session and computed the median correlation 1015 

across pairs of repetitions as the reliability metric. For control, we repeated the same process, but with 1016 

a total of 106 stimulation pairs randomly selected not from the same neuron but from different neurons. 1017 

Neurons with a reliability metric exceeding a threshold (mean plus standard deviation of the random 1018 

control values: >0.13) were defined as reliably responding interneurons. 1019

vector correlation matrices as shown in Figure 3L. This analysis quantifies the similarity of pairs of 1024
