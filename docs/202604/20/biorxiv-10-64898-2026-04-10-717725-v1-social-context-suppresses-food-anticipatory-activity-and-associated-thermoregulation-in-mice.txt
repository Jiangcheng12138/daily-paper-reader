Thermoregulation in Mice 23Audrey I. Paik 1, Jacqueline R. Trzeciak 1, Cameron Harrington 1, and Andrew D. Steele 1*41Department of Biological Sciences, California State Polytechnic University Pomona; 5Pomona, CA; USA 6*Corresponding author adsteele@cpp.edu 7

Abstract 8Food anticipatory activity (FAA) is a robust behavioral output of food-entrained circadian 9rhythms, characterized by increased locomotor activity prior to scheduled feeding. 10 Despite the social nature of rodents, FAA is almost exclusively studied in singly housed 11 animals, leaving the influence of social context largely unexplored. Here, we used 12 implanted wireless devices to measure individual locomotor activity and subcutaneous 13 body temperature in group-housed mice and compared these measures to singly 14 housed controls. Social housing significantly suppressed FAA in both male and female 15 mice. In parallel, preprandial increases in body temperature were markedly attenuated 16 in group-housed animals. These findings demonstrate that FAA is a flexible, state-17 dependent behavior that reflects both circadian timing and energetic demand. Together, 18 these results identify social context as an important and underappreciated determinant 19 of food-entrained circadian biology. 20

Introduction 29 Circadian rhythms are intrinsic, self-sustaining oscillations with a period of 30 approximately 24 hours that regulate diverse physiological and behavioral processes, 31 including sleep, temperature regulation, metabolism, and activity. 1,2 These rhythms are 32 synchronized to the external environment by zeitgebers, or time cues, the most 33 prominent of which is the light–dark (LD) cycle. However, food availability is also a 34 potent zeitgeber, capable of entraining circadian rhythms in both central and peripheral 35 tissues, particularly under time-restricted feeding (TRF) or timed, calorie-restricted 36 conditions. 3,4 37 One of the most striking behavioral consequences of food-based entrainment is 38 food anticipatory activity (FAA), a circadian-controlled state characterized by a strong 39 increase in locomotor activity and a synchronized pre-prandial rise in core body 40 temperature that occurs in the hours preceding scheduled feeding. 5,6 FAA is the output 41 of the food-entrainable oscillator (FEO), a timing system that is traditionally studied by 42 feeding mice during the daytime when they are normally inactive. 7,8 Notably, FAA 43 manifests in both the light and dark cycles and possesses unique timing properties, 44 including specific limits of entrainment and independence from known core clock 45 components. 9-11 FAA persists even in constant darkness and in animals lacking a 46 functional suprachiasmatic nucleus (SCN), suggesting the presence of a separate and 47 potentially decentralized FEO network. 12-15 Unlike the relatively rigid hierarchy of light-48 based entrainment centered in the SCN, the system governing food anticipation 49 appears to be highly distributed across multiple brain regions, 12-14 potentially allowing 50

Discussion 240 Using implanted sensors to obtain individual-level activity and body temperature 241 measurements in group-housed animals, we show that social housing robustly 242 attenuates FAA and blunts preprandial increases in body temperature. This suppression 243 was observed in both sexes, with the strongest effect in male mice. These findings 244 identify social context as a powerful and underappreciated modulator of food-entrained 245 circadian behavior. 246 FAA has traditionally been viewed as a robust circadian phenomenon driven by a 247 FEO operating independently of the light-entrained suprachiasmatic nucleus. 5,6,9 248 However, nearly all prior studies examining FAA have been conducted in singly housed 249 animals implicitly assuming that FAA magnitude and expression are invariant across 250 social environments. 8 Our results challenge this assumption. Group housing 251 substantially reduced anticipatory locomotor activity without abolishing it, indicating that 252 while food-based entrainment persists, its behavioral expression is strongly shaped by 253 social context. 254

The interpretation of these findings is informed by prior work demonstrating that 255 locomotor and thermal components of FAA can be differentially regulated. For example, 256 preprandial increases in body temperature can be observed under conditions where 257 locomotor anticipatory activity is reduced or absent, and conversely, behavioral 258 anticipation can occur with attenuated temperature rhythms. 23,27,28 These observations 259 indicate that FAA is not an output of a single oscillator, but instead reflects the 260 coordinated activity of multiple food-entrainable oscillators that regulate distinct 261 physiological and behavioral processes. In this context, the parallel suppression of both 262 activity and temperature observed under social housing suggests a coordinated shift in 263 underlying physiological state rather than a simple attenuation of motor output. 264 A key insight from this study is that the effects of social housing on anticipatory 265 behavior are paralleled by changes in body temperature rhythms. Single-housed mice 266 exhibited robust preprandial increases in temperature, whereas group-housed mice 267 showed markedly attenuated temperature responses. Because body temperature is 268 tightly coupled with metabolic rate and energy expenditure, 29-31 these findings suggest 269 that FAA is not solely a behavioral readout of circadian timing, but also reflects 270 underlying physiological state. Consistent with this, preprandial increases in body 271 temperature are a well-established feature of FAA. 6,8,9 272 One plausible mechanism linking social housing to these effects is altered 273 thermoregulatory demand. Standard laboratory temperatures (~20–24 °C) fall below the 274 murine thermoneutral zone (~29–32 °C), placing singly housed mice under chronic cold 275 stress. 31,32 Under these conditions, animals must increase metabolic heat production to 276 maintain core temperature. 33 In contrast, group-housed mice benefit from social 277

Mouse strains and husbandry 368 

369 C57BL/6J mice (Jackson Laboratory, stock #000664) were used for all experiments. 370 Male and female mice were 11–14 weeks of age at the start of restricted feeding. 371 Animals were housed in a temperature-controlled vivarium (~22 °C) under a 12:12 h 372 light-dark cycle (lights on at zeitgeber time [ZT] 0, lights off at ZT12) with ad libitum 373 access to water. 374 Following recovery from surgery (see below), mice were assigned to either single 375 housing or group housing conditions.
