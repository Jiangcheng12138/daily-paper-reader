Abstract: As ubiquitous features of every natural environment, microbes have profoundly 24 

shaped eukaryotic biology throughout evolution. Circadian clocks evolved in all domains of life 25 

as central regulators that align physiology with environmental cycles, yet whether they respond 26 

directly to microbial signals remains unknown. Here, we demonstrate that evolutionarily diverse 27 

microbes potently reset mammalian cellular clocks and can drive phase shifts in plants and algae, 28 

indicating cross-kingdom effects of microbes on circadian rhythms. In mammals, exposure to 29 

soluble bacterial components distinct from canonical innate immune ligands induces acute PER2 30 

upregulation independently of Bmal1 or nascent transcription. A targeted inhibitor screen and 31 

biochemical assays implicate p38 MAPK as a modulator of this response. Taken together, this 32 

positions bacterial exposure as a previously unrecognized circadian clock input, revealing a new 33 

axis of host-microbe interaction that modulates biological timing at the cellular level. 34

Introduction 37 

Microorganisms are ubiquitous and evolutionarily enduring life forms that inhabit every 38 

environment on Earth. Approximately 2 billion years ago, microbial symbiosis enabled the 39 

formation of eukaryotic cells, allowing complex life to evolve 1. Today, microbes continue to 40 

influence core biological processes including immunity 2, metabolism 3 , and behavior 4 , yet 41 

microbial influence is often overlooked in biological models. Only in the past two decades, for 42 

example, have we begun to appreciate how the human microbiome shapes health and 43 

physiology. We now understand that products such as bacterial cell wall and metabolites 44 

influence diverse processes including epithelial turnover, nutrient absorption, and systemic 45 

immune tone 2,3,5 . These signals fluctuate with microbial composition and activity, which 46

Microbial communities across ecosystems undergo robust daily oscillations in both composition 51 

and biomass shaped by changes in light, temperature, and other environmental signals 7–12 . Even 52 

the air we breathe is rhythmic, with microbial biomass rising several-fold at night and bacterial 53 

versus fungal taxa alternating in dominance across the day 8,13 . In soil, the rhizosphere 54 

microbiome also undergoes diurnal restructuring, partly influenced by plant circadian rhythms 10 .55 

In parallel, roughly 15% of gut taxa oscillate daily 14 , driven by host behaviors such as feeding 56 

and circadian rhythms. Together, these examples underscore that microbes are temporally 57 

dynamic, raising the provocative possibility that microbial signals themselves act as zeitgebers 58 

for eukaryotic circadian clocks. 59 

60 

Circadian clocks are cell-autonomous oscillators that evolved to align physiology with 61 

environmental cycles. In mammals, the core circadian clock is driven by the heterodimeric 62 

transcription factor CLOCK:BMAL1, which drives expression of its repressors, PER and CRY, 63 

to generate a ~24-hr transcriptional translational feedback loop (TTFL) 15 . To stay aligned with 64 

Earth’s 24-hour cycle, circadian clocks synchronize to recurring environmental cues such as 65 

light 16 , temperature 17 , and feeding 18 . Despite widespread microbial influence on host biology 19– 

66 21 , it remains unknown whether microbial signals can act directly on the core circadian clock. 67 

Here, we address this question, revealing a previously unrecognized layer of communication 68 

between microbial signals and circadian timing. 69

Results 71 

Circadian phase shifts are a common outcome of exposure to bacteria 72 

To determine whether bacterial exposure directly affects the mammalian clock, we monitored the 73 

core circadian protein PER2 using mouse lung fibroblasts (MLF) derived from 74 

PERIOD2::LUCIFERASE (P2L) mice. This strain expresses luciferase fused to PER2 at the 75 

Per2 endogenous locus, such that bioluminescence tracks PER2 protein abundance 22 . To enable 76 

multi-day recordings without bacterial overgrowth, all bacteria were heat-killed (HK). P2L 77 

MLFs were exposed to a panel of evolutionarily diverse bacteria and PER2 rhythms were 78 

monitored (experimental schematic, Fig. 1A). Addition of an equivalent volume of phosphate-79 

buffered saline (PBS) served as our bacteria-free control. Surprisingly, in our initial screen, 80 

evolutionarily diverse, pathogenic Gram-positive and Gram-negative bacteria all shifted the 81 

circadian phase relative to PBS controls (Fig. 1B). Exposure to bacteria triggered an acute 82 

increase in PER2 evident within 1-hr that peaked between 4 to 6-hrs (Extended data Fig. 1A). 83 

We subsequently tested exposure to the pathogen Listeria monocytogenes , environmental 84 

organisms Mycobacterium smegmatis and Bacillus subtilis , and a non-pathogenic strain of 85 

Escherichia coli – all of which induced phase shifts in P2L MLF (Extended data Fig. 1B-C). 86 

Because responses might depend on circadian phase, we repeated exposures to Streptococcus 87 

pneumoniae (hereafter abbreviated as Spn ), Staphylococcus aureus and Escherichia coli. These 88 

again shifted rhythms, leading to almost a complete inversion when applied near the PER2 89 

trough (Fig. 1C). 90

Many bacteria produce an extracellular polysaccharide capsule to avoid detection by the host 24 .99 

Over 100 capsular serotypes of Spn with different sugar composition, linkage, and length have 100 

been identified, which alter host-bacterium interactions 25,26 . In addition to the previously tested 101

capsules that lead to localized infections. We also used a capsule knockout (TIGR4 Δcps ) which 105 

is severely attenuated in virulence 27 . However, despite these marked differences in biological 106 

phenotypes, all tested strains of Spn induced equal phase shift responses (Fig. 1F). 107

Initial detection of bacteria is sufficient to drive circadian resetting 109 

To test whether closely spaced bacterial exposures produce additive effects on phase, we 110 

stimulated P2L MLFs with HK E. coli at one or both of two timepoints spaced 7 hours apart. 111 

Consistent with phase-dependent responsiveness, stimulation at time 0 produced a phase delay, 112 

whereas a single stimulation 7 hours later produced a phase advance. Dual stimulation resulted in 113 

a phase delay, similar to time 0 alone, indicating that the second stimulus did not produce an 114 

additional phase shift under these conditions (Fig. 2A). This result is consistent with the cellular 115 

sensing of various stimuli, as many physiological stimuli induce a period of temporary 116 

desensitization. For instance, the autophosphorylating insulin receptor loses kinase activity upon 117 

activation 28,29 , and many G-protein coupled receptors internalize into intracellular vesicles upon 118 

binding of their respective agonist, preventing further activation of downstream pathways 30,31 .119 

Importantly, both classes of receptors 32–34 have been reported to transduce signals to the 120 

circadian clock, suggesting that desensitization to acute, repetitive stimuli does not necessarily 121 

exclude a given sensory pathway from a circadian role. 122 

123 

We next sought to test if continued bacterial exposure is necessary to drive changes in phase. We 124 

stimulated P2L MLFs with HK E. coli for two hours before removing the bacteria by washing 125 

with PBS then replacing the media. Exposure of cells to HK E. coli resulted in a ~4 hour phase 126 

shift regardless of whether bacteria remained for the duration of the experiment or were removed 127 

two hours after stimulation, demonstrating that bacteria do not need to remain present to drive a 128 

phase shift (Fig. 2B). Consistent with known effects, media replacement alone shifted phase in 129 

PBS-treated controls. Interestingly, media exchange had no effect on cells exposed to bacteria, 130 

which mirrors the results obtained with dual bacterial stimulation (Fig. 2B-C). 131

Bacterial signals reset circadian clocks across kingdoms 140 

We next asked whether microbial stimulation shares other defining features of circadian input 141 

signals such as dose- and phase-dependent effects on the circadian clock 35 . As Gram-positive and 142 

Gram-negative bacterial lineages diverged over 2 billion years ago, we selected a member of 143 

each for further characterization 36 . Spn is a clinically relevant Gram-positive bacterium that 144 

colonizes the upper respiratory tract of humans and remains the leading cause of community-145 

associated bacterial pneumonia 37 ; the D39 strain remains highly virulent in murine models. In 146 

contrast, E. coli is Gram-negative, and DH5 α is a lab-adapted, non-pathogenic strain that 147 

diverged from wild-type isolates through long-term culture since its isolation in 1922 38 .148 

149 

First, we generated dose-response curves by stimulating P2L MLFs with varying doses of HK 150 

Spn or E. coli and measuring the resulting phase shifts. To prevent media-related artifacts, 151 

treatments were limited to 5% of the culture volume. Similar to previously-described resetting 152 

cues 35 , the magnitude of the phase shift that occurred in response to either bacterial species was 153 

dose-dependent (Fig. 3A-B). The largest shift occurred with 4-5x10 8 colony forming units (CFU) 154 

/ mL, quantified before heat-inactivation. We next stimulated cells at various circadian phases to 155 

generate phase response curves (PRC) and phase transition curves (PTC). The PRC confirmed 156 

that the shift magnitude depends on stimulation timing (Fig. 3C; double-plotted). Remarkably, 157 

the PTCs for both Spn and E. coli exhibited near-zero average slopes (Extended data Fig. 2A-B), 158 

indicating that bacterial exposure sets cells to approximately equal circadian times regardless of 159 

the initial phase – a feature of strong resetting cues. 160 

161 

We next asked whether this response extends across mammalian cell types. In NIH/3T3 murine 162 

embryonic fibroblasts expressing a Per2:Luciferase transcriptional reporter 39 , exposure to Spn 163 

resulted in a 1-hr phase advance when stimulation occurred at CT8 and a ~5-hr phase delay when 164 

stimulation occurred at CT20). (Fig. 3D). As with the MLFs, the magnitude of the resultant 165 

phase shift was dependent on the phase at the time of stimulation. Human osteosarcoma cells 166 

(U2-OS) carrying a Per2:luciferase 39 reporter (Fig. 3E) and mouse fibroblasts expressing a 167 

Cry1:luciferase reporter 34 (Fig. 3F) also exhibit phase shifts, indicating that microbial cues can 168 

reset circadian timing across mammalian cell lineages and with different genetic reporters for the 169 

molecular clock. Finally, we exposed lung and testis tissue from P2L mice to HK bacteria ex vivo 170 

to determine if organotypic tissue slices also respond to microbial stimulation. Lung slices 171 

treated with Spn exhibited a robust ~8-hr phase delay compared to PBS controls (Fig. 3G). While 172 

rhythmicity in PBS-treated testes damped too quickly to assess phase, E. coli -treated samples 173 

displayed restored oscillations, indicating a clear resetting cue upon microbial exposure (Fig 3H). 174

Circadian resetting is driven by a soluble factor distinct from canonical immune ligands 189 

Our finding that diverse microbes elicit circadian phase shifts suggests a shared host signaling 190 

pathway may be involved. Pattern recognition receptors (PRR) such as Toll-Like Receptors and 191 

NOD-Like Receptors 41 sense distinct microbial signals to activate common downstream 192 

inflammatory cascades 42 . We tested whether PRR agonists could replicate the observed phase 193 

shift; however, none of the tested ligands altered PER2 phase (Fig. 4A-C, Extended data Fig. 194 

3A-E). Our P2L MLFs robustly produced IL-6 in response to these stimuli, confirming intact 195 

sensing capacity (Extended data Fig. 3F). We also tested purified Spn cell wall, a highly 196 

immunostimulatory complex containing peptidoglycan and wall-linked teichoic acids 43 , but this 197 

also failed to recapitulate the Spn -induced phase shift (Fig. 4D). Thus, neither activation of 198 

individual innate receptors nor inflammation is likely sufficient to drive cellular resetting. 199 

Finally, because the Spn cell wall remains intact following purification 44,45 , its lack of effect 200 

suggests that mechanosensory input is also insufficient to drive phase resetting. 201 

202 

To test whether mechanical stimulation is necessary for activity, we centrifuged HK Spn or E. 203 

coli bacteria and collected the soluble fraction. The soluble fraction induced similar PER2 phase 204 

shifts as HK bacteria, indicating that activity is associated with a component that is released upon 205 

lysis in both Spn and E. coli (Fig. 4E-F). We further passed these soluble fractions through a 3 206 

kDa molecular weight cutoff (MWCO) filter and found that the filtrates retained full activity 207 

(Fig. 4E, F). Boiling releases cytoplasmic and some surface-associated molecules, though the 208 

effect varies as Gram-negative outer membrane components are more easily liberated than wall-209 

anchored structures in Gram-positives. However, that activity is retained following boiling and 210 

filtration through a 3 kDa MWCO filter demonstrates that changes in PER2 phase can be driven 211 

by detection of heat-stable small molecule(s) produced by bacteria. 212

p38 MAPK is a mediator of the bacteria-induced phase shift response 214 

We next examined how bacterial exposure acts to induce PER2 by utilizing Bmal1 -/- PER2::LUC 215 

cells, which lack transcriptional circadian cycles. Similar to previous experiments, bacterial 216 

exposure drove a rapid increase in PER2 that reached a maximum at several hours post-217 

stimulation, suggesting that an intact circadian TTFL is not required for this response (Fig. 5A). 218 

To determine if nascent transcription is necessary for PER2 protein induction, we treated cells 219 

with  -amanitin, an inhibitor of RNA polymerase II and III, 30 minutes prior to Spn stimulation. 220 

PER2 levels still increased, indicating de novo RNA synthesis is not required for PER2 protein 221 

induction (Fig. 5B). Leptomycin B, which blocks nuclear mRNA export, showed similar results 222 

(Fig. 5C), suggesting reliance on transcripts already in the cytoplasm. In contrast, cycloheximide 223 

abolished acute PER2 induction, confirming translation is necessary for the observed induction 224 

(Fig. 5D). All three inhibitors caused PER2 rhythms to flatline within 12-hrs, consistent with the 225 

need for ongoing transcription and translation to sustain production of the short-lived PER2 226 

protein 15 (Fig. 5B-D). We do not exclude the possibility that transcriptional regulation of Per2 is 227

We next investigated whether known mechanisms of PER2 translational regulation may 231 

contribute to the response following microbial exposure. Previous studies demonstrate that miR-232 

24, miR-29a, and miR-30a bind the 3’-UTR of Per1 and Per2 mRNA to suppress translation 46 .233 

Downregulation of these miRNAs contribute to the resetting response to insulin, during which 234 

PER2 experiences a similar acute increase in abundance 34 . Following Spn stimulation, we found 235 

a modest decrease in the amount of miR-30a but not miR-24 or miR-29a (Extended data Fig. 4). 236 

As suppression of multiple miRNAs is typically required to enhance PER translation 34,46 , this 237 

limited change is unlikely to mediate the bacterial PER2 response. 238

Given the diversity of upstream signaling pathways capable of influencing the clock 47,48 , we next 240 

screened pharmacological inhibitors targeting diverse nodes of signal transduction. Because 241 

some compounds produced independent effects on phase or period, we established a 242 

prioritization metric to rank their impact on bacteria-induced resetting. For each inhibitor, we 243 

calculated the absolute value of the phase difference between Spn -treated MLFs in the presence 244 

or absence of the inhibitor. This approach captures both addition and reduction in phase as 245 

equivalent modulatory effects, and values are presented as a percent of the theoretical 12-hour 246 

maximum (Fig. 5E). 247 

248 

Interestingly, the mTOR inhibitor Torin-1 did not affect the phase shift response to Spn (Fig. 5E, 249 

Extended data Fig. 5A). mTOR is a central regulator of translation and has previously been 250 

shown to mediate circadian responses to temperature 49 , insulin 34 , and hypoxia 50 . As previously 251 

reported 51 , Torin-1 lengthens period (Extended data Fig. 5A i). To disentangle period effects 252 

from phase changes, we additionally visualized the PER2 traces in modulo-tau format, starting 253 

from when oscillations stabilize post-stimulation. Here, the data is normalized by the period of 254 

each group, such that the x-axis unit ( τ) reflects 1 circadian cycle for all groups. This 255 

demonstrated that Torin-1 has no effect on the phase resulting from Spn -stimulation (Extended 256 

data Fig. 5A ii ). 257 

258 

Of all the inhibitors tested, only SB203580, a p38 MAPK inhibitor, significantly altered the 259 

response to Spn (Fig. 5F, Extended data Fig. 5B). Like Torin-1, SB203580 lengthened the period 260 

in both PBS- and Spn -treated cells. However, in contrast to Torin-1, the phase shift induced by 261 

Spn was markedly altered in the presence of SB203580 (Fig. 5F, Extended data Fig. 5A-B). 262 

Additional studies demonstrated that BIRB796, a structurally distinct molecule that inhibits p38 263 

MAPK, but not an inhibitor of ERK, also leads to an altered Spn -induced phase shift (Extended 264 

data Fig. 5C-D). Given that phase is cyclical, it is not intuitive whether the final phase results 265 

from inhibition or augmentation of the Spn response as compared to PBS controls. However, 266 

when the phase difference between ( Spn + p38 inhibitor) and ( Spn + mock control) is calculated, 267 

all p38 inhibitor experiments show an additional phase delay, demonstrating a consistent effect 268 

of p38 inhibition (Extended data Fig. 5E). In contrast, inhibition of mTOR or ERK has no 269 

significant effect. 270

Specifically, ERK participates in light-induced phase shifts through the ERK–MNK–eIF4E axis, 278 

a translational control mechanism that culminates in phosphorylation of the cap-binding protein 279

to circadian oscillations in locomotor activities 52 and cognitive functions 53 , implicating it as a 281 

potential mediator of diverse clock outputs. While our inhibitor screen suggested ERK is not 282 

involved in the response to bacteria (Extended data Fig. 5D), p38 can also activate MNK to 283 

phosphorylate eIF4E 54,55 . Thus, we investigated if the phase shift to bacterial exposure may be 284 

mediated by a p38–MNK–eIF4E axis. 285 

286 

We asked whether Spn engages this axis by examining phosphorylation status following 287 

microbial stimulation. Phosphorylation of p38 is induced by Spn within 5 minutes and remains 288 

elevated 60 minutes later (p-p38, Fig. 5G-H, Extended data Fig. 6A). In contrast, ERK 289 

phosphorylation transiently increases in both Spn - and PBS-treated cells before returning to 290 

baseline, consistent with a generalized handling response rather than a microbe-specific signal. 291 

We next tested whether p-p38 might act through a canonical translational pathway involving 292 

MNK and eIF4E, as was described for light 52 . However, we did not detect changes in p-eIF4E 293 

following Spn exposure, suggesting that microbial stimulation activates a distinct translational 294 

mechanism that bypasses this axis. These findings, combined with our pharmacological studies, 295 

point to p38 MAPK as a modulator of the bacterial resetting response, operating outside 296 

established translational axes linked to circadian control. More broadly, these findings suggest 297 

that bacterial exposure can engage a noncanonical signaling mechanism to rapidly influence 298 

circadian timing, distinct from previously characterized clock-resetting pathways. 299

Repeated bacterial exposure continuously modifies circadian timing 301 

Although microbes are ubiquitous in the environment, their levels and community composition is 302 

highly dynamic. Microbial loads in the atmosphere follow a daily rhythm, with a 10-100 fold 303 

greater abundance reported at night as compared to the day, due to reduced atmospheric 304 

mixing 13 . The gut microbiome shows a comparable rhythmicity, with up to 15% of taxa 305 

oscillating daily in concert with host feeding rhythms 14 . This raised the question of whether 306 

continuous presence of microbes, combined with modest changes in microbial abundance are 307 

sufficient to alter the behavior of a circadian oscillator. To address this, we constructed a simple 308 

model of a hypothetical oscillator (Extended data Fig. 7A). In this framework, an activator 309 

protein A represents the positive arm of the TTFL, while a repressive protein B represents the 310 

negative arm. 311 

312 

As shown in this study, bacterial exposure is capable of inducing PER2 (part of the negative arm 313 

of the mammalian clock) in a Bmal1 -independent manner (part of the positive arm). We 314 

therefore chose to model bacterial exposure such that an increase in bacterial signal directly 315 

induces repressor B independently of enhancer A. In silico modeling of a single bacterial 316 

exposure was able to produce a 4-hr phase shift (Extended data Fig. 7B), which is similar to the 317 

phase shift observed when bacteria are applied at a similar circadian phase (Fig. 3C, Extended 318 

data Fig. 2C-D). We next adjusted bacterial concentrations so that the signal was always present 319

Previous studies examining the properties of in vitro circadian recording have shown that 327 

individual cells exhibit stable circadian oscillations, yet across a community of cells, stochastic 328 

fluctuations cause individual cells to deviate over time from the overall average, leading to an 329 

observed loss of amplitude over time 56–58 . Therefore, to model this community dynamic, we 330 

simulated random fluctuations in the circadian oscillator model, then averaged the measurements 331 

of 3000 independently simulated oscillators. When this community model was simulated with 332 

bacterial exposure at repeated 24-hr intervals, the model predicted that the community remains 333 

synchronized and resists amplitude loss (Extended data Fig. 7E). 334 

335 

We tested this model experimentally by evaluating whether repeated exposure to HK Spn every 336 

24-hrs is sufficient to maintain synchrony in P2L MLF. As shown in Figure 6, repeated 337 

stimulations with HK Spn allowed P2L MLFs to maintain their amplitude for the duration of 338 

stimulation, after which damping occurred in a manner consistent with desynchronization. PBS 339 

(bacteria-free; handling control) had no effect at any timepoint, and amplitude in these cells 340 

damped with kinetics similar to untreated cells (grey vs light blue traces in Fig. 6B-D). In 341 

contrast, additional stimulation with HK Spn resulted in maintenance of amplitude (dark blue 342 

traces compared to grey or light blue in Fig 6A-D). As we did not remove HK Spn during the 343 

experiment, the observation that MLFs were able to respond to subsequent stimulations suggests 344 

that MLFs sense changes in bacterial abundance rather than merely the presence or absence of 345 

bacteria. This is consistent with the prediction that daily fluctuations in bacterial abundance are 346 

sufficient to influence mammalian circadian clocks. As microbial exposure is ubiquitous on 347 

Earth and as bacteria are known to oscillate in response to well-established entraining cues such 348 

as light, temperature, and feeding rhythms, we propose that diverse organisms may use bacterial 349 

detection as a mechanism to reinforce circadian synchrony with external time. 350

Discussion 352 

Our findings reveal that microbial exposure can serve as a cue capable of resetting the 353 

mammalian clock. Microbial exposure thus emerges as a novel circadian clock input, joining 354 

well-characterized cues such as light 16 , temperature 17 , and feeding 18 in shaping biological timing. 355 

The observation that similar effects occur in plants and algae suggests that sensitivity to 356 

microbial cues may be a general feature of biological timekeeping across kingdoms. 357 

358 

Across ecosystems, microbial communities fluctuate predictably in response to environmental 359 

changes, offering a rich and ancient source of temporal information 7–12 . Microbial cues may 360 

therefore act as circadian amplifiers – reinforcing or substituting for classical zeitgebers, 361 

especially in environments where cues like light or temperature are weak or inaccessible (e.g., 362 

soil, internal tissues). Notably, germ-free mice exhibit disrupted circadian oscillations despite 363 

standard light–dark housing 21 . While the extensive metabolic and immune defects present in 364 

germ-free mice likely contribute to perturbations in overall circadian rhythmicity in vivo , the data 365

In principle, circadian responsiveness to microbes could also confer local advantages. During 369 

infection, bacteria can reach high densities within host tissues, resulting in rapid exposure to 370 

microbial signals. The ability of host cells to adjust cellular phase upon detection of microbial 371 

signals could reflect an adaptive alignment that optimizes immune or stress responses without 372 

disturbing systemic rhythms. Given that pathogens engage host tissues through diverse 373 

mechanisms, resolving this question will require distinct analyses appropriate for each infection 374 

model. Notably, during Spn infection, high bacterial density triggers regulated bacterial lysis 375 

through at least two genetically separable pathways, LytA-mediated autolysis 59,60 and Blp-376 

dependent bacteriocin activity 61,62 , which together cause the release of intracellular bacterial 377 

components into the local environment. An important open question is therefore whether 378 

microbial-derived phase shifts occur during natural infections, and if so, how this alters disease 379 

outcome. 380 

381 

While these hypotheses are tempting to speculate over, such ideas must also be qualified by the 382 

current lack of understanding in the field of circadian host-microbe interactions 63 . For instance, 383 

although we have not observed differences in the bacteria-induced phase shift response across 384 

our tested mammalian models, it is possible that specific organs or cell types may respond to 385 

microbial exposure differently than we have observed in vitro . Additionally, recent research has 386 

revealed that some species of non-photosynthetic bacteria – such as Bacillus subtilis 64 , Klebsiella 387 

aerogenes 65,66 , and Acinetobacter baumanii 67 – contain circadian clocks. While circadian clocks 388 

have not been reported in our representative bacterial species ( Spn and E. coli ), it is possible that 389 

one may be revealed in future studies. If an intrinsic circadian oscillator is discovered in these 390 

organisms, an exciting future direction would be to determine if it influences the microbe-391 

induced phase shift. Understanding the biological function and evolutionary benefit of the 392 

observed phase shift effect will therefore require collective effort to explore this emerging 393 

interface between microbial biology and circadian timing. 394 

395 

Our studies point to a soluble bacterial factor as the effector of circadian resetting. While this 396 

activity is not mimicked by canonical PRR ligands, our data do not exclude the possibility of a 397 

combinatorial signal. Given the vast repertoire of microbial products and potential for synergistic 398 

interactions, identifying the precise signal(s) will be an important avenue for future investigation. 399 

Functionally, microbial stimulation triggers acute PER2 induction, but whether this drives the 400 

observed phase shifts or reflects a parallel consequence of upstream signaling remains unclear. 401 

Notably, while acute changes in transcriptional Per2 reporters can be observed, we show that 402 

protein PER2 induction does not require Bmal1 , transcription, or canonical translational control 403 

through mTOR or eIF4E. Given the complexity of MAPK signaling and the wide array of 404 

downstream targets 68 , it remains unresolved whether p38 potentiates, constrains, or gates the 405 

phase-resetting response 69 . These data define mechanistic constraints on the response but do not 406 

resolve the upstream sensing mechanism or the direct p38 targets responsible for PER2 407 

induction. Unbiased approaches such as proteomic or phosphoproteomic profiling will be a 408 

valuable future direction to define the broader signaling landscape linking bacterial exposure to 409 

regulation of the molecular clock. Microbial exposure is well known to influence a wide range of 410

Together, these findings position microbial signals as a novel class of circadian input and 414 

uncover a previously unrecognized axis of host–microbe communication. That this signaling 415

Bacterial strains and preparations 638 

Detailed information on bacterial strains, phenotypes, media type and culture conditions can be 639 

found in Table S1 . Briefly, most bacterial strains were inoculated into overnight liquid culture, 640 

then subcultured and grown to mid log (OD 600 0.4-0.5) with the exception of Streptococcus 641 

pneumoniae , which was always maintained in log phase (to prevent autolysis that occurs in 642 

stationary phase), and E. coli DH5 α which exclusively used stationary phase cultures. 643 

Streptococcal species ( S. pneumoniae and S. agalactiae) were grown in liquid media without 644 

agitation. All other bacteria were grown under shaking conditions when in liquid culture. 645 

Bacterial colony forming units (CFU) were enumerated by 10-fold serial dilution and counted 646 

following overnight growth on solid agar media. For experiments involving inactivated bacteria, 647 

CFU was calculated prior to inactivation and is subsequently referred to as “CFU equivalents”. 648 

649 

Heat Killed (HK) bacteria were grown as described and a sample was collected for CFU 650 

enumeration. Cultures were then pelleted by centrifugation and resuspended in minimal PBS (for 651 

mammalian cells), half-strength Murashige and Skoog media (for A. thaliana ), or salt water (for 652 

O. tauri ), typically resulting in at least 100-fold concentration (resuspend 50mL bacterial culture 653 

in < 500uL). Bacteria were heat killed by boiling in a heat block at 100C for 30-60 min or until 654 

plating revealed no viable bacteria. As B. subtilis produces heat-resistant endospores, these 655 

samples were additionally autoclaved after boiling. For fractionation experiments, HK Spn or E. 656 

coli were centrifuged at max speed (21,000 x g) to separate insoluble pellet from the soluble 657 

fraction. The soluble fraction was then passed through a 3kDa MWCO filter (Millipore Sigma 658 

UFC5003) according to manufacturer’s directions. All samples were stored at -20°C until use. 659 

660 

Formalin Fixed (FF) bacteria underwent identical enumeration and concentration steps as HK 661 

bacteria. After resuspension in PBS, bacterial pellets were diluted in 10% Neutral Buffered 662 

Formalin and incubated at room temperature for 15 minutes. Formalin was then quenched and 663 

removed by thorough washing with 3M Tris-HCl pH 8 (a minimum of three 15-minute washes 664 

was necessary to prevent formalin-mediated toxicity in downstream steps), and bacterial pellets 665 

were resuspended in minimal PBS. Neutralization of formalin was confirmed by adding 666 

formalin-inactivated bacteria to confluent P2L cells at a final concentration of 5% (v/v) and 667 

checking for the lack of morphological signs of cell stress over a period of 48 hours. 668

Mathematical modeling of the circadian clock 894 

A custom script was written in Python to simulate the functional mechanism of a circadian clock. 895 

The script generates the simulation by continuously iterating upon the current state of the 896 

simulated clock according to a set of rules. Briefly, activated enhancer protein A produces the 897 

repressor protein B in a linear fashion. In turn, protein B inactivates protein A. At every iteration, 898 

a set percentage of protein B is degraded. To simulate the kinetics of protein binding, translation, 899 

and translocation, changes in the production of protein B or the inactivation of protein A are 900 

delayed according to a production_change_rate variable and a repression_rate variable, 901 

respectively. These variables, along with the protein B degradation rate, were optimized by 902 

iterative testing to produce oscillations with a 24-hour period. To model community-driven 903 

dynamics, a stochastic modeling step was added to the production and degradation of protein B, 904 

such that the production and degradation rates are multiplied by a Gaussian random variable with 905 

a mean of 1 and a standard deviation of 0.20. This randomized oscillator was then independently 906
