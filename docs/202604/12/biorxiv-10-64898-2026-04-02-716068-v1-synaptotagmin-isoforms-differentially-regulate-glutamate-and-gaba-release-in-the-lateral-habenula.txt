ABSTRACT 18 

Neurotransmitter co-transmission contributes to diverse physiological processes 19 

throughout the mammalian brain, including sensory integration, motivational control, 20 

and social behaviors. Projections from the globus pallidus internus (GPi; the 21 

entopeduncular nucleus, EPN, in rodents) to the lateral habenula (LHb) are well-22 

characterized by the co-transmission of both GABA and glutamate. These dual-release 23 

inputs modulate behavioral states in chronically learned helpless (cLH) rats, influencing 24 

both the onset and recovery of pathological phenotypes. Here, we employed confocal 25 

3D reconstructions that confirmed the presence of both vesicular transporters VGAT 26

GABA and glutamate are packaged in distinct vesicle populations within individual 28 

presynaptic terminals. Notably, the calcium (Ca² ⁺) sensors Synaptotagmin-2 (Syt2) and 29 

Synaptotagmin-3 (Syt3) are highly expressed in the EPN whereas expression of the 30

confocal microscopy, we observed selective spatial correlations of Syt2 and VGLUT2 32

suggested that Syt2 serves as the predominant Ca² ⁺ sensor for glutamatergic vesicle 34 

fusion, and Syt3 serves as the predominant Ca² ⁺ sensor for GABAergic vesicle fusion in 35 

the LHb. To test this hypothesis, we employed targeted antisense oligonucleotide (ASO) 36 

knockdown of Syt2 and Syt3 in EPN neurons and measured LHb glutamatergic and 37

amplitude, half-width and decay, suggesting increased glutamate vesicle release 39 

probability and increased glutamate vesicle packing. However, Syt2 knockdown had no 40 

influence on mIPSCs amplitude or frequency. On the other hand, Syt3 knockdown had 41 

no apparent effect on glutamate release but caused an increase in mIPSC frequency 42 

suggesting increased quantal release probability of GABA. Together, these findings 43 

identify a molecular mechanism by which synaptotagmin isoforms govern differential 44 

glutamate and GABA release at EPN dual-transmitter terminals in the LHb. These 45 

results provide evidence for presynaptic mechanisms regulating excitatory –inhibitory 46 

balance within this brain structure and importantly provide molecular targets for 47 

pharmacological intervention. 48

Henry Dale first postulated that each neuron releases a single neurotransmitter at its 50 

synapses, but accumulating evidence has overturned this seemingly universal rule 1.51 

Once thought nonexistent or anomalous, fast dual-release neurons have now been 52 

identified in major circuits throughout the mammalian brain 2–6. In particular, glutamate 53 

and GABA, are co-released in EPN →LHb pathways that exhibit hyperexcitability in 54 

animal models with depressive phenotypes 7,8 . These neurotransmitters are often 55 

viewed dichotomously —glutamate as excitatory and GABA as inhibitory 9,10 . While their 56 

broad roles are generally consistent, the strict anatomical separation between 57 

glutamatergic and GABAergic neurons is less rigid than previously believed. Indeed, 58 

multiple examples demonstrate that glutamate and GABA can be synthesized, 59 

trafficked, and released at single axon terminals 11 –13 and more recently it has been 60 

proposed that glutamate and GABA are co-packaged within the LHb 14 .61 

We reasoned that if regulated release of opposing neurotransmitters does occur, 62 

it is likely due to isoform variations in synaptotagmin Ca² ⁺ sensors 15 . Of the 63 

approximately seventeen known synaptotagmin isoforms 16 , several act as presynaptic 64 

Ca² ⁺ sensors with distinct affinities and kinetic properties, with Syt1 being the most well 65 

established Ca 2+ sensor in the mammalian brain 17 –19 . We utilized the Allen Brain Atlas 20

and the increase of Syt2 and Syt3 co-expression suggested a potential mechanism for 70 

regulating transmitter-specific vesicle fusion by segregating neurotransmitter 71 

transporters and Ca² ⁺ sensors to individual synaptic vesicles. In alignment with this 72

DISCUSSION 164 

In this study we provide evidence for a molecular mechanism by which specific 165 

synaptotagmin isoforms govern differential neurotransmitter release in a dual ‐166 

transmitter circuit. Using anatomical, molecular, and functional approaches, we 167 

demonstrate that projections from the globus pallidus internus (GPi; entopeduncular 168 

nucleus, EPN, in rodents) to the lateral habenula (LHb) contain distinct vesicle 169 

populations for the accumulation of glutamate (via VGLUT2) and GABA (via VGAT), 170 

each associated with a specific synaptotagmin isoform. Syt2 and Syt3 are both enriched 171 

in the EPN, but their localization and function diverge: Syt2 localizes with VGLUT2 ‐172 

containing vesicles in the EPN LHb pathway, and Syt2 knockdown results in increased 173 

excitatory events within the LHb. Syt3 colocalizes with VGAT-containing vesicles in the 174 

EPN LHb pathway, and Syt3 knockdown results in increased inhibitory events in the 175 

LHb. This distribution of calcium sensors provides a molecular explanation for how a 176 

single presynaptic terminal can independently tune excitatory and inhibitory output 177 

within the LHb. By segregating neurotransmitter-specific machinery into distinct vesicle 178 

pools —paired with either Syt2 for glutamate or Syt3 for GABA —the neuron effectively 179 

establishes a flexible "molecular switch". Rather than being constrained by a fixed 180

Shared mechanisms across dual-transmitter pathways 183 

These findings expand on earlier observations of dual glutamate/GABA release from 184 

mesohabenular and pallidal afferents, which established that transmitter co ‐release in 185 

the LHb modulates mood ‐related behaviors 8,13 . The identification of Syt2 and Syt3 as 186 

transmitter ‐specific Ca 2+ sensors refines this model by introducing a presynaptic 187 

mechanism for temporal and kinetic control. Syt2 is a low ‐affinity, fast ‐binding Ca 2+ 

188 

sensor associated with synchronous vesicle fusion 25 , whereas Syt3 exhibits higher Ca 2+ 

189 

affinity, slower kinetics, and governs asynchronous release 17,21,22,26 . The near ‐exclusive 190 

pairing of Syt2 with glutamatergic vesicles and Syt3 with GABAergic vesicles within the 191 

EPN LHb pathway therefore offers a biophysical basis for differential release timing. 192 

Such temporal staggering could fine-tune postsynaptic integration within LHb neurons, 193 

generating dynamic excitatory –inhibitory balance rather than fixed transmitter ratios 194 

which may be a shared mechanism across dual release synapses. How can our 195 

observations be reconciled with a prior study 14 reporting that EPN LHb dual release 196 

terminals function via co-packaging of glutamate and GABA based on highly correlated 197 

trial-to-trial post-synaptic currents? The correlated signaling observed in the Kim et al. 198 

study can be re-interpreted as functional synchrony. While further studies are needed to 199 

fully reconcile these differences, the observation that both Syt2 and Syt3 isoforms are 200 

present within the same terminal implies that a single presynaptic action potential can 201

Synaptotagmin balance and behavioral regulation 204 

Functionally, the proposed dual release mechanism may underlie the bidirectional 205 

behavioral effects attributed to the LHb in mood regulation. Pallido-habenular neurons 206 

influence both aversive and reward-related signaling, and manipulations that alter the 207 

glutamate/GABA release ratio modify depressive phenotypes in animal models 8,12,27 .208 

Our data suggest that synaptotagmin isoform composition could act as a molecular 209 

switch controlling this ratio. Loss of Syt2 enhanced glutamatergic transmission, 210 

potentially increasing LHb excitability and promoting aversive output, whereas loss of 211 

Syt3 enhanced inhibitory tone. These complementary effects highlight a presynaptic 212 

mechanism by which Ca 2+ sensor balance modulates circuit polarity and, ultimately, 213 

behavioral state. 214 

Future work should address whether Syt2/Syt3 expression is dynamically regulated by 215 

activity, stress, or antidepressant treatment. Experience-dependent or neuromodulatory 216 

changes in isoform abundance could bias transmitter release toward excitation or 217 

inhibition, contributing to the plasticity of LHb output. At a broader level, the segregation 218 

of synaptotagmin isoforms to transmitter-specific vesicle pools may represent a general 219 

principle of dual-release organization throughout the brain. Extending these 220 

observations with high-resolution ultrastructural mapping, optogenetic stimulation, and 221 

behavioral assays will clarify how isoform diversity shapes computation in mixed-222 

transmitter circuits. Together, our findings provide a mechanistic framework linking 223

Surgical procedures 238

Image and Data Analysis 368 

Stacks used for 3D image reconstruction comprised ~100 10nm slices totaling a Z-369 

section measuring 1uM through the LHb. The resulting file was imported directly into 370 

Imaris Cellsens for analysis. GFP labeled projections from the EPN were isolated in 371 

their respective channel, reconstructed as a volume, and saved as an object. The same 372 

step was completed for each fluorescence channel, creating volumetrically 373 

representative objects for both VGLUT2/VGAT and Syt2/Syt3 puncta. Proximity analysis 374 

was performed using these objects, measuring from the centroids. A threshold 375 

approach was used for co-localization determination. To account for spectral shift and 376 

natural variations in focal lengths or staining specificity, only puncta within 100nm of 377 

GFP labelled EPN projections were included in analysis. This filtered out any 378 

anomalous signal from background or partial captures. From the remaining puncta, only 379 

signals from fluorophores within 1000nm of one another were considered. This was to 380 

ensure that the labeled proteins were likely contained within the same synaptic terminal, 381 

the average size of which is approximately 1 m28 . We then measured the distances 382 

between all remaining puncta, excluding duplicates, and exported all data points 383 

(supplementary material) for subsequent spatial analysis. 384
