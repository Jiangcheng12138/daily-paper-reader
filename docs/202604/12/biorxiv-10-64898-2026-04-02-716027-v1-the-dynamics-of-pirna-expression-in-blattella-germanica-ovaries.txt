19 Abstract 

20 The cockroach Blattella germanica possesses panoistic ovaries, in which oocytes lack 

21 nurse cells and therefore need to rely on their own transcriptional activity to support 

22 embryogenesis. Ovarian development in this species involves the development of a 

23 single basal ovarian follicle (BOF) per gonadotropic cycle, a process strictly regulated 

24 by endocrine signals, primarily juvenile hormone and ecdysone, which act at both the 

25 transcriptional and translational levels. In addition, transcriptional activity in these 

26 ovaries is necessary for both regulating and genome protection, and at this level, PIWI-

27 interacting RNAs (piRNAs) play an essential role. Although insect ovaries are known to 

28 be particularly rich in piRNAs, their function in ovary maturation is still not well 

29 defined. For this purpose, we characterize the piRNA expression dynamics across 

30 seven key developmental and reproductive stages, ranging from late nymphal instars 

31 to post-vitellogenic adults. 

32 piRNA expression in B. germanica shows coordinated fluctuations. Expression remains 

33 stable in previtellogenic ovaries, whereas vitellogenic ovaries show pronounced 

34 changes. Moreover, vitellogenic ovaries exhibit reduced piRNA diversity due to strong 

35 enrichment of a subset of highly expressed piRNAs. 

36 Our data show that although piRNAs predominantly map to transposable elements, 

37 particularly LINEs, there is a notable increase in gene-derived piRNAs toward the end 

38 of the cycle. Our results suggest regulatory roles of piRNAs in modulating both TEs and 

39 mRNAs during BOF maturation, likely related to changes in the follicular cell program.

47 Introduction 

48 Ovarian development in insects is a complex and highly coordinated process regulated 

49 by molecular signaling cascades and hormonal control (1–3). Using Blattella germanica 

50 as a model, we investigate a new regulatory layer in insect oogenesis mediated by 

51 Piwi-interacting RNAs (piRNAs). 

52 piRNAs are small non ‑coding RNAs (sncRNAs) initially identified in the male germline of 

53 Drosophila melanogaster (4,5). They have since been described in a wide range of 

54 eukaryotes, from plants to vertebrates. Their canonical function involves the 

55 regulation of transposable elements (TEs), from which they can also originate (5,6), 

56 thereby acting as essential genome guardians (7). However, accumulating evidence has 

57 revealed that the piRNA pathway extends beyond TE silencing. In insects, piRNAs 

58 participate in growth regulation, gonadal and wing development, embryo patterning, 

59 and sex determination (8–12). These findings show the importance of piRNAs as 

60 regulatory molecules, with some cases showing functional parallels to microRNAs. 

61 In a previous study based on B. germanica development, we identified a total of 

62 866,980 unique piRNAs across 11 developmental stages, from egg and embryo through 

63 successive nymphal instars to adult females (13). The abundance of piRNAs detected in 

64 unfertilized eggs suggests a significant role in ovarian physiology. These piRNAs appear 

65 to accumulate during oocyte growth, supporting ovarian maturation and potentially 

66 influencing early embryogenesis. Notably, B. germanica oogenesis is characterized by 

67 the growth of a single basal ovarian follicle (BOF) per ovariole in each gonadotropic 

68 cycle. Thus, most molecular changes observed in the ovary correspond to BOF 

69 development (reviewed in 2 and 3). This process is tightly regulated by juvenile 

70 hormone (JH) and ecdysone. 

71 72 To elucidate piRNA function during oogenesis, we used small RNAseq to identify the 

73 piRNAs expressed in B. germanica ovaries throughout the gonadotropic cycle by 

74 sampling key developmental stages. These included ovaries with previtellogenic

77 morphological changes in the follicular epithelium in the BOF (Fig 1). 

78 Three stages corresponded to the last nymphal instar, when prothoracic-gland-derived 

79 ecdysone predominates in the hemolymph (14). During this instar, the basal oocyte 

80 becomes fully transcriptionally active and initiates the synthesis and organization of 

81 macromolecules required later for embryogenesis. The selected nymphal stages were: 

82 newly emerged last-instar nymphs, marking the onset of the first gonadotropic cycle 

83 (15); 6 ‑day ‑old last-instar nymphs, when the number of ovarian follicles per ovariole is

85 of 20 ‑hydroxyecdysone (20E) occurs, activating the ecdysone signaling pathway 

86 (14,16,17); 8 ‑day ‑old last-instar nymphs, just before the imaginal molt, when the basal 

87 oocyte has completed the accumulation of mRNAs and proteins required for 

88 embryogenesis (18). 

89 After adult emergence, ovarian physiology is governed primarily by JH, which triggers 

90 the onset of vitellogenesis and BOF growth (2,19). In adults, ovarian piRNAs were 

91 analyzed at four stages: newly emerged adults, before the rise of JH in the 

92 hemolymph; 3 ‑day ‑old adults, when the cytokinesis in BOF follicular cells arrests, 

93 ovarian ecdysone synthesis begins, and an increase in JH induces vitellogenesis in the 

94 fat-body; 5 ‑day ‑old adults, when follicular cells become binucleated and polyploid (20) 

95 (15,20), JH levels peak (21), patency is established, and ovarian 20E increases to 

96 activate early chorion gene expression (16); 7 ‑day ‑old adults, a post-vitellogenic stage 

97 in which choriogenesis is completed (20) just before ovulation and oviposition; JH 

98 levels decline (21), while ovarian 20E reaches its maximum (14). 

99 Our results reveal a substantial proportion of ovarian piRNAs originating from genic 

100 regions. Their abundance varies during BOF maturation, increasing markedly in pre-

101 oviposition stages while remaining relatively stable in immature ovaries. Together, 

102 these findings support a central role for piRNAs in regulating ovarian development and 

103 maturation in the cockroach B. germanica .

104 105 Materials and methods 

106 Cockroach colony and sampling 

107 Adult and last nymphal instar females of the cockroach B. germanica (L.) were 

108 obtained from a colony fed ad libitum on Panlab 125 dog chow and water, reared in 

109 the dark at 29 ± 1 °C and 60–70% relative humidity. Females were selected and used at 

110 appropriate ages: 1) newly emerged last instar nymphs (N6D0); 2) 6-day-old last instar 

111 nymphs (N6D6); 3) 8-day-old last instar nymphs (N6D8); 4) newly emerged adults 

112 (AD0); 5) 3-day-old adults (AD3); 6) 5-day-old adults (AD5); 7) 7-day-old adult females 

113 in choriogenesis (AD7). All dissections were performed on specimens anesthetized 

114 with carbon dioxide. Tissues were stored at -80 ºC until use. 

115 RNA extraction and small RNA libraries preparation 

116 Total RNA was isolated using the GenElute Mammalian Total RNA Kit (Sigma, Madrid, 

117 Spain). RNA quantity and quality were estimated by spectrophotometric absorption at 

118 260/280 nm in a Nanodrop Spectrophotometer ND-1000® (NanoDrop Technologies, 

119 Wilmington, DE, USA). Small RNA libraries from B. germanica ovaries were prepared 

120 and sequenced by BMK (Münster, Germany) using the Illumina Novaseq 6000 (PE50, n 

121 = 3), except for AD3 samples (SE50, n = 4). Small RNA-seq data are publicly available at 

122 the NCBI Gene Expression Omnibus (22) under the accession code GEO: GSE255380. As 

123 a reference genome for the analyses, the B. germanica assembly generated by 

124 Harrison and colleagues (23) was used. 

125 Annotation of Blattella germanica transposable elements 

126 B. germanica Transposable elements (TE) were identified using the EarlGrey TE 

127 annotation pipeline (v5.1.1; Docker image tobybaril/earlgrey_dfam3.7:latest). The 

128 Arthropoda repeat library from Dfam (v3.8) was used to mask known repeats, and TE

133 piRNA identification 

134 Library quality was assessed using FastQC (v0.12.1) (25). Sequencing adapters were 

135 removed from the samples using Trimmomatic (v0.39, parameters: ILLUMINACLIP: 

136 TruSeq2-PE.fa: 2:30:10 LEADING:20 MINLEN:18) (26), and the trimmed libraries were 

137 subsequently subjected to another round of quality control with FastQC. Overlapping 

138 read pairs were merged using PANDAseq (v2.11) (27), and potential piRNA reads were 

139 then selected using Cutadapt (v3.5) (28), with a read length threshold set between 26 

140 and 31 nucleotides. Reads were aligned to the B. germanica genome assembly (v1.1) 

141 (23) with Bowtie2 (v2.4.4, parameters: -k 1, --score-min L,0,0) (29). Only the reads that 

142 mapped with no mismatches were retained, while mapped reads containing one or 

143 more mismatches were removed using samtools (v1.19.2) (30) by selecting alignments 

144 with “NM = 0”. 

145 To have a more consistent set of B. germanica piRNAs, reads that mapped to the same 

146 genomic locus and shared the first 26 nucleotides at the 5’ end, while only varying in 

147 their length at the 3’ end, were collapsed. The sequence with the highest expression 

148 among the sequences sharing the first 26 nucleotides was taken as the representative,

150 the total expression of the piRNA. Then, extremely infrequent piRNAs with an overall 

151 expression lower than 10 reads across all libraries were filtered out. The piRNAs

160 piRNA expression analysis 

161 Read counts were normalized using the DESeq2 size-factor normalization method 

162 (DESeq2 v1.38.3) (34). The resulting DESeq2-normalized counts were used for 

163 downstream quantitative analyses, such as the application of expression thresholds. 

164 For visualization analyses, variance-stabilized transformed (VST) values were obtained 

165 using the varianceStabilizingTransformation function from DESeq2. VST values were 

166 used for Principal Component Analysis (PCA), the computation of Shannon’s diversity 

167 index (vegan v2.6-4), and for heatmap generation (pheatmap v1.0.12). Differential 

168 expression analysis between contiguous ovarian development stages was performed 

169 with DESeq2 (FDR < 0.05; log 2FC > 2 | < -2).

187 188 Results 

189 The piRNA in the Blattella germanica ovary 

190 Since piRNAs are primarily derived from genomic regions associated with TEs, we 

191 examined the B. germanica genome to determine the frequency of TEs in this species. 

192 Our annotation of the total repetitive content of the B. germanica genome identified 

193 47.65% of the assembly as repetitive sequences. DNA transposons and LINEs are the 

194 most representative TE orders, spanning 10.93% and 9.71% of the genome, 

195 respectively (Table S1). The genome span of other TEs, such as LTRs, SINEs, and Rolling 

196 Circle, is much lower (0.49%, 0.45%, and 0.12%, respectively). Additionally, we 

197 identified other types of repeats, including simple repeats, microsatellites, low-

198 complexity repeats, and unclassified repeats, which together account for 25.95% of the 

199 genome. 

200 Here, we obtained 22 small RNA libraries from nymphal and adult B. germanica ovaries 

201 at different developmental stages, generating, on average, 13.13 ± 2.36 million reads 

202 per library. We selected reads ranging from 26 to 31 nucleotides, obtaining an average 

203 of 3.54 ± 0.80 million reads per library, of which 67.5% mapped to the reference 

204 genome (Table S2). After collapsing 3’ variants (identical sequences from the same 

205 genomic locus but differing at the 3' end), we obtained 3,979,726 unique sequences. 

206 Due to the significant number of lowly expressed sequences, a filtering step was 

207 performed, removing sequences with fewer than 10 identical reads across samples,

210 B. germanica piRNAs (78.34%) are 28-29 nucleotides long, with 28-nucleotide piRNAs 

211 being the most common length (45.08%; Fig 2A). A smaller proportion of putative 

212 piRNAs are 27 nucleotides long (12.90%), while the rest corresponds to sequences of 

213 26, 30, and 31 nucleotides (Fig 2A). As expected, the piRNA sequences display the 

214 typical U-bias at the first 5' nucleotide (Fig 2B). A search for piRNAs involved in the 

215 ping-pong pathway, namely with a 10-nucleotide overlap at their 5’ end and mostly 

216 with an A in position 10 (Fig 2D), revealed 84,369 piRNA sequences forming 274,606 

217 overlaps, representing 27.8% of all the identified piRNAs, most of them with a length 

218 of 28-29 nucleotides (Fig 2C). 

219 220 piRNA expression dynamics in the B. germanica ovary 

221 The dynamics of piRNA expression in the ovaries, during the first gonadotropic cycle ,

222 were analyzed using PCA (Fig 2E). The analysis revealed a clear divergence of samples 

223 along the developmental timeline, reflecting substantial changes during BOF 

224 development (Fig 2E). The first principal component (PC1) accounted for 44% of the 

225 variance, while the second (PC2) explained 27%. Ovaries of the last nymphal instar and 

226 newly emerged adults (AD0) formed a tight cluster, indicating that piRNA expression 

227 remains highly similar along those stages. Within this cluster of previtellogenic ovaries, 

228 the earliest stage (N6D0) is slightly separated along the first principal component 

229 (PC1), suggesting a minor shift in piRNA expression following the emergence to the last 

230 nymphal instar. At this moment, the BOF oocytes are previtellogenic, undergoing 

231 capacitation to enter a vitellogenic stage, while the follicular epithelia exhibit a high 

232 degree of proliferation (Fig 1). 

233 In contrast, samples of AD3, AD5, and AD7 ovaries show a broader dispersion along the 

234 PC1, indicating substantial changes in piRNA expression over oocyte maturation (Fig 

235 2E). The PCA analysis revealed a clear difference between ovaries from previtellogenic

243 The analysis of piRNA diversity, using Shannon’s diversity index (H’), revealed two 

244 distinct profiles (Fig 2F), which correspond to the clustering observed in the PCA. 

245 Libraries from immature ovaries exhibited greater diversity of piRNAs, as reflected by 

246 higher H’ values (Fig 2F), suggesting that the expression in these stages is more evenly 

247 distributed across different piRNAs. In contrast, vitellogenic ovaries exhibited reduced 

248 piRNA diversity, suggesting that during these stages, a smaller subset of distinct 

249 piRNAs is expressed at higher levels (Fig 2F). 

250 We examined piRNA abundances across a range of expression thresholds to clarify 

251 how the expression of several piRNAs contributes to the patterns seen in the PCA and 

252 Shannon’s diversity analysis (Figs 2E and 2F). The number of piRNAs with expression 

253 levels above 10 DESeq2-normalized counts is consistent across all ovarian libraries (Fig 

254 2G). However, when the threshold is raised to 100 DESeq2-normalized counts, a clear 

255 distinction emerges between mature and immature ovaries (Fig 2G’). The highest 

256 expressed piRNAs are more abundant in mature ovaries. When we further extend the 

257 threshold to piRNAs expressed above 1,000 DESeq2-normalized counts, the difference 

258 between mature and immature ovaries becomes even more evident, with mature 

259 ovaries containing about twice the amount of piRNAs compared to immature stages 

260 (Fig 2G’’).

270 The transition from an immature to a mature BOF (AD0 to AD3) is critical in the B. 

271 germanica ovary. In AD3, the proliferation of follicular cells in the BOFs begins to 

272 arrest, while oocytes grow by accumulating storage proteins, mainly vitellogenin (Fig 

273 1). During this transition, a significant number of piRNAs (17,683) are differentially 

274 expressed (Fig 3A). One major group is downregulated in AD3 as the BOFs begin to 

275 mature; subsequently, some of these piRNAs increase in expression again as the BOFs 

276 mature, approaching the end of the gonadotropic cycle. In contrast, a second group of 

277 piRNAs expressed at lower levels in previtellogenic ovaries shows elevated expression 

278 in AD3, maintaining these levels until the end of the gonadotropic cycle. 

279 In B. germanica females, during the transition from AD3 to AD5, JH levels in the 

280 hemolymph increase, and 20E begins to accumulate in the ovary. The follicular cells in 

281 the BOF arrest cytokinesis and become binucleated, entering polyploidy (Fig 1). During 

282 this transition, a notable change in piRNA expression occurs, with 20,994 piRNAs 

283 identified as differentially expressed (Fig 3B). Many of the piRNAs appear upregulated 

284 in AD5, and mostly maintain their expression levels in AD7, suggesting a specific role in 

285 the later stages of the gonadotropic cycle. Conversely, a group of piRNAs is 

286 downregulated in AD5. These downregulated piRNAs maintain the low expression 

287 levels until oviposition. 

288 The final transition, from AD5 to AD7, is critical for completing the gonadotropic cycle.

360 361 Discussion 

362 The piRNAs are a class of molecules with gene regulatory properties on TEs and RNAs 

363 (mRNAs and long non-coding RNAs) (39). In arthropods, piRNA expression has been 

364 found in various gonadal and somatic tissues (40–42). Among these, the ovaries, 

365 containing both somatic and germinal cell lineages, exhibit the highest abundance of 

366 piRNAs (43,44). 

367 TEs represent the primary genomic locations where piRNAs originate, TEs represent 

368 the primary genomic locations where piRNAs originate, and Petersen et al. (45) and 

369 Ylla et al. (46), suggest that TE occupancy generally increases with genome size in 

370 insects, although notable variability across species has also been documented. In the 

371 wasp Nasonia oneida , 32.29% of the genome consists of repetitive sequences (47), 

372 while in the honey bee Apis mellifera , only 9.46% of the genome is covered by 

373 repetitive sequences, while TEs represent only 3% (48). In Diptera, the percentage of 

374 TEs also varies significantly, from 20% in the Drosophila melanogaster genome (49) to 

375 47.7% of TEs in Aedes aegypti (50). This range is also present in hemimetabola insects. 

376 Among Orthoptera, in the cricket Gryllus bimaculatus , the TEs represent 28.94% of its 

377 genome (46), while in the locust Locusta migratoria , the presence of TEs reaches 65% 

378 (45). Our analysis determined that in the cockroach B. germanica , 47.65% of the

384 Remarkably, SINEs, although present in all insect orders, are less abundant (45). 

385 Given the abundance of TEs in insect genomes, it is expected to find a very high 

386 number of piRNAs in insects. This is the case of R. prolixus (53) and Aedes albopictus 

387 (54), with about 36 million and 12 million piRNAs reported, respectively. These 

388 numbers, however, may be overestimated, suggesting that more stringent criteria 

389 should be applied to accurately identify biologically active piRNAs and enable studies 

390 of their potential regulatory roles (55). Applying stringent conditions in B. germanica ,

391 collapsing identical sequences with varying levels of trimming and mapping at the 

392 same genome locus, and selecting the piRNA sequence with the highest expression, we 

393 significantly reduced the number of piRNAs previously identified (13). With the 

394 resulting 303,954 B. germanica piRNAs, it was possible to analyze their dynamic 

395 expression in ovaries. The PCA analysis provides initial evidence of the potential role of 

396 piRNAs in B. germanica ovarian maturation, showing clear distances between 

397 previtellogenic and vitellogenic ovaries, which could be determined by piRNA origin 

398 and number, at each stage. 

399 We found that the different TE families contribute similarly to the ovarian piRNA pool. 

400 Although the number of piRNAs mapping to some TE families is maintained throughout 

401 the gonadotropic cycle, it is in the last days of the cycle when it varies substantially. 

402 This is the case of piRNAs mapping the sense strand of SINA or the antisense strand of 

403 RC, which reduces their number significantly. Conversely, piRNAs mapping on DNA TEs, 

404 sense or antisense, increase significantly on the last day of the cycle. This data suggests 

405 that TEs play a role at the end of the cycle, either by providing piRNAs or being 

406 depleted by them.

407 The piRNAs mapping to genic regions (CDS and UTRs) in B. germanica are 

408 approximately 3.5%, a relatively low proportion compared to L. migratoria , another 

409 species with panoistic ovaries, where 11% of piRNAs are located in genic regions, 

410 including 8% in CDS (41). In other species with meroistic ovaries, such as the bug 

411 Oncopeltus fasciatus and the bumblebee Bombus terrestris , the proportion of piRNAs 

412 mapping to CDS is notably higher (25% and 48%, respectively), while the proportion 

413 mapping to UTRs remains relatively constant (41). 

414 In B. germanica , we observed significant changes in the abundance of piRNAs mapping 

415 to genic regions when comparing different stages of ovarian development. Notably, at 

416 the end of the gonadotropic cycle, in adult females, 5- and 7-day-old, we detected the 

417 greatest variability in the abundance of piRNAs mapping to the 5’ UTR and CDS and 

418 introns, in both the sense and the antisense strand. While for those piRNAs mapping 

419 the 3’UTR, the differences at the end of the cycle correspond to the piRNAs mapping 

420 the sense strand, while the ones mapping the antisense strand remain stable. 

421 All this data suggests a role of the piRNAs participating in the changes observed in the 

422 follicular cell program that occurs in the basal ovarian follicles (BOF) at the end of the 

423 gonadotropic cycle (Fig 1), where big changes occur at the genome level, to facilitate 

424 the chorion synthesis (3,20). 

425 Analyzing the piRNA expression across the different stages of B. germanica ovarian 

426 development, a few differentially expressed piRNAs (76 piRNAs) were detected in 

427 immature ovaries (N6D0-AD0), where piRNA expression is very stable. However, some 

428 piRNAs modify their expression just after the molt to the last nymphal instar, showing 

429 a significant downregulation. These expression changes could be related to the 

430 entrance of the B. germanica ovary in the maturing period, which starts early in this 

431 last nymphal instar and is marked primarily by the stabilization of the number of 

432 ovarian follicles in the ovarioles and the initiation of BOF maturation (3,16). Thus, the 

433 piRNA content in B. germanica ovary, and their expression levels clearly categorize the 

434 previtellogenic ovaries (with immature BOF), including those of newly molted adults, 

435 and separate them from mature ovaries (Fig 2E). In mature ovaries, the changes in
