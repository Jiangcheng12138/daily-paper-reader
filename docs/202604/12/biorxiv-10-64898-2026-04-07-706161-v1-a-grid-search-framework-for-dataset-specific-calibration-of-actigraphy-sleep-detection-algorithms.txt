Actigraphy is widely used for long -term sleep monitoring, but established sleep –7

wake scoring algorithms often require parameter tuning, which is commonly 8

performed manually and can reduce reproducibility. In this study, a grid -search -9

based calibration framework is presented for established actigraphy algorithms 10 

and evaluate whether it can serve as a practical alternative to manual tuning. The 11 

method was evaluated using two datasets: a multi -subject polysomnography -12 

validated actigraphy dataset and a self -collected dual -device dataset. In the 13 

polysomnography -validated dataset, grid -search optimization produced 14 

performance patterns similar to manual parameter selection, while slightly 15 

improving detection of sleep onset and sleep offset and yielding modest gains in 16 

wake -sensitive metrics. In the dual -device dataset, consensus and majority 17 

voting were useful for reducing the influence of brief wake episodes occurring 18 

within the main sleep period, including micro -awakenings that can fragment sleep 19 

predictions across individual algorithms. Overall, these findings show that grid -20 

search can replace manual parameter tuning with a more explicit and 21 

reproducible procedure while providing small improvements in sleep timing 22 

estimation and benefiting ensemble -based handling of within -sleep wakefulness. 23

1. Introduction 24 

Actigraphy, the continuous measurement of physical activity using wearable 25 

accelerometers, has become a cornerstone tool in sleep research and clinical 26 

practice. Unlike polysomnography (PSG), which requires specialized equipment 27 

and laboratory settings, actigraphy enables long -term, non -invasive monitoring of 28 

sleep patterns in naturalistic environments. Consequently, actigraphy is widely 29 

used in large -scale epidemiological studies, psychiatric research, and 30 

longitudinal monitoring of circadian rhythms (Fekedulegn et al., 2020; Smith et 31 

al., 2018) .32 

A fundamental step in actigraphy analysis is the classification of sleep and wake 33 

states from activity signals. Over the past decades, numerous rule -based 34 

algorithms have been developed for this purpose, including the Cole –Kripke, 35 

Sadeh, Oakley, MASDA, and Crespo methods (Cole et al., 1992; Crespo et al., 36 

2012; Loock et al., 2021; NR Oakley, 1997; Sadeh et al., 1994) . These 37 

algorithms differ in their mathematical formulations, temporal smoothing 38 

strategies, and assumptions regarding the relationship between movement and 39 

sleep –wake states. Each algorithm also includes one or more tunable 40 

parameters, such as activity thresholds or smoothing window lengths, which 41 

strongly influence classification outcomes (Blackwell et al., 2011; Quante et al., 42 

2018) .43 

A major practical challenge in actigraphy research is that optimal parameter 44 

settings vary substantially across devices, populations, and recording contexts 45 

(Cellini et al., 2013; Meltzer et al., 2012) . Differences in sensor sensitivity, device 46

parameter values validated in one study often perform poorly when applied to 48 

new datasets (Hjorth et al., 2012) . As a result, researchers frequently rely on 49 

manufacturer defaults or informal manual tuning, leading to inconsistent results 50 

and reduced reproducibility across studies (De Souza et al., 2003; Quante et al., 51 

2018) .52 

Existing approaches typically treat algorithm selection and parameter tuning as 53 

independent decisions, optimizing each algorithm separately . However, this 54 

practice neglects an important observation: classical actigraphy algorithms often 55 

exhibit complementary strengths and weaknesses. For example, temporally 56 

smoothed algorithms may accurately capture consolidated sleep periods but fail 57 

to detect brief awakenings (Sadeh, 2011; Sadeh et al., 1994) , whereas threshold -58 

based algorithms may detect short wake bouts but produce fragmented sleep 59 

estimates. Consequently, selecting a single algorithm or tuning it in isolation may 60 

introduce systematic bias. 61 

A systematic calibration framework is proposed in this work to address this 62 

problem, harmonizing multiple classical sleep detection algorithms for a given 63 

dataset and individual. Rather than developing a new predictive model, the goal 64 

is to automate the process of dataset -specific parameter tuning while reducing 65 

researcher degrees of freedom (Simmons et al., 2011) .66 

The central idea explored in this work is that agreement between diverse sleep 67 

detection algorithms can be used as a practical criterion for selecting stable 68 

parameter configurations in the absence of labeled training data. Because 69

2. Methods 117 

2.1 Study Design and Datasets 118 

The proposed framework is designed as a dataset -specific calibration pipeline 119 

for harmonizing classical actigraphy sleep detection algorithms. Rather than 120 

training a predictive model, the method optimizes algorithm parameters 121 

independently for each recording to account for device characteristics, individual 122 

activity patterns, and recording conditions .123 

The framework was evaluated using two datasets with distinct purposes. 124 

2.1.1 Dataset 1: Polysomnography -Validated Actigraphy Dataset 125 

A multi -subject dataset containing concurrent wrist -worn actigraphy and 126 

polysomnography (PSG) recordings was used to evaluate calibrated algorithm 127 

outputs against physiological sleep staging. Activity signals were recorded at 1 -128 

second resolution and later aggregated to 1 -minute epochs. 129 

PSG sleep stages were binarized into wake (stage 0) and sleep (stages N1, N2, 130 

N3, REM) to provide a reference standard for epoch -level comparison (Walch et 131 

al., 2019) .132 

2.1. 2 Dataset 2: Dual -Device Self -Recording Dataset 133 

A self -recorded dataset was collected to develop and illustrate the calibration 134 

pipeline. A single participant simultaneously wore a research -grade actigraph 135 

(ActiLumus) and an Apple Watch on opposite wrists for ten consecutive days. 136

2.2 Data Preprocessing 141 

All datasets underwent standardized preprocessing to ensure consistency across 142 

recordings. 143 

2.2.1 Temporal Alignment and Epoch Standardization 144 

Activity data were resampled to uniform 60 -second epochs. For high -resolution 145 

signals, missing timestamps were first interpolated to create continuous timelines 146 

before aggregation .147 

2.2.2 Complete -Day Filtering 148 

Only complete recording days containing exactly 1,440 epochs were retained to 149 

avoid edge effects in circadian analyses. 150 

2.3 Non -Wear Detection 151 

Off -wrist periods were identified using an adapted Choi algorithm based on 152 

extended zero -activity runs with artifact tolerance (Choi et al., 2012) . Continuous 153 

low -activity segments exceeding a minimum duration threshold were classified as 154 

non -wear. 155 

2.4 Activity Imputation 156 

A two -stage imputation procedure replaced activity values during non -wear 157 

periods using time -of -day baseline activity patterns (dataset 1 and 2) . For 158

2. 5 manual adjustment 161 

For comparison with the automated calibration framework, each algorithm was 162 

also evaluated using manually selected parameter settings. In this configuration, 163 

parameter values were adjusted iteratively while visually inspecting the resulting 164 

sleep –wake classifications alongside the raw activity signal. 165 

Parameter combinations were modified until the detected sleep periods aligned 166 

with extended intervals of reduced activity that visually resembled consolidated 167 

sleep bouts. This procedure reflects common practice in exploratory actigraphy 168 

analyses, where parameter settings are often adjusted to match observable 169 

activity patterns in the absence of a physiological ground truth. 170 

Because actigraphy is frequently used in long -term behavioral monitoring without 171 

concurrent polysomnography, this manual tuning approach provides a practical 172 

baseline representing typical real -world usage conditions. The manually selected 173 

configurations were therefore used as a reference for evaluating the potential 174 

benefits of the proposed automated calibration framework. 175

coefficient (MCC). MCC and balanced accuracy were prioritized to account for 179 

the inherent class imbalance between sleep and wake states in actigraphy data 180

2. 7 Implementation and Reproducibility 184 

The sleep -wake scoring algorithms used in this study were implemented based 185 

on their original published descriptions and validated against the open -source 186 

pyActigraphy software package (Hammad et al., 2021) . The present work does 187 

not introduce novel implementations of these algorithms; rather, the 188 

methodological contribution lies in the proposed multi -objective calibration and 189 

harmonization framework. 190 

2. 8 Sleep Onset and Offset Detection and Evaluations 191 

Sleep onset and offset times were derived from the binary sleep –wake 192 

sequences produced by each algorithm and by the reference labels. For each 193 

subject, records were ordered by timestamp and short wake interruptions of ≤10 194 

consecutive minutes occurring within sleep were bridged to reduce the influence 195 

of brief awakenings. After this gap -filling step, the main sleep episode was 196 

defined as the longest contiguous run of sleep epochs in the recording. Sleep 197 

onset was taken as the first epoch of this main sleep bout, and sleep offset as 198 

the last epoch of the same bout. 199 

Algorithm -derived onset and offset times were compared with the corresponding 200 

reference times obtained from PSG (Dataset 1) or Apple Watch -derived binary 201 

labels (Dataset 2). Timing errors were calculated in minutes as algorithm 202 

estimate minus reference estimate, such that positive values indicate a later 203 

estimated event and negative values indicate an earlier estimated event. 204

3 Grid -Search workflow and rational e208 

The proposed calibration framework was applied to identify parameter 209 

configurations for multiple classical actigraphy sleep –wake algorithms that 210 

produce physiologically plausible and mutually consistent sleep classifications. 211 

Rather than optimizing algorithm parameters against a labeled reference dataset, 212 

the framework uses a consensus -based optimization strategy, selecting 213 

parameter configurations that maximize agreement between algorithms while 214 

enforcing physiological plausibility constraints. 215 

The calibration procedure consisted of three main stages: candidate parameter 216 

filtering, consensus -based optimization, and selection of the final parameter 217 

configuration (Fig. 1). These stages progressively reduced the parameter search 218 

space and identified a parameter combination that yielded consistent sleep –wake 219 

classifications across algorithms. 220 

3.1 Candidate Parameter Filtering 221 

The calibration process began with a broad grid search over candidate 222 

parameter values for each algorithm. The evaluated algorithms included Cole –223 

Kripke, Sadeh, Oakley, Crespo, and MASDA (Roenneberg), each of which 224 

contains parameters controlling classification thresholds, temporal smoothing, or 225 

activity weighting. 226

For each algorithm, a predefined parameter grid was constructed spanning a 227 

range of plausible values based on algorithm design and prior usage. Each 228 

parameter combination was applied to the activity signal to produce a binary 229 

sleep –wake mask, representing epochs classified as sleep or wake. 230 

Because some parameter configurations can produce unrealistic sleep 231 

estimates, a physiological plausibility filter was applied to remove implausible 232 

results. Specifically, configurations were retained only if the proportion of epochs 233 

classified as sleep fell within a predefined range of 10 –50 % of the recording 234 

duration. This filtering step eliminated parameter settings that yielded extreme 235 

outcomes such as near -continuous sleep or almost no detected sleep. 236 

Even after this filtering stage, the number of valid parameter configurations could 237 

remain large. To ensure computational feasibility in the subsequent optimization 238 

stage, candidate configurations were further reduced by retaining a diverse 239 

subset of valid runs for each algorithm. Diversity was assessed based on 240 

predicted sleep percentages and mask variability, ensuring that the remaining 241 

candidates covered a broad range of plausible behaviors while limiting the total 242 

number of configurations. 243 

This stage therefore produced a manageable set of candidate parameterizations 244 

for each algorithm while ensuring that all retained configurations generated 245 

physiologically plausible sleep estimates. 246 

3.2 Consensus -Based Optimization 247

After identifying valid parameter candidates for each algorithm, the next stage 248 

aimed to determine which combination of parameter settings across algorithms 249 

produced the highest agreement in sleep –wake classification. 250 

Candidate configurations from all algorithms were combined to form multi -251 

algorithm parameter sets. For each combination, agreement between the 252 

resulting sleep masks was quantified using the mean pairwise Jaccard similarity, 253 

which measures the overlap between binary sleep –wake sequences. High 254 

Jaccard similarity indicates that multiple algorithms classify the same epochs as 255 

sleep or wake, suggesting greater consistency in sleep detection. 256 

The primary optimization objective was to maximize the mean pairwise Jaccard 257 

similarity across all algorithms. This approach treats agreement between 258 

established algorithms as a proxy for reliable classification when an external 259 

ground truth is unavailable. 260 

When multiple parameter combinations produced identical agreement scores, 261 

two secondary selection criteria were applied to break ties: 262 

1.  Consistency of predicted sleep duration. Preference was given to 263 

parameter combinations that minimized the standard deviation of 264 

predicted sleep percentages across algorithms, ensuring that different 265 

algorithms estimated similar total sleep durations. 266 

2.  Physiological plausibility of sleep duration. Solutions were favored 267 

when the mean predicted sleep percentage across algorithms was closer 268 

to a target value representing typical nightly sleep proportions. 269

Through this consensus -based optimization process, the framework identified 275 

parameter combinations that produced the most mutually consistent sleep –wake 276 

classifications across algorithms while maintaining physiologically plausible sleep 277 

estimates. 278 

3.3 Selected Parameter Configurations 279 

The parameter combination achieving the highest consensus score was selected 280 

as the final calibrated configuration for each algorithm. These optimized 281 

parameters were then applied to generate the final binary sleep –wake masks for 282 

all algorithms. 283 

Using these calibrated parameters, two ensemble sleep -wake masks were 284 

additionally constructed: 285 

• Strict consensus mask , where an epoch was labeled as sleep only if all 286 

algorithms agreed. 287 

• Majority voting mask , where an epoch was labeled as sleep when more 288 

than half of the algorithms classified the epoch as sleep. 289 

These ensemble masks provide complementary perspectives on sleep detection. 290

4.3 Comparison between tuning approaches 394 

To evaluate the impact of parameter selection on algorithm performance, results 395 

obtained using manually selected parameter configurations ( Fig. 2 ) were 396 

compared with those obtained using grid -search optimized parameters ( Fig. 3 ). 397 

Overall performance patterns were broadly similar between the two approaches. 398 

Across all algorithms, sleep detection remained highly sensitive, with recall 399 

values consistently approaching unity under both parameter configurations. This 400 

indicates that both tuning strategies preserved strong detection of PSG -defined 401 

sleep epochs. However, differences emerged in metrics reflecting wake detection 402 

and overall classification balance. Grid -search optimization generally resulted in 403 

modest improvements in balanced accuracy, specificity, and agreement -based 404 

metrics, including Cohen’s κ and Matthews Correlation Coefficient (MCC). These 405 

improvements suggest that automated parameter optimization provided better 406 

calibration of algorithm thresholds, leading to improved discrimination between 407 

sleep and wake epochs. In contrast, manually selected parameters tended to 408 

maintain high overall accuracy and F1 -scores but showed comparatively lower 409 

performance in metrics sensitive to class imbalance. Per -subject metric 410 

distributions further illustrated these differences. While both approaches 411 

demonstrated limited variability in sleep sensitivity across subjects, the grid -412 

search optimized configuration showed slightly reduced variability in specificity 413 

and agreement -based metrics, indicating more consistent performance across 414 

the cohort. Despite these improvements, the overall classification patterns 415

5. Discussion 530 

The main contribution of this study is demonstrating that classical actigraphy 531 

sleep –wake algorithms can be calibrated simultaneously using an unsupervised, 532 

consensus -based grid -search framework without compromising performance 533 

relative to manual parameter adjustment. This addresses a practical challenge in 534 

wearable sleep research: many real -world studies, particularly long -term 535 

behavioral monitoring, lack physiological references such as PSG, yet 536 

investigators must still select plausible and reproducible algorithm parameters. 537 

Current sleep medicine guidance supports the use of actigraphy but emphasizes 538 

that interpretation depends strongly on the scoring approach and context of use 539 

(Smith et al., 2018) .540 

A central finding is that the grid -search strategy identified parameter sets that 541 

produced high agreement across algorithms while maintaining physiologically 542 

plausible sleep estimates. Traditional workflows often rely on default settings, 543 

proprietary software parameters, or manual tuning guided by visual inspection. 544 

Although practical, these approaches are difficult to standardize and reproduce 545 

across studies or raters. Even structured manual scoring introduces subjectivity 546 

and requires explicit procedures to achieve reproducibility (Breneman et al., 547 

2024) .548 

The proposed framework replaces subjective adjustment with an explicit 549 

optimization rule. Instead of relying on visual judgment of whether detected 550 

intervals “look like sleep,” the algorithm searches a predefined parameter space, 551 

removes implausible solutions, and selects the configuration that maximizes 552

It is made The copyright holder for this preprint (which this version posted April 9, 2026. ;https://doi.org/10.64898/2026.04.07.706161 doi: bioRxiv preprint

inter -algorithm agreement. Although the method still depends on assumptions 553 

about plausible sleep proportions and algorithm behavior, it makes the calibration 554 

procedure transparent, auditable, and repeatable. Future work will extend this 555 

approach by incorporating short -term activity averaging (e.g., 30 –60 minute 556 

sliding windows) to detect sustained low -activity intervals that may correspond to 557 

resting periods. 558 

Importantly, consensus does not imply validity. Algorithms may agree while 559 

sharing systematic biases. In PSG comparisons, the calibrated algorithms 560 

showed the typical actigraphy pattern of high sleep sensitivity but lower wake 561 

specificity, with moderate balanced accuracy, kappa, and MCC. This reflects a 562 

known limitation of wrist actigraphy rather than a failure of the framework. 563 

Numerous studies show that actigraphy detects sleep well but tends to 564 

overestimate sleep and underestimate wake, particularly quiet wakefulness and 565 

wake after sleep onset (Quante et al., 2018) .566 

Thus, consensus -based calibration improves reproducibility and provides a 567 

principled way to tune parameters without labels but cannot overcome the 568 

fundamental sensing limitations of wrist accelerometry. Because accelerometers 569 

measure movement rather than neurophysiological sleep, immobile wakefulness 570 

remains difficult to distinguish from sleep. This explains why high recall and 571 

overall accuracy were accompanied by lower specificity and moderate chance -572 

corrected agreement metrics, consistent with previous actigraphy studies 573 

(Quante et al., 2018) .574

This perspective also clarifies the comparison with manual tuning. In this study, 575 

manual parameter selection involved visually adjusting outputs until detected 576 

sleep periods appeared to align with low -activity intervals. While this incorporates 577 

contextual human judgment and can adapt to unusual recordings, it is difficult to 578 

standardize and vulnerable to operator bias. The grid -search framework offers a 579 

rule -based alternative that can be applied consistently across recordings. The 580 

fact that optimized configurations generally improved classification balance and 581 

timing agreement relative to manual tuning suggests that systematic parameter 582 

search can outperform ad hoc adjustment under the conditions examined here. 583 

Human scorers tend to prefer outputs that appear physiologically plausible and 584 

internally coherent; the framework operationalizes similar principles through 585 

explicit constraints and agreement criteria. This is particularly valuable in large -586 

scale or technical studies where reproducibility is essential. The importance of 587 

subject -specific calibration is also supported by prior work showing that 588 

personalized models can outperform generalized ones in actigraphy -based sleep 589 

estimation (Khademi et al., 2019) .590 

The ensemble results provide a second contribution. Combining calibrated 591 

algorithms through strict consensus and majority voting produced interpretable 592 

outputs. Ensemble approaches are attractive because classical actigraphy 593 

algorithms differ in their assumptions about thresholds, smoothing, and sleep 594 

continuity. Majority voting reduces the influence of individual algorithm biases, 595 

whereas strict consensus emphasizes high -confidence intervals where all 596

These strategies involve different trade -offs. Strict consensus is conservative and 599

continuity of the main sleep episode but may inherit actigraphy’s tendency to 602 

classify quiet wake as sleep. Accordingly, ensemble choice should depend on 603 

the application: majority voting may be preferable for estimating overall sleep 604 

windows, whereas strict consensus may better identify high -confidence sleep 605 

intervals. 606 

Sleep onset and offset analyses further highlight where consensus -based 607 

optimization is beneficial. Grid -searched configurations generally aligned more 608 

closely with reference timing and reduced error relative to manual tuning, 609 

particularly for sleep onset. Because onset and offset represent transition points, 610 

parameter settings that improve agreement around transitions naturally improve 611 

timing estimates. Nevertheless, Bland –Altman analyses showed wide limits of 612 

agreement for some algorithms, reflecting persistent uncertainty in actigraphy -613 

based timing estimation. This is consistent with literature indicating that 614 

actigraphy is more reliable for estimating overall sleep schedules and sleep 615 

opportunity than for precisely resolving sleep latency, wake after sleep onset, or 616 

fragmentation relative to PSG (Conley et al., 2019) .617

agreement among existing algorithms, so performance is constrained by their 648 

shared biases. Second, physiological plausibility constraints such as sleep -649 

percentage bounds encode prior assumptions that may vary across populations 650 

or study designs. Third, the manual baseline reflects realistic exploratory practice 651 

but did not involve multiple raters, so comparisons between manual and 652 

automated tuning should be interpreted as proof of principle. Finally, the multi -653 

day dataset involved a single participant with a smartwatch -derived reference, 654 

limiting generalizability. 655

Future work should evaluate the framework across larger and more diverse 657

disruption, or neurological disorders. Additional unsupervised objectives —such 659 

as transition consistency, day -to -day regularity, or alignment with behavioral 660 

markers like sleep diaries, light exposure, or heart -rate -derived sleep periods —661 

could also be incorporated. Extending the ensemble approach toward weighted 662 

or probabilistic consensus may also improve robustness. 663

and scientifically defensible approach for tuning classical actigraphy algorithms 665
