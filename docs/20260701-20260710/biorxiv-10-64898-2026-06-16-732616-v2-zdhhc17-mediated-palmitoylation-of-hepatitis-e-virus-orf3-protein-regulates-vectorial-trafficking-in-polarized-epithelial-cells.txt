Abstract 24 

The hepatitis E virus (HEV) is a leading cause of acute hepatitis worldwide and is transm itted 25 

enterically along the gut –liver axis. Epithelial cell polarity in the gut and liver plays a critical 26 

role in HEV transmission. In intestinal epithelial cells, HEV enters through the apical 27 

membrane and is released basolaterally to access the bloodstr eam, whereas in hepatocytes, 28 

the virus enters basolaterally and is secreted apically into the bile duct. In this study, we sought 29 

to identify the viral and host determinants governing directional HEV secretion in both tissue 30 

types. Using polarized intestinal and hepatocyte models, we found that the small 31 

phosphoprotein ORF3, which is essential for progeny secretion, localizes predominantly to the 32 

apical membrane, in contrast to the ORF2 capsid protein. We further identified the 33 

palmitoyltransferase ZDHHC17 as a specific ORF3 interactor that mediates its apical 34 

localization and promotes HEV progeny release. Using automated cell segmentation and 35 

quantitative image analysis, we screened a panel of ORF3 mutants and found that positively 36 

charged residues within the N -terminus regulate membrane association. In addition, we 37 

identified a conserved PIFIQP motif that mediates the critical interaction with the ankyrin 38 

repeat domain (ARD) of ZDHHC17. Leveraging this interaction, we generated high -confidence 39 

struc tural models of ORF3 in complex with the ZDHHC17 ARD using AlphaFold. These 40 

models, further validated by molecular dynamics simulations, revealed additional ORF3 41 

residues involved in the interaction. Collectively, our findings define key mechanisms 42 

underly ing directional HEV release and provide broader insights into trafficking processes in 43 

polarized epithelial cells. 44

Introduction 46 

Hepatitis E virus (HEV) is a major cause of acute hepatitis worldwide and belongs to 47 

the Hepeviridae family 1. Human -infecting HEV genotypes are classified within the 48 

Paslahepevirus balayani species, which comprises eight genotypes (HEV -1 to HEV -8) 2. HEV -49 

1 and HEV -2, are restricted to humans and transmitted via the fecal –oral route, mainly through 50 

contaminated water. Although infection is typically self -limit ing in healthy individuals, HEV -151 

and HEV -2 are associated with high mortality rates in pregnant women 3. In contrast, HEV -352 

and HEV -4 are zoonotic and are primarily transmitted to humans through consumption of 53 

contaminated meat products, e.g. from domestic pigs or wild boar 4. These genotypes are most 54 

prevalent in industrialized countries. While the majority of HEV -3 and HEV -4 infections are 55 

asymptomatic, they can become chronic in immunosuppressed individuals, who have an 56 

elevated risk of developing liver cirrhosis, and co nsequently, liver failure 5.57 

The positive -sense, single -str anded HEV RNA genome of approximately 7.2 kilobases 58 

encodes at least three open reading frames (ORFs). ORF1 is thought to be translated as a 59 

polyprotein and encodes the nonstructural proteins required for viral replication (reviewed in 6).60 

ORF2 encodes the capsid protein and plays a critical role in counteracting innate and adaptive 61 

host immune responses (reviewed in 7). ORF3 is a small phosphoprotein of approximately 113 62 

amino acids that is proposed to function as an ion channel 8. Phosphorylation at serine 80 is 63 

thought to mediate interaction with the capsid protein ORF2 9. At its N -terminus, eight cysteine 64 

residues undergo palmitoylation, which is essential for membrane association of ORF3 10 . One 65 

of the most prominent roles of ORF3 is the secretion of HEV progenies 11 . Through its 66 

conserved proline –serine –alanine –proline (PSAP) motif, ORF3 interacts with the endosomal 67 

sorting complexes required for transport (ESCRT) protein TSG101 12 , thereby facilitating virus 68 

budding into multivesicular bodies (MVBs) in a non -cytolytic manner. During this proces s, and 69 

despite the absence of viral glycoproteins, HEV particles acquire a host cell –derived 70 

membrane envelope that is decorated with ORF3 13 . Consequently, HEV particles released 71 

into the cell culture supernatant or circulating in the blood of infected patients are quasi -72 

enveloped 14 . Although this envelope, derived from the trans -Golgi network (TGN) 73 

membrane 15 , reduces cell attachment and entry , it protects viral particles from neutralizing 74 

antibodies, likely facilitating spread within the infected host. In contrast, HEV shed in feces or 75 

present in HEV -replicating cells are non -enveloped , highly stable, and infectious, enabling the 76 

transmission to new hosts 16 .77 

Epithelial cells are polarized in order to maintain their barrier function while enabling 78 

tr ansport functions. To this end, they have distinct membranes : On the one hand , the apical 79 

membrane, fac ing the outside environment and on the other hand , the basolateral membrane, 80 

facing neighboring cells and underlying tissue. HEV uniquely depends on tiss ue epithelial cell 81 

polarity for its enteric transmission. Following entry via the gastrointestinal tract, HEV is 82 

thought to infect the intestinal epitheli um through the apical membrane and enters the 83 

bloodstream through release from the basolateral membran e into the bloodstream . Once in 84 

circulation, the virus reaches the liver, where it enters hepatocytes via the basolateral 85 

membrane and is primarily released from the apical membrane into the bile duct 16 . When 86 

transported with the bile back to the intestine, the quasi -envelope is removed and HEV is 87 

ultimately shed in feces, enabling transmission to new host s. Until now, knowledge of how the 88 

virus hijacks the polarized trafficking machinery of intestinal epithelial cells and hepatocytes 89 

has been lacking .90 

Recent studies have shown that the ORF3 protein is required for apical HEV progeny 91 

release from polarized hepatocytes and liver organoids 17 –19 . In agreement, ORF3 was mainly 92

ORF3, but not the ORF2 protein, is mainly apically localized in polarized intestinal and liver 109 

epithelial cells 110 

In order to identify the structural determinant governing vectorial release of HEV in 111 

either tissue context, we first investigated the subcellular localization of ORF2 and ORF3 112 

proteins in hepatocytes and intestinal epithelial cells infected with the HEV -3 Kernow -C1/p6 113 

strain. Notably, whereas intestinal epithelial cells typically exhibit a 2 -dimensional (2D) 114 

columnar polarization, with an apical membrane facing t he lumen and the basolateral side 115 

facing the bloodstream, hepatocytes display a more complex, 3 -dimensional (3D) architecture 116 

with multiple apical and basolateral membrane domains in vivo (review ed in 23 , Fig. 1A). 117 

First, we employed hepatocellular model systems that recapitulate cell polarity at 118 

increasing levels of complexity. In no n-polarized hepatoma S10 -3 cells infected with HEV, 119 

ORF2 was distributed throughout the cytoplasm and formed fibrillar structures, whereas ORF3 120 

localized predominantly to a confined perinuclear region, consistent with previous reports 24 .121 

Notably, a fraction of ORF2 colocalized with ORF3 within this perinuclear compartment. (Fig. 122 

1B). Then , we infected columnar polariz ed human induced pluripotent stem cell (hiPSC) -123 

derived hepatocyte -like cells (HLCs) 25 . Similar to non -polarized hepatocytes, ORF2 was 124 

detected throughout the cell body, while ORF3 localized almost exclusively to the apical 125 

membrane in polarized HLCs (Suppl. Fig. 1). Finally, we used bipotent HepaRG cells, which 126 

upon differentiation acquire a complex 3D polarization characterized by multiple apical and 127 

basolateral membrane domains, and the formation of functional biliary canaliculi -like 128 

structures 26 . In HEV -infected differentiated HepaRG cells, ORF2 again showed a broad 129 

intracellular distribution, whereas ORF3 localized predominantly to apical membranes, as 130 

evidenced by strong co -localization with F -actin or MRP2 , which is enriched at the apical 131 

region of polarized hepatocytes (Fig. 1C , Suppl. Fig. 2 )27 .132

the cells while ORF3 was confined to a defined perinuclear region (Fig. 1D). We then infected 136

ZDHHC17 mediates ORF3 palmitoylation to control its apical localization and HEV progeny 148 

release 149 

To identify potential ORF3 -interacting host factors involved in its apical trafficking and 150 

directional progeny release, we perfo rmed an ORF3 interactome screen. For this purpose, we 151 

ectopically expressed ORF3 in hepatoma HepG2/C3A cells as an N -terminal fusion protein 152 

with an HA -tag, alongside a mEGFP control. We immunoprecipitated ORF3::HA from cell 153 

lysates and identified associat ed proteins by mass spectrometry. 154 

As shown in Fig. 2A, this approach identified numerous components of the vesicle 155 

sorting machinery, such as VPS28 or VPS37C. Among the strongest candidates was the 156 

ESCRT protein TSG101, which has previously been reported to interact with ORF3 30 .157 

Importantly, we also identified ZDHHC17, a member of the aspartate –histidine –histidine –158 

cysteine (DHHC) family of S -acyltransferases. This fam ily spans at least 23 integral membrane 159 

enzymes called palmitoyl acyl transferases (designated ZDHHC 1 –23) that mediate S -160 

palmitoylation on cysteines. All members of the ZDHHC family share a conserved DHHC 161 

catalytic motif, located within a cysteine -rich do main that faces the cytosol and that is essential 162 

for enzymatic activity. Other important domains include transmembrane , SH3, PDZ binding, 163 

and ankyrin repeat domains (ARD) 31 .164 

To validate the role of ZDHHC17 in HEV proge ny release from intestinal cells, we 165 

performed siRNA -mediated knockdown of ZDHHC17 alongside ZDHHC13, the only other 166 

ZDHHC family member containing an ARD, which is implicated in protein -protein interactions 167 

(Fig. 2B). We also knocked down ZDHHC9, a distan tly related family member that lacks the 168 

ARD. Intestinal Caco -2 cells were electroporated with in vitro -transcribed Kernow -C1/p6 RNA, 169 

followed by transfection with siRNAs to downregulate the respective transferase. We 170 

assessed the release of infectious par ticles into the supernatant by titration on naïve 171 

HepG2/C3A cells and quantif ied ORF2 -positive foci forming units. 172 

Compared to the non -targeting (NT) control, knockdown of ZDHHC9 and ZDHHC13 173 

had no detectable effect, whereas downregulation of ZDHHC17 reduc ed the release of 174 

infectious HEV progeny by approximately 50% (Fig. 2C). This reduction correlated with the 175 

efficiency of ZDHHC17 mRNA knockdown, as assessed by qRT -PCR (Suppl. Fig. 3A). 176 

Western blot analysis of transfected cell lysates further revealed th at only ZDHHC17 177 

knockdown, but not that of ZDHHC9 or ZDHHC13, abolished ORF3 palmitoylation (Fig. 2D). 178 

Together, these findings indicat e that ZDHHC17 specifically , rather than other palmitoyl 179 

acyltransferases, is required for the trafficking and secretion of infectious HEV particles. 180 

To achieve a more efficient downregulation of ZDHHC17, we generated a stable Caco -181 

2 cell line using a selectable lentiviral system expressing a doxycycline (dox) -inducible shRNA 182 

targeting ZDHHC17 (Caco -2_shZDHHC17), which is bi cistronically linked to a red fluorescent 183 

protein (RFP). When c ompared to siRNA -mediated knockdown, this approach resulted in a 184 

more pronounced reduction of ZDHHC17 mRNA levels (Suppl. Fig. 3B). 185

Mutational screening of charged residues in ORF3 reveals novel motifs critical for its apical 210 

localization in polarized cells 211 

Viral proteins hijac k the cellular trafficking machinery to navigate in polarized cells and 212 

reach specific subcellular regions (reviewed in 33 ). To access this machinery, viruses often 213 

encode residues that mediate binding to host trafficking factors and/or acquire post -214 

translational modifications that serve as sorting signal s34,35 . Thus, we next performed an 215 

alanine -scanning mutagenesis screen across ORF3 to assess which residues could be 216 

involved in its apical localization in polarized cells .217 

To analyze the impact of mutations on apical ORF3 localization, we developed an 218 

automated image analysis pipeline that enables quantitative assessment of fluorescence 219 

signals in 2D -columnar polarized cells (Fig. 3A). To this end, we stained polarized Ca co -2220 

cells with MemBrite ®-Fix , which is a specific dye for plasma membranes, and acquired ~130 221 

z-stack images (spacing of 0.13 μm) per cell on a confocal mic roscope. 222 

Region of interest (ROI) selection was performed by maximum -intensity projection of 223 

the GFP channel of the 3D image along the z -axis after exclusion of the lowest five z -slices, 224 

thresholding the resulting maximum -intensity projection image using Otsu’s method 36 ,225 

morphological hole filling, and identifying the connected components. If the largest connected 226 

component covered 5 –20% of the maximum -intensity projection image, the bounding box of 227 

this component was expanded symmetrically by 25% along the x -axis and the y -axis, and then 228 

selected as ROI . The ap ical region was identified based on its enrichment of upper membrane 229 

signals. To localize the apical re gion, the membrane channel was partitioned into 12 × 12 pixel 230 

patches in the xy -plane. For each patch, membrane inten sity profiles along the z -axis were 231 

computed and the uppermost z -position satisfying one of two conditions was selected as the 232 

top border o f the apical region: (1) The membrane intensity at this z -position and the next two 233

Structural model ing and molecular dynamics simulations define the molecular determinants of 303 

ORF3 –ZDHHC17 interaction 304 

Previous studies have described ORF3 as an intrinsically disordered protein 38 , which 305 

heavily impairs reliable modeling of its structure and protein -protein interactions. Despite this, 306 

we aimed to furt her characterize the molecular basis of the interaction between ORF3 and 307 

ZDHHC17 using Alpha Fold . To this end, we generated full -length complex models using 308 

AlphaFold3, considering both palmitoylated and non -palmitoylated ORF3 together with the 309 

ZHHC17 ARD . Consistent with its intrinsically disordered nature, ORF3 exhibited globally low 310 

confidence scores (pLDDT , Suppl. Fig. 8A, 8B). Nevertheless, in models incorporating 311 

palmitoylation, the PIFIQP motif was more frequently positioned in proximity to the ZDHHC17 312 

ARD, as reflected by a shorter average distance between the center of geometry (COG) of 313 

the ARD and the PIFIQP motif (19.8 Å ± 5.7 Å , Suppl. Fig. 8C) compared to non -palmitoylated 314 

models (31.1 Å ± 8.1 Å , Suppl. Fig. 8D). While these differences are modest, they supported 315 

the rationale for exploring alternative modeling strategies using truncated ORF3 constructs 316 

focused on the interaction region. This does not necessarily reflect a true stabilizing effect of 317 

lipidation, but rather the fact that Alpha Fold performs better when more of the biological 318 

context is included. 319 

In the model with the highest confidence score, palmitoylated cysteines consistently 320 

localized in proximity to the catalytic DHHC motif (residues 464 –467), while the membrane 321 

insertion predicted using the PPM webserver, supported a coherent orientation in which the 322 

transmembrane region of ZDHHC17 is correctly embedded and palmitoylated ORF3 cysteines 323 

are positioned within the membrane plane (Fig. 5A) 39,40 . Interestingly, basic residues within 324 

the N -terminal region of ORF3 identified in our mutagenesis screen (RHR23 –25 and R29) 325 

were oriented toward the membrane interface, suggesting a role in enhancing electrostatic 326 

interact ions with the membrane (Fig. 5Ai). Together, these observations support a model in 327

indicating a reproducible binding mode rather than a model -specific arrangement. 340 

We next performed an analysis (based on < 4 Å distance cutoff) to define contacts, 341 

identifying three principal ARD regions that contribute to the interaction (Fig. 5C): a short 342 

helical segment (residues 67 –68) and two helix –loop regions (residues 90 –103 and 122 –137). 343 

Key residues involved included TRP97, ILE100, ASN101, ASP123, LEU124, TRP131, 344 

ARG134, and GLN135. The interface is characterized by a combination of hydrophobic and 345 

polar contacts, with a pronounced contribution from hydrophobic residues forming a patch on 346 

the ARD surface. Importantly, residues immediately adjacent to the PIFIQP motif (THR64, 347 

PRO65, SE R66, and ILE69) also contributed significantly to binding, indicating that the 348 

interaction extends beyond the core hexapeptide and involves a broader sequence context. 349 

To assess the stability of the predicted interface, we performed molecular dynamics 350 

(MD ) simulations using the same ORF3 fragment spanning residues 37 –83. Four 351 

representative models were selected from extensive AlphaFold2 sampling to ensure robust 352 

statistics and capture conformational diversity within the truncated ORF3 construct. 353 

Free -energ y landscapes projected along principal components revealed 354 

heterogeneous behavior across these models: some conformations remained confined to 355 

narrow energy basins (models 0 and 1), whereas others sampled broader conformational 356 

space (models 10 and 16; Fig . 6A). Notably, we superposed structures on the ARD and 357 

performed principle component analysis on a minimal ORF3 segment encompassing the 358 

interaction interface (±4 residues around the PIFIQP motif), reducing noise to focus on the 359 

flexibility of this segmen t. Although the ARD was not restrained, it remained relatively stable 360 

throughout the simulations. 361 

Despite the observed variability among the four models, residue -wise contact stability 362 

(R -value 41 , calculated using a ≤5 Å distance cutoff) showed that the interaction between ORF3 363 

and the ARD was consistently maintained (Fig. 6A). The same ARD residues retained high 364 

contact frequencies, supporting a stable binding interface once formed. In addition to the wild -365 

type PIFIQP sequence, we simulated the AAFIAA mutant (both shown in Sup pl . Vid . 1) . This 366 

substitution disrupted G LN 62 within the motif and reduced interact ion density in the C -terminal 367 

region of the ARD (Fig. 6B , 6C ), highlighting the importance of this residue for complex 368 

stabilization. The MD simulations further confirmed the involvement of key C -terminal ARD 369 

residues in interacting with the ORF3 PIFIQP mo tif (Fig. 6B , 6C ), consistent with the 370 

AlphaFold2 -based contact analysis (Fig. 5C ). 371 

Interestingly, residues flanking the canonical motif (THR64, PRO65, SER66) showed 372 

increased contact stability in the AAFIAA mutant, suggesting compensatory interactions. This 373 

behavior likely reflects the physicochemical properties of the interface: the ARD binding region 374 

forms a partially hydrophobic patch, and substitution with alanine residues does not strongly 375

Discussion 384 

The directional release of HEV particles along the gut –liver axis has previously 385 

suggested the existence of a cell type -specific traf ficking machinery that differs between 386 

polarized hepatocytes and intestinal epithelial cells. In this study, we demonstrate that 387 

directional HEV trafficking toward apical membranes in polarized epithelial cells appears 388 

largely tissue -context independent. W e identi fi ed ORF3 as the key structural viral determinant 389 

that requires lipidation by the palmitoyltransferase ZDHHC17. Previous studies showed that 390 

palmitoylation of ORF3 is important for membrane association 10 ; here, we demonstrate that 391 

this modification also regulates its apical trafficking . An unbiased mutagenesis screen, 392 

combined with AlphaFold modeling and molecular dynamics simulations, further indicated that 393 

this process is governed by N -terminal basic residues that facilitate membrane anchoring, 394 

thereby likely positioning O RF3 in proximity to the catalytic site and enabling interaction with 395 

the ZDHHC17 ARD through its PIFIQP motif. Importantly, we show that this interaction is 396 

critical for efficient ORF3 palmitoylation, which in turn mediates apical trafficking of ORF3 and 397 

promotes HEV progeny release. 398 

Conserved apical HEV release from polarized epithelial cells along the gut -liver axis 399 

Despite the distinct functions and polarization of epithelial cells in the liver and 400 

intestine, apical ORF3 trafficking appears to be conserved across these tissues. Consistent 401 

with this, enteric viruses are typically released from the apical membrane of the intestinal 402 

epithelium 42,43 , enabling transmission via the intestinal lumen and facilitating rapid, efficient 403 

spread between hosts, as observed, for example, for rotaviruses. In contrast, viruses targeting 404 

the liver are generally released from the basolateral surface of hepatocytes 44,45 , promoting 405 

systemic dissemination via the bloodstream. 406 

Although we have used apical ORF3 trafficking as a proxy for apical release, our 407 

findings are consistent with recent reports demons trating active infection of the gut and apical 408 

progeny release from intestinal cultures 17,22 . Together, these observations support the notion 409 

that the intestine represents a major si te of HEV infection , particularly during acute, self -410 

limiting infections, which are often asymptomatic or mild and lack classical hepatitis symptoms 411 

such as jaundice. 412 

While the majority of HEV particles are released apically from polarized enterocytes, a 413 

fraction is also secreted basolaterally, likely as quasi -enveloped particles 17 . Although this 414 

pathway appears inefficient, it still permits viral spread to the liver and contributes to intra -host 415 

dissemination. Subsequent liver infection could, in turn, favor prolonged or even persistent 416 

infection, potentially due to the relatively immunotolerant hepatic environment 46 .417

ZDHHC17 is the specific enzyme mediating palmitoylation of ORF3 and thereby its apical 419

While polarized trafficking pathways in epith elial cells are generally well characterized, 421 

it remains poorly understood how viruses exploit these mechanisms to achieve directional 422 

release 47 . In particular, the molecular determinants that direct viral proteins into apical 423 

tra fficking routes remain largely undefined. 424 

Our findings indicate that palmitoylation is a key determinant in this process. This lipid 425 

modification promotes membrane association and influences partitioning into specific 426 

membrane microdomains, such as lipid r afts, which are linked to apical sorting via recycling 427 

endosomes 48 –50 . Previous studies have shown that palmitoylation of ORF3 mediates 428 

membrane association and facilitates HEV progeny release 10 . Here, we extend these 429 

observ ations by demonstrating that palmitoylation also functions as a sorting signal that favors 430 

apical trafficking. 431 

Future studies, for example, through biochemical isolation and characterization of lipid 432 

raft -associated ORF3 complexes in polarized cells, will be required to further dissect how lipid 433 

modifications contribute to the spatial organization of directional viral trafficking pathways. In 434 

addition, it will be important to determine the intracellular fate of non -palmitoylated ORF3, 435 

which is the predomina nt form either in the presence of downregulated ZDHHC17 (Fig. 2) or 436 

following mutation of the ORF3 PIFIQP motif (Fig. 4), and to identify the compartments to 437 

which it is redirected instead of trafficking to the apical membrane. 438 

The specificity of ZDHHC17 i n mediating ORF3 palmitoylation was unexpected, 439 

particularly given the close homology to ZDHHC13, which did not show a comparable effect 440 

in our system. However, substrate recognition within the ZDHHC family is not completely 441 

understood, and both overlappin g and selective enzyme -substrate relationships have been 442 

reported 51 . In addition, specific interaction motifs within substrates can be recognized by 443 

dedicated domains suc h as the ARD of ZDHHC17, which mediates selective protein 444 

recruitment 52 . Importantly, MD analysis revealed that residues within AR domain 5—445 

specifically TRP197, TYR200, and ARG201 —also contribute to the ORF3 –ZDHHC17 446 

interaction. Although ZDHHC13 is generally homologous to ZDHHC17, these critical residues 447 

differ between the two proteins (Suppl. Fig. 10 ). This highlights that, despite overall 448 

redundancy within the ZDHHC family, individual enzymes can display pronounced substrate 449 

specificity, a feature that has been described for other viral proteins exploiting host 450 

palmitoylation machinery 53,54 . This specificity makes ZDHHC17 an attractive candidate for 451 

targeted antiviral inter vention, as modulation of individual palmitoyltransferases is increasingly 452 

considered a viable therapeutic strategy 55 .453 

Interaction with ZDHHC17 enables modeling of ORF3 structure 454 

As ORF3 is predicted to be largely intrinsically disordered traditional structure -based 455 

modeling has been notoriously challenging and the structural basis of ORF3 function has 456 

remained elusive 38 . Here, by placing ORF3 in a more physiological context, including 457 

palmitoylation and membrane embedding, we obtained a high -confidence model using 458 

AlphaFold3 for the first time. 459 

This model provided first structural insights into the ORF3 –ZDHHC17 interaction 460 

despite the overall disorder of the protein. First, this model revealed that N -terminal basic 461 

residues adjacent to the critical cysteines do not appear to play a role in ZDHHC17 interaction 462 

per se , but are rather oriented toward the membrane interface, suggesting a role in enhancing 463 

electrostatic interactions with the membrane. This is consistent with previous reports showing 464 

that positively charged residues are often located near acylated cysteines in diverse cellular 465

addition, an automated image analysis pipeline for quantification of apical signals was 491

polarized epithelial cells. 493
